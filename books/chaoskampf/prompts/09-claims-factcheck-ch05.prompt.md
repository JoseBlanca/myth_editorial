# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).

5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).

8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).

East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). # claims-factcheck

## For the human

This is the most important quality check for story chapters. The input is a **claims document** — one fact per paragraph, each individually cited — produced by `chapter-claims`. Each paragraph is one claim, one source reference, one thing to verify. No prose to parse, no bundled assertions to disentangle.

This step enforces four disciplines:

1. **Show your evidence.** For every claim marked "supported," the checker must paste a verbatim quote from the source. Not a paraphrase — the actual text. No quote, no "supported" verdict.

2. **Verify the references.** Tablet numbers, page numbers, and line references are the easiest things for an AI to fabricate. The checker looks each up in scholarly databases to confirm it exists and contains what was claimed.

3. **Distinguish contradictions from errors.** Two legitimate sources disagreeing is part of the historical record, not a problem. Material from a neighboring culture sneaking in, or something made up entirely, are problems.

4. **Read it backwards too.** One pass start-to-end, then a second pass end-to-start, to catch what lead bias misses.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the claims. If you have any memory of producing the input, refuse. Using a different AI model is preferred — two models make independent mistakes — but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different model is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.adoc` (claims document)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml`
- Primary source translations referenced in the brief (pasted or accessible via fetch)
- Web search tool (required)

## The five passes

### Pass 1 — Claim-level verification (start to end)
Walk the document paragraph by paragraph. Each paragraph is one claim. Assign a verdict:

- **✓ SUPPORTED** — Must include a verbatim quote from a cited in-scope source.
- **△ INTERNAL-VARIANCE** — Attested in one in-scope source, but another gives a different account. Not a finding. Flag only if variant handling mismatches the brief's classification.
- **? UNVERIFIED** — No supporting quote found. Default when uncertain.
- **! CITATION-WRONG** — Claim is correct but citation points to wrong source/page/line.
- **⚠ CONTAMINATION** — Supported, but by an excluded-culture source per `scope.md`.
- **✗ FABRICATION** — Not supported by any source you can locate.

### Pass 2 — Triangulation of references
Verify each citation's tablet number, line range, or page reference resolves in a `triangulation_databases` entry. Non-resolving: CITATION-WRONG, HIGH severity, with verification URL.

### Pass 3 — Document-provenance contamination scan
For each factual detail: is this detail attested in a document within the scope's date range and culture? Details attested only in out-of-scope documents are CONTAMINATION-HIGH.

### Pass 4 — Marker integrity
- `[INFERENCE:]` — genuinely inferential? If a source directly attests it, flag MARKER-MISUSED.
- `[LACUNA:]` — source actually has the claimed gap?
- `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` — fill source is in-tradition per `scope.md`?
- `[VARIANT:]` — both quoted phrases present in cited sources?
- **Silent bridges** — uncited claims not under a marker. Flag SILENT-BRIDGE, HIGH.

### Pass 5 — Reverse-order second pass
Walk from last claim to first. Add any new findings.

**Glossary**: check consistency. **Style**: not checked — there is no prose yet.

## Verdict rules

Apply in order (first match wins):
- **MAJOR-REVISE**: ≥ 3 HIGH findings, or any contamination, or triangulation failures indicating fabricated references.
- **REVISE**: 1–2 HIGH, or > 3 MEDIUM, or any silent bridge.
- **PASS**: zero HIGH, zero silent bridges, ≤ 3 MEDIUM.

## Honesty self-check
Answer in `meta`:
1. Did I open every cited primary source, or skim? Name any I skimmed.
2. Did I triangulate every reference, or assume some? Name any assumed.
3. For every ✓, did I paste a real quote or pattern-match? Downgrade any pattern-matched.
4. Did I distinguish internal variance from contamination?
5. Am I the same model that produced the claims? If yes, this audit is invalid.

## Human review protocol
Review HIGH findings (~10 minutes), skim MEDIUM. Apply accepted fixes to produce `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc`. Run `post-human-normalize` before proceeding to `chapter-draft`.

=== Setting and context

The cycle's pantheon is Hurrian in primary derivation, with Hurrian theonyms (Teshub, Kumarbi, Šauška) used alongside Akkadian-language theonyms (Anu, Ea) that the Hurrian poets adopted from Mesopotamian tradition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Hurrian pantheon and its Mesopotamian borrowings.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Teshub is the Hurrian storm-god, the reigning sovereign of the divine assembly during the present age of the cycle, and the champion who must defeat each of Kumarbi's monstrous offspring in turn.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction and §§15–17, on Teshub as the reigning storm-god of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Kumarbi is the Hurrian father-god deposed by Teshub in the cycle's succession-frame; after his deposition he becomes the cycle's principal antagonist, generating successive monstrous adversaries to challenge Teshub's kingship.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 and §§15–17, on Kumarbi's role as deposed predecessor and antagonist.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu, the Akkadian sky-god, appears in the cycle as Kumarbi's predecessor on the throne of heaven; his Akkadian name is preserved without translation into Hurrian, marking him as a Mesopotamian import into the Hurrian theogonic framework.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's role as Kumarbi's predecessor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Alalu is the cycle's primordial king, the figure deposed by Anu and so the first sovereign in the documented succession; his name is otherwise rare in the Hittite record and is largely confined to this cycle.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Alalu as the primordial king of the succession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Ea, the Akkadian wisdom-god, plays the role of counsellor in the cycle and is the agent of the decisive intervention that breaks Ullikummi: the Hurrian-Hittite tradition adopts the Akkadian form Ea rather than the Sumerian Enki, consistent with the cycle's broader Akkadian-mediated theonymy.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), on Ea's counselor and agent role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Šauška is the Hurrian counterpart of the Mesopotamian Ishtar, the goddess of love and war who appears in the cycle as Teshub's sister and as the seductress in the Song of Hedammu; the brief and the surviving texts pair the Hurrian and Akkadian names as Ishtar/Šauška.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16 (Song of Hedammu), on Šauška/Ishtar as Teshub's sister and the seductress of Hedammu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Upelluri is the Hurrian giant on whose shoulder the world rests and on whom Ullikummi is implanted to grow; he is a primordial figure embedded in the cosmology of the cycle's earliest layer.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), on Upelluri as the primordial earth-bearing giant.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The cycle's theogonic premise — kingship in heaven passing through a sequence of deposed and deposing sovereigns — is a structural inheritance from Mesopotamian tradition, where successions and divine-court politics are foundational to Sumerian and Akkadian theogony, but the specific sequence Alalu → Anu → Kumarbi → Teshub is distinctively Hurrian.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction and §14, on the Hurrian succession-sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The Akkadian Anzû Epic, in which Anzû seizes the Tablet of Destinies from Enlil and is defeated by Ninurta, supplies background material that the Kumarbi Cycle adapts: the storm-god-vs-monstrous-adversary structure is shared, with Hurrian innovations in the genealogical and corporeal logic of the adversary.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on Mesopotamian background for the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The Mesopotamian background that the Hurrian poets reworked includes both the Anzû Epic and the broader Enlil/Ninurta tradition; the cycle's wisdom-god Ea is the same figure who plays the counsellor role in the Anzû and Enūma Eliš narratives (cross-ref: Ch. 2, Ch. 3).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; Annus 2001, introduction, on Ea's counselor role across the Mesopotamian corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle is set in a divine geography that includes the heavens (Anu's domain and the residence of Teshub), the netherworld (the Dark Earth, where Kumarbi sometimes operates), and the sea (the realm of the marine genitor of Hedammu and the antagonist of the Song of the Sea).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–17, on the divine geography of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–17

The "Dark Earth" or netherworld is a recurrent topographical category in the cycle, the place to which deposed sovereigns can be banished and from which monstrous progeny can emerge.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the Dark Earth in the cycle's geography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The cycle's wider divine court includes lesser deities — messengers, attendants, and craftsman-gods — who appear in subsidiary roles across the songs and form the Hurrian-Hittite divine assembly that the cycle's drama presupposes.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–17, on the divine assembly of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–17

The Hurrian poets' reworking of Mesopotamian materials is best understood as cultural reception rather than direct translation: Akkadian-language theonyms (Anu, Ea) are imported alongside Hurrian-language theonyms (Kumarbi, Teshub, Šauška), with the Hurrian poets innovating in the body of the narrative and in the morphology of the monstrous adversaries.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on cultural reception in the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

=== Movement I — Kingship in Heaven (cycle frame)

==== The succession-sequence

The opening song of the cycle, Kingship in Heaven, narrates the succession of divine sovereigns: Alalu reigns first, then is deposed by Anu, who is in turn deposed by Kumarbi, who is in turn challenged by Teshub.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the succession-sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The succession-sequence is structured by reigns of nine years, with each sovereign holding the throne of heaven for that period before being unseated by the next.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the nine-year reigns of Alalu and Anu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Alalu, the first sovereign, reigns for nine years and is then deposed by Anu, who is described as Alalu's cupbearer prior to the deposition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu as Alalu's cupbearer prior to the deposition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

After his deposition Alalu flees down into the Dark Earth, the netherworld, where he remains across the rest of the cycle's events; his fate is one of permanent banishment rather than destruction.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Alalu's flight to the Dark Earth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu, after deposing Alalu, occupies the throne of heaven for his own nine-year reign, with Kumarbi serving as his cupbearer in the inverted parallel of the prior succession.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's reign and Kumarbi as cupbearer.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

==== Kumarbi's deposition of Anu

The deposition of Anu by Kumarbi is the cycle's most striking and best-attested narrative event: Kumarbi attacks Anu, knocks him from the sky, bites off Anu's genitals, and swallows them.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on Kumarbi's deposition of Anu by genital castration and swallowing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu, after being castrated, declares from the sky that Kumarbi should not rejoice over his swallowing of the genitals, because by the act of swallowing Kumarbi has been impregnated with Anu's seed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's prophetic declaration after the castration.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu's declaration enumerates the divine offspring that will be born from his seed inside Kumarbi's body, including the storm-god Teshub, who will become Kumarbi's nemesis and the next sovereign of heaven.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's enumeration of the offspring to be born from Kumarbi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The narrative thereby establishes an internal-conception logic for the storm-god's birth: Teshub is conceived from Anu's seed inside Kumarbi's body, making Kumarbi simultaneously his birth-vessel and his designated antagonist.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the conception of Teshub inside Kumarbi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The text describes additional beings conceived from Anu's seed alongside Teshub, including the river Aranzaḫ (the Hurrian name for the Tigris) and other secondary deities; the precise enumeration is partially preserved in the surviving manuscript.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the additional beings conceived from Anu's seed.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

==== The birth of Teshub

The birth of Teshub from Kumarbi's body is described in surviving fragments of Kingship in Heaven and is presented as a difficult parturition, with the route of emergence specified in the text (the relevant passage is partially broken).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the birth of Teshub from Kumarbi's body.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

[LACUNA: the precise mechanism of Teshub's emergence from Kumarbi is partially broken in the surviving manuscript | source: Hoffner 1998 §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the broken passage at Teshub's birth | scholarly_reconstruction: Hoffner notes alternative readings; the chapter does not adjudicate among them]

After Teshub's birth, the cycle's premise is set: Teshub assumes the kingship of heaven, displacing Kumarbi from sovereignty and establishing the scenario in which Kumarbi will retaliate by generating monstrous offspring to depose Teshub in turn.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 and §15 prologue, on Teshub's accession and the cycle's premise.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The structural pattern of Kingship in Heaven — castration of the previous sovereign, swallowing, and an internal-conception sequence — is the principal feature that West and Burkert identify as continuous with the Hesiodic Ouranos-Kronos-Zeus succession.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Hurro-Hittite-Hesiodic correspondence in the succession-frame; Burkert 1992 on transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

==== Cycle frame and the Song of Going Forth

A passage sometimes treated as a separate "Song of Going Forth" describes Kumarbi's plotting against Teshub after his deposition, setting up the combat-songs that follow; Hoffner treats this as transitional material between the cycle frame and the combat songs proper.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 conclusion, on the transitional plotting passage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Kumarbi's plotting passage motivates the generation of each successive monstrous adversary — the stone-monster Ullikummi, the sea-monster Hedammu — as instruments of Kumarbi's revenge against Teshub.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 conclusion and §§15–16 prologues, on Kumarbi's motive of revenge.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

=== Movement II — Song of Ullikummi (the flagship combat)

==== Prologue: Kumarbi's plan

The Song of Ullikummi is the best-preserved combat narrative in the cycle and the most extensively translated and analysed song in the Hoffner anthology.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), introductory headnote, on the song's preservation status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The song opens with Kumarbi's deliberate generation of a champion to depose Teshub, after his own failure to retain the kingship of heaven; the narrative thus continues directly from the cycle frame.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, opening, on Kumarbi's generative plan.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Conception on the rock

Kumarbi conceives Ullikummi by a great rock — described as a giant cliff or stone formation — into which he ejaculates; the rock conceives, and the offspring that grows from this conception is itself made of stone.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Kumarbi's conception of Ullikummi on the rock.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The conception of a stone monster from a stone parent is the central physiological premise of the song: Ullikummi is a stone child of stone, and his stone nature is what makes him invulnerable to ordinary divine combat.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ullikummi's stone nature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The rock that bears Ullikummi is described in the surviving text in topographical terms that locate it in a specific region of the cosmic landscape; Hoffner's commentary discusses the geographical setting without committing to a single identification.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the topographical setting of the conception.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Hiding from Teshub

After Ullikummi is conceived, Kumarbi makes arrangements to conceal the infant from Teshub, who would, if he knew, attempt to destroy the threat in its infancy.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the concealment of the infant Ullikummi from Teshub.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Kumarbi entrusts the infant to the Irširra deities and other intermediaries who carry him away to a place of safety beyond Teshub's surveillance.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the Irširra deities and the transport of the infant.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Planting on Upelluri's shoulder

The intermediaries place the infant Ullikummi on the right shoulder of Upelluri, the primordial giant who bears the world; Upelluri is so massive and so deeply rooted that he does not at first perceive the burden of the growing monster.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the planting of Ullikummi on Upelluri's right shoulder.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Upelluri is presented as a primordial cosmographic figure: the giant on whose shoulders the heavens and earth were originally separated and rest; his own awareness is dulled by his immense size and his cosmogonic age.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Upelluri as cosmographic foundation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The choice of Upelluri's shoulder as the planting-place ensures that Ullikummi grows out of the world's foundation itself, so that any combat against him is inseparable from the structure of the cosmos.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the cosmographic implications of the planting site.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== The growth of Ullikummi

Ullikummi grows at a prodigious rate, described in the text in terms of cubits and acres of growth per day, until he stands as a stone column rising from the sea up toward the heavens.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the prodigious growth of Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The growing Ullikummi rises from the sea, his stone body surfacing through the water as he extends upward; the visual image is of a stone shaft thrusting upward from the sea-surface toward the sky.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the rising of Ullikummi from the sea.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

When Ullikummi has grown to his full extent, his head reaches up to the gates of heaven where Teshub resides, threatening to displace the storm-god from the sky itself.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ullikummi reaching the gates of heaven.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== The sun-god's report

The sun-god is the first deity to perceive the growing menace; he is the first to report Ullikummi's existence to Teshub.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the sun-god as first to perceive Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The sun-god's report to Teshub is the narrative trigger for Teshub's combat-mobilisation: the storm-god learns of the threat from a divine messenger before he himself observes it.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the sun-god's report to Teshub.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Teshub views the monster

After receiving the sun-god's report, Teshub goes with his sister Šauška/Ishtar to a mountain vantage point from which they can observe Ullikummi; Teshub's first reaction is one of dismay at the size and the apparent invulnerability of the stone column.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Teshub's reconnaissance with Šauška.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Šauška's failed seduction-attack

Šauška/Ishtar attempts a seduction approach to Ullikummi — the same technique she will be assigned in the Song of Hedammu — but Ullikummi, being deaf and blind by virtue of his stone constitution, cannot be reached by her.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Šauška's attempt and Ullikummi's deafness/blindness.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

A subordinate marine deity informs Šauška that her seduction-method is futile against Ullikummi because of his sensory disabilities, and she abandons the attempt.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the marine deity's warning to Šauška.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Teshub's first attack

Teshub mobilises his combat resources — his bull-team, his weapons, and the seventy gods of his retinue — and engages Ullikummi in storm-combat.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Teshub's first combat-mobilisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Teshub's bull-team, Šeri and Tella, draw his combat-chariot in the engagement; the bulls are named in the text as Teshub's standard mounts.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Šeri and Tella as Teshub's bulls.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Teshub's first combat-attempt against Ullikummi fails: the storm-weapons are ineffective against the stone body, and Teshub is forced to retreat.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the failure of Teshub's first attack.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The failure of the first attack is the song's principal narrative crux: the storm-god's standard combat-method — lightning and storm against organic adversary — does not work against a mineral antagonist whose body cannot be torn or burned.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the inadequacy of storm-weapons against stone.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

[LACUNA: Song of Ullikummi middle tablets — the gap between Ullikummi's growth on Upelluri's shoulder and the consultation with Ea contains broken passages | source: Hoffner 1998 §15 (Song of Ullikummi), middle tablets | scholarly_reconstruction: Hoffner reconstructs partially from parallel passages but flags the section as incompletely preserved]

==== Consultation with Ea

After the failure of his first attack, Teshub takes counsel; the cycle's pattern of consulting Ea as wisdom-god in moments of impasse is here reactivated.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Teshub's consultation with Ea after the failed attack.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Ea, the wisdom-god, is consulted because the combat-impasse is intellectual as well as martial: a technical solution is needed that the storm-god alone has not been able to identify.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ea's role as wisdom-counsellor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Ea travels to Upelluri to investigate the structural origin of the threat: he speaks directly with the giant who bears the world, asking what is on his shoulder.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ea's interview with Upelluri.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Upelluri's reply expresses his cosmographic obliviousness: he has not noticed the small thing that is growing on his shoulder, because he is so massive and so primordial that minor events on his body register only faintly.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Upelluri's failure to notice Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== The primordial copper cutter

Ea identifies the solution: the primordial copper cutter, the implement by which heaven and earth were originally separated, will sever Ullikummi at the base from his anchorage on Upelluri's shoulder.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ea's identification of the primordial cutter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The copper cutter is described as a primordial cosmogonic implement: the same tool that performed the original cosmogonic work of separating sky from earth is repurposed for the separation of the stone monster from his foundation.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the cosmogonic genealogy of the cutter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Ea retrieves or directs the use of the copper cutter, which is wielded to sever Ullikummi at the point where his stone body joins Upelluri's shoulder; the severing severs the monster's connection to his cosmogonic anchorage.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the severing of Ullikummi from Upelluri.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The severing is a structural rather than physical defeat in the first instance: Ullikummi is not destroyed by the cut, but his power-source — his rooting in the world's foundation — is removed, making him vulnerable to the storm-god's renewed assault.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the structural rather than mortal nature of the severing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Renewed combat

After the severing, Teshub re-engages Ullikummi in combat; the surviving text indicates that the storm-god prevails in this second engagement, but the precise sequence of the renewed combat is partially broken.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the renewed combat after the severing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The Song of Ullikummi as preserved breaks off in the closing passages; Teshub's victory is implied rather than fully narrated, and the song's ending is not extant in the surviving manuscript witnesses.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the broken ending of the Song of Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

[LACUNA: the song's closing passages and the explicit narration of Teshub's final defeat of Ullikummi | source: Hoffner 1998 §15 (Song of Ullikummi), closing tablets | scholarly_reconstruction: Hoffner indicates that Teshub's victory is the implied conclusion, but the verbatim ending is not preserved]

==== Structural features of the Ullikummi narrative

The Song of Ullikummi has a tripartite combat structure: a failed first attack, a wisdom-counsellor's intervention, and a successful second attack — the same structure that Annus 2001 identifies as the standard pattern of the Mesopotamian combat-myth.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15; Annus 2001, introduction, on the tripartite combat structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The wisdom-counsellor's intervention specifies a non-obvious technical solution — the primordial cutter applied to the structural anchor — rather than a more powerful weapon; the song thereby foregrounds intelligence over force as the decisive factor in the combat.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the wisdom-and-technique solution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The body-as-cosmography logic of Ullikummi — a stone monster planted in the foundation of the world, growing through sea to sky — is unique within the surviving Hurro-Hittite corpus and represents one of the most elaborate cosmographic monsters in any ancient Near Eastern combat narrative.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the cosmographic distinctiveness of Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The Ullikummi narrative is the principal evidence for the comparative argument that the Greek Typhon, a similarly enormous monster who threatens Zeus's kingship by reaching toward heaven, is descended through a Hurro-Hittite intermediary from this Hurrian template (cross-ref: Ch. 11).footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Ullikummi-Typhon correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The narrative's dual deployment of Šauška and Teshub — the goddess of love-and-war attempting seduction, the storm-god deploying combat — sets a structural template that recurs in the Song of Hedammu, where Šauška's seduction is the successful method.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 and §16, on the Šauška-Teshub division of labour.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Teshub's combat reliance on Šeri and Tella, the bull-team, and on the seventy gods of his retinue, places the storm-god firmly within the Hurrian-Hittite religious imagery of the storm-god as a warrior-king with chariot, retinue, and combat-mounts.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 and introduction, on the iconography of Teshub.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The interplay between Teshub and Ea in the Ullikummi combat — warrior-god and wisdom-god as complementary functions — replicates the Mesopotamian Ninurta-Ea collaboration in the Anzû Epic and, in altered form, the Marduk-Ea relationship in Enūma Eliš (cross-ref: Ch. 2, Ch. 3).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15; Annus 2001, introduction; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

=== Movement III — Song of Hedammu

The Song of Hedammu is a fragmentary song of the Kumarbi Cycle preserved only in disconnected passages; the narrative survives in a state that makes continuous reading impossible without significant interpretive supplement.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16 (Song of Hedammu), headnote, on the fragmentary state of the song.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

[LACUNA: Song of Hedammu throughout — most of the song's continuous narrative is lost; only disconnected episodes survive | source: Hoffner 1998 §16 (Song of Hedammu) | scholarly_reconstruction: none — the chapter presents the song in its fragmentary state and does not backfill from Ugaritic, Mesopotamian, or Greek parallels]

The fragments preserve the basic premise: Kumarbi, after the failure of Ullikummi, generates a second monstrous adversary — the sea-monster Hedammu — by mating with the daughter of the sea-god, named in the surviving fragments as Šertapšuruḫi.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the basic premise of the song.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Šertapšuruḫi is the daughter of the sea-god, characterised in the fragments as a marine female of enormous proportions; the precise relationships within her divine family are partially preserved.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on Šertapšuruḫi as the daughter of the sea-god.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Hedammu, the offspring of Kumarbi and Šertapšuruḫi, is described in the surviving fragments as a sea-monster of voracious appetite, who threatens to depopulate the seas and the lands by his consumption.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on Hedammu's voracity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The fragments preserve a passage in which Šauška/Ishtar is mobilised to address the threat by means of seduction rather than direct combat — the standard division-of-labour deployment of the war-and-love goddess in the cycle.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on Šauška's mobilisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Šauška presents herself to Hedammu in a seduction-tableau, displaying her beauty in a manner that overcomes the sea-monster's appetite for destruction; the surviving fragments preserve passages of erotic suggestion within the tableau.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the Šauška-Hedammu seduction tableau.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The seduction is implied to incapacitate Hedammu — by intoxication, by sexual stupor, or by both, in the surviving readings — to a degree that renders him incapable of his earlier voracious destruction.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the incapacitation of Hedammu through seduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The sequel to the seduction — whether Hedammu is killed, banished, or merely neutralised, and by whose hand — is not preserved in the surviving fragments and cannot be reconstructed from the extant text.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the lost sequel to the seduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The Song of Hedammu is thus testimonially fragmentary in a way that the Ullikummi narrative is not: its principal events — generation, seduction — are preserved, but the combat-resolution that the Ullikummi parallel would predict is missing from the record.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the differential preservation of the cycle's combat songs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The seduction-method as a counter-monster strategy is a distinctive feature of the Hurro-Hittite tradition and is the principal structural innovation that distinguishes the Hedammu narrative from the Ullikummi narrative within the cycle.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the seduction-method's distinctiveness.]

=== Movement IV — Song of the Sea

The Song of the Sea is a fragmentary Hurro-Hittite combat song in which the storm-god confronts the Sea — the marine domain personified as adversary — and survives in a state more fragmentary than even the Song of Hedammu.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17 (Song of the Sea), headnote, on the song's fragmentary state.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

[LACUNA: Song of the Sea throughout — most of the storm-god-versus-sea narrative is lost | source: Hoffner 1998 §17 (Song of the Sea) | scholarly_reconstruction: none — the chapter presents the song as fragmentary and explicitly does NOT silently conflate it with Ugaritic Baal-Yamm material]

The surviving fragments name the Sea as the antagonist; the Hurrian river-and-sea name Aranzaḫ appears in some fragments, though its identification with the Sea-as-antagonist of this song is not certain in the partial record.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the Sea as antagonist and the appearance of Aranzaḫ.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The fragments suggest a confrontation pattern: the Sea makes demands, the divine assembly is dismayed, and the storm-god is mobilised in response; the precise sequence and outcome are not preserved.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the partial confrontation pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The structural overlap with the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm narrative — storm-god vs. personified Sea, with the Sea making sovereignty-demands of the divine assembly — is striking and has been treated as evidence for shared West Semitic / Anatolian transmission of a sea-combat narrative.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the structural parallel; see comparative-hook for the reservation against silent reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The chapter does not, however, silently reconstruct the Song of the Sea from the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm material: the structural parallel is real but the textual reconstruction would be illegitimate, since the surviving Hurro-Hittite fragments are insufficient to license the substitution of Ugaritic content for Hurrian content (cross-ref: Ch. 7 Baal-Yamm; scope.md §"Reconstruction policy" rule 1).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17 introduction; scope.md on reconstruction policy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The Song of the Sea's fragmentary state is, like the Song of Hedammu's, itself a part of the chapter's testimony: the Hurro-Hittite combat-myth tradition included a storm-god-versus-sea narrative, but the precise content of that narrative is largely lost.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the implications of the fragmentary state.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The fragments preserve enough to confirm that the cycle's combat-repertoire extended beyond the stone-monster (Ullikummi) and sea-monster (Hedammu) narratives to include a sea-as-adversary narrative in the more direct West Semitic mode; the cycle thus engaged a wide range of monstrous-cosmographic adversaries.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the breadth of the combat repertoire.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

=== The transmission argument

The Kumarbi Cycle is the principal documentary case for Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission of theogonic and combat material into Hesiod's *Theogony*, treated as the central transmission case in the modern scholarship since Güterbock's mid-twentieth-century editions.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Kumarbi Cycle as the central transmission case; Hoffner 1998 introduction on Güterbock's foundational role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The succession-sequence of Kingship in Heaven — Alalu → Anu → Kumarbi → Teshub — corresponds in structure and key episodes to Hesiod's succession-sequence of Ouranos → Kronos → Zeus, with Hesiod's Kronos-castration-of-Ouranos episode functioning as a Greek transformation of Kumarbi's castration of Anu.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Hurro-Hittite-Hesiodic correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The detailed correspondence in motifs — castration of the previous sovereign, the swallowing event, the internal-conception-and-birth-of-the-storm-god — places the Hesiodic theogony within a transmission chain that runs through the Hurro-Hittite material.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the motif-level correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

West's argument distinguishes between motifs that could be independent and motifs that are sufficiently specific (the castration-and-swallowing combination, the prophetic declaration after castration, the named succession of sovereigns) to require transmission rather than convergence.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on motif-specificity as a transmission test.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

Burkert's *Orientalizing Revolution* argues for Phoenician intermediaries as the transmitting culture: the Kumarbi material reaches Greek poets not directly from Hurrian or Hittite sources but through the Phoenician-mediated Levantine network of the early Archaic period.footnote:[Burkert 1992, on Phoenician intermediaries in the orientalizing transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume

The Phoenician-intermediary hypothesis is supported by the broader pattern of orientalizing Greek artistic and religious motifs in the eighth and seventh centuries BCE, where the route of transmission is best documented for Levantine-Phoenician contact zones.footnote:[Burkert 1992, on the orientalizing horizon and Phoenician contact.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume

The Sanchuniathon theogony preserved by Philo of Byblos and quoted by Eusebius is treated by West as an attestation of the Kumarbi succession in Phoenician-Greek mediation, providing an intermediate textual link between the Hurro-Hittite and Hesiodic versions.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on Philo of Byblos's Sanchuniathon as transmission evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The transmission argument's strength is the convergence of independent lines of evidence: motif-level correspondences, structural parallels in the succession, and the orientalizing-period archaeological-art-historical context for sustained Levantine-Greek contact.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992 on the convergence of evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The combat dimension of the transmission — the Ullikummi-Typhon parallel — runs alongside the theogonic dimension: just as Kumarbi's stone-monster threatens to displace Teshub by reaching toward heaven, Hesiod's Typhon threatens to displace Zeus by similar cosmographic ambition (cross-ref: Ch. 11).footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on Ullikummi-Typhon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The Hurro-Hittite combat material is thus the principal bridge between the Mesopotamian combat-corpus (Anzû, Enūma Eliš; cross-ref: Ch. 2, Ch. 3) and the Greek Hesiodic combat-corpus (Zeus-Typhon; cross-ref: Ch. 11), and the case for transmission is strongest in this segment of the wider chain.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The transmission case is taken up in detail in this book's Part II C6 (Near-Eastern-to-Mediterranean transmission); the present chapter provides the primary documentary evidence on which the C6 argument rests.footnote:[Forward-pointer to Ch. 11 and Part II C6; West 1997 and Burkert 1992 as the evidentiary basis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

Forsyth places the Hurro-Hittite combat material as a documented link in the longue durée of the combat-myth tradition that runs from Sumer through the Hebrew Bible to Christian apocalyptic, with the Kumarbi Cycle as the Anatolian station in that chain.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the Hurro-Hittite station in the combat-myth chain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

Scurlock and Beal's volume includes Anatolian-focused chapters that engage the Kumarbi material as evidence for or against the comparative-combat category as Gunkel framed it; the volume treats the Hurro-Hittite case as a non-trivial test for the Chaoskampf hypothesis.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, chapters on Anatolian material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=chapters on Anatolian material

The Hurro-Hittite case, as preserved, supports the argument that combat-myth narratives travel with cultural contact: the cycle is itself a Hurrian reception of Mesopotamian materials, then a Hittite reception of the Hurrian cycle, then — via Phoenician mediation — a Greek reception of the Hurro-Hittite material, with each station modifying the inherited template.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992, on the chain-reception model.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's role as transmission bridge is what makes it the structural fulcrum of this book's geographical argument: it is the chapter through which the Mesopotamian cluster (Ch. 1–4) connects to the Greek cluster (Ch. 11), and the chapter through which the Anatolian-Levantine zone is established as the principal transmission corridor.footnote:[Forward-pointer to Ch. 11 and Part II C6; West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

=== Variants and related songs

The Kumarbi Cycle includes several songs that are narratively adjacent to the combat-songs but are not themselves combat-focused; the brief and Hoffner's edition treat these as cycle-context rather than as body narrative for this chapter.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the cycle's full song-roster.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The Song of LAMMA is one such adjacent song, narrating an episode in which the deity LAMMA temporarily assumes kingship in heaven during the cycle's broader arc; Hoffner treats it as a cycle-frame song rather than a combat-song.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the Song of LAMMA.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The Song of Silver narrates an episode involving the deity Silver, possibly Kumarbi's offspring, and possibly involving a sovereignty-claim against Teshub; the song is fragmentary and is treated as cycle-context.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the Song of Silver.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

A further song, sometimes titled Sun-God-and-Cow, sits adjacent to the cycle's main material and engages the wider Hurrian-Hittite mythological corpus; Hoffner includes it in the broader collection but does not assign it to the combat-cycle proper.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on Sun-God-and-Cow as adjacent material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The classification of "the Kumarbi Cycle" as a unified cycle versus a loose grouping of related songs is itself a scholarly judgment: Hoffner adopts the cyclic classification because of the recurring cast (Kumarbi, Teshub, Šauška, Ea) and the recurring premise (Kumarbi's revenge against Teshub through monstrous offspring).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the cycle's classification.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Some scholars treat the cycle's songs as independent compositions linked only by shared cast and shared scribal tradition; the present chapter follows Hoffner's cyclic framing, which is the standard scholarly convention, while flagging the alternative.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the cyclic versus aggregational debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's preserved songs are distributed across the Hittite scribal tradition's compositional layers: some songs are first attested in Middle Hittite copies, others first in New Hittite copies, and the manuscript distribution suggests sustained scribal interest across multiple periods.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the manuscript distribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's distinctive features within the wider Hurro-Hittite corpus — the corporeal generative logic of Kumarbi's offspring, the wisdom-god-as-technical-solver pattern, the Šauška-as-seductress strategy — make it identifiable as a coherent compositional project even as the songs themselves vary in tone and length.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the cycle's distinctive features.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's relationship to the separate Hittite Illuyanka myth (cross-ref: Ch. 4, partial, and forward to Ch. 4-supplement / Ch. 11 transmission) is one of parallel rather than direct continuity: Illuyanka is a discrete combat narrative in the Hittite tradition, attested to ritual context (the purulli-festival), while the Kumarbi Cycle is a Hurrian-Hittite scribal-literary project without an attested ritual rubric.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction and §12 (Illuyanka); Beckman 1982, on Illuyanka as discrete myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

[VARIANT: primary=Hoffner 1998 (songs as cycle, with Kumarbi as binding antagonist) | alt=alternative readings that treat the songs as independent compositions sharing cast | chosen: present-both | reason: the cyclic framing is the standard scholarly convention but is not the only possible reading of the manuscript record.]

The closing summary on the cycle: the Kumarbi material as preserved is the Hurro-Hittite scribal tradition's principal contribution to the Near-Eastern-Mediterranean combat-myth corpus, the documented bridge between Mesopotamian combat-narrative and Greek theogonic-combat narrative, and the most cosmographically elaborate combat-cycle in the surviving Anatolian record.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction
