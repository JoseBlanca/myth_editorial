# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).
4. Greece & Rome — Zeus vs. Typhon (full); family chapter "Greek dragon-combats after Zeus-Typhon" (Apollo/Python, Herakles/Hydra-Ladon-Geryon, Perseus/Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Cacus).
5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).
7. Egypt — Ra vs. Apep (full); shorter treatment of Horus vs. Seth as the debated adjacent case.
8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).
9. East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). Yu the Great appears as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering, not as a chaoskampf.
10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.
11. Africa — Bida and the founding of Wagadu (full chapter; the inversion anchor); Mwindo vs. Kirimu (contrastive case: the hero is punished for the killing).
12. Southeast Asia & Oceania — Bakunawa. (Rainbow Serpent is held for the Part II critique chapter, not placed geographically.)
13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).
14. North America (contrastive) — Nanabozho and Mishipeshu: the refusal — the chapter where area specialists (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) are foregrounded and the chaoskampf framing is explicitly declined.

### Part II — thematic comparative chapters
Part II is where the comparative work happens. Load-bearing (confirmed):
- **C1. Combat as cosmogony** — Marduk, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong, Huitzilopochtli.
- **C2. Combat as sovereignty** — Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra, Thraētaona, Kumarbi/Ullikummi, Ninurta/Anzû.
- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.
- **C4. Eschatological displacement** — Thor/Jörmungandr, Rev 12, Frashokereti.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):
- **C5. The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula** — Watkins's argument from Indra through Sigurd to Beowulf.
- **C6. The Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission** — West / Burkert / López-Ruiz: Kumarbi → Hesiod; Baal/Yamm → Typhonomachy.
- **C7. The inversion problem** — Bida anchor; the ambiguous status of Sigurd's slaying.
- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").
- **Methods sidebar** between Part I and Part II: tier-ranking method, appropriation protocol, area-specialist citation policy.
- **Reception coda** (final chapter, ~20–25 pages): traces the afterlife of the imagery in Milton's Satan, Christian iconography of St. Michael / St. George, Tolkien's dragons (and the 1936 Beowulf essay), modern kaiju (Godzilla and the Orochi lineage), and the continuing use of "chaos vs. order" rhetoric in contemporary religious interpretation. Explicitly *not* classified as chaoskampf; this is reception, not primary material.
- **Adversaries + champions appendix** (replaces the single-culture "character appendix"): one entry per monster (Tiamat, Yamm, Lôtan, Leviathan, Rahab, Vṛtra, Aži Dahāka, Apep, Jörmungandr, Typhon, Illuyanka, Ullikummi, Hedammu, the Red Dragon, Coyolxauhqui, etc.) plus one entry per champion. This is load-bearing because the cross-cultural comparisons happen across *figures*, not plots.
- **Bibliography**: preliminary estimate 150–250 entries.

### Terminology default (locked)

Body prose uses "combat myth" as the default, reserving "Chaoskampf" for quotations, discussions of Gunkel's term, and the Part I and Part II passages where the specificity matters. This lets the book engage the Ballentine critique substantively rather than tokenistically.

# Skill instructions: chapter-draft

---
name: chapter-draft
description: Transforms the verified facts into Asimov-style narrative prose. The AI's only job is writing — the facts are already checked. No new facts may be introduced. Every verified claim must appear in the narrative, and nothing unverified may be added.
---

# chapter-draft

## For the human

This is the creative stage — and it is much safer now that the facts are already locked. The AI is not simultaneously researching, citing, and writing. It is only writing. The factual skeleton has already been written and verified. The AI's job is to turn a list of numbered, checked facts into a narrative that reads like Asimov at his best.

The main risk is no longer inventing facts (those are locked) but **drift during the prose transformation**: dropping a verified fact because it doesn't fit the narrative flow, softening a claim until its meaning changes, or inserting a "helpful" detail that was never in the verified facts. The next step (`narrative-fidelity`) will catch these, but the writing AI should work to prevent them.

Importantly, the writing AI does **not** receive the original source texts. It only receives the verified claims document. This is deliberate: giving it the sources would tempt it to "improve" the chapter with additional details that were never fact-checked.

## Hard rules
1. **Every factual assertion in the narrative must correspond to a claim in the approved claims document.** No new facts, no new citations, no "I happen to know" additions.
2. **Every claim in the approved document must appear in the narrative.** Do not silently drop claims because they are inconvenient for the prose flow. If a claim is genuinely irrelevant, flag it rather than omitting it.
3. Use exactly the English renderings locked in `glossary.yaml`.
4. All markers from the claims document survive into the narrative. Do not paraphrase them away.
5. Citations carry over from the claims document. Do not add, remove, or reassign footnotes.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc` (fact-checked, human-reviewed claims)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml` (for variant classification, target length, special instructions)

**Not** primary source translations. The drafter does not need the sources — the claims document already contains everything verified. Providing sources would tempt the drafter to "improve" claims with additional details.

## Style: the Asimov method

Treat the reader as intelligent and uninformed. Gloss proper nouns with functional roles, not poetic epithets. Short paragraphs, one idea each. Plain modern English. Where the source is ambiguous, say so. Where scholars disagree, name it briefly. Use etymology and geography when they illuminate a specific point, never as mandatory ornament.

### Exemplar passage

> The Sumerians believed that before anything else existed, there was a primeval sea. They called her Nammu, and she seems to have been thought of as a kind of cosmic mother — though the texts that survive are maddeningly brief on this point. From Nammu came heaven and earth, conceived of as a single mountain with the sky (An) on top and the earth (Ki) below. An and Ki were the first divine couple, and their union produced Enlil, the god of the air.
>
> Enlil then did something that requires explanation. He separated his father An from his mother Ki, pushing the sky upward and leaving the earth below. This is a motif found in many mythologies — the violent separation of sky and earth by a younger god — but the Sumerian version is unusually matter-of-fact about it. There is no battle, no castration, no cosmic drama. Enlil simply separates them, and the world as we know it comes into being.

### Forbidden words

Do not use, in this register: *shimmering, ancient* (as ornament), *mystical, unfathomable, primordial* (as ornament), *ineffable, sacred* (as filler), *mighty, fearsome, dread, eternal, boundless, infinite, veiled, shrouded*. Avoid openings like "Long ago...", "In the time before time...", "It is said that...". These mark imitation-sacred prose, not Asimov.

## Variant handling (from brief)

- If `variants.classification` is **single-prevalent**: present the prevalent version in the body. Put alternate versions in AsciiDoc footnotes.
- If **co-equal**: present the variants inline in the body, each introduced with its source ("One tradition, preserved in [source], describes... Another, in [source], gives a different account...").

## Narrative transitions

The claims document has no connective tissue — that is your job. You may add:
- **Transitional sentences** that connect one claim to the next ("Having established the cosmic order, the narrative turns to...")
- **Orienting context** that helps the reader follow the story ("At this point in the text, the setting shifts to...")
- **Explanatory asides** that help the reader understand why something matters ("This is worth pausing on, because...")

These are **not** factual claims — they are prose scaffolding. They must not introduce new information about the myth. If a transition requires a factual bridge, that bridge should already be in the claims document as an `[INFERENCE:]` marker.

// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 555-578, English of the Anzu Epic

The Ninurta-Anzû combat is the chapter's paradigm case of pure sovereignty without cosmogony — Annus 2001's introduction explicitly names Ninurta-as-model-king as the central thematic of the epic, and the combat does not remake the cosmos but returns the kingship-token to its rightful holder and confirms the warrior-champion's status; the narrative structurally prefigures the Marduk-Tiamat sovereignty-elevation, an inheritance that §IV develops at length.footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the Ninurta-as-model-king theme and on the combat as sovereignty-restoration-not-cosmogony; Lambert, W.G., *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Mesopotamian Civilizations 16 (Winona Lake: Eisenbrauns, 2013), introduction on the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* literary genealogy.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, Anzû-to-Enūma Eliš genealogy

The seventh case is Yahweh against Leviathan and Rahab in the Hebrew Bible's poetic corpus (Psalms 74, 89, 104; Isaiah 27, 51; Job 26 with ch. 41; Habakkuk 3): the corpus does not narrate a Yahweh-Leviathan combat as a story but alludes across at least seven distinct poetic passages to Yahweh's past combat-victory, with the imagery undergirding royal-psalm theology and Yahweh's claims to kingship over rival gods — Day 1985 reads the corpus as direct continuity of the Ugaritic Baal-Lôtan imagery, while Ballentine 2015 reads it as a rival-sovereignty rather than a Gunkel-1895 primordial-disorder frame.footnote:[Day, J., *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1985), chs. 1–5 on the Hebrew Bible combat-myth corpus (Pss 74, 89, 104; Isa 27, 51; Job 26 with ch. 41; Hab 3) as direct continuity of the Ugaritic Baal-Lôtan imagery; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, chs. 2–4 on the same corpus read as rival-sovereignty.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=chs. 2-4, Hebrew corpus as rival-sovereignty

The Yahweh-Leviathan-Rahab combat-corpus is the chapter's most contested case for the Gunkel-Ballentine debate, because it is the corpus on which Gunkel's 1895 hypothesis was originally anchored: Psalms 74:13–14 (Yahweh broke the heads of the dragons in the waters; broke the heads of Leviathan in pieces) and Isaiah 27:1 (on that day Yahweh will slay Leviathan the twisting serpent) are the load-bearing passages on both sides of the debate, with Ballentine arguing that the rival-sovereignty reading better accounts for the philological and structural evidence than the Gunkel-1895 primordial-disorder reading does.footnote:[Gunkel, H., *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* (Göttingen: Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht, 1895), Teil I on Pss 74 and Isa 27 as the Hebrew Bible corpus that anchors the Chaoskampf hypothesis; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, chs. 2–4 on the rival-sovereignty reading of the same passages as more philologically-grounded.]

The seven cases share the three-term sovereignty-combat structure — champion, rival-sovereign adversary, combat-as-elevation-or-restoration — but differ in the texture of the rival-sovereignty: Yamm is initially handed kingship by El (rival who has already been invested); Typhon is a challenger generated specifically to contest Olympian sovereignty (rival who has not been invested but is positioned to be); Vṛtra obstructs the waters and thereby holds the gods' resource hostage (rival as hostage-taker); Aži Dahāka rules lands and is positioned as the principal evil-spirit creature within Zoroastrian dualism (rival as cosmic counter-sovereign); Ullikummi is generated by Kumarbi as instrument to displace Teshub (rival as proxy of a deposed predecessor); Anzû steals the kingship-token (rival as usurper); Leviathan and Rahab are alluded to as defeated past-rivals whose defeat undergirds Yahweh's present kingship (rival as defeated-and-cited).footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml comparative_argument on the seven-cases-share-yet-differ disciplinary requirement; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 2 on the typological diversity of rival-sovereignty roles within the alternative-sovereign reading.]

The seven cases also differ in the role of the ritual-and-architectural embedding: Baal's victory is followed by the founding of the palace at Ṣapānu (architecture as institution of kingship); Marduk-Tiamat (the §IV supporting case) is recited annually at the akītu before Esagil; Ninurta-Anzû is invoked in the Neo-Assyrian royal ritual and in the akītu framework; Zeus-Typhon and Indra-Vṛtra are embedded in cult and sacrifice respectively (Olympian succession; soma sacrifice) without a single architectural anchor; Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka is embedded in the Avestan yasht-prayer cycle and in the Zoroastrian eschatological framework; Yahweh-Leviathan-Rahab is embedded in the Psalms-Isaiah-Job poetic-cultic corpus without a single fixed ritual occasion — §VI develops the ritual-and-state pattern at length.footnote:[Smith and Pitard 2009, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume II*, on Ṣapānu palace-founding as institution of kingship; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, pp. 463–465 on the akītu recitation context; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the Anzû epic in Neo-Assyrian royal ritual; Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, on the Psalms cultic framing.]

The seven cases share a structural property that distinguishes them collectively from the cosmogonic cases of C1: the cosmos is already in place when the combat occurs, so the combat changes who rules but not what is ruled — and this property is what makes Lincoln 2012's political-ideological reading especially apt for the sovereignty-combat subtype, because the political work of legitimating-rule-over-existing-order is more direct here than in the cosmogonic subtype where the combat both makes the world and installs its ruler.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml comparative_argument on the cosmos-already-in-place property as distinguishing C2 from C1; Lincoln, B., *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars: Critical Explorations in the History of Religions* (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2012), on the political-ideological reading as sovereignty-legitimating work.]

=== II. Ballentine's reading

Ballentine's 2015 book *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* argues, against Gunkel's 1895 framing, that the adversaries in the combat-myth corpus are better understood as agents of an alternative divine power structure than as personifications of "chaos": Yamm, Tiamat, Leviathan, Rahab, and (by extension) the related figures across the wider corpus are presented in the ancient texts as rival sovereigns with their own retinues, courts, and legitimate-seeming claims to rule, not as undifferentiated disorder against which the storm-god restores cosmic order.footnote:[Ballentine, D.S., *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (New York: Oxford University Press, 2015), ch. 1 introducing the alternative-sovereign reading as a text-critical alternative to Gunkel's "chaos" framing, and chs. 2–4 developing the reading across the Ugaritic, Hebrew, and broader Near-Eastern witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, the alternative-sovereign reading

The alternative-sovereign reading rests on per-tradition philological observations rather than on a top-down theoretical reframing: Ballentine reads the actual language of the Ugaritic, Hebrew, and Akkadian texts and finds that the adversaries are described in the same vocabulary as the gods (designations of kingship, divine epithets, retainer relationships, court-and-assembly settings) rather than in a vocabulary of disorder, formlessness, or unmade-ness — and the philological observation is what supports the reading, not a prior commitment to demythologising "chaos" or to elevating "sovereignty" as a theoretical category.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 2 on the philological observation that the adversaries are described in divine-political vocabulary across the Ugaritic and Hebrew corpora; Smith 1994, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume I*, on the Ugaritic philological evidence on which Ballentine's Ugaritic readings rest.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2, philological grounding
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Ugaritic philological evidence underlying alternative-sovereign

The Yamm case is Ballentine's clearest evidence: the Ugaritic Baal Cycle does not describe Yamm as formless ocean, primordial chaos, or unmade matter — it describes him as a god, a son of El, the holder of a court, and (load-bearingly) the recipient of a kingship-investiture from El before the combat with Baal; the Ugaritic text uses the verbs and divine-epithets standard for a sovereign, and Ballentine notes that "chaos" as a translation choice systematically obscures this evidence.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 2 on the Yamm case as the clearest text-critical evidence for the alternative-sovereign reading; Smith 1994, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume I*, on El's investiture of Yamm and on the divine-epithet language used for Yamm in the Cycle's opening tablets.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2, Yamm case
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.1-1.2, El's investiture of Yamm

The Tiamat case is Ballentine's structural showcase for the reading at the Babylonian end of the corpus: in *Enūma Eliš* Tiamat appoints a consort-general (Qingu) and gives him the Tablet of Destinies — the same kingship-token that is at issue in the Anzû Epic — and the Tablet's possession is the philological evidence that Ballentine reads as installation of an alternative-sovereign court rather than as personification of unformed primordial waters; Marduk's combat with Tiamat is therefore a contest between two sovereignty-claimants whose courts both wield kingship-tokens, not a champion's restoration of order from disorder.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the Tiamat case and on the Tablet-of-Destinies-to-Qingu evidence as installation of alternative-sovereign court; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablets I–II on Qingu's installation as Tiamat's consort-general and on the Tablet of Destinies given to him.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, Tiamat-Qingu evidence
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets I-II, Tiamat installs Qingu with Tablet of Destinies

The Leviathan-Rahab case is Ballentine's principal Hebrew Bible evidence and the reason the book is titled *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*: across Pss 74, 89, 104; Isa 27, 51; Job 26, 41; Hab 3, the philological evidence is that the adversaries are described as named figures with attributes (heads, twisting, fleeing, dragon-form) and that the verbs of the combat are verbs of subjugation-and-defeat rather than verbs of formless-into-form transformation, so the rival-sovereignty reading is directly supported by the text-critical evidence even where the inherited Gunkel framing reads "chaos" into the same passages.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, chs. 2–4 on the Hebrew Bible philological evidence for the alternative-sovereign reading; Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, chs. 1–5 on the same Hebrew Bible philological evidence read within the continuity-with-Ugaritic frame that Ballentine substantially accepts on text-critical grounds.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=chs. 2-4, Hebrew Bible philological evidence
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-5, Hebrew Bible philological evidence

The Aži Dahāka case extends the alternative-sovereign reading into the Iranian material: the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* describes Aži Dahāka as a tyrant-king ruling lands, with subjects and a tyrannical court — the language is the language of usurping-rule, not of formless chaos, and the legitimate-against-tyrant reading of Thraētaona's victory is a sovereignty-combat reading internal to the Iranian textual tradition rather than an imposition of the alternative-sovereign frame from outside.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna*, on the Aži Dahāka tyrant-king reading internal to the Avestan-Pahlavi material; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 2 on the alternative-sovereign reading's compatibility with the Iranian internal-evidence reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=on Aži Dahāka as tyrant-king
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2, alternative-sovereign and Iranian internal evidence

The Anzû case is the present chapter's strongest in-tradition support for Ballentine's reading because the kingship-token (the Tablet of Destinies) is the load-bearing object of the combat: Anzû steals the Tablet, Anzû's possession of the Tablet is itself a sovereignty-claim, and Ninurta's defeat of Anzû is structurally a return of the kingship-token to its rightful holder — this is sovereignty-combat reasoning operating internally to the Akkadian text rather than imposed on it from outside, and the alternative-sovereign reading is therefore independently confirmed by the Mesopotamian evidence.footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the Tablet of Destinies as kingship-token internal to the Akkadian text and on the in-tradition sovereignty-combat reading; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the alternative-sovereign reading's compatibility with the Mesopotamian internal-evidence reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Tablet of Destinies as kingship-token
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, Mesopotamian internal-evidence convergence

The terminological consequence of the alternative-sovereign reading is that "chaos," "chaos-figure," and "chaos-monster" are problematic translation choices that prejudge the philological evidence in favor of the disorder-vs-order frame, when the text-critical evidence in this subtype runs the other way; Ballentine therefore argues that the inherited Gunkel terminology should be retained only as the historical name of a category whose internal evidence the term distorts, and that the working scholarly vocabulary should shift toward "rival sovereign," "the adversary," or the named figure.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on "chaos" as prejudging translation choice and on the working-vocabulary shift toward rival-sovereign or named-figure terminology; per glossary.yaml entries on "the adversary (category term)" with "chaos-figure" and "chaos-monster" listed as never_use.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, terminological consequence

The strength of Ballentine's reading is that it is grounded in per-tradition philological evidence rather than in a comparativist-typological pre-commitment: where a comparativist might worry that the rival-sovereignty reading is being read into the texts to support a sovereignty-typology argument, Ballentine's argument runs the other direction — the philological evidence is what supports the typology, and the typology is therefore a description of the corpus rather than an imposition on it.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the philological-evidence-as-warrant rather than typology-as-imposition direction of argument; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the comparativist-typological tradition Ballentine's text-critical work corrects.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, philological evidence as warrant
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=on comparativist-typological tradition

The chapter adopts Ballentine's reading as its principal interpretive frame for the sovereignty-combat subtype but does not extend the strong terminological claim to the cosmogonic-combat subtype treated in C1: the reasoning is that for the sovereignty cases the alternative-sovereign reading is directly supported by the philological evidence (rivals have courts, retinues, kingship-tokens, and titles), while for the cosmogonic cases (Marduk-Tiamat at the cosmogonic stage of the combat; Ninurta-Asag) the body-dismemberment-and-cosmos-construction outcome makes "chaos" defensible as informal shorthand even if not as technical category — a position that follows the brief's special_instructions and that does not require resolving the Gunkel-Ballentine debate at the level of the whole corpus.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml special_instructions on the C2-specific tightening of the chaos-terminology discipline and on the chapter's adoption of Ballentine's reading as principal frame for this subtype; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the differential force of the alternative-sovereign reading across the cosmogonic and sovereignty subtypes.]

=== III. The difference from cosmogony

The cosmogonic-combat subtype (Ch34, C1) and the sovereignty-combat subtype (the present chapter) are operationally distinguished by a single test: in the cosmogonic combat the cosmos itself is the aftermath of the combat — present features of the world (heaven and earth, rivers, stones, calendar, the diurnal solar order) are explicitly aetiologised to the combat — whereas in the sovereignty combat the cosmos is already in place and what changes is who rules it.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §III on the operational distinction between C1 and C2 subtypes; per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the four-subtype operationalisation; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, chs. on cosmogonic and sovereignty combat as the long-arc framing within which the operational distinction is drawn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic and sovereignty combat

The Marduk-Tiamat *Enūma Eliš* combat is the case that most clearly performs both kinds of work simultaneously: Tablets I–IV present the sovereignty-establishment phase (Marduk is elevated by the divine assembly to face Tiamat; the Tablet of Destinies is contested between the two courts), and Tablets IV–V present the cosmogony phase (Tiamat's body is split to construct heaven and earth; the rivers Tigris and Euphrates flow from the wounds in her eyes; the calendar and the stations of the moon are set) — the case is both a sovereignty combat and a cosmogonic combat, which is why it is engaged in both C1 and the present chapter's §IV.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablets I–V on the sovereignty-establishment and cosmogony phases of the *Enūma Eliš* combat as sequential rather than alternative; Foster 2005, *Before the Muses*, pp. 436–486 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets I-V, sovereignty-and-cosmogony phases
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-486, sovereignty-and-cosmogony phases

The seven sovereignty cases of §I do not perform the cosmogonic work that Marduk-Tiamat does at its cosmogony phase: Baal does not construct heaven and earth from Yamm's body — he founds his palace at Ṣapānu after the victory; Zeus does not split Typhon to make the cosmos — he confines Typhon under Etna or in Tartarus; Indra splits the mountain to release the waters but does not make heaven and earth from Vṛtra; Thraētaona binds Aži Dahāka beneath Damāvand without dismembering him; Teshub defeats Ullikummi after Ea's primordial cutter severs the monster from Upelluri's shoulder, with no cosmogonic outcome; Ninurta returns the Tablet of Destinies and confirms warrior-champion status; Yahweh's combats are alluded to as past defeats that ground present kingship rather than as cosmogonic events.footnote:[Smith 1994, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume I*, KTU 1.1–1.2 on Baal's victory without cosmos-construction; West 1966, *Hesiod: Theogony*, on Typhon's confinement rather than dismemberment; Jamison and Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, RV 1.32 on water-release without cosmos-construction; Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, §15 on Teshub-Ullikummi without cosmogonic outcome; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, on the Tablet-restoration outcome; Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, on the Hebrew Bible allusions as past-defeats-grounding-present-kingship.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32, water-release without cosmos-construction
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=Tablet-restoration outcome

The Indra-Vṛtra case is the chapter's most carefully balanced near-case: Indra's combat does cosmogonic work (release of waters, separation of heaven and earth, production of dawn) without performing a cosmogony in the strong Mesopotamian sense (no body-dismemberment, no construction of cosmic structure from the adversary's substance), and the case therefore bridges C1 and C2 — engaged in C1 §V as cosmogonic-work-without-construction and engaged in the present chapter §I as a strong sovereignty case in its own right because the same combat establishes Indra's kingship over the gods.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, Rigveda 1.32 on the cosmological-and-sovereignty double function; per the Ch34 (C1) §V engagement of the case as cosmogonic-work-without-construction near-case; West 2007, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, ch. 6 on Indra's combat as IE storm-god-sovereignty case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32, cosmological-and-sovereignty double function
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6, Indra as IE storm-god-sovereignty

The structural difference between cosmogonic and sovereignty combat manifests in the temporal relationship between the combat and the cosmos: the cosmogonic combat is a once-and-done event in mythic time whose outcome is constitutive of the present cosmos, while the sovereignty combat — even when it occurs in mythic time — is positioned as the establishment of a kingship that the present cosmos already presupposes; the cosmos persists in either case after the combat, but the sovereignty cases do not require the cosmos to be different than it was before the combat for the combat to have done its work.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §III on the temporal relationship between combat and cosmos as distinguishing C1 and C2 subtypes; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological diversity of combat-myth temporality.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=on combat-myth temporality

The structural difference also manifests in the role of the kingship-token: in sovereignty combats the kingship-token is often the explicit object of the combat (the Tablet of Destinies in Anzû; the throne and palace at Ṣapānu in the Baal Cycle; the Olympian throne in Zeus-Typhon; the kingship of the gods in Indra-Vṛtra) — the combat is for-the-token or about-the-token — while in cosmogonic combats the kingship-token, where it appears at all, is one element among the many cosmogonic outcomes (the Tablet of Destinies in *Enūma Eliš* is recovered as part of the combat's cosmogonic-and-sovereignty work, but the cosmos-construction is at least equally load-bearing).footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the Tablet of Destinies as load-bearing combat-object in the sovereignty-combat subtype; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablets IV–V on the Tablet-of-Destinies as one element among the many cosmogonic outcomes of the *Enūma Eliš* combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Tablet of Destinies as combat-object
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets IV-V, Tablet as one element among cosmogonic outcomes

=== IV. The Anzû-to-Marduk genealogy

The Anzû-to-Marduk-Tiamat genealogy is the chapter's best-attested case of a sovereignty-combat being deliberately reworked within one tradition into a cosmogonic-combat: the Standard Babylonian Anzû Epic and *Enūma Eliš* are independently preserved Akkadian texts copied across the Old Babylonian, Middle Babylonian, and Neo-Assyrian periods, and Lambert 2013 and Annus 2001 both argue from the parallel structural-and-verbal evidence that *Enūma Eliš* is a deliberate literary descendant of Anzû.footnote:[Lambert, W.G., *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Mesopotamian Civilizations 16 (Winona Lake: Eisenbrauns, 2013), introduction on the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* literary genealogy; Annus, A., *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu* (SAACT 3, Helsinki: Neo-Assyrian Text Corpus Project, 2001), introduction on Anzû as the structural and thematic model for the *Enūma Eliš* combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, Anzû-to-Enūma Eliš genealogy
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Anzû as model for Enūma Eliš

The Anzû Epic narrates a pure sovereignty-combat: Anzû, originally a minor divine servitor, steals the Tablet of Destinies from Enlil, the gods are paralysed, the assembly seeks a champion, several gods refuse, Ninurta accepts, the first engagement is unsuccessful, Ea provides counsel through the animate-mace Šarur, the second engagement succeeds, and Ninurta returns the Tablet to Enlil and is confirmed as warrior-champion — there is no cosmogony in the narrative, the cosmos is already in place, and the combat is purely about the kingship-token.footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, Tablets I–III on the Anzû Epic narrative sequence; Foster 2005, *Before the Muses*, pp. 555–578 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=Tablets I-III, sovereignty-combat narrative
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 555-578, Anzu Epic English

The *Enūma Eliš* narrative reproduces and outbids the Anzû structure: the kingship-token theft is reproduced (Tiamat installs Qingu as consort-general and gives him the Tablet of Destinies, just as Anzû took the Tablet from Enlil); the gods' paralysis is reproduced (the elder gods are unable to face Tiamat, just as the elder gods were unable to face Anzû); the assembly's search for a champion is reproduced (Marduk is sought as champion just as Ninurta was); the multiple-attempt structure is reproduced; and the Tablet's return to its rightful holder is reproduced — but with the cosmogonic outcome added on top, so that Marduk's victory does the same sovereignty-work Ninurta's did and additionally constructs the cosmos.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the structural reproduction of the Anzû sequence in *Enūma Eliš*; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the same parallel-structure-with-additional-cosmogony reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, structural reproduction
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, parallel-structure-plus-cosmogony

The escalation from sovereignty-combat to cosmogonic-combat is therefore the philological evidence for what the brief calls "deliberate Babylonian theological engineering": the *Enūma Eliš* poet (likely commissioned by Nebuchadnezzar I, c. 1125–1104 BCE, on Lambert's reading of the historical context) takes the Anzû structure as template and outbids it by adding the body-dismemberment cosmogony, the calendar-and-astronomical work, the founding of Babylon and Esagil, the creation of humans from Qingu's blood, and the fifty-names doxology — every cosmogonic element of *Enūma Eliš* is added to the inherited Anzû sovereignty-combat skeleton.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning context (c. 1125–1104 BCE) and on the cosmogonic-element addition reading; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the deliberate-engineering reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning context
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, deliberate engineering

The cosmogonic outbidding is theologically motivated: the *Enūma Eliš* poet's project is to elevate Marduk — patron god of Babylon — to a position of cosmic-and-political supremacy that exceeds any prior Mesopotamian deity's portfolio, and the cosmogonic body-dismemberment is the load-bearing literary device by which that elevation is performed; the elevation cannot be performed by a sovereignty-combat alone (because Ninurta has already done that), so the poet escalates the combat into a cosmogonic-and-sovereignty combat in which Marduk does both the sovereignty-establishment Ninurta did *and* the cosmos-construction no prior Mesopotamian champion did.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the Marduk-elevation theological project and on the cosmogonic-outbidding device; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the political-ideological reading of *Enūma Eliš* as Babylonian state-charter that elevates Marduk.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, Marduk-elevation project
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on Enūma Eliš as Babylonian state-charter

The Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* genealogy specifically reuses Anzû as a defeated subordinate: Annus 2001's introduction notes that Anzû appears in *Enūma Eliš* not as the rival-king (that role is filled by Tiamat-and-Qingu) but as one of Tiamat's subordinate monsters in her brood — the very figure whose defeat established Ninurta is repurposed as one of the eleven monsters Tiamat creates and Marduk overcomes en route to the cosmogonic combat with Tiamat herself.footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on Anzû's reuse as one of Tiamat's subordinate monsters in *Enūma Eliš*; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet I on Tiamat's brood of eleven monsters including Anzû.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Anzû as Tiamat subordinate
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I, Tiamat's brood including Anzû

The reuse of Anzû as a Tiamat-subordinate is itself the literary evidence that the *Enūma Eliš* poet is consciously reworking the Anzû tradition: Anzû's reduced-status appearance is gratuitous (the poet did not need to include Anzû in the brood at all) and is therefore best read as a deliberate citation that signals to the audience that the new combat-narrative subsumes and supersedes the Anzû tradition — Marduk's combat absorbs Ninurta's combat by including Ninurta's adversary as one of Marduk's secondary defeats.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the deliberate-citation reading of Anzû's reuse; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the supersession-by-citation device.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, deliberate-citation reading
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, supersession-by-citation

The Anzû-to-Marduk genealogy is the chapter's principal documented case of a phenomenon that the brief identifies as load-bearing for the C1-C2 typological argument: sovereignty-combat material can be escalated within one tradition into cosmogonic-combat material, and when that escalation is documented (as it is here), the cosmogonic-combat outcome is best read as an addition to a sovereignty-combat skeleton rather than as a free-standing cosmogony — which in turn supports the brief's central claim that sovereignty is the primary function and cosmogony is added on top where the political-theological project requires it.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §IV on the Anzû-to-Marduk genealogy as documenting the sovereignty-to-cosmogony escalation pattern; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the same; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, sovereignty-to-cosmogony escalation
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, sovereignty-to-cosmogony escalation

[SPECULATION: that the sovereignty-to-cosmogony escalation observed in the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* genealogy is a general typological mechanism rather than a Babylonian-specific innovation — that some other cases of the chapter's seven (e.g., Yahweh-Leviathan-Rahab, where the eschatological reuse of Leviathan in Isaiah 27 may be a cognate escalation) might be reading as comparable cosmogony-additions to sovereignty-skeletons | basis: the parallel structural-and-verbal reproduction with cosmogonic-element addition is documented in detail for the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* case (Lambert 2013, Annus 2001), and the documented pattern is what makes the speculation textually grounded for that case rather than ad hoc | counterargument: the deliberate-engineering reading is tightly tied to the specific Babylonian theological-political context (Marduk-elevation under Nebuchadnezzar I) and may not generalise; the Yahweh-Leviathan-Rahab eschatological reuse in Isaiah 27 lacks the line-by-line structural-and-verbal reproduction that documents the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* escalation, and applying the escalation-template across the corpus risks projecting the Babylonian pattern onto less-documented cases]

The Anzû-to-Marduk escalation case has direct relevance to the broader Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission question developed in §V and in C6: the same Mesopotamian sovereignty-combat-into-cosmogonic-combat material that *Enūma Eliš* assembled was available downstream to Hurro-Hittite, Ugaritic, and (via further mediations) Hebrew and Greek tradents, and the documented within-Mesopotamian escalation provides a model for what an escalation-on-transmission might look like at the inter-tradition level — though no comparable across-tradition escalation is as cleanly documented as the within-Mesopotamian Anzû-to-Marduk case.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §IV on the relevance of the within-Mesopotamian escalation to the §V transmission discussion; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the within-Mesopotamian case as model; West 1997, *The East Face of Helicon*, ch. 6 on the broader Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission as the inter-tradition context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, within-Mesopotamian escalation as model
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6, broader transmission context

The Anzû-to-Marduk escalation also explains why the brief instructs the chapter to treat Marduk-Tiamat as a supporting case rather than as a primary case: the *Enūma Eliš* combat is the one place where the cosmogonic-combat (C1) and the sovereignty-combat (C2) typologies meet within a single text, and the chapter's argument is that the meeting is itself a documented escalation — reading *Enūma Eliš* as a cosmogonic-combat in C1 captures one half of its work, and reading it as a sovereignty-combat in the present chapter's §IV captures the historically-prior half that the cosmogonic-half is escalation-of.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml chapters_drawn_from on Marduk-Tiamat as supporting case for the §IV escalation argument; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the *Enūma Eliš* combat as meeting-point of the two typologies; per the Ch34 (C1) treatment of Marduk-Tiamat as primary cosmogonic case.]

=== V. Transmission vs. typology

The transmission-vs-typology question is the question of how much of the seven cases' shared sovereignty-combat structure is due to documented transmission of material between traditions versus to independent typological recurrence: the brief's settled position is that some of the cluster's coherence is due to transmission (specifically the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew, the Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek, and the IE inheritance), not just to independent typology, and the chapter examines each transmission route in turn.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §V on the transmission-and-typology balance; West 1997, *The East Face of Helicon*, ch. 6 on the documented Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission lines that supply the chapter's transmission-side evidence; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, on the IE inheritance lines that supply the philological-side evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6, Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=on IE inheritance

The Ugaritic-into-Hebrew transmission is the chapter's best-documented case: Day 1985's *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* argues that the Hebrew Bible's Leviathan-Rahab corpus is direct continuity of the Ugaritic Baal-Lôtan imagery, with Lôtan (ltn) and Leviathan (lwytn) as linguistically cognate and several biblical passages preserving Ugaritic epithets verbatim — Isaiah 27:1's "twisting serpent" preserves the Ugaritic *bṯn brḥ* "fleeing serpent" and *bṯn ʿqltn* "twisting serpent" epithet-pair from KTU 1.5 i 1–3.footnote:[Day, J., *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1985), chs. 1–3 on the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew transmission and on the Lôtan/Leviathan cognancy with verbatim epithet preservation in Isa 27:1; Smith and Pitard 2009, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume II*, on KTU 1.5 i 1–3 as the Ugaritic source-text for the epithet-pair.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3, Ugaritic-Hebrew transmission with epithet preservation
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3, Ugaritic source for epithets

The Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek transmission is the chapter's second-best-documented case: West 1997 and Burkert 1992 argue that the Kumarbi Cycle (succession in heaven, monstrous offspring, storm-god combat with stone-monster) supplied the literary-and-mythological template for Hesiod's *Theogony* (succession Ouranos → Kronos → Zeus, genital mutilation, stone-swallowing, Zeus-Typhon combat) — Teshub's defeat of Ullikummi and Zeus's defeat of Typhon are structurally near-identical, and the transmission likely passed via Phoenician intermediaries during the eighth-and-seventh-century-BCE Orientalising period.footnote:[West, M.L., *The East Face of Helicon: West Asiatic Elements in Greek Poetry and Myth* (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1997), ch. 6 on the Kumarbi-Hesiod relationship and on the Phoenician-mediated transmission route; Burkert, W., *The Orientalizing Revolution: Near Eastern Influence on Greek Culture in the Early Archaic Age* (Cambridge MA: Harvard University Press, 1992), on the Levantine transmission to Greece in the Orientalising period.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6, Kumarbi-Hesiod relationship
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=Orientalizing Revolution, Levantine transmission

The Indo-European inheritance lines are the chapter's third major transmission-or-inheritance route: Watkins 1995 reconstructs the *áhann áhim* "he slew the serpent" formula across Vedic (Indra-Vṛtra), Avestan (Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka), Hittite (Teshub-Illuyanka), and Greek (Apollo-Python; Heracles-Hydra; possibly Zeus-Typhon) witnesses — the formula is preserved with verbal-and-syntactic precision sufficient to support a reconstruction back to a Proto-Indo-European poetic tradition, and the IE inheritance therefore explains the cluster's coherence at four of the chapter's seven cases (Vedic, Avestan, Hittite, Greek).footnote:[Watkins, C., *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1995), chs. 41–48 on the *áhann áhim* formula and its reconstruction across Vedic, Avestan, Hittite, and Greek witnesses; West 2007, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, ch. 6 on the same IE inheritance from a comparative-mythological angle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48, áhann áhim across Vedic-Avestan-Hittite-Greek
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6, IE inheritance from comparative-mythological angle

The Vedic-and-Avestan Indo-Iranian sub-cluster is even more specifically related: the Vedic *ahi* and the Avestan *aži* are the same word (both meaning "serpent"), Vedic Trita Āptya (Rigveda 10.8.8–9 on the three-headed serpent) and Avestan Thraētaona are philologically cognate (the Vedic and Avestan hero-names derive from the same Indo-Iranian root), and Benveniste & Renou 1934's foundational philological study established the Indo-Iranian shared phase of the formula at the level of cognate-pair textual evidence rather than at the level of structural typology alone.footnote:[Benveniste, E. and Renou, L., *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna: Étude de mythologie indo-iranienne* (Paris: Imprimerie Nationale, 1934), philological study on the Vedic-Avestan cognate-pair evidence (ahi/aži, Trita/Thraētaona) and on the Indo-Iranian shared phase of the serpent-slayer formula; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, chs. 41–48 on the same Indo-Iranian sub-cluster within the wider IE inheritance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Vedic-Avestan cognate-pair evidence
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48, Indo-Iranian sub-cluster

The Mesopotamian-Sumerian internal transmission line — Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* developed at length in §IV — is a fourth documented route within the corpus: Lambert 2013 and Annus 2001 read *Enūma Eliš* as a deliberate literary descendant of the Anzû Epic, with the cosmogonic-element addition treated in §IV as the within-tradition escalation that makes the genealogy a transmission-with-transformation rather than a transmission-with-preservation case.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* genealogy as within-Mesopotamian transmission-with-transformation; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, within-Mesopotamian transmission
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, within-Mesopotamian transmission

The four documented transmission-and-inheritance lines (Ugaritic-into-Hebrew; Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek; IE across Vedic-Avestan-Hittite-Greek; within-Mesopotamian Anzû-to-Marduk) collectively explain a substantial portion of the cluster's coherence, but they do not exhaust it: the Ugaritic-Hebrew, Mesopotamian, and Hurro-Hittite-Greek lines are West-Eurasian, and the IE inheritance lines are partially overlapping with them at the Hittite and Greek nodes — the result is that the seven cases fall within an overlapping network of transmission-and-inheritance routes that does not fully reduce the typological pattern to documented descent.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §V on the partial-but-not-complete reduction of the typological pattern to documented descent; West 1997, *The East Face of Helicon*, ch. 6 on the West-Eurasian transmission network; West 2007, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, ch. 6 on the IE inheritance network's overlap with the West-Eurasian routes.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6, IE inheritance network

[SPECULATION: that the residual typological coherence — what is left after the four documented transmission-and-inheritance lines are subtracted — is itself best explained by a combination of (a) further but less-cleanly-documented contact between West-Eurasian traditions, (b) shared political-theological function (Lincoln 2012's reading), and (c) limited independent typological recurrence | basis: the documented transmission lines explain much of the coherence but not all (e.g., the Ugaritic-internal-evidence sovereignty-combat structure does not depend on inheritance-from-Mesopotamia, even though contact is plausible); the political-theological function is independently attested in the per-tradition material (state-charter framing of Babylonian, Hittite, Aztec, and Vedic combat-narratives); the residual independent-typology component is therefore likely small but non-zero | counterargument: that the residual independent-typology component is itself an artefact of asymmetric documentation rather than a real category — what looks like residual typological recurrence may simply be undocumented further contact, in which case the cluster's coherence would reduce fully to transmission-network effects and the typological claim would lose its independent warrant; the asymmetric documentation across the seven cases (well-documented Ugaritic and Mesopotamian texts vs. partially-oral-traditional Vedic and Avestan horizons) makes this counter-reading difficult to rule out]

The transmission-vs-typology question matters for the chapter's central argument because it bears on whether the sovereignty-as-primary-function reading is a description of the corpus's structural-philological evidence or merely an artifact of the corpus's overlapping descent: the chapter's settled position is that the reading is a description of the evidence rather than an artifact of descent, because the per-tradition philological evidence (the alternative-sovereign reading from §II) supports the sovereignty function within each tradition independently of the transmission lines that connect the traditions.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §V on the matter-for-the-central-argument reading; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, chs. 2–4 on the per-tradition philological evidence as independent-of-transmission warrant for the sovereignty reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=chs. 2-4, per-tradition philological independent-of-transmission

The transmission lines do not, however, undermine the typological argument: a documented transmission line still leaves the receiving tradition with a sovereignty-combat narrative whose internal philological evidence supports the rival-sovereignty reading, and the receiving tradition's adoption of the inherited material is itself evidence that the material's sovereignty-combat function was readable as such by the receiving tradition; transmission therefore explains how the cluster came to be co-extensive at the structural level, but the typological argument is about what the cluster's members do within each tradition, which is not displaced by the transmission account.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §V on the non-displacement of the typological argument by the transmission account; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological reading as compatible with documented transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=on typological-and-transmission compatibility

The chapter's conclusion on transmission-vs-typology is that the sovereignty-combat cluster is partly a transmission-network effect (Ugaritic-Hebrew, Hurro-Hittite-Greek, IE inheritance, within-Mesopotamian escalation) and partly an independent typological recurrence — and that the partial reducibility to transmission does not undermine the typological reading, because what the receiving traditions do with the inherited material (use it for sovereignty-establishment work) is itself the evidence the typological argument rests on.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §V on the partly-transmission-partly-typology settled position; West 1997, *The East Face of Helicon*, ch. 6 on the transmission-network component; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, on the IE inheritance component; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the residual independent-typology component.]

=== VI. Ritual and state

Many of the chapter's seven sovereignty-combats are tied to coronation, palace-founding, or state ritual: Marduk's combat is recited annually at the akītu New Year festival before the cult-image of Marduk in Esagil; Baal's victory is followed by the founding of his palace at Mount Ṣapānu (KTU 1.3–1.4); Zeus's defeat of Typhon seals the Olympian order on Olympus; Ninurta's combat is invoked in Neo-Assyrian royal ritual and in the akītu framework — the combat is not merely told, it is performed-by-proxy as the rite that constitutes or maintains the king's legitimacy.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, pp. 463–465 on the akītu recitation context and on the once-and-done cosmogony being ritually re-actualised at every recitation; Smith and Pitard 2009, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume II*, on the post-combat palace-founding at Ṣapānu; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the Anzû epic in Neo-Assyrian royal ritual.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465, akītu ritual context
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=palace-founding at Ṣapānu

The akītu New Year festival is the chapter's best-attested case of sovereignty-combat-as-state-ritual: on the fourth day of the festival the *Enūma Eliš* text is recited before Marduk's cult-image in Esagil; the king performs a ritual humiliation in which the high priest strikes him on the cheek and pulls his ears, after which the king is reinvested with the kingship-tokens; and the recitation-and-reinvestment performs at the political-ritual level the same sovereignty-establishment that the *Enūma Eliš* narrative performs at the divine-mythic level.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, pp. 463–465 on the akītu fourth-day recitation, on the king's ritual humiliation, and on the reinvestment with kingship-tokens; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the akītu festival as political-ritual performance of the *Enūma Eliš* sovereignty-establishment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465, akītu fourth-day recitation
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on akītu as political-ritual performance

The Ṣapānu palace-founding is the Ugaritic case's structural analogue: KTU 1.3–1.4 narrates the construction of Baal's palace on Mount Ṣapānu (Mount Zaphon / Jebel al-Aqra) immediately following the defeat of Yamm, with Kothar-wa-Khasis as the divine architect and the palace's completion as the ratification of Baal's kingship — Smith and Pitard 2009 read the palace-building cycle as the post-combat institution of Baal's kingship and as the structural climax of the entire Baal Cycle, with the architectural-ritual outcome (palace, throne, court) as the explicit completion of the sovereignty-combat begun in KTU 1.1–1.2.footnote:[Smith and Pitard 2009, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume II*, on KTU 1.3–1.4 as the post-combat palace-founding cycle and on the architectural-ritual outcome as ratification of Baal's kingship; Smith 1994, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume I*, introduction on the cycle's overall sovereignty-focused structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3-1.4, palace-founding cycle
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=introduction, sovereignty-focused structure

The Olympian-throne case is the Greek structural analogue: Hesiod's *Theogony* presents Zeus's defeat of Typhon (820–880) as the climactic combat of the succession-and-sovereignty narrative, with the consequent ratification of Zeus's kingship and the distribution of *timai* (honours, prerogatives) among the Olympians as the institutional outcome — the cosmos is already in place but the political order of the Olympians under Zeus's rule is the post-combat institution.footnote:[West 1966, *Hesiod: Theogony*, on the Typhonomachy as climactic combat and on the consequent ratification of Zeus's kingship and the distribution of *timai*; Ogden 2013, *Drakōn*, chs. 2–3 on the Greek dragon-combat tradition's sovereignty-ratification function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1966-theogony ; loc=Theogony 820-880, Typhonomachy and consequent timai-distribution
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=chs. 2-3, sovereignty-ratification function

The Vedic case's ritual embedding is the soma sacrifice rather than a single annual festival: the Indra-Vṛtra combat is invoked at every soma sacrifice as ritual replay of the divine-kingship-establishment, with Indra needing the strengthening drink (soma) to fight Vṛtra and the ritual offering of soma as the human-side replication of the divine-time act — the result is that the combat is ritually re-performed not annually but at every major Vedic sacrifice, with the king or sponsor of the sacrifice as the human beneficiary of Indra's continued kingship.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, introduction on the soma sacrifice as ritual context for the Vṛtra combat; West 2007, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, ch. 6 on the soma sacrifice as IE storm-god-sovereignty ritual.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction, soma sacrifice as ritual context
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6, IE storm-god-sovereignty ritual

The Iranian case's ritual embedding is the Avestan yasht-prayer cycle and the Zoroastrian eschatological framework: Aži Dahāka features in Avestan prayers (Yt. 5.33–35; Yt. 14; Yt. 19) and in the Frashokereti narrative that closes cosmic time, and the eschatological pendant places a final version of the combat within the Zoroastrian liturgical-and-cosmological cycle — the combat is therefore embedded in both daily-prayer and end-of-time-renovation contexts, with the end-of-time-renovation context closing the sovereignty-combat narrative in a manner that the other six cases do not.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna*, on Avestan Yt. 5.33–35, Yt. 14, and Yt. 19 as the Avestan yasht-prayer ritual context; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, Part V chs. 44–48 on the Frashokereti pendant as eschatological closure of the sovereignty-combat narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35, Yt. 14, Yt. 19, Avestan ritual
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48, Frashokereti pendant

The Hebrew Bible case's ritual embedding is the Psalms-Isaiah-Job poetic-cultic corpus: Psalms 74 and 89 have cultic framing, with Day 1985 arguing that they draw on liturgies that preserved the Canaanite combat-myth imagery in Yahwistic adaptation; Isaiah 27:1's eschatological reuse ("on that day Yahweh will slay Leviathan the twisting serpent") projects the combat forward into a yet-to-come event whose anticipation is itself part of the Hebrew Bible's prophetic-liturgical horizon — the ritual embedding is more diffuse than the Mesopotamian or Ugaritic cases but is present across the corpus.footnote:[Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, chs. 1–5 on the Psalms-Isaiah-Job ritual framing and on the eschatological reuse in Isa 27:1; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, chs. 2–4 on the same corpus as ritual-embedded sovereignty-combat material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-5, Psalms-Isaiah-Job ritual framing
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=chs. 2-4, ritual-embedded sovereignty-combat

The Hurro-Hittite case's ritual embedding is less directly attested in the surviving record than the Mesopotamian, Ugaritic, or Vedic cases: Hoffner 1998 notes that the Kumarbi Cycle is not explicitly liturgical in the surviving record, but the cycle's succession-narrative is embedded in the Hurrian-Hittite religious framework that under-girds royal-ideology of the king-as-storm-god's-representative; the Teshub-Ullikummi combat is therefore ritually-embedded indirectly via the wider Hittite royal-ideology rather than directly via a known annual rite.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, introduction on the Kumarbi Cycle's non-explicit liturgical status and on the embedding-via-royal-ideology reading; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 3 on the Hurro-Hittite combat-myth as state-religious-framework material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction, embedding-via-royal-ideology
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3, state-religious-framework

The Mesopotamian Anzû case's ritual embedding is the Neo-Assyrian royal ritual: Annus 2001 notes that the Tablet of Destinies theft-and-recovery narrative is invoked in Neo-Assyrian royal ritual and in the akītu framework, with the king's relationship to the kingship-token modelled on Ninurta's restoration of the Tablet to its rightful holder — the ritual embedding is at the level of royal-ideological background rather than at the level of a single annual recitation, but the ritual function is continuous with the akītu pattern that *Enūma Eliš* recitation participates in.footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the Anzû Epic's Neo-Assyrian royal-ritual embedding; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, pp. 463–465 on the akītu framework that the Anzû ritual material participates in.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Neo-Assyrian royal ritual
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465, akītu framework

The chapter's ritual-and-state pattern is that the sovereignty combat is the rite that the king performs-by-proxy: the divine champion's combat-narrative is the divine-time precedent for the king's continued political-religious legitimacy, and the recitation, palace-founding, sacrifice, prayer, or prophetic-eschatological projection that ritually embeds the narrative is the human-side performance of the divine-time event — a pattern Lincoln 2012 reads as the political-ideological work of combat-myth as state-charter material.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml structure §VI on the king-performs-by-proxy reading; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the political-ideological reading of combat-myth as state-charter performance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as state-charter performance

The ritual-and-state pattern is the chapter's closing evidence for the brief's central claim that sovereignty is the primary function of most combat myths in the book's corpus: when the combat is ritually embedded in a state-charter context (akītu, palace-founding, soma sacrifice, royal-ideology, prophetic-eschatology), the embedding is itself evidence that the combat's primary function is sovereignty-establishment-and-maintenance, because the ritual contexts are themselves political-religious institutions whose work is sovereignty-related rather than cosmogonic.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml comparative_argument on the ritual-and-state pattern as closing evidence for the sovereignty-as-primary-function claim; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the political-religious institutions as sovereignty-related.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on political-religious institutions as sovereignty-work

The chapter's settled position closes with a return to the Ballentine framing engaged at length in §II: the alternative-sovereign reading is the strongest available interpretive frame for this subtype, the per-tradition philological evidence supports it independently across the seven cases, the documented transmission lines explain part but not all of the cluster's coherence, and the ritual-and-state pattern shows that what the combat does in its primary ritual context is sovereignty-establishment-and-maintenance — so the inherited "chaos" terminology, scare-quoted here, is an artifact of nineteenth-century framing that this subtype's evidence does not support.footnote:[Per brief 35-C2-combat-as-sovereignty.yaml special_instructions on the settled-position summary and on the C2-specific tightening of the chaos-terminology discipline; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the inherited "chaos" terminology as artifact of nineteenth-century framing; per scope.md §"Terminological position" on the contested status of the Chaoskampf category.]
