# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).
4. Greece & Rome — Zeus vs. Typhon (full); family chapter "Greek dragon-combats after Zeus-Typhon" (Apollo/Python, Herakles/Hydra-Ladon-Geryon, Perseus/Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Cacus).
5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).
7. Egypt — Ra vs. Apep (full); shorter treatment of Horus vs. Seth as the debated adjacent case.
8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).
9. East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). Yu the Great appears as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering, not as a chaoskampf.
10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.
11. Africa — Bida and the founding of Wagadu (full chapter; the inversion anchor); Mwindo vs. Kirimu (contrastive case: the hero is punished for the killing).
12. Southeast Asia & Oceania — Bakunawa. (Rainbow Serpent is held for the Part II critique chapter, not placed geographically.)
13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).
14. North America (contrastive) — Nanabozho and Mishipeshu: the refusal — the chapter where area specialists (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) are foregrounded and the chaoskampf framing is explicitly declined.

### Part II — thematic comparative chapters
Part II is where the comparative work happens. Load-bearing (confirmed):
- **C1. Combat as cosmogony** — Marduk, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong, Huitzilopochtli.
- **C2. Combat as sovereignty** — Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra, Thraētaona, Kumarbi/Ullikummi, Ninurta/Anzû.
- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.
- **C4. Eschatological displacement** — Thor/Jörmungandr, Rev 12, Frashokereti.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):
- **C5. The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula** — Watkins's argument from Indra through Sigurd to Beowulf.
- **C6. The Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission** — West / Burkert / López-Ruiz: Kumarbi → Hesiod; Baal/Yamm → Typhonomachy.
- **C7. The inversion problem** — Bida anchor; the ambiguous status of Sigurd's slaying.
- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").
- **Methods sidebar** between Part I and Part II: tier-ranking method, appropriation protocol, area-specialist citation policy.
- **Reception coda** (final chapter, ~20–25 pages): traces the afterlife of the imagery in Milton's Satan, Christian iconography of St. Michael / St. George, Tolkien's dragons (and the 1936 Beowulf essay), modern kaiju (Godzilla and the Orochi lineage), and the continuing use of "chaos vs. order" rhetoric in contemporary religious interpretation. Explicitly *not* classified as chaoskampf; this is reception, not primary material.
- **Adversaries + champions appendix** (replaces the single-culture "character appendix"): one entry per monster (Tiamat, Yamm, Lôtan, Leviathan, Rahab, Vṛtra, Aži Dahāka, Apep, Jörmungandr, Typhon, Illuyanka, Ullikummi, Hedammu, the Red Dragon, Coyolxauhqui, etc.) plus one entry per champion. This is load-bearing because the cross-cultural comparisons happen across *figures*, not plots.
- **Bibliography**: preliminary estimate 150–250 entries.

### Terminology default (locked)

Body prose uses "combat myth" as the default, reserving "Chaoskampf" for quotations, discussions of Gunkel's term, and the Part I and Part II passages where the specificity matters. This lets the book engage the Ballentine critique substantively rather than tokenistically.

# Skill instructions: chapter-draft

---
name: chapter-draft
description: Transforms the verified facts into Asimov-style narrative prose. The AI's only job is writing — the facts are already checked. No new facts may be introduced. Every verified claim must appear in the narrative, and nothing unverified may be added.
---

# chapter-draft

## For the human

This is the creative stage — and it is much safer now that the facts are already locked. The AI is not simultaneously researching, citing, and writing. It is only writing. The factual skeleton has already been written and verified. The AI's job is to turn a list of numbered, checked facts into a narrative that reads like Asimov at his best.

The main risk is no longer inventing facts (those are locked) but **drift during the prose transformation**: dropping a verified fact because it doesn't fit the narrative flow, softening a claim until its meaning changes, or inserting a "helpful" detail that was never in the verified facts. The next step (`narrative-fidelity`) will catch these, but the writing AI should work to prevent them.

Importantly, the writing AI does **not** receive the original source texts. It only receives the verified claims document. This is deliberate: giving it the sources would tempt it to "improve" the chapter with additional details that were never fact-checked.

## Hard rules
1. **Every factual assertion in the narrative must correspond to a claim in the approved claims document.** No new facts, no new citations, no "I happen to know" additions.
2. **Every claim in the approved document must appear in the narrative.** Do not silently drop claims because they are inconvenient for the prose flow. If a claim is genuinely irrelevant, flag it rather than omitting it.
3. Use exactly the English renderings locked in `glossary.yaml`.
4. All markers from the claims document survive into the narrative. Do not paraphrase them away.
5. Citations carry over from the claims document. Do not add, remove, or reassign footnotes.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc` (fact-checked, human-reviewed claims)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml` (for variant classification, target length, special instructions)

**Not** primary source translations. The drafter does not need the sources — the claims document already contains everything verified. Providing sources would tempt the drafter to "improve" claims with additional details.

## Style: the Asimov method

Treat the reader as intelligent and uninformed. Gloss proper nouns with functional roles, not poetic epithets. Short paragraphs, one idea each. Plain modern English. Where the source is ambiguous, say so. Where scholars disagree, name it briefly. Use etymology and geography when they illuminate a specific point, never as mandatory ornament.

### Exemplar passage

> The Sumerians believed that before anything else existed, there was a primeval sea. They called her Nammu, and she seems to have been thought of as a kind of cosmic mother — though the texts that survive are maddeningly brief on this point. From Nammu came heaven and earth, conceived of as a single mountain with the sky (An) on top and the earth (Ki) below. An and Ki were the first divine couple, and their union produced Enlil, the god of the air.
>
> Enlil then did something that requires explanation. He separated his father An from his mother Ki, pushing the sky upward and leaving the earth below. This is a motif found in many mythologies — the violent separation of sky and earth by a younger god — but the Sumerian version is unusually matter-of-fact about it. There is no battle, no castration, no cosmic drama. Enlil simply separates them, and the world as we know it comes into being.

### Forbidden words

Do not use, in this register: *shimmering, ancient* (as ornament), *mystical, unfathomable, primordial* (as ornament), *ineffable, sacred* (as filler), *mighty, fearsome, dread, eternal, boundless, infinite, veiled, shrouded*. Avoid openings like "Long ago...", "In the time before time...", "It is said that...". These mark imitation-sacred prose, not Asimov.

## Variant handling (from brief)

- If `variants.classification` is **single-prevalent**: present the prevalent version in the body. Put alternate versions in AsciiDoc footnotes.
- If **co-equal**: present the variants inline in the body, each introduced with its source ("One tradition, preserved in [source], describes... Another, in [source], gives a different account...").

## Narrative transitions

The claims document has no connective tissue — that is your job. You may add:
- **Transitional sentences** that connect one claim to the next ("Having established the cosmic order, the narrative turns to...")
- **Orienting context** that helps the reader follow the story ("At this point in the text, the setting shifts to...")
- **Explanatory asides** that help the reader understand why something matters ("This is worth pausing on, because...")

These are **not** factual claims — they are prose scaffolding. They must not introduce new information about the myth. If a transition requires a factual bridge, that bridge should already be in the claims document as an `[INFERENCE:]` marker.

(trans.), *Florentine Codex*, Book 3 ("The Origin of the Gods").]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=interpretive chapters on Coatepec as Templo Mayor

The Huitzilopochtli cosmogony is unparalleled in the present book in the explicitness of its architectural and ritual embedding: the Templo Mayor at the centre of Tenochtitlán is itself architecturally Coatepec, with the great serpent-headed balustrades flanking the southern (Huitzilopochtli) staircase, the great Coyolxauhqui stone (3.25 metres in diameter, discovered 21 February 1978) at the foot of that staircase depicting the dismembered moon-goddess at the base of the cosmic mountain, and the structural design of the four-tiered pyramid as the cosmic mountain on which the combat occurred.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, interpretive chapters on the Templo-Mayor-as-Coatepec architectural identification, on the serpent-headed balustrades, on the Coyolxauhqui stone discovered in 1978, and on the four-tiered structural design as the cosmic mountain; Carrasco, D., *City of Sacrifice: The Aztec Empire and the Role of Violence in Civilization* (Boston: Beacon Press, 1999), chapter on Coatepec.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=interpretive chapters, architectural-iconographic evidence
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

The Panquetzaliztli winter-solstice festival re-enacted the Coatepec combat annually at the Templo Mayor, with mock combats between warriors representing Huitzilopochtli's forces and the Centzon Huitznahua, the casting of sacrificial victims down the southern staircase onto the Coyolxauhqui stone, and the ritual feasting on a dough-image (*tzoalli*) of the god — a public-ritual sequence that, on Carrasco 1999's reading, performs the cosmogonic combat on a cosmic-political scale.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, ch. on Coatepec on the Panquetzaliztli festival's ritual sequence (the *tzoalli* dough-image, the mock combats, the casting of victims onto the Coyolxauhqui stone, the ritual feasting); Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, on the same festival sequence and its cosmogonic re-enactment role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec, Panquetzaliztli festival

The Huitzilopochtli combat is co-constitutive with the political identity of the Mexica state: López Austin 1993 places the Coatepec narrative within the wider Mesoamerican cosmogonic typology of the Suns cycle (in which the present age is the Fifth Sun under Huitzilopochtli), and the Mexica state explicitly claims Coatepec as its founding combat with the Templo Mayor as the architectural embodiment of that founding — a feature that no other case in the present chapter's four-way comparison matches in explicitness.footnote:[López Austin, A., *The Myths of the Opossum: Pathways of Mesoamerican Mythology* (Albuquerque: University of New Mexico Press, 1993), chapter on cosmic combat on the Coatepec narrative within the wider Mesoamerican cosmogonic typology and on the Fifth-Sun framing; Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, on the architectural-embodiment claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=ch. on cosmic combat
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=interpretive chapters, architectural-embodiment

The four cases share the combat-as-origin-of-present-geography pattern but differ in the texture of the cosmogonic outcome: Marduk constructs the cosmos from Tiamat's dismembered body (substance-cosmogony); Ninurta reorders the existing landscape after defeating Asag (rearrangement-cosmogony); Gonggong's defeat tilts the existing cosmos and the surviving structure is the asymmetric outcome (catastrophe-cosmogony); Huitzilopochtli's victory establishes the diurnal solar order over the defeated lunar-and-stellar siblings (recurrence-cosmogony).footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml special_instructions on the four-cases-share-yet-differ disciplinary requirement; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological diversity of cosmogonic-outcome modes within the combat-myth corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological diversity

The four cases also differ in the role of architecture: Marduk founds Esagil and Babylon as the cosmic axis after the combat (architecture as consequence of cosmogony); Huitzilopochtli's combat is built into the Templo Mayor as Coatepec (architecture as embodiment of cosmogony); Ninurta and Gonggong have no comparable architectural-embedding (the Tigris embankment and Mount Buzhou are landscape-features rather than constructed architecture).footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet VI on the founding of Esagil; Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, interpretive chapters on the Templo Mayor as architectural embodiment of the Coatepec narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet VI, Esagil-founding
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=interpretive chapters, Templo-Mayor-as-Coatepec

The four cases differ in geopolitical framing: Marduk's combat is a Babylonian state-charter that legitimates the imperial elevation of Babylon (Lambert 2013; Lincoln 2012); Huitzilopochtli's is a Mexica state-charter that legitimates the political centrality of Tenochtitlán (Carrasco 1999); Ninurta's is part of Sumerian royal ideology of the warrior-ruler but predates a specific imperial centre; Gonggong's, embedded in the Han-period sky-emperor succession, is part of the early-imperial Chinese cosmographic-and-political narrative without a single state-charter framing.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the Babylonian state-charter framing; Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, on the Mexica state-charter framing; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the political-ideological reading of combat-myth as state-charter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, Babylonian state-charter
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as political-cosmogonic

The four cases differ in the textual situation of the primary witnesses: *Enūma Eliš* survives in over seventy Akkadian-language manuscripts from the Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian periods (Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15); *Lugal-e* is a Sumerian-language composition surviving in dozens of Old Babylonian school-tablet copies from Nippur and Ur with later Akkadian-bilingual receptions (van Dijk 1983); the Gonggong narrative is a Han-period reconstruction from compilation-witnesses without a continuous Warring-States primary text (Lewis 2006); the Coatepec narrative is a post-conquest textual reconstruction (1521 CE onward, principally the *Florentine Codex*) corroborated by the pre-conquest archaeological record at the Templo Mayor (Matos Moctezuma 1988).footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, pp. 3–15 on the *Enūma Eliš* manuscript distribution; van Dijk 1983, *LUGAL UD ME-LÁM-bi NIR-ĜÁL*, vol. 1 introduction on the *Lugal-e* school-tablet transmission; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on the Han-period reconstruction; Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, on the post-conquest textual situation and the archaeological corroboration; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the four-case textual-situation diversity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15, Enūma Eliš manuscript distribution
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, textual-situation diversity

The four cases differ in the relation of cosmogony to ritual re-enactment: the Marduk-Tiamat cosmogony is recited (not re-fought) annually at the akītu festival; the Huitzilopochtli combat is re-enacted (in performative ritual mock-combat-and-sacrifice) annually at Panquetzaliztli; the Ninurta-Asag combat is excerpted into school-curriculum and bilingual religious-pedagogical use across two millennia without a clear festival-re-enactment context; the Gonggong combat is cited in Han ritual-and-political texts as cosmographic aetiology rather than re-enacted in a specific festival liturgy (Lewis 2006).footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, pp. 463–465 on the akītu recitation; Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, ch. on Coatepec on the Panquetzaliztli re-enactment; van Dijk 1983, *LUGAL UD ME-LÁM-bi NIR-ĜÁL*, vol. 1 introduction on the *Lugal-e* school-curriculum transmission; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on the Gonggong cosmographic-aetiology citation pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=ch. on Coatepec, Panquetzaliztli re-enactment
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3, Gonggong cosmographic-aetiology citation

The four cases also differ in the gender of the principal adversary: Tiamat is female (the primordial salt-sea, mother of the first gods) and Coyolxauhqui is female (the moon-goddess, Huitzilopochtli's elder sister); Asag and Gonggong are male (the mountain-demon and the water-god respectively); the gendered distribution does not pattern with the cosmogonic-outcome mode, but it does bear on the kinship-and-political reading of the combat in the Marduk-Tiamat and Huitzilopochtli cases, where the adversary is a maternal-or-sororal figure within the divine genealogy and the combat is therefore a within-family contest as well as a cosmogonic act.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablets I-IV on Tiamat's maternal-and-genealogical position; Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, ch. on Coatepec on Coyolxauhqui's sororal position; ETCSL 1.6.2 (*Lugal-e*), lines 24–47 on Asag's parentage from An and Ki; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on Gonggong's water-god status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets I-IV, Tiamat's maternal-genealogical position
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=ch. on Coatepec, Coyolxauhqui's sororal position

The four cases differ in chronological depth of attestation: *Lugal-e* is preserved in Old Babylonian copies of an underlying composition probably from the late third millennium BCE (Ur III period), making the Ninurta-Asag combat the earliest in the four-case set by approximately a millennium; *Enūma Eliš* dates from the late second millennium BCE under Nebuchadnezzar I (c. 1125–1104 BCE); the Han-period Gonggong material is preserved in compilations from c. 139 BCE onward, drawing on earlier oral-and-literary traditions; the Coatepec narrative is preserved in post-conquest Mexica sources from the mid-sixteenth century CE corroborated by the pre-conquest archaeological record at the late-pre-conquest Templo Mayor (the Stage IV phase contemporary with the Coyolxauhqui stone is dated to the late fifteenth century CE).footnote:[van Dijk 1983, *LUGAL UD ME-LÁM-bi NIR-ĜÁL*, vol. 1 introduction on the Ur-III dating of *Lugal-e*'s underlying composition; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the Nebuchadnezzar-I-commissioning hypothesis for *Enūma Eliš*; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on the Han-period attestation of the Gonggong material; Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, on the Stage IV dating of the Templo Mayor and the Coyolxauhqui stone.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1 introduction, Ur-III dating
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=Stage IV dating, Templo Mayor

The chronological-depth differences mean that the four §I cases sample a broad swathe of the long-arc history of state-level cosmogonic narrative — from late-third-millennium-BCE Sumerian temple-state through second-millennium Babylonian imperial period through Han imperial China through late-pre-conquest Mexica state — with no transmission route reconstructible across the breaks; the across-time-as-well-as-across-space distribution of the four attestations is part of what makes the typological-recurrence claim methodologically interesting.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the chronological-depth distribution; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the across-time political-ideological recurrence as a methodologically interesting datum; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the chronological-depth distribution as a feature of the combat-myth corpus.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, chronological-depth distribution

=== II. What "cosmogony" means here

The chapter's working definition of "cosmogony" is narrower than the popular sense of "creation myth" or "origin story": cosmogony in the present sense is not merely a narrative of origins but specifically a narrative of the origin-of-the-shape-of-the-present-world, in which the present features of the cosmos (geography, hydrography, sky-structure, calendrical-and-political order) are the explicit aftermath of the narrated events.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section II on the working definition of cosmogony; per glossary.yaml entry on "cosmogony / cosmogonic" with "creation myth" listed as informal-only and as broader than the technical category; Forsyth, N., *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1987), on the present-cosmos-aetiology requirement that distinguishes combat-cosmogony from generic creation-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, present-cosmos-aetiology

The narrower definition is what allows the chapter to exclude generic creation-myths (the dismemberment cosmogonies of Ymir, Puruṣa, and Pangu, which are order-making but not present-cosmos-aetiologising in the same explicit way) from the four-case set, and it is what allows the chapter to include the Cipactli case in §IV as the dismemberment-without-combat boundary that sharpens the category.footnote:[Per scope.md §"Excluded adjacent motifs" #1 on the dismemberment cosmogonies (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) as adjacent-but-not-combat-cosmogonic; brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on Cipactli as boundary-marker; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the cosmogonic-combat / dismemberment-cosmogony distinction as a typological feature of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological boundaries

The chapter's working definition follows Forsyth 1987 in treating cosmogonic combat as a structurally distinctive subtype within the wider combat-myth corpus, distinguished from sovereignty combat (where the kingship of the victor is established but the cosmos is not), cyclical maintenance (where the combat is re-enacted to preserve an order already established), and eschatological combat (where the combat closes cosmic time rather than initiating it).footnote:[Per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the four-subtype operationalisation; Forsyth, N., *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1987), on the cosmogonic-combat subtype as structurally distinct from sovereignty, cyclical, and eschatological combats within the wider corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, subtype distinction

Lincoln 2012's reading of cosmogonic combat as political-ideological work supplies the second strand of the chapter's working definition: the cosmogony is not only an aetiology of the present cosmos but an ideological charter that grounds present political-and-religious order in a divine combat, and the combat's cosmogonic and political functions are co-constitutive rather than separable.footnote:[Lincoln, B., *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars: Critical Explorations in the History of Religions* (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 2012), on the *Enūma Eliš* cosmogony as paradigm case of myth as political-ideological work and on the co-constitutive character of cosmogonic and political functions in combat-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as political-cosmogonic

Scurlock & Beal 2013's reassessment of Gunkel's Chaoskampf hypothesis supplies the third strand: the volume's individual chapters re-examine the cosmogonic-combat material on a tradition-by-tradition basis and qualify Gunkel's framing where individual specialists find the evidence does not support the strong-cosmogonic reading, while preserving the cosmogonic-combat category as a useful comparative tool — a stance the present chapter follows in retaining the category while acknowledging the case-by-case qualifications.footnote:[Scurlock, J. and Beal, R. (eds.), *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis* (Winona Lake: Eisenbrauns, 2013), passim on the case-by-case reassessment that retains the cosmogonic-combat category while qualifying Gunkel's framing where individual specialists find the evidence underdetermines it.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=cosmogony chapters, case-by-case reassessment

Gunkel's 1895 thesis itself remains the historical anchor for the cosmogonic-combat category: the term *Chaoskampf* and the identification of Marduk-Tiamat as the type-specimen are Gunkel's, and the chapter retains both because they are the terms in which the comparative literature has organised its discussion for one hundred and thirty years — even where, as Scurlock & Beal 2013 and Ballentine 2015 argue, Gunkel's framing requires substantive qualification.footnote:[Gunkel, H., *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* (Göttingen: Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht, 1895), Teil I, on Marduk-Tiamat as the type-specimen of the Chaoskampf category; per scope.md §"Terminological position" on the retention of the term as historical anchor while acknowledging the critique.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I, original cosmogony argument

The chapter does not adopt Witzel 2012's "Laurasian" macro-scheme as a framework for cosmogonic combat, even though Witzel's volume engages combat-cosmogonic material across cultures: the Laurasian scheme posits a deep-time inherited macro-narrative covering Eurasia and the Americas, and current comparative-mythology scholarship (including Lincoln 2012, Forsyth 1987, and the contributors to Scurlock & Beal 2013) treats the macro-scheme as methodologically controversial; Witzel is therefore cited only where his treatment of specific stories is at issue rather than as the chapter's framing.footnote:[Witzel, M., *The Origins of the World's Mythologies* (New York: Oxford University Press, 2012), on the "Laurasian" macro-scheme as a comparative framework for cosmogonic material; per sources.yaml Witzel-2012 notes on the "use with caution" disciplinary stance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=witzel-2012 ; loc=pan-cultural cosmogony — cited with caveats

The chapter's working definition of cosmogony is therefore a Forsyth-Lincoln-Scurlock-Beal definition rather than a Witzel definition: it treats cosmogonic combat as a typological pattern that recurs across non-cognate traditions without committing to a deep-time inheritance claim, and treats the combat's political-ideological function as load-bearing rather than as a secondary application of the cosmogonic content.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on typological-recurrence-without-monomyth; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, and Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, as the principal methodological warrants for the typological-without-deep-inheritance framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as political-cosmogonic

The chapter's working definition has two operational consequences for what counts as a cosmogonic combat in the §I four-case set: first, the combat's outcome must explicitly aetiologise a feature of the present-day cosmos in the tradition's own primary sources (not only in modern comparativist reading); second, the area-specialist tradition for the case must read the outcome as cosmogonic rather than as merely sovereignty-establishing or cult-foundational — a criterion that filters out cases like Apollo–Python, where Greek-area scholars (Ogden 2013) treat the killing primarily as a Delphic cult-foundation rather than as a cosmogony.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section II on the operational criteria; per Ch33's tier-ranking rubric (claims document, section I) on the area-specialist requirement; Ogden, D., *Drakōn: Dragon Myth and Serpent Cult in the Greek and Roman Worlds* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2013), on the Greek-area treatment of Apollo–Python as cult-foundation rather than cosmogony.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=Drakōn, Apollo-Python as Delphic cult-foundation

The four cases in §I all pass both operational criteria: each is explicitly aetiologised to a present-day cosmos-feature in its own primary sources (the construction of sky-and-earth from Tiamat; the Tigris embankment and lapidary order; the asymmetric tilt and east-flowing rivers; the diurnal solar-kingship at Templo Mayor), and each is read as cosmogonic by the area-specialist tradition that treats it (Lambert 2013 and Foster 2005 for Marduk; van Dijk 1983 and the ETCSL editors for Ninurta; Lewis 2006 and Birrell 1993 for Gonggong; Matos Moctezuma 1988, Carrasco 1999, and López Austin 1993 for Huitzilopochtli).footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section II on the four-case operational pass; the per-tradition area-specialist anchors (Lambert 2013, Foster 2005, van Dijk 1983, ETCSL, Lewis 2006, Birrell 1993, Matos Moctezuma 1988, Carrasco 1999, López Austin 1993) supplying the cosmogonic readings; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological-cosmogonic placement of all four.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological placement

=== III. Independence vs. transmission

The four cases in §I divide into two pairs on the question of independence vs. transmission: Marduk-Tiamat (*Enūma Eliš*) and Ninurta-Asag (*Lugal-e*) are genealogically linked within the Mesopotamian tradition, with *Enūma Eliš* a deliberate literary descendant of the earlier Sumerian and Akkadian combat-myth corpus (including the intermediate Anzû Epic); Gonggong and Huitzilopochtli are geographically and linguistically independent of the Mesopotamian pair and of each other.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section III on the two-pairs independence-vs-transmission distribution; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the Mesopotamian-internal genealogical continuity from Sumerian Ninurta material through Akkadian Anzû to *Enūma Eliš*; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction, Mesopotamian genealogical continuity
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Anzû-to-Marduk genealogy

The Mesopotamian-internal genealogy from Ninurta-Asag through Ninurta-Anzû to Marduk-Tiamat is one of the clearest documented cases of within-tradition combat-myth rewriting in the present book's corpus: Annus 2001 and Lambert 2013 both read *Enūma Eliš* as a deliberate literary descendant of the Anzû Epic (in which Ninurta defeats the rebel Anzû and recovers the Tablet of Destinies), with the Anzû combat reused-and-outbid as the Marduk-Tiamat combat — a feature that the chapter treats as transmission rather than independent recurrence.footnote:[Annus, A., *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu* (SAACT 3, Helsinki: Neo-Assyrian Text Corpus Project, 2001), introduction on the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* literary genealogy; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the same; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 2 on the Mesopotamian-internal continuity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, Anzû-to-Marduk genealogy
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2, Mesopotamian-internal continuity

Within the Mesopotamian genealogy, Anzû appears in *Enūma Eliš* itself as one of the eleven monsters in Tiamat's brood (Lambert 2013, Tablet I) — demoted from rebel-protagonist of the older epic to subordinate creature in the new one — which is Annus's load-bearing textual evidence that the *Enūma Eliš* poet had the Anzû Epic in front of him and was deliberately outbidding it.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet I on Anzû's appearance among Tiamat's eleven monsters; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the demotion-of-Anzû detail as load-bearing evidence of the literary genealogy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I, Anzû among Tiamat's monsters

The Tablet of Destinies in *Enūma Eliš* (where Tiamat bestows it on Qingu and Marduk reclaims it after the combat) is itself an inheritance from the Anzû Epic (where Anzû seizes it from Enlil and Ninurta restores it), with the mode of transfer differing (theft in Anzû, gift in *Enūma Eliš*) but the artefact's narrative function preserved — a structural-feature inheritance that the chapter treats as further evidence of the Mesopotamian-internal continuity.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet IV on the Tablet of Destinies in *Enūma Eliš* and the introduction on the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* artefact-inheritance; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the same Tablet-of-Destinies inheritance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet IV, Tablet-of-Destinies inheritance

The threefold-refusal pattern in *Enūma Eliš* Tablet II (Ea is dispatched against Tiamat and turns back; Anu is dispatched and turns back; Marduk is then proposed as the only candidate who can face her) is structurally inherited from the Anzû Epic, where three candidates decline the combat before the eventual champion is named — Annus 2001 reads the structural inheritance as deliberate, with the new poet making the audience hear the older composition's pattern even as he gives it a new resolution.footnote:[Foster 2005, *Before the Muses*, Tablets II–III on the threefold-refusal pattern in *Enūma Eliš*; Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the same threefold-refusal pattern as inherited from the Anzû Epic; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, threefold-refusal inheritance

The Mesopotamian-internal genealogy means that Marduk-Tiamat and Ninurta-Asag do not constitute two independent attestations of the cosmogonic-combat type for typological purposes; they are one genealogically-linked tradition with two principal texts, and the typological-recurrence claim cannot rest on the within-tradition pair alone — it requires the geographically-and-linguistically independent attestations to function as independent witnesses to the same pattern.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section III on the genealogically-linked-vs-independent distinction; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the within-tradition continuity as transmission rather than independent recurrence; Lambert 2013 and Annus 2001 on the Mesopotamian-internal genealogy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on Mesopotamian-internal continuity

Gonggong's combat-cosmogony is geographically and linguistically independent of the Mesopotamian tradition: the Han-period Chinese sources (*Huainanzi*, *Lienü zhuan*, *Shanhaijing*-and-commentaries, *Liezi*) draw on a Warring-States-and-earlier oral-and-literary tradition with no demonstrable transmission route from the Mesopotamian corpus, and Lewis 2006 treats the broken-pillar narrative as a Chinese-tradition-internal cosmogonic-combat without comparative-tradition origin claims.footnote:[Lewis, M.E., *The Flood Myths of Early China* (Albany: SUNY Press, 2006), chs. 2-3 on the Gonggong narrative as a Chinese-tradition-internal cosmogonic-combat without comparative-tradition origin claims; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony on the same Han-period source-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3, Chinese-tradition-internal independence

Huitzilopochtli's combat at Coatepec is geographically and linguistically independent of both the Mesopotamian and the Chinese cases: the Mexica Nahuatl-language tradition is preserved in post-conquest sources (the *Florentine Codex*, the *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas*, Durán's *Historia*) and in the pre-conquest archaeological record at the Templo Mayor, and the area-specialist scholarship (Matos Moctezuma 1988, Carrasco 1999, López Austin 1993) treats the Coatepec narrative as a Mexica-and-wider-Mesoamerican tradition without comparative-tradition origin claims.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, interpretive chapters on the Mexica-and-wider-Mesoamerican character of the Coatepec narrative; Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, ch. on Coatepec; López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, ch. on cosmic combat on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=interpretive chapters, Mexica-Mesoamerican independence

The geographical-and-linguistic independence of Gonggong and Huitzilopochtli from the Mesopotamian pair (and from each other) is the load-bearing premise of the typological-recurrence argument: three independent attestations of the cosmogonic-combat pattern (the Mesopotamian tradition counted as one, plus Gonggong, plus Huitzilopochtli) on three continents and across three unrelated language-families converge on the same structural feature (combat-as-origin-of-present-cosmos), and the convergence supports the claim that combat-cosmogony is a recurring typological pattern rather than a single-origin inheritance.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the four-cases-but-three-independent-attestations distribution as the load-bearing typological-recurrence premise; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on typological-recurrence claims as methodologically distinct from single-origin claims in comparative myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as political-cosmogonic, typological-recurrence framing

The typological-recurrence claim does not entail a universal-monomyth claim: the chapter explicitly does not claim that combat-cosmogony is a universal feature of human cosmogonic narrative, that all (or even most) cosmogonies are combat-cosmogonies, or that the recurrence implies a deep-time inheritance from a Pleistocene-era ancestral macro-narrative — three claims that the present comparative literature (Lincoln 2012, Forsyth 1987, Scurlock & Beal 2013) treats as either false or methodologically unwarranted.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on typological-recurrence-without-monomyth; per Ch33 (methods sidebar) §VII on the explicit not-monomyth-not-universal disciplinary stance the present chapter inherits; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the methodological priority of typological-recurrence over monomyth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as political-cosmogonic, not-monomyth discipline

The independence-and-recurrence distribution is the chapter's central evidential datum — three independent attestations on three continents converge on the cosmogonic-combat pattern — and the recurrence is methodologically more interesting than universality would be, because the recurrence is consistent both with deep-typological-pattern explanations and with independent-emergence-from-shared-cognitive-or-political-conditions explanations, and the chapter does not adjudicate between them.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the recurrence's methodological status; per Ch33 §VII on the present book's disciplinary stance against premature explanatory closure; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on typological recurrence as a comparative datum that can support multiple explanatory hypotheses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological recurrence

[SPECULATION: the convergence of three independent traditions (Mesopotamian, Chinese, Mexica) on a strongly-cosmogonic combat-pattern in which present-day geographic-or-political features are explicitly aetiologised to the combat's outcome may reflect a shared typological response to the political-and-cognitive task of grounding state-level political authority in a divine-combat charter, rather than a deep-time inheritance from a single ancestral narrative; on this reading the typological recurrence is what one would expect to find when state-level polities articulate their founding ideology, and the recurrence-without-transmission is therefore typologically meaningful but does not require a monogenetic explanation. | basis: Lincoln 2012's political-ideological reading of combat-myth as state-charter work in *Enūma Eliš*; Carrasco 1999's parallel reading of Coatepec as Mexica state-charter; Lewis 2006 on the Han-imperial cosmographic-and-political function of the Gonggong narrative; the structural co-presence of cosmogonic content and political-charter function in all three independent attestations; Forsyth 1987 on typological-recurrence-as-shared-response. | counterargument: the political-charter reading does not by itself explain why the charter takes the specific form of a combat-with-monstrous-adversary rather than the dismemberment-cosmogony form (Cipactli, Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu) that area specialists also document for state-level polities; Witzel 2012's "Laurasian" reading would treat the recurrence as evidence of deep-time inheritance rather than as independent typological response; the present chapter does not claim to adjudicate between the political-typology and deep-inheritance hypotheses, and the [SPECULATION] here is offered as one plausible reading rather than as the chapter's settled position.]

The Mesopotamian-internal genealogy from Ninurta to Marduk is, separately, a documented case of how a sovereignty combat (Anzû, where the Tablet of Destinies is the prize and the cosmos is not remade) gets escalated into a cosmogonic combat (Marduk-Tiamat, where the cosmos is constructed from the adversary's body), and Annus 2001 and Lambert 2013 treat the escalation as a deliberate literary move rather than a passive transmission — a feature that bears on the next comparative chapter (C2, sovereignty) where the Anzû case is treated in its own right.footnote:[Annus 2001, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu*, introduction on the deliberate-escalation reading of the Anzû-to-*Enūma Eliš* genealogy; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction on the same; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 2 on sovereignty-to-cosmogonic escalation as a feature of the Mesopotamian corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction, sovereignty-to-cosmogonic escalation
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2, sovereignty-to-cosmogonic escalation

The geographical separation between the four cases is substantial enough to make transmission accounts methodologically unworkable: the Mesopotamian centre of attestation (Babylonia and Sumer) and the Han-Chinese centre of attestation are separated by approximately 6,000 kilometres of intervening territory across which no continuous documentary trail of the cosmogonic-combat narrative exists, and the Mexica centre of attestation is separated from both by Atlantic and Pacific distances with no pre-Columbian transmission route plausibly reconstructible across either ocean; the typological-recurrence claim is therefore consistent with the geographical-separation evidence in a way that a single-origin claim is not.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the geographical-separation evidence; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on the Han-Chinese tradition's autonomy from Near Eastern transmission; Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs*, on the Mexica-and-wider-Mesoamerican tradition's pre-Columbian autonomy; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological-recurrence-without-transmission methodological stance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3, Chinese-tradition autonomy
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=interpretive chapters, Mexica pre-Columbian autonomy

The linguistic separation reinforces the geographical: the Mesopotamian materials are in Sumerian and Akkadian (Afro-Asiatic / language-isolate), the Chinese in Old Chinese (Sino-Tibetan), and the Mexica in Classical Nahuatl (Uto-Aztecan); the three language-families have no demonstrable historical-comparative connection within any reconstructible time-depth, and the technical-philological methodology for establishing transmission (cognate vocabulary in cult terminology, formulaic-poetic inheritance, parallel grammatical-syntactic shaping of the combat-narrative) finds no supporting evidence across the three traditions.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the linguistic-separation evidence; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, on the philological methodology for establishing transmission within the Indo-European corpus and the absence of comparable evidence across non-cognate language-families; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological-without-transmission disciplinary stance.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological-without-transmission

=== IV. The boundary case — Cipactli

The Cipactli narrative — preserved in the *Histoyre du Mechique* (c. 1543) and the *Leyenda de los Soles* (1558) — recounts that Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl pull apart the primordial earth-monster Cipactli and use her dismembered body to make the sky and the earth, with no combat in the strict sense; the two gods do not fight Cipactli but disassemble her, and the cosmogony is dismemberment-without-combat.footnote:[López Austin, A., *The Myths of the Opossum: Pathways of Mesoamerican Mythology* (Albuquerque: University of New Mexico Press, 1993), on the Cipactli narrative in the *Histoyre du Mechique* and *Leyenda de los Soles* and on the dismemberment-without-combat character of the cosmogony; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml family_members entry on Cipactli.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=Cipactli dismemberment-cosmogony

The Cipactli case is included in the present chapter precisely as a boundary-marker for the cosmogonic-combat category: it is the same Mesoamerican cultural region that produces Huitzilopochtli's combat-cosmogony at Coatepec and that produces Cipactli's dismemberment-cosmogony, and the Mexica tradition holds both as legitimate cosmogonic narratives without treating them as competing — a feature that sharpens what "combat" adds to "cosmogony" in the present chapter's working definition.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on Cipactli as boundary-marker; brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml on the Mesoamerican tradition as holding both Cipactli (dismemberment-without-combat) and Huitzilopochtli (combat-cosmogony) as legitimate cosmogonic narratives; López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, on the within-tradition co-existence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=Cipactli boundary-marker, within-tradition co-existence

Cipactli converges with the four §I cases on the cosmogonic outcome (the present sky and earth are made from her body) but diverges on the combat criterion: there is no contest, no resistance, no narrative tension between the cosmogonic agents and Cipactli; the dismemberment is performed on a passive (or at any rate non-combatant) primordial figure — and that absence of combat is what makes Cipactli a boundary case rather than a cosmogonic-combat case.footnote:[López Austin, A., *The Myths of the Opossum* (Albuquerque: University of New Mexico Press, 1993), on the no-combat character of the Cipactli dismemberment; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml on the same; per scope.md §"Excluded adjacent motifs" #1 on dismemberment cosmogonies as adjacent-but-not-combat-cosmogonic.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=Cipactli no-combat character

The Cipactli case sits in scope.md's §"Excluded adjacent motifs" #1 alongside Ymir (Norse), Puruṣa (Vedic), and Pangu (Chinese) as the canonical dismemberment-cosmogonies that the book excludes from the chaoskampf category proper; the Cipactli case is treated in the §25 family chapter rather than excluded outright, precisely so that the boundary can be made explicit rather than papered over.footnote:[Per scope.md §"Excluded adjacent motifs" #1 on Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, and arguably Cipactli as canonical dismemberment-cosmogonies; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml on Cipactli's inclusion in the family chapter as boundary-marker rather than excluded outright; López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, on the wider Mesoamerican context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=Cipactli within wider Mesoamerican context

What "combat" adds to "cosmogony" in the present chapter's typology is therefore not the body-dismemberment outcome (which Cipactli, Ymir, Puruṣa, and Pangu also produce) but the agonistic structure: a contest between the cosmogonic agent and an adversary whose resistance must be overcome, and whose defeat — not whose passive cooperation — is what produces the cosmogonic outcome.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on what combat adds to cosmogony; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, chs. on cosmogonic combat on the agonistic-structure feature as the load-bearing distinction; López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, on Cipactli as the absence of that feature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, agonistic-structure feature
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=Cipactli, absence of agonistic structure

The agonistic-structure distinction has a textual-feature consequence in the four §I cases that Cipactli does not have: in each of Marduk-Tiamat, Ninurta-Asag, Gonggong, and Huitzilopochtli, the primary sources include some version of a verbal-or-political challenge — Tiamat's mobilisation of her army and Marduk's challenge before the combat (Lambert 2013, Tablet IV); Asag's reign in the mountains and Šarur's intelligence reports (ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47); Gonggong's contest for sovereignty with Zhuanxu (Lewis 2006, chs. 2–3); the Centzon Huitznahua's plot against the pregnant Coatlicue at Coatepec (Anderson-Dibble *Florentine Codex*, Book 3) — and the verbal-or-political challenge is one of the features that mark the agonistic structure as load-bearing.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet IV on Marduk's challenge before the combat; ETCSL 1.6.2 (*Lugal-e*), lines 24–47 on Asag's reign and Šarur's intelligence reports; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on Gonggong's contest for sovereignty; Anderson-Dibble *Florentine Codex*, Book 3 on the Centzon Huitznahua's plot; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on the verbal-or-political challenge as agonistic-structure marker.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet IV, Marduk's challenge
// EVIDENCE: source_id=anderson-dibble-florentine ; loc=Book 3, Centzon Huitznahua's plot

The Cipactli boundary case also illustrates that a single tradition can hold multiple ordering-templates simultaneously: the Mesoamerican corpus holds Cipactli as the dismemberment-without-combat cosmogony alongside Huitzilopochtli at Coatepec as the combat-cosmogony, and the same tradition that produces the Templo Mayor architectural-embedding of Coatepec also produces the *Histoyre du Mechique*'s Cipactli dismemberment without treating the two as in competition — a within-tradition diversity feature that parallels the Chinese tradition's within-tradition co-presence of the Gonggong combat and the Yu hydraulic-ordering (Ch21 sidebar).footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, on the within-tradition co-presence of Cipactli and Huitzilopochtli in the Mesoamerican corpus; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 and Yu chapters on the parallel Gonggong / Yu within-tradition co-presence; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on within-tradition diversity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=Cipactli / Huitzilopochtli within-tradition co-presence
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3 and Yu chapters, Gonggong / Yu within-tradition co-presence

The within-tradition diversity feature undercuts any reading on which combat-cosmogony is the privileged or sole cosmogonic template within a culture: the same Mesoamerican tradition produces both, the same Chinese tradition produces both, and the same Indo-European corpus produces both (Indra-Vṛtra in §V alongside the Vedic Puruṣa dismemberment-cosmogony of *Rigveda* 10.90, the latter not engaged in the present chapter).footnote:[López Austin 1993 on the Mesoamerican within-tradition diversity; Lewis 2006 on the Chinese within-tradition diversity; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on the within-tradition-diversity claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=within-tradition diversity, Mesoamerican

The chapter's claim is therefore that combat-cosmogony is a recurring typological pattern across non-cognate traditions but is not the sole or privileged cosmogonic template within any of them; the four §I cases share the pattern, and the §IV Cipactli boundary-case sharpens what "combat" adds (the agonistic structure), and the within-tradition diversity makes the typological-recurrence claim more interesting rather than less.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section IV on the not-sole-or-privileged-template disciplinary stance; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological-pattern reading as compatible with within-tradition diversity; López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological pattern
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=within-tradition diversity

=== V. The near-case — Indra-Vṛtra

Indra's combat with Vṛtra in the *Rigveda* (principally RV 1.32, with supplementary hymns RV 2.11–12, RV 4.18, RV 5.32, RV 6.17, and RV 10.113) does cosmogonic work in the strong sense — the combat separates heaven and earth, releases the obstructed waters, and produces the dawn — but it does not produce the cosmos from the body of Vṛtra in the way that *Enūma Eliš* produces the cosmos from the body of Tiamat; this distinction is what makes Indra-Vṛtra a near-case rather than a member of the §I four-case set.footnote:[Jamison, S.W. and Brereton, J.P., *The Rigveda: The Earliest Religious Poetry of India* (3 vols., New York: Oxford University Press, 2014), translation and commentary on RV 1.32 on Indra's combat as separating heaven and earth, releasing the waters, and producing dawn; per brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the same; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablets IV-V on the body-of-Tiamat construction-cosmogony as the contrast.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32, Indra's cosmogonic-work-without-cosmogony
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets IV-V, body-of-Tiamat construction-cosmogony

RV 1.32 — the *Rigveda*'s most concentrated and complete hymn (15 verses) on the Vṛtra combat — explicitly credits Indra with three cosmogonic acts that follow the slaying: the separation of heaven and earth (which had been undivided before Vṛtra's killing), the release of the waters that Vṛtra had been obstructing (the "seven rivers" flow once Vṛtra is dead), and the appearance of dawn (which had been suppressed under Vṛtra's blockage); the three acts together amount to a cosmogonic outcome in the strong sense that present-day cosmic features are explicitly aetiologised to the combat.footnote:[Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, translation and commentary on RV 1.32 on the three cosmogonic acts (separation of heaven and earth, release of the waters, appearance of dawn); per brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32, three cosmogonic acts

The combat is named in the *Rigveda* by the formula *áhann áhim* "he slew the serpent" (RV 1.32.1), which Watkins 1995 reconstructs as an Indo-European poetic formula (subject + verb-of-killing + serpent-object) attested in cognate form across Vedic, Hittite (kuenta…illuyanka), and Greek, and which is the chapter's principal cross-reference to the C5 Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT chapter (Ch38) where the philological reconstruction is engaged at length.footnote:[Watkins, C., *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1995), chs. 41-48 on Indra-Vṛtra as central case for the IE HERO SLAY SERPENT formula; Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, on RV 1.32.1 *áhann áhim*; per glossary.yaml entries on "HERO SLAY SERPENT (formula)" and "áhann áhim".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48, IE HERO SLAY SERPENT formula
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.1, áhann áhim

Vṛtra's name itself derives from the Vedic root *vṛ-* "to obstruct, to enclose" (Benveniste & Renou 1934; Jamison & Brereton 2014), and the *Rigveda*'s consistent characterisation of Vṛtra is as an obstacle that blocks the waters rather than as a primordial-disorder figure or a chaos-substance; the obstruction-frame is what Indra's combat releases, and the cosmogonic outcome is therefore the release-of-the-obstructed rather than the construction-of-the-cosmos-from-substance.footnote:[Benveniste, É. and Renou, L., *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna: Étude de mythologie indo-iranienne* (Paris: Imprimerie Nationale, 1934), on the *vṛ-* etymology and the obstruction-frame; Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, on the consistent obstruction-characterisation across the Vṛtra hymns; per glossary.yaml entry on Vṛtra.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=*vṛ-* etymology, obstruction-frame

The release-of-the-obstructed cosmogony differs from the construction-from-substance cosmogony in mode rather than in degree: both are strong-cosmogonic in the sense that present-day cosmic features are explicitly aetiologised to the combat, but the *Enūma Eliš* construction-cosmogony makes the cosmos *out of* the adversary's substance while the *Rigveda* release-cosmogony makes the cosmos *available* by removing the adversary's obstruction — a distinction that the chapter treats as substantive rather than as a difference of emphasis.footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablets IV-V on the construction-from-substance cosmogony of *Enūma Eliš*; Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, on RV 1.32 on the release-cosmogony of Indra-Vṛtra; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section V on the construction-vs-release distinction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets IV-V, construction-from-substance
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32, release-cosmogony

The Indra-Vṛtra near-case is therefore important to the chapter's argument as a near-miss: a combat that does cosmogonic work in the strong sense without being a cosmogony in the §I sense (no body-of-the-adversary physical cosmos-construction); the near-miss helps locate where the §I four-case set's distinguishing feature is — the construction-from-substance or rearrangement-around-the-combat-outcome — and shows that the cosmogonic-combat type is not exhausted by even the strong-cosmogonic combats that resemble Marduk-Tiamat structurally.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section V on Indra-Vṛtra as near-case and its location-of-distinguishing-feature role; Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, on the strong-cosmogonic character of Indra's combat; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological diversity of cosmogonic-outcome modes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32, strong-cosmogonic-without-body-construction
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological diversity

The Indra-Vṛtra case has been compared structurally to Marduk-Tiamat by West 2007 and others — both are storm-god-defeats-water-or-water-obstruction combats — but the structural parallel does not carry a transmission claim: the present comparative-mythology consensus (West 2007; Watkins 1995) treats the Indra-Vṛtra material as a primary witness to the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula and as a structural parallel to (rather than a member of) the Mesopotamian cosmogonic-combat tradition.footnote:[West, M.L., *Indo-European Poetry and Myth* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2007), ch. 6 on Indra-Vṛtra and Marduk-Tiamat as structural parallels without transmission; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, on Indra-Vṛtra as primary witness to the IE HERO SLAY SERPENT formula; Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, on the structural-parallel reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48, structural-parallel without transmission
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6, Indra-Vṛtra and Marduk-Tiamat as structural parallels without transmission

Indra's cosmogonic work in *Rigveda* hymns beyond RV 1.32 reinforces the release-cosmogony reading: RV 2.11–12 on the splitting of the mountain to release the waters; RV 4.18 as the principal "embryonic Indra" hymn, in which Indra's irregular sideways birth (his forced exit from his mother's womb against the natural mode of birth) is staged as anticipating the irregular and combat-decisive character of his later career; RV 10.113 on the cosmogonic consequences as confirmed by the assembled gods — together the supplementary hymns establish the cosmogonic-without-construction reading as the *Rigveda*'s own framing rather than as a modern editorial gloss.footnote:[Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, headnote and translation of RV 2.11-12, RV 4.18, and RV 10.113, with the RV 4.18 framing taken from Ch14's reading of the hymn as the "embryonic Indra" hymn whose load-bearing motif is the irregular-sideways-birth-as-anticipation of the combat-decisive career; per brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the supplementary hymn-cycle as supporting the cosmogonic-without-construction reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 2.11-12, 4.18, 10.113, supplementary cosmogonic hymns

The Indra-Vṛtra near-case therefore serves a sharpening function in the chapter parallel to the Cipactli boundary-case in §IV: where Cipactli sharpens what "combat" adds to "cosmogony" (the agonistic structure), Indra-Vṛtra sharpens what kind of cosmogonic-outcome the §I cases share (construction-or-rearrangement of the cosmos, not merely release of an obstruction); the two near-or-boundary cases together specify the §I category by its limits.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure sections IV and V on the parallel-sharpening role of Cipactli and Indra-Vṛtra; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the typological-category-by-its-limits methodology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological-by-limits methodology

The Indra-Vṛtra near-case is engaged at length in the C2 sovereignty-combat chapter (Ch35) where Indra's victory is read as a sovereignty-establishing combat (Indra becomes king of the gods) and in the C5 Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT chapter (Ch38) where the philological reconstruction is engaged in detail; the present chapter's role for Indra-Vṛtra is the cosmogonic-near-case role, with the sovereignty-and-philology dimensions deferred forward.footnote:[Jamison & Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda*, on the sovereignty-establishing dimension of Indra's victory; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, chs. 41-48 on the philological reconstruction; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml on the cosmogonic-near-case-only role within the present chapter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=on Indra's sovereignty-establishing dimension
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48, philological reconstruction

=== VI. Terminological caveat — the Ballentine critique

Debra Scoggins Ballentine's *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (2015) argues that "chaos" is the wrong analytical category for the adversaries in the combat-myth corpus: the adversaries are presented in their primary sources as rival sovereigns with their own retinues and power-structures, not as inchoate disorder, and the term "chaos" — and *a fortiori* the compound "Chaoskampf" — therefore mistranslates the conceptual structure of the texts.footnote:[Ballentine, D.S., *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (New York: Oxford University Press, 2015), ch. 1 on the alternative-sovereign critique and on the methodological problem of "chaos" as analytical category for the combat-myth adversaries; per glossary.yaml entry on "Ballentine critique".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, alternative-sovereign critique

The Ballentine critique applies with force to the four §I cases when the adversaries are read at their primary-source level: Tiamat is a sovereign with a court (in *Enūma Eliš* Tablet I she has a vizier, an army of eleven monstrous champions, and a consort-general — Qingu — to whom she bestows the Tablet of Destinies as a counter-coronation), and to read her as "chaos" is to import a category that the text itself does not deploy.footnote:[Ballentine, D.S., *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (2015), ch. 1 on Tiamat as alternative-sovereign with court rather than chaos; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, Tablet I on Tiamat's vizier, eleven-monsters army, and counter-coronation of Qingu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, Tiamat as alternative-sovereign
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I, Tiamat's court structure

Asag in *Lugal-e* is similarly a sovereign-figure with structure rather than a "chaos-figure": he reigns among the mountains, commands an army of stone-warriors with a named catalogue of stone-types (*šu*, *saĝkal*, *esi*, *usium*, *kagena*), and is presented in the *Lugal-e* proem and in Šarur's intelligence reports as a rival to Ninurta's kingship — again a counter-sovereign with a court and a territory, not an undifferentiated "chaos".footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 (*Lugal-e*), lines 24–47 on Asag's reign in the mountains, the stone-warrior catalogue, and the rival-kingship framing; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the alternative-sovereign critique as applicable to non-Tiamat adversaries; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section VI on Asag's sovereign-not-chaos status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47, Asag as rival sovereign
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, alternative-sovereign critique extended

Gonggong in the Han-period sources is a water-god with status within the early-imperial Chinese pantheon — a rival to the sky-emperor Zhuanxu in the *Huainanzi* version, with combats that are framed as contests for sovereignty rather than as eruptions of inchoate disorder; the Ballentine-style alternative-sovereign reading is straightforwardly applicable to the Gonggong case on Lewis 2006's reading of the *Huainanzi* sovereignty-contest framing.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2-3 on the *Huainanzi* sovereignty-contest framing of the Gonggong-Zhuanxu combat; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the alternative-sovereign critique; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section VI on Gonggong's water-god-not-chaos status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3, Gonggong as rival sovereign
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, alternative-sovereign critique

Coyolxauhqui and the Centzon Huitznahua at Coatepec are similarly not "chaos-figures" but kin-rivals with status: Coyolxauhqui is Huitzilopochtli's elder sister and the Centzon Huitznahua are his four hundred stellar brothers, and the combat is a kinship-and-political contest within the divine family rather than a cosmogonic agent's confrontation with primordial disorder; Carrasco 1999 reads the combat in this kinship-and-political register throughout.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, ch. on Coatepec on Coyolxauhqui as elder sister and the Centzon Huitznahua as four hundred stellar brothers, and on the kinship-and-political-contest framing; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the alternative-sovereign critique; Anderson-Dibble *Florentine Codex*, Book 3 on the kinship structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=ch. on Coatepec, kinship-and-political contest
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, alternative-sovereign critique

The Ballentine critique therefore lands a substantive point on the "chaos" half of the term "Chaoskampf": none of the four §I adversaries is best read as primordial-undifferentiated-disorder, and reading them that way risks importing a Greek-philosophical *chaos* (or a more recent disorder-vs-order metaphysical scheme) into texts whose own conceptual structure organises the adversary as a rival-sovereign-with-status rather than as the absence-of-order.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the methodological problem of importing post-Hesiodic chaos-as-disorder into ancient Near Eastern combat-myth texts whose own conceptual structure does not deploy such a category; per glossary.yaml entry on "the adversary (category term)" with "chaos-figure" listed as banned for begging the question.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, chaos-as-disorder import problem

The book accordingly bans "chaos-figure" and "chaos-monster" from un-quoted body-prose use (per glossary.yaml's "the adversary (category term)" entry, never_use list), and adopts "the adversary" as the default neutral category-term for the figure the champion confronts; the present section names "chaos-figure" only in scare-quotes in the context of registering the Ballentine critique, where the typology's own terminology is the topic of discussion.footnote:[Per glossary.yaml entry on "the adversary (category term)" on the banning of "chaos-figure" and "chaos-monster" and the adoption of "the adversary" as default; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the terminological critique that motivates the ban.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, terminological critique

The Ballentine critique does not, however, undermine the cosmogonic claim about the four §I cases: that the present features of the cosmos are explicitly aetiologised to the combat in each tradition's own primary sources is independent of whether the adversary is best characterised as "chaos" or as "rival sovereign" — Tiamat-as-alternative-sovereign is still the figure from whose body Marduk constructs the cosmos; Asag-as-rival-king is still the figure whose defeat reorders the Tigris and the lapidary economy; Gonggong-as-rival-water-god is still the figure whose head-blow tilts the cosmos; Coyolxauhqui-as-elder-sister-and-rival-claimant is still the figure whose dismemberment establishes the diurnal solar order.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section VI on the cosmogony-survives-the-critique disciplinary stance; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the alternative-sovereign reading as compatible with the cosmogonic outcome; Lambert 2013, ETCSL 1.6.2, Lewis 2006, and Matos Moctezuma 1988 on the four cosmogonic outcomes as independently attested.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, cosmogony-survives-the-critique

The chapter's settled position is therefore that the cosmogonic-combat typology survives the Ballentine critique — the §I four-case convergence on combat-as-origin-of-present-cosmos remains the chapter's central evidential datum — but that the Chaoskampf label may not survive in its strong form: "combat-cosmogony" is the more precise term for what the chapter is comparing, and "Chaoskampf" is retained only as a historical anchor (per scope.md §"Terminology default") and not as a substantive analytical category that would commit the book to reading the adversaries as inchoate disorder.footnote:[Per scope.md §"Terminology default (locked)" on the Chaoskampf-as-historical-anchor framing; per glossary.yaml entry on "cosmogonic combat" as the preferred subtype-term; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the case for terminological restraint.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, case for terminological restraint

The chapter does not assert resolution of the Gunkel-Ballentine debate: Gunkel 1895's identification of Marduk-Tiamat as the type-specimen of a cosmogonic-combat pattern remains the historical anchor of the comparative literature, and Ballentine 2015's alternative-sovereign reading is the principal contemporary critical position; the present chapter retains the typological-cosmogonic claim while acknowledging that the conceptual-philosophical question about "chaos" remains contested within current scholarship.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml special_instructions on the terminological-humility stance; Gunkel 1895, *Schöpfung und Chaos*, Teil I, on the historical anchor; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the contemporary critical position; per scope.md §"Terminological position" on the retain-with-acknowledgment policy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I, historical anchor
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, contemporary critical position

The Ballentine critique is engaged in further detail in the C7 inversion-problem chapter (Ch40) and in the C8 refusal-and-appropriation chapter (Ch41), where the area-specialist objections to the chaoskampf framing are developed at length on a tradition-by-tradition basis; the present chapter's role for the critique is the cosmogonic-typology context, with the wider methodological-and-terminological discussion deferred forward.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml structure section VI on the deferred-forward role of the wider Ballentine engagement; per scope.md §"Book structure" Part II on the C7 and C8 chapters as the venues for the area-specialist objections; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 as the present chapter's terminological caveat.]

The Scurlock & Beal 2013 reassessment occupies a position between Gunkel and Ballentine that the present chapter's settled position substantially follows: the volume's contributors retain the Chaoskampf category as a useful comparative tool while qualifying its application case-by-case, and the volume's individual chapters on the Mesopotamian and Levantine cosmogonic-combat material run the same case-by-case-qualified-but-typology-retaining argument that the present chapter applies across the wider four-case set.footnote:[Scurlock, J. and Beal, R. // EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim, case-by-case-qualified-typology-retaining position

The Lincoln 2012 political-ideological reading supplies a third-position alternative that the chapter also endorses: rather than asking whether the adversaries are "chaos" or "rival sovereigns" — a question framed at the level of conceptual-ontological category — Lincoln asks what political-ideological work the cosmogonic-combat narrative performs, and finds that all four §I cases perform charter-work for state-level political authority through the construction of a divine-combat origin for the present cosmographic-and-political order; the political-ideological reading is compatible with both Gunkel's typological reading and Ballentine's terminological critique, and is the chapter's principal methodological warrant for treating the four cases as a single comparative set.footnote:[Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the political-ideological reading of cosmogonic-combat as state-charter work and on the compatibility of that reading with both Gunkel and Ballentine; per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the political-ideological framing as methodological warrant.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=on combat-myth as political-ideological state-charter

The chapter closes with the position that combat-cosmogony is a recurring typological pattern across at least three independent traditions (the Mesopotamian-internal pair counted as one, plus Gonggong, plus Huitzilopochtli), that the pattern is sharpened by the Cipactli dismemberment-without-combat boundary case and the Indra-Vṛtra cosmogonic-work-without-construction near-case, and that the typological claim is robust enough to survive the Ballentine terminological critique even where the "chaos" half of the inherited Chaoskampf label may not be — a settled position that acknowledges the typological-recurrence as the chapter's central datum without overclaiming on monomyth, single-origin, or universalist grounds.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the settled-position summary; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, chs. on cosmogonic combat on the typological-recurrence-as-datum reading; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 1 on the terminological-but-not-typological critique; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the without-monomyth disciplinary stance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=chs. on cosmogonic combat, typological-recurrence-as-datum
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1, terminological-but-not-typological critique

The chapter's final caveat is on what the typological-recurrence claim does not prove: it does not prove that the four §I cases share a common origin, that the cosmogonic-combat pattern is a feature of all human cosmogonic narrative, that the pattern reflects a deep cognitive universal of human myth-making, or that the present-day prominence of the chaoskampf category in comparative-mythology scholarship is independent of the Eurocentric and biblical-studies-centred origins of the category in nineteenth-century Old-Testament scholarship; the chapter's claim is more modest — that the four cases share a structurally distinctive feature (combat-as-origin-of-present-cosmos) that warrants comparative treatment within the chaoskampf typology — and the modesty is methodologically load-bearing.footnote:[Per brief 34-C1-combat-as-cosmogony.yaml comparative_argument on the modesty of the typological-recurrence claim; per scope.md §"Terminological position" on the Eurocentric-and-biblical-studies-centred origins of the chaoskampf category; Lincoln 2012, *Gods and Demons, Priests and Scholars*, on the methodological priority of modest typological-recurrence claims over universal cognitive claims; Witzel 2012, *The Origins of the World's Mythologies*, as a reference example of the more ambitious cognitive-universal framing that the present chapter explicitly does not adopt.]
