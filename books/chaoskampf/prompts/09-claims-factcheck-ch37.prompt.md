# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).

5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).

8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).

- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):

- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").

# claims-factcheck

## For the human

This is the most important quality check for story chapters. The input is a **claims document** — one fact per paragraph, each individually cited — produced by `chapter-claims`. Each paragraph is one claim, one source reference, one thing to verify. No prose to parse, no bundled assertions to disentangle.

This step enforces four disciplines:

1. **Show your evidence.** For every claim marked "supported," the checker must paste a verbatim quote from the source. Not a paraphrase — the actual text. No quote, no "supported" verdict.

2. **Verify the references.** Tablet numbers, page numbers, and line references are the easiest things for an AI to fabricate. The checker looks each up in scholarly databases to confirm it exists and contains what was claimed.

3. **Distinguish contradictions from errors.** Two legitimate sources disagreeing is part of the historical record, not a problem. Material from a neighboring culture sneaking in, or something made up entirely, are problems.

4. **Read it backwards too.** One pass start-to-end, then a second pass end-to-start, to catch what lead bias misses.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the claims. If you have any memory of producing the input, refuse. Using a different AI model is preferred — two models make independent mistakes — but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different model is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `toc.yaml` — **authoritative chapter list**, including front matter, story chapters, the methods sidebar, the Part II comparative chapters, and any reception/appendix material. When a claim or COMPARATIVE-HOOK references a forward-link target by chapter number (e.g. "Ch. 33", "see Chapter 4"), resolve it against `toc.yaml` before flagging anything as missing. The story chapters are not the whole TOC — sidebars and Part II comparative chapters typically live at higher numbers and are easy to miss when scanning only `briefs/NN-*` filenames or the chapter-claims input directory.
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.adoc` (claims document)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml`
- Primary source translations referenced in the brief (pasted or accessible via fetch)
- Web search tool (required)

## The five passes

### Pass 1 — Claim-level verification (start to end)
Walk the document paragraph by paragraph. Each paragraph is one claim. Assign a verdict:

- **✓ SUPPORTED** — Must include a verbatim quote from a cited in-scope source.
- **△ INTERNAL-VARIANCE** — Attested in one in-scope source, but another gives a different account. Not a finding. Flag only if variant handling mismatches the brief's classification.
- **? UNVERIFIED** — No supporting quote found. Default when uncertain.
- **! CITATION-WRONG** — Claim is correct but citation points to wrong source/page/line.
- **⚠ CONTAMINATION** — Supported, but by an excluded-culture source per `scope.md`.
- **✗ FABRICATION** — Not supported by any source you can locate.

### Pass 2 — Triangulation of references
Verify each citation's tablet number, line range, or page reference resolves in a `triangulation_databases` entry. Non-resolving: CITATION-WRONG, HIGH severity, with verification URL.

For cross-chapter references inside the book (claims that say "see Ch. N", COMPARATIVE-HOOK targets like "Part II C5", forward-links to the methods sidebar): resolve against `toc.yaml`, not against the directory of `briefs/NN-*` files. The TOC is authoritative; the briefs directory is not exhaustive (Part II comparative chapters and the methods sidebar live at numbers above the last story chapter). Only flag a forward-link as missing when `toc.yaml` does not list a chapter at that number or with that anchor.

**Identifier verification on recently-pinned sources.** When `sources.yaml` shows a source added or last edited recently (typically a chapter-briefs source-pinning round), web-verify the published identifier against AbeBooks / WorldCat / publisher catalogue / archive.org. Common failure modes:
- ISBN digit-transposition (e.g. `9789561109315` for what should be `9789561109124`).
- ISBN belongs to a different edition of the same work (hardcover vs. paperback; reprint vs. first printing).
- ISBN/publisher mismatch — ISBN comes from publisher A but the entry attributes the work to publisher B.
- OCLC number guessed rather than verified; correct value is a different WorldCat record.
- archive.org URL slug malformed; correct slug is different.

The bound-or-encircled-rather-than-killed feature has often been read in the comparative literature as the eschatological subtype's most distinctive structural marker: the adversary's continued existence in the present age, in a constrained but not destroyed form, is what sets the eschatological cosmology apart from the cosmogonic cosmology in which the adversary is converted into the cosmos's substance and from the cyclical-maintenance cosmology in which the adversary is rebuffed but regenerates.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the bound-but-living-adversary structure as the eschatological subtype's load-bearing feature; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the Endzeit-deferred-killing as the structural mark of the eschatological projection.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II (Endzeit-deferred-killing)

==== Case 1 — Thor and Jörmungandr at Ragnarök

The Norse case opens with Jörmungandr's pre-eschatological encircling. *Gylfaginning* ch. 34 records that Óðinn, learning of the threat that Loki's three monstrous offspring (Hel, Fenrir, Jörmungandr) posed to the gods, dispatched Jörmungandr into the ocean encircling Miðgarðr, where the serpent grew so large that it could encircle the world and bite its own tail.footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* ch. 34, on Óðinn's dispatch of Jörmungandr into the ocean and on the serpent's growth to encircle the world and bite its own tail.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning ch. 34

The encircling is the Norse version of the binding-or-reservation feature: Jörmungandr is not killed at the gods' establishment of Miðgarðr but is positioned around the world's edge in a configuration that is at once a cosmographic boundary and a constraint, and the serpent's continued existence in the ocean is the narrative precondition for the eschatological combat that *Gylfaginning* ch. 51 narrates.footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* ch. 34, on the cosmographic-and-constraining function of Jörmungandr's ocean-encircling; West 2007, ch. 6, on the encircling as the Norse articulation of the bound-but-living feature shared with the Iranian Aži Dahāka and the Christian-apocalyptic dragon-Satan.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning ch. 34 (encircling-as-constraint)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6 (encircling as bound-but-living)

The two pre-eschatological encounters between Thor and Jörmungandr (the Útgarðaloki cat-lift at *Gylfaginning* chs. 46–48 and the fishing-encounter at *Hymiskviða* sts. 17–24 / *Gylfaginning* ch. 48) are read by the Norse tradition itself as deferred-and-incomplete combat: in the cat-lift the serpent is lifted but not killed, in the fishing-encounter the serpent is hooked but released when Hymir cuts the line — neither encounter resolves the combat, and the resolution is reserved for Ragnarök.footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* chs. 46–48, on the Útgarðaloki cat-lift and on the fishing-encounter without resolution; Larrington 2014, *Hymiskviða* sts. 17–24, on the fishing-encounter and the cut-line denouement; per the three-encounter completion structure of brief 18-thor-jormungandr.yaml.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning chs. 46-48
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Hymiskviða sts. 17-24

The release of Jörmungandr is presented in *Gylfaginning* ch. 51 as a feature of Ragnarök's opening sequence: at the unmaking of the cosmographic boundary by which the serpent had encircled the world, Jörmungandr emerges from the ocean onto the land, advances toward the field of Vígríðr (the appointed Ragnarök battle-plain) spewing venom across sky and water as it advances, and meets Thor in the principal storm-god-versus-serpent combat of the Æsir's final array.footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* ch. 51, on Jörmungandr's emergence from the ocean, on the venom-spewing approach to Vígríðr, and on the meeting of Thor and Jörmungandr in the final paired single-combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning ch. 51

The final combat at *Völuspá* st. 56 and *Gylfaginning* ch. 51 ends with mutual killing: Thor strikes the killing-blow with Mjöllnir against Jörmungandr, slays the world-encircling serpent in a single stroke that is at once the unmaking of the cosmic boundary and the close of the Æsir's combat-history with their long-deferred adversary, and then staggers nine paces from the kill-site before falling dead from the serpent's venom — the storm-god kills the serpent and dies of the killing.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Völuspá* st. 56, on Thor's victorious strike against the serpent and the staggering nine paces and Thor's death from the serpent's venom; Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* ch. 51, on the same mutual-killing outcome and the venom-death.]

The post-combat condition is the Norse renewal-narrative: *Völuspá* sts. 59–66 record that a new earth rises green from the sea, a small surviving group of gods (Móði and Magni inheriting Mjöllnir, Víðar and Váli, the returning Baldr) re-establishes the cosmic order, and the human pair Líf and Lífþrasir survives to repopulate the new earth — a renovation rather than restoration, with Thor's role inherited by his sons rather than continued by Thor himself.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Völuspá* sts. 59–66, on the post-Ragnarök renewal-narrative with the new earth, the surviving gods, the inheritance of Mjöllnir, and the human pair Líf and Lífþrasir; Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* chs. 52–53, on the same renewal-narrative.]

==== Case 2 — Michael and the red dragon (Revelation 12 with Revelation 20)

The Christian-apocalyptic case is structurally bipartite, with the dragon's expulsion at Revelation 12 and the dragon's binding-and-final-loosing-and-final-defeat at Revelation 20 jointly constituting the eschatological-displacement structure: Yarbro Collins 1976 and Aune 1997–98 read the two chapters together as the Apocalypse of John's articulation of the bound-but-living adversary whose final destruction is deferred to the close of the millennium.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on Rev 12 and Rev 20 as the bipartite articulation of the eschatological combat; Aune 1997–98, commentary on Rev 12:7–9 and on Rev 20:1–10, on the binding-and-final-loosing structure that links the two chapters.]

=== III. The common structural element — the binding

The signature of the eschatological-displacement subtype, on the chapter's reading, is the binding — the configuration in which the adversary is constrained, encircled, or held in reserve in the present cosmic age rather than killed outright at a foundational combat. The three principal cases articulate the binding in three different cosmographic and narrative registers, but in each the binding is the structural feature that makes the eschatological combat both possible and necessary.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §III on the binding as the common structural element; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 6, on the bound-but-living adversary as the eschatological subtype's load-bearing feature; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 6 (bound-but-living adversary)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II (binding as Endzeit precondition)

In the Norse case the binding is cosmographic-encircling: Jörmungandr lies in the ocean encircling Miðgarðr, large enough to bite his own tail, and the encircling is at once a constraint (the serpent is positioned at the world's edge rather than freely at large within it) and a cosmographic feature (the serpent's body is the world's outer boundary, with the inhabited world Miðgarðr defined by the encircling).footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* ch. 34, on Jörmungandr's encircling-and-tail-biting structure; West 2007, ch. 6, on the encircling as cosmographic-and-constraining configuration.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning ch. 34 (encircling)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6 (cosmographic encircling)

The Norse encircling is not described in the surviving sources as a chained or shackled binding — there is no narrative of fetters being applied to Jörmungandr — but the structural function (continued existence in a constrained position from which the serpent can be released for the eschatological combat) is identical with the Iranian Damāvand-binding and the Christian-apocalyptic Rev 20 thousand-year imprisonment, and the chapter reads the three articulations as variant inflections of the same structural feature rather than as distinct configurations.footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* ch. 34, on the encircling as constraint without fetters; per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §III on the structural-functional equivalence among the three articulations; West 2007, ch. 6, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning ch. 34 (constraint without fetters)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6 (structural-functional equivalence)

In the Iranian case the binding is explicitly chained-and-mountain-sealed: the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* presents Thraētaona as the one who chains Aži Dahāka under Mount Damāvand and seals him beneath the mountain for the duration of the present cosmic age, with the cosmographic feature of the Iranian landscape (a real Alborz peak, c. 5,610 m, in northern Iran) anchoring the binding to a specific geographical locus.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* narrative of Thraētaona's chaining of Aži Dahāka under Damāvand and on the mountain's cosmographic role; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same Pahlavi-localisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48 (Damāvand chaining)

The Iranian binding makes Aži Dahāka a permanent under-mountain captive whose continuing presence in the sacred landscape structures the cosmography of the present age — the dragon is not merely overthrown but is a continuing cosmogeographic feature whose under-mountain location is part of the geography of the present age and whose eventual escape is therefore an upheaval at the level of the inhabited landscape rather than merely at the level of the heavens.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the cosmogeographic role of Aži Dahāka under Damāvand in the Pahlavi tradition; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46 (cosmogeographic role)

In the Christian-apocalyptic case the binding is bipartite: the dragon is first cast down from heaven at Rev 12:9 (a heavenly-spatial constraint that removes the adversary from his original locus without imprisoning him) and then bound in the bottomless pit at Rev 20:1–3 (a temporal-and-spatial constraint that seals the adversary for a thousand years before the final loosing) — the two-stage constraint structure articulates the binding-feature across the bipartite Rev 12 / Rev 20 framing.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the Rev 12 expulsion as first-stage constraint; Aune 1997–98, commentary on Rev 20:1–3, on the bottomless-pit binding as second-stage constraint and on the bipartite structural articulation.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=aune-revelation ; loc=WBC 52C on Rev 20:1-3 (bottomless-pit binding)

The thousand-year duration of the Rev 20:1–3 binding is the most temporally explicit of the three cases — neither the Norse encircling nor the Iranian Damāvand-chaining is given an explicit duration in the surviving sources — and the Rev 20 millennium is the principal piece of evidence for reading the eschatological combat as one whose temporal placement (after a long but finite intervening period) is part of the subtype's articulation rather than a vague projection.footnote:[Aune 1997–98, commentary on Rev 20:2 and 20:7, on the thousand-year binding as the duration-explicit case among the three principal eschatological-combat traditions; Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the apocalyptic-calendar embedding of the millennium.]

The contrast with the cosmogonic subtype's adversary-disposal is structural rather than gradual: in *Enūma Eliš* IV, Marduk kills Tiamat outright and constructs the cosmos from her body — the adversary is converted into the substance of the present cosmos, and there is no remaining adversary for an eschatological combat to be fought against; in the eschatological-displacement subtype the adversary survives the founding event in a constrained but living form, and the present cosmos is built around or alongside the bound adversary rather than from the adversary's remains.footnote:[Per Ch34 (C1) on the Marduk-Tiamat construction-from-substance structure as the cosmogonic-subtype type-specimen; Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, on the cosmogonic disposal of Tiamat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the bound-and-surviving structure as the eschatological-subtype contrast.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 6 (bound-and-surviving contrast)

The contrast with the sovereignty subtype's adversary-disposal is similarly structural: in the Baal Cycle, Baal kills Yamm in the *qrb ym* combat and the sovereignty over the cosmos is settled — the adversary is dispatched, the kingship established, and there is no further combat with Yamm in the surviving Ugaritic corpus; in the eschatological-displacement subtype the analogous foundational combat (Thraētaona's overthrow of Aži Dahāka, Rev 12's expulsion of the dragon, Óðinn's casting of Jörmungandr into the ocean) does not settle but defers — the adversary survives, the kingship is provisionally established, and the final settlement awaits the eschatological combat.footnote:[Per Ch35 (C2) on the Baal-Yamm sovereignty-settlement structure as the sovereignty-subtype type-specimen; Smith 1994 / Smith and Pitard 2009, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle*, on the once-and-settled outcome of the *qrb ym* combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the deferred-settlement structure as the eschatological-subtype contrast.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 6 (deferred-settlement contrast)

The contrast with the cyclical-maintenance subtype's adversary-disposal is the third structural axis: in the Egyptian Ra-Apep combat, Apep is rebuffed nightly and regenerates by the next night — the adversary is neither killed nor bound but recurrently engaged on a daily cycle, with the cosmos's continuance contingent on the recurrence; in the eschatological-displacement subtype the adversary is bound or encircled in a non-recurring configuration, and the cosmos's continuance is contingent on the binding's persistence rather than on a recurring liturgical engagement.footnote:[Per Ch36 (C3) §II on the Ra-Apep recurrent-rebuff structure as the cyclical-maintenance type-specimen; Assmann 2001, on the recurrence-as-constitutive structure of Egyptian solar theology; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the binding-persistence structure as the eschatological-subtype contrast.]

The binding is, on the chapter's reading, the structural fulcrum on which the eschatological-displacement subtype turns: the bound adversary is the precondition for the eschatological combat, the eschatological combat is the resolution of the binding, and the post-combat renovation is the configuration that the binding's eventual undoing makes possible. === IV. Historical influence or typological convergence?

The three principal cases are independent in their textual-traditional descent (Norse Eddic-and-Snorra-Edda; Christian-apocalyptic; Avestan-and-Pahlavi), and the question whether their structural convergence reflects historical influence — Zoroastrian-to-Jewish-and-Christian apocalyptic; Jewish-Christian-to-Norse via the Christianisation contact horizon — or independent typological recurrence under shared structural pressure (linear-time cosmology, the political-theological need for an end-of-time resolution to a present-age combat-thread) is the principal historical-comparative question of the present chapter. The brief's special_instructions ask the chapter to present the evidence charitably without forcing a verdict.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §IV on the three possibilities (Zoroastrian → Jewish-Christian; Jewish-Christian → Norse; independent convergence) and on the chapter's discipline of presenting evidence without forcing a verdict; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the long-trajectory transmission-and-convergence question.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=passim (transmission-and-convergence question)

The Zoroastrian-influence-on-Jewish-and-Christian-apocalyptic argument is the oldest and best-developed of the three influence-hypotheses: the argument was articulated in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries within the *religionsgeschichtliche Schule* (whose 1895 representative is Gunkel's *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* itself, with later developments by Bousset, Reitzenstein, and others), and proposes that the dualistic eschatology of Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic and its Christian continuations was substantively shaped by Iranian dualistic-eschatological thinking during the Achaemenid period (sixth to fourth centuries BCE) and the subsequent Hellenistic horizon.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, Teil II, on the original *Endzeit*-comparative argument; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 5, on the *religionsgeschichtliche Schule* development of the Iranian-influence thesis and on its long subsequent reception.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II (Endzeit-comparative argument)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 5 (religionsgeschichtliche development)

The historical conditions for Iranian influence on Second Temple Jewish thinking are textually well-attested: the Persian-period administration of Yehud / Judaea (538–332 BCE) under the Achaemenid empire placed the Judahite community under direct Iranian political-and-administrative authority for two centuries, with the post-exilic Hebrew Bible itself preserving evidence of contact (the Cyrus decree at Ezra 1, the Persian-loanword vocabulary of late Biblical Hebrew, the Aramaic of Daniel and Ezra) and with the Hellenistic-period continuation of contact via the Seleucid administration of the same region.footnote:[Per Day 1985, ch. 3, on the post-exilic Persian-period setting of Isaiah 27 and on the historical-contact context; Forsyth 1987, ch. 5, on the Persian-period and Hellenistic-period contact horizon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 3 (Persian-period contact)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 5 (Persian-Hellenistic contact)

The structural features of Second Temple Jewish apocalyptic that the Iranian-influence argument identifies as candidates for Zoroastrian-derivation include: a sharply dualistic moral cosmology (good vs. evil as cosmic principles in tension); an articulated eschatology with a definite end-of-cosmic-time pivot at which evil is finally extinguished; a doctrine of resurrection of the dead at or near the end-time; and an explicit periodisation of cosmic time (Daniel 7's four-empire schema, the Daniel 9 and 12 numerical eschatological calculations) that maps onto the Zoroastrian millennial-period schema known from later Pahlavi sources but plausibly attested in earlier Iranian tradition.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 5, on the four candidate features for Zoroastrian-derivation; Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 3, on the Daniel-7-and-12 eschatological-calculation material as articulated in Rev 12:6 (1,260 days) and Rev 12:14 (three and a half times).]

The Aži Dahāka-binding-and-Frashokereti-killing structural feature — the chapter's principal Iranian datum for the eschatological-displacement subtype — has been read by Forsyth 1987 ch. 5 as the structural pivot on which any Iranian-to-Jewish-and-Christian influence on the Rev 20 binding-and-final-loosing material would most plausibly turn: both narratives present a bound-but-living adversary, a long intervening period during which the binding holds, an eschatological release-and-final-combat, and a post-combat renovation of the cosmos.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 5, on the structural pivot of the Aži Dahāka / Rev 20 parallel; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the doubled-combat structural alignment of the Iranian and Christian-apocalyptic cases.]

The principal counterevidence-or-counterargument-set that has been raised against the Iranian-influence thesis in the twentieth-and-twenty-first century scholarship includes: the late textual fixation of the most striking Iranian features (the Pahlavi attestation of Damāvand-binding etc.), which pushes the influence-direction question toward a Jewish-Christian-to-Iranian rather than an Iranian-to-Jewish-Christian reading on certain reconstructions; the partial availability of the relevant features within the inherited Israelite-Hebrew Bible material itself (Isaiah 27:1's eschatological projection, treated at §V below, is the principal Hebrew Bible text on which the alternative inheritance-rather-than-influence reading rests); and the methodological observation that structural-functional convergence does not by itself entail historical influence, with the Norse case (where direct Iranian influence is geographically and historically implausible) supplying the parallel that makes structural convergence under shared cosmological pressure a serious alternative hypothesis.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on the inherited Israelite-Hebrew Bible material as alternative to Iranian-influence reading; Forsyth 1987, ch. 5, on the late-textual-fixation difficulty and on the methodological-observation about structural-functional convergence.]

The Jewish-Christian-influence-on-Norse hypothesis is the second of the three influence-possibilities the brief identifies. === V. Isaiah 27:1 as transition

The Hebrew Bible's combat imagery is, with one principal exception, retrospective rather than prospective: the seven core passages that Day 1985 identifies as the Hebrew Bible combat corpus (Pss 74:12–17, 89:9–13, 104:6–9 and 26; Job 26:12–13; Job 41; Habakkuk 3; Isa 51:9–10) refer to Yahweh's combat against Leviathan, Rahab, the Tannīn, and the sea as a past accomplishment, with verbs in past or perfective aspect, recalled as a precedent for present action rather than projected forward as a future event.footnote:[Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, chs. 3–4, on the seven-passage corpus and on the dominantly-retrospective character of the combat imagery; per Ch8 (Yahweh-Leviathan-Rahab) on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (retrospective combat imagery)

Psalm 74:12–17 invokes Yahweh's past combat as warrant for present petition: in the RSV rendering, "You divided the sea by your might; you broke the heads of the dragons in the waters. You crushed the heads of Leviathan; you gave him as food for the creatures of the wilderness," with the verbs explicitly past-tense — Yahweh has already broken the heads of Leviathan, and the psalmist recalls this past combat to ground a request for present intervention against the temple's destroyers.footnote:[RSV translation of Ps 74:13–14, as quoted in Day 1985 ch. 1; Day 1985, ch. 1, on the past-tense framing of the Ps 74 combat imagery.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (Ps 74 past-tense)

Job 26:12–13 deploys the same retrospective framing in wisdom-poetry register: "By his power he stilled the Sea; by his understanding he struck down Rahab. By his wind the heavens were made fair; his hand pierced the fleeing serpent," with the past-tense verbs and the cosmogonic register placing the combat at the foundational rather than the eschatological moment of cosmic time.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 4, on Job 26:12–13 as retrospective wisdom-poetry framing of the combat; per Ch8 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 4 (Job 26 retrospective)

Isaiah 27:1 is the principal Hebrew Bible exception to the retrospective framing — the only canonical passage in the Hebrew Bible combat corpus in which the combat imagery is projected forward to an eschatological future rather than recalled as a past accomplishment — and the present chapter's reading of Isa 27:1 follows Day 1985 ch. 3 in identifying the verse as the textual moment at which the inherited Canaanite-into-Hebrew combat imagery is given its first explicitly eschatological deployment within the Hebrew Bible.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on Isa 27:1 as the Hebrew Bible's principal eschatological-projection of the combat imagery; per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §V on Isa 27:1's transitional position.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 3 (Isa 27:1 eschatological projection)

The text of Isaiah 27:1 in the RSV rendering: "On that day the Lord with his cruel and great and strong sword will punish Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent, and he will kill the dragon that is in the sea." The opening phrase "on that day" (Hebrew *bayyôm hahû'*) is the prophetic-eschatological idiom that locates the action in a future eschatological moment, and the future-tense verbs ("will punish," "will kill") confirm the prospective grammatical aspect.footnote:[RSV translation of Isa 27:1, as quoted in Day 1985 ch. 3; Day 1985, ch. 3, on the "on that day" idiom and on the future-tense verbs as the textual marks of the eschatological projection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 3 (Isa 27:1 grammatical aspect)

The dual-epithet naming of Leviathan in Isa 27:1 — "Leviathan the fleeing serpent, Leviathan the twisting serpent" — is the verbatim Hebrew correspondent of the Ugaritic Baal Cycle's epithet-pair for Lôtan (KTU 1.5 i 1–3: *bṯn brḥ* "fleeing serpent" and *bṯn ʿqltn* "twisting serpent"), and is one of the most philologically precise pieces of evidence anywhere for the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew continuity of combat-myth imagery — the inherited Canaanite material is taken over by the Hebrew prophet without alteration of the epithet-pair and is then redeployed in the new eschatological-projection register.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on the verbatim epithet-correspondence between Isa 27:1 and KTU 1.5 i 1–3 as the philologically-precise evidence for Ugaritic-into-Hebrew continuity; Smith and Pitard 2009, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume II*, on KTU 1.5 i 1–3 as the Ugaritic source-text.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 3 (verbatim epithet correspondence)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 (Ugaritic source)

The dating of Isaiah 27 to the post-exilic period — the so-called "Isaiah Apocalypse" of Isa 24–27 is, on the dominant scholarly reading, a post-exilic addition to the Isaianic corpus rather than a Proto-Isaian composition — places the eschatological projection in a context where Israelite poets had already been exposed to Persian-period eschatological thinking, raising the comparative question whether Isa 27:1 reflects awareness of the Zoroastrian Frashokereti pattern (the principal Iranian eschatological-combat material) or whether the eschatological projection is internal to the Israelite-prophetic tradition's redeployment of the inherited Canaanite material.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on the post-exilic dating of the "Isaiah Apocalypse" and on the Persian-period comparative context; Ballentine 2015, ch. 5, on the Persian-period Frashokereti-comparative question; Forsyth 1987, ch. 5, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 3 (post-exilic dating)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 5 (Frashokereti comparative)

Day 1985 ch. 3 reads the eschatological projection at Isa 27:1 as primarily an internal-to-Israelite-prophetic-tradition development: the inherited Canaanite Leviathan-Lôtan material was the available combat-myth resource for Israelite poets, and the post-exilic prophet's eschatological redeployment of the inherited material is best read as an inner-Israelite eschatologisation of imagery whose Canaanite substrate was already at hand, rather than as a Persian-influenced innovation.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on the inner-Israelite eschatologisation reading of Isa 27:1; per the discipline of citing Iranian-influence as a comparative possibility rather than a demonstrated dependence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 3 (inner-Israelite eschatologisation)

Ballentine 2015 ch. 5 reads Isa 27:1 in a different but compatible register: the future-tense verb projects Yahweh's victory over rival sovereignties forward, with the eschatological projection as a political-theological move rather than a chaos-ordering cosmogony deferred to the eschaton — Ballentine accepts the philological observation that Isa 27:1 reuses Canaanite Lôtan-imagery but reads the reuse through her wider rival-sovereignty thesis rather than through a chaos-cosmogonic framing.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, ch. 5, on the political-theological reading of Isa 27:1 within the rival-sovereignty thesis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 5 (political-theological reading)

The bridge-function of Isa 27:1 to the Christian-apocalyptic eschatological-combat material is structurally direct: Rev 12:9's identification of the dragon as "that ancient serpent" who is also "the great dragon" (the seven-headed sea-monster) glosses the Apocalypse of John's adversary in language that draws on both the Genesis 3 serpent and the Hebrew Bible Leviathan-corpus, and Isa 27:1's eschatological redeployment of the Leviathan epithets supplies the principal Hebrew Bible precedent for projecting the combat forward "on that day" — Rev 12 is therefore not the originating eschatologisation of the inherited Israelite combat material but the elaboration of an eschatologisation whose first-text within the Hebrew Bible is Isa 27:1.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on the trajectory from Isa 27 to Rev 12; Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 3, on Rev 12:9's drawing on the Hebrew Bible Leviathan-tradition; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on Rev 12 as elaboration of the Hebrew Bible eschatologisation.]

The single-text status of Isa 27:1 within the Hebrew Bible combat corpus — the principal eschatological-projection text against the dominantly-retrospective register of the rest of the corpus — is structurally important for the chapter's reading: Isa 27:1 is not a robust eschatological-displacement attestation in the way the Norse Thor-Jörmungandr, Christian-apocalyptic Rev 12 / Rev 20, and Zoroastrian Aži Dahāka / Frashokereti cases are, but is a transitional text that registers the shift from retrospective to projected combat within the Israelite prophetic tradition without itself developing the bound-and-released-and-killed structure that the three principal cases articulate.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 3, on the single-text status of Isa 27:1 within the Hebrew Bible combat corpus; per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §V on the transitional-text reading.]

The transitional-text reading of Isa 27:1 — as the moment within the Hebrew Bible at which the combat imagery is first explicitly projected forward, without being developed into a four-feature eschatological-displacement structure — is the chapter's principal reason for treating Isa 27:1 as a precondition for the Christian-apocalyptic articulation rather than as a fourth principal case alongside the Norse, Zoroastrian, and full Rev 12 / Rev 20 attestations: Isa 27:1 supplies the projection-grammar without the binding-and-deferred-combat structure, and the binding-and-deferred-combat structure is what the present chapter's typology requires.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §V on Isa 27:1 as precondition rather than principal case; Day 1985, ch. 3, on the same; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the structural requirements of the eschatological-displacement subtype.]

The structural difference between Isa 27:1 and the three principal cases is therefore that Isa 27:1 supplies the eschatological projection (verb tense, "on that day" idiom) without supplying the bound-and-eventually-released structure: Yahweh's "on that day" combat with Leviathan does not presuppose that Leviathan has been bound in the present age and is awaiting release, but presupposes only that the combat will occur at the eschatological future, with the sword and the killing as the action. === VI. What eschatology changes

The chapter's main analytical contribution — the contribution the brief identifies as the chapter's "theoretical contribution" — is the observation that eschatological displacement is not merely "combat at the end instead of the beginning," but a distinct configuration with distinct cognitive implications for believers in the traditions that articulate it. A terminal combat functions differently in a tradition than a perpetual or primordial one: it changes the believer's relationship to the monster, to history, and to the hero, and these three changes together constitute what the chapter argues is the eschatological-displacement subtype's distinctive functional signature.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §VI on the three-axis analytical contribution; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, ch. 6, on the cognitive-functional reading of the eschatological subtype; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 6 (three-axis cognitive reading)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II (cognitive-functional articulation)

The first axis on which eschatological displacement reorients the believer is the relationship to the monster. In the cosmogonic subtype the monster is past and dispatched (Tiamat is dead and the cosmos is built from her body); in the cyclical-maintenance subtype the monster is present and recurringly engaged on a fixed liturgical schedule (Apep is rebuffed nightly through the Bremner-Rhind effigy-destruction rite); in the eschatological-displacement subtype the monster is present and bound, neither dispatched nor recurringly engaged, and the believer's relationship to the monster is therefore one of patient awaiting rather than ritual rebuff or commemorative recall.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §VI on the patient-awaiting feature; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the same; per Ch36 (C3) on the contrasting recurrent-engagement structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 6 (patient awaiting)

The cognitive-and-affective implication of patient awaiting — patience rather than ritual, deferral rather than engagement — is articulated within each of the three traditions in different theological-political registers: the Apocalypse of John frames the dragon's redirected wrath as the framing-condition of the persecuted Christian community's lived experience, with the heavenly hymn at Rev 12:10–12 announcing that the dragon has come down "with great wrath, knowing that his time is short," and the chapter's didactic-encouragement function (Yarbro Collins 1976 ch. 4) operates precisely through the patient-awaiting framing.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on Rev 12's didactic-encouragement function as operating through the patient-awaiting framing; Aune 1997–98, commentary on Rev 12:12, on the "his time is short" formulation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=yarbro-collins-1976 ; loc=ch. 4 (patient-awaiting in didactic register)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=aune-revelation ; loc=WBC 52B on Rev 12:12 ("his time is short")

The Zoroastrian articulation of the patient-awaiting feature is differently framed: Aži Dahāka under Damāvand is a continuing cosmogeographic feature of the present age whose eventual escape is part of the tradition's eschatological-cosmological doctrine, and the believer's patient-awaiting takes the form of moral-and-ritual participation in the ongoing struggle against Angra Mainyu's *druj* ("falsehood," "deception") — the cosmic combat is fought in the present age at the level of moral-and-ritual practice, with the eschatological combat at Frashokereti as the deferred resolution of the present-age moral struggle.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Frashokereti eschatology and on the moral-and-ritual participation feature; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Iranian dualistic-eschatological framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48 (moral-ritual participation)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim (dualistic-eschatological framing)

The Norse articulation of the patient-awaiting feature is the most agnostic of the three: the Eddic and Snorra-Edda Ragnarök corpus does not develop an explicit doctrine of moral-or-ritual participation in the deferred combat — Thor is not theologised as awaiting the eschatological combat through the believer's intervening practice — and the Norse case's patient-awaiting is more cosmographic than ethical, with the world's continuance held in being by the encircling that Jörmungandr sustains until Ragnarök undoes it.footnote:[Faulkes 1987, *Gylfaginning* chs. 34, 51, on the cosmographic rather than ethical character of the Norse patient-awaiting; West 2007, ch. 6, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkes-snorri ; loc=Gylfaginning chs. 34, 51 (cosmographic awaiting)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6 (Norse cosmographic register)

The second axis on which eschatological displacement reorients the believer is the relationship to history. In the cosmogonic subtype history is the unfolding of a once-completed cosmos; in the cyclical-maintenance subtype history is the recurrence of a never-completed combat; in the eschatological-displacement subtype history is the meaningful-linear interval between the present age's foundational binding and its eventual eschatological resolution — history has a direction (toward the eschatological combat) and a meaningful structure (the working-out of the deferred combat-thread) that the cyclical and once-and-done subtypes do not articulate.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §VI on the meaningful-linear-history feature; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the same; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 6 (meaningful-linear history)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II (meaningful-linear history)

[SPECULATION: that the meaningful-linear-history feature of the eschatological-displacement subtype is a precondition for, rather than a consequence of, the subtype's structural articulation — that the development of linear-time cosmologies in the late Iron Age and early post-classical period (Zoroastrian dualistic eschatology with its fixed millennial periods, Jewish and Christian apocalyptic with its Daniel-derived calendar, Norse Ragnarök with its terminal-but-not-final structure) is what made the subtype available, with traditions whose cosmologies did not articulate linear time (Egyptian solar theology with its daily recurrence; Mesopotamian akītu with its annual cosmogonic recitation) consequently not articulating the subtype | basis: the temporal correlation between the appearance of linear-time cosmologies and the appearance of the eschatological-displacement subtype is striking — the three principal cases all post-date the development of the relevant linear-time cosmologies, and earlier traditions with non-linear cosmologies do not develop the subtype despite having the necessary combat-myth substrate; per Forsyth 1987 ch. 5 on the historical-cosmological correlation; per Gunkel 1895 Teil II on the *Urzeit*-und-*Endzeit* parallelism as a feature of traditions that articulate both ends of cosmic time | counterargument: that the linear-time-cosmology / eschatological-displacement-subtype correlation is itself a construct of the comparative-typology framework rather than a feature of the underlying traditions — the Norse case in particular has been read by some as articulating a partial-cycle rather than a strictly linear cosmology (the post-Ragnarök new earth complicates the linear-time reading), and the correlation may therefore be partially an artifact of the typology rather than a feature of the traditions; the chapter notes the worry without abandoning the linear-time-cosmology hypothesis.]

The meaningful-linear-history feature has direct political-theological implications: in the Christian-apocalyptic case the persecuted community's experience of Roman imperial authority is reframed as a finite intervening period of suffering whose end is guaranteed by the dragon's already-declared defeat (Yarbro Collins 1976 ch. 4); in the Zoroastrian case the moral-and-ritual struggle of the present age is reframed as participation in a cosmic combat whose resolution is guaranteed by the Frashokereti renovation; in the Norse case the heroic-aristocratic ethos of dying-honourably-in-combat is given cosmic-religious warrant by the projection of the same structure onto the gods themselves at Ragnarök.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the Christian-apocalyptic political-theological framing; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Zoroastrian moral-cosmic framing; West 2007, ch. 6, on the Norse heroic-aristocratic projection.]

The third axis on which eschatological displacement reorients the believer is the relationship to the hero. In the cosmogonic subtype the hero is the past-and-victorious god whose foundational combat is the source of the cosmos's structure; in the cyclical-maintenance subtype the hero is the recurringly-engaged god whose daily-or-annual victory is the cosmos's continuance; in the eschatological-displacement subtype the hero is, distinctively, promised rather than already-victorious — Kərəsāspa is held in stasis awaiting Frashokereti, the Apocalypse of John's Christ is already enthroned but the dragon is not yet finally destroyed, Thor's Ragnarök victory is foreseen by the *Völuspá* prophecy but is not yet accomplished — and the believer's relationship to the hero is therefore one of expectation rather than commemoration.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §VI on the promised-rather-than-already-victorious feature; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on Kərəsāspa's stasis-and-revival as the structural articulation of the promised-hero feature; Larrington 2014, *Völuspá* sts. 56 and 59–66, on the same in the Norse case; Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the same in the Christian-apocalyptic case.]

The Christian-apocalyptic articulation of the promised-hero feature is structurally distinctive in a way the other two cases do not parallel: the male child of Rev 12:5 is enthroned-prior-to-the-combat (the throne-ascent precedes the war in heaven), and the inverted royal-legitimation structure (Yarbro Collins 1976 ch. 4) means that Christ is presented as already-cosmically-victorious in his enthronement while the dragon's final destruction is still deferred — the combat enacts a kingship already secured rather than establishing one through the combat itself, with the believer's expectation directed to the deferred final act rather than to the establishment of the kingship.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the inverted royal-legitimation structure; Aune 1997–98, commentary on Rev 12:5–9, on the throne-ascent-precedes-combat structural ordering.]

The three-axis reorientation (monster, history, hero) collectively articulates the cognitive-functional signature of the eschatological-displacement subtype, and supplies the chapter's principal answer to the brief's analytical question of what eschatology changes: the subtype is not merely a different placement of the same combat-narrative on the cosmic-time axis, but a different configuration of the believer's relationship to the cosmos as a whole, with patient awaiting in place of ritual engagement, meaningful-linear history in place of cyclical or once-and-done time, and promised heroism in place of already-victorious or recurringly-victorious heroism.footnote:[Per brief 37-C4-eschatological-displacement.yaml structure §VI on the three-axis cognitive-functional articulation; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the same; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same.]

The cognitive-functional signature has consequences for the lived religious practice of the believer in each tradition: the Christian-apocalyptic believer of the Domitianic-era Asia Minor community is to read present persecution through the lens of the dragon's already-declared defeat and his coming final destruction; the Zoroastrian believer is to read present moral-and-ritual practice as participation in the cosmic combat whose Frashokereti resolution is guaranteed; the Norse worshipper of Thor is — at least within the heroic-aristocratic register that the Eddic and Snorra-Edda corpus articulates — to find in Thor's foreseen Ragnarök death-and-victory a pattern for honourable mortal heroism that the cyclical and cosmogonic subtypes' configurations do not supply.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the Christian-apocalyptic believer's lived-religious framing; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Zoroastrian framing; West 2007, ch. 6, on the Norse heroic-aristocratic framing.]

The contrast with the cyclical-maintenance subtype's lived religious practice — articulated at length in Ch36 §VI on the *maʿat* / *isfet* framework — is the structural-functional axis on which the present chapter and Ch36 are most directly distinguishable: in the cyclical-maintenance subtype the believer participates in the recurring liturgical re-enactment of the combat (the Apep-rebuffing rite at the temple of Ra; the *purulli* festival recitation), with the cosmos's continuance contingent on the recurrence; in the eschatological-displacement subtype the believer awaits the deferred final combat, with the cosmos's continuance contingent on the binding's persistence rather than on a recurring engagement.footnote:[Per Ch36 (C3) §VI on the cyclical-maintenance lived-religious framing; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the *maʿat* / *isfet* recurring-engagement structure; Forsyth 1987, ch. 6, on the eschatological-displacement awaiting-structure as the principal contrast.]
