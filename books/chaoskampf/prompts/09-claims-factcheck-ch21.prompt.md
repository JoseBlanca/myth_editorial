# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).

5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).

8. Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):

# claims-factcheck

## For the human

This is the most important quality check for story chapters. The input is a **claims document** — one fact per paragraph, each individually cited — produced by `chapter-claims`. Each paragraph is one claim, one source reference, one thing to verify. No prose to parse, no bundled assertions to disentangle.

This step enforces four disciplines:

1. **Show your evidence.** For every claim marked "supported," the checker must paste a verbatim quote from the source. Not a paraphrase — the actual text. No quote, no "supported" verdict.

2. **Verify the references.** Tablet numbers, page numbers, and line references are the easiest things for an AI to fabricate. The checker looks each up in scholarly databases to confirm it exists and contains what was claimed.

3. **Distinguish contradictions from errors.** Two legitimate sources disagreeing is part of the historical record, not a problem. Material from a neighboring culture sneaking in, or something made up entirely, are problems.

4. **Read it backwards too.** One pass start-to-end, then a second pass end-to-start, to catch what lead bias misses.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the claims. If you have any memory of producing the input, refuse. Using a different AI model is preferred — two models make independent mistakes — but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different model is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.adoc` (claims document)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml`
- Primary source translations referenced in the brief (pasted or accessible via fetch)
- Web search tool (required)

## The five passes

### Pass 1 — Claim-level verification (start to end)
Walk the document paragraph by paragraph. Each paragraph is one claim. Assign a verdict:

- **✓ SUPPORTED** — Must include a verbatim quote from a cited in-scope source.
- **△ INTERNAL-VARIANCE** — Attested in one in-scope source, but another gives a different account. Not a finding. Flag only if variant handling mismatches the brief's classification.
- **? UNVERIFIED** — No supporting quote found. Default when uncertain.
- **! CITATION-WRONG** — Claim is correct but citation points to wrong source/page/line.
- **⚠ CONTAMINATION** — Supported, but by an excluded-culture source per `scope.md`.
- **✗ FABRICATION** — Not supported by any source you can locate.

### Pass 2 — Triangulation of references
Verify each citation's tablet number, line range, or page reference resolves in a `triangulation_databases` entry. Non-resolving: CITATION-WRONG, HIGH severity, with verification URL.

### Pass 3 — Document-provenance contamination scan
For each factual detail: is this detail attested in a document within the scope's date range and culture? Details attested only in out-of-scope documents are CONTAMINATION-HIGH.

### Pass 4 — Marker integrity
- `[INFERENCE:]` — genuinely inferential? If a source directly attests it, flag MARKER-MISUSED.
- `[LACUNA:]` — source actually has the claimed gap?
- `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` — fill source is in-tradition per `scope.md`?
- `[VARIANT:]` — both quoted phrases present in cited sources?
- **Silent bridges** — uncited claims not under a marker. Flag SILENT-BRIDGE, HIGH.

### Pass 5 — Reverse-order second pass
Walk from last claim to first. Add any new findings.

**Glossary**: check consistency. **Style**: not checked — there is no prose yet.

## Verdict rules

Apply in order (first match wins):
- **MAJOR-REVISE**: ≥ 3 HIGH findings, or any contamination, or triangulation failures indicating fabricated references.
- **REVISE**: 1–2 HIGH, or > 3 MEDIUM, or any silent bridge.
- **PASS**: zero HIGH, zero silent bridges, ≤ 3 MEDIUM.

## Honesty self-check
Answer in `meta`:
1. Did I open every cited primary source, or skim? Name any I skimmed.
2. Did I triangulate every reference, or assume some? Name any assumed.
3. For every ✓, did I paste a real quote or pattern-match? Downgrade any pattern-matched.
4. Did I distinguish internal variance from contamination?
5. Am I the same model that produced the claims? If yes, this audit is invalid.

## Human review protocol
Review HIGH findings (~10 minutes), skim MEDIUM. Apply accepted fixes to produce `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc`. Run `post-human-normalize` before proceeding to `chapter-draft`.

The *Lienü zhuan* (列女傳, "Categorised Biographies of Women"), conventionally attributed to Liu Xiang (c. 79–8 BCE) and dated to the late Western Han, supplies a parallel Han-period account in which the Gonggong combat is followed by a flood that Yu the Great tames by opening channels — presenting the cosmogony as a sequential drama in which combat-ordering and hydraulic-ordering are linked stages of a single narrative rather than two separate stories.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Lienü zhuan* sequential-drama presentation of the Gonggong cosmogony followed by Yu's flood-taming; on Liu Xiang as conventional compiler and the late Western Han date; brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on the *Lienü zhuan* sequential reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The *Shanhaijing* (山海經, "Classic of Mountains and Seas") supplies a further Han-period (with Warring States stratum elements) witness in which Gonggong is named alongside other primordial figures and attached to multiple, often-incompatible mini-traditions; the *Shanhaijing*'s commentaries (notably the 3rd-century-CE Jin-dynasty commentary of Guo Pu) supply additional Gonggong references that name different opponents (Zhuanxu, Zhurong, and others) for the combat.footnote:[Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the *Shanhaijing* as a fragmentary witness to the Gonggong material and on the multiple-opponents pattern across the *Shanhaijing* and its commentaries; brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on the *Shanhaijing*-and-commentaries multiple-opponents pattern.]

=== Setting and combatants

The Gonggong of the *Huainanzi*-and-related Han-period sources is a primordial water-god, conventionally described in the surviving accounts and later commentaries as red-haired and serpent-bodied (or human-faced with serpent body), and is positioned within the early-imperial Chinese pantheon as one of the pre-human emperor-figures whose combats with one another are part of the succession of cosmogonic episodes that establishes the present cosmos.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Gonggong as a primordial water-god of the early-imperial Chinese pantheon, with red-haired and serpent-bodied descriptions in the surviving Han and post-Han sources; brief 21-gonggong.yaml entities_glossary entry on Gonggong as water-god, often red-haired and serpent-bodied; glossary 'Gonggong' first_mention_gloss on the same description.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

Gonggong's role as water-god supplies one of the load-bearing motifs of the cosmogonic-combat narrative: his combats are connected in the Han-period sources with floods, with the obstruction or release of waters, and with the channelling of rivers, and the post-combat hydrography of the cosmos (the eastward flow of the Chinese rivers to the sea) is one of the chief aetiological outcomes of the broken-pillar episode.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Gonggong's role as water-god in the cosmogonic combat and on the post-combat hydrography of the eastward-flowing rivers; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the same hydrographic-aetiology pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

Zhuanxu (顓頊), the sky-emperor who defeats Gonggong in the most widely attested *Huainanzi* version of the combat, is one of the so-called Five Emperors of the early-imperial Chinese tradition — a sequence of pre-human or semi-divine sovereign figures whose successive reigns supply the framework within which the cosmogonic episodes are organised — and his defeat of Gonggong is read in the Han sources as part of the establishment of his legitimate sovereignty over the cosmos.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Zhuanxu as the sky-emperor of the most widely attested *Huainanzi* version of the combat and on his place within the Five-Emperors sequence; brief 21-gonggong.yaml entities_glossary entry on Zhuanxu as "the sky-emperor who defeats Gonggong in the most widely attested version"; glossary 'Zhuanxu' first_mention_gloss on the same role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

Zhurong (祝融), a fire-god, appears in some *Shanhaijing* and commentary variants as the alternative combatant who defeats Gonggong, with the substitution of fire-god for sky-emperor reflecting the multiple-mini-traditions character of the Gonggong corpus rather than a single privileged opponent across the surviving sources.footnote:[Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on Zhurong as fire-god alternative combatant in *Shanhaijing* and commentary variants; brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on the *Shanhaijing*-and-commentaries multiple-opponents pattern; glossary 'Zhurong' first_mention_gloss on the fire-god / alternative-combatant role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=chapters on cosmogony

Nüwa (女媧), the serpent-tailed creator-goddess who repairs the broken sky after the combat, is one of the principal cosmogonic figures of the early-imperial Chinese pantheon and is associated more broadly with the creation of humans (in some Han sources, by moulding humans from yellow earth) and with the mending and ordering of the cosmos; her role in the Gonggong narrative is the post-combat repair-cosmogony rather than the combat itself.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Nüwa as serpent-tailed creator-goddess and post-combat repairer of the sky in the Gonggong cycle; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on Nüwa's wider creation-and-mending role across the Han sources; brief 21-gonggong.yaml entities_glossary entry on Nüwa as serpent-tailed creator-goddess; glossary 'Nüwa' first_mention_gloss on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

Mount Buzhou (不周山), the cosmic pillar broken by Gonggong's head-blow, has a name conventionally rendered "mountain-that-is-not-whole" or "incomplete mountain", and is positioned in the Han-period cosmography as one of the pillars supporting the sky in the northwest quadrant of the cosmos; the name itself is read by Lewis 2006 as etymologically connected to the post-combat tilted-cosmos outcome — the mountain is "not-whole" because it is broken by the combat.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Mount Buzhou's name "mountain-that-is-not-whole" and on its position as a sky-pillar in the northwest of the Han-period cosmography; brief 21-gonggong.yaml entities_glossary entry on Mount Buzhou; glossary 'Mount Buzhou' first_mention_gloss on "mountain-that-is-not-whole" rendering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The Han-period cosmography that the *Huainanzi* presupposes positions the cosmos as a roughly square earth covered by a roughly circular heaven, with sky-supporting pillars at the cardinal-and-intercardinal points; Mount Buzhou is one such pillar, and its breaking is therefore a structurally-cosmological event with consequences for the whole geometry of the cosmos rather than a merely local catastrophe.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the Han-period cosmography of square-earth-and-circular-heaven with sky-pillars and on Mount Buzhou's structurally-cosmological role; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the same cosmographic framework.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

=== The combat and the broken pillar (Huainanzi version)

In the *Huainanzi* 天文訓 version, the combat between Gonggong and Zhuanxu is precipitated by a contest for sovereignty: Gonggong, defeated in the contest, reacts with rage and turns his violence on the cosmic pillar itself rather than on his victorious opponent — a displacement of the combat from a duel between persons to an attack on the cosmographic structure that the Han text presents as the mechanism by which the cosmogonic outcome is produced.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Huainanzi* 天文訓 sovereignty-contest framing of the Gonggong-Zhuanxu combat and on the displacement of Gonggong's rage from his opponent to the cosmic pillar; brief 21-gonggong.yaml on the Huainanzi version as "Gonggong fights Zhuanxu and loses; in rage, he strikes Mount Buzhou".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

Gonggong, in his rage at the defeat, strikes Mount Buzhou with his head: the *Huainanzi* presents the head-blow as the specific physical mechanism by which the sky-pillar is broken, with Gonggong's serpent-bodied form supplying the impulse and the head supplying the destructive contact, and the resulting fracture of the pillar is the load-bearing event of the broken-pillar cosmogony.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Huainanzi*'s head-blow mechanism for the breaking of Mount Buzhou and on the load-bearing role of the head-blow in the broken-pillar cosmogony; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the same head-blow detail in the surviving Han sources.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The breaking of Mount Buzhou produces a structural tilt in the cosmos: the *Huainanzi* records that with the northwestern sky-pillar broken, the heavens tilt down toward the northwest and the earth correspondingly tilts down toward the southeast, and the tilt is the principal aetiological outcome of the combat — it is what the Han-period reader is meant to learn the broken-pillar story in order to understand.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Huainanzi*'s northwest-heaven-tilt and southeast-earth-tilt and on the tilt as the principal aetiological outcome of the Gonggong combat; brief 21-gonggong.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the cosmic tilt as the load-bearing cosmogonic outcome.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The cosmic tilt explains, on the *Huainanzi*'s reading, the empirical asymmetry of the Han-period observed cosmos: the heavenly bodies (sun, moon, stars) appear to move toward the northwest because heaven is tilted toward the northwest, and the rivers of the Chinese landscape flow eastward to the sea because the earth is tilted toward the southeast — the broken-pillar combat is the aetiology of the present geography of the world.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Huainanzi*'s aetiological reading of the cosmic tilt as the explanation for the apparent northwest-motion of heavenly bodies and the east-flowing rivers; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the same hydrographic and astronomical aetiology pattern; brief 21-gonggong.yaml on the post-combat geography (tilted cosmos, rivers flowing east) as the cosmogonic credential for Part II C1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The eastward flow of the rivers is one of the load-bearing cosmogonic outcomes that the chapter presents as the Gonggong narrative's principal contribution to the present book's Part II C1 (combat as cosmogony) argument: the Han text explicitly connects an empirical-and-everyday feature of Chinese geography (the rivers flowing east to the sea) to the post-combat tilt of the earth, and the connection makes the broken-pillar combat a cosmogony in the strong sense — the present cosmos is what the combat produced.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the eastward-rivers aetiology as a cosmogony in the strong sense; brief 21-gonggong.yaml cultural_relevance.cross_cultural_parallels on Gonggong as cosmogonic-combat parallel to Marduk in the present book's Part II C1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

=== Nüwa and the repair of the sky

After the breaking of the sky-pillar and the resulting tilt of the cosmos, the *Huainanzi* and parallel Han sources record that Nüwa undertakes the repair of the broken sky: she melts five-coloured stones (五色石) and uses the molten material to patch the gap in the heavens — the smelting-and-patching is the central technical operation of the repair-cosmogony.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Nüwa's smelting of five-coloured stones to patch the broken sky as the central technical operation of the repair-cosmogony; brief 21-gonggong.yaml entities_glossary entry on five-coloured stones (五色石) as the artefact Nüwa melts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The five-coloured stones are presented in the Han sources as a technical-cosmological material rather than as a generic patch: their five colours are read in subsequent Han and post-Han Chinese cosmological writing as corresponding to the Five Phases or Five Elements (wuxing 五行) — wood, fire, earth, metal, water — that organise the cosmographic system within which the repair makes sense.footnote:[Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the five-coloured stones' connection with the Five Phases / wuxing system in subsequent Han and post-Han cosmological writing; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3, on the technical-cosmological character of the five-coloured-stones material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=chapters on cosmogony

In the *Huainanzi*'s account, Nüwa supplements the smelted-stone patch with a further structural intervention: she cuts the legs from a giant turtle (in some versions, a giant tortoise) and uses them to prop up the corners of the heaven, restoring a measure of structural support to replace the broken Mount Buzhou and to stabilise the tilted cosmos against collapse.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Nüwa's use of the legs of a giant turtle to prop up the corners of the heaven after the breaking of Mount Buzhou; brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on Nüwa's repair "with five-coloured stones and the legs of a giant turtle" in the *Huainanzi* version.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The turtle-leg intervention is presented in the *Huainanzi* as a partial rather than a full repair: the broken Mount Buzhou is not restored to its original integrity, the cosmic tilt is not undone, and the post-combat cosmos retains the asymmetry that the combat produced — Nüwa's repair stabilises the cosmos in its tilted state rather than returning it to a pre-combat condition.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on Nüwa's repair as a partial-rather-than-full intervention that stabilises the tilted cosmos in its post-combat state rather than restoring the pre-combat integrity; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, chapters on cosmogony, on the partial-repair character of the Han sources' treatment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The combined combat-and-repair sequence — Gonggong breaks the sky-pillar; Nüwa stabilises the tilted cosmos with smelted stones and turtle-legs — is therefore presented in the Han sources as a single connected cosmogony in which the present asymmetric cosmos emerges through a two-stage process: a destructive combat that generates the tilt, and a constructive repair that fixes the tilt in its present form.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the combat-and-repair sequence as a single connected cosmogony in two stages (destructive combat + constructive repair); brief 21-gonggong.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the combat-plus-repair as the explanation for the present asymmetric cosmos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

=== The Lienü zhuan and the flood (co-equal Han variant)

The *Lienü zhuan* version of the Gonggong narrative connects the broken-pillar combat to a subsequent flood: the combat damages the cosmos in such a way that water either obstructs or escapes its proper channels, and the resulting flood becomes the disorder that Yu the Great is then summoned to remedy through hydraulic means.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Lienü zhuan* connection of the Gonggong combat to a subsequent flood that Yu remedies hydraulically; brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on "Lienü zhuan and other Han sources: Gonggong's combat is followed by the flood, which Yu the Great tames by opening channels".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

In the *Lienü zhuan*'s sequential reading, the Gonggong combat and the Yu flood-taming are not two separate stories but stages of a single connected drama: the combat breaks the cosmographic structure that ordinarily contains the waters; the broken structure releases a flood; Yu then tames the flood by opening channels and re-channelling the rivers; and the post-Yu hydrography is the present-day flow of the Chinese rivers to the eastern sea.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the *Lienü zhuan*'s sequential drama of Gonggong-combat → flood → Yu-taming → present-day hydrography; brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on the sequential cosmogonic-drama character of the *Lienü zhuan* presentation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The *Lienü zhuan* sequential-drama presentation is therefore not a competing account of the Gonggong combat but a parallel Han-period framing in which the cosmogonic combat is one stage of a longer ordering-process that includes both the combat-disruption and the hydraulic-restoration; the chapter's co-equal variant-handling presents this framing alongside the *Huainanzi*'s combat-and-Nüwa-repair framing rather than choosing between them.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the parallel-rather-than-competing relationship of the *Huainanzi* combat-and-Nüwa-repair version and the *Lienü zhuan* combat-and-Yu-taming version; per brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants.classification = co-equal on side-by-side presentation of the two Han variants without scholarly privileging.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=chs. 2-3

The two co-equal Han variants share their cosmogonic outcome (the present-day asymmetric cosmos with east-flowing rivers) but differ in their distribution of the ordering-work between divine repair (Nüwa's smelted stones and turtle-legs in the *Huainanzi*) and human-or-semi-divine technical labour (Yu's channel-opening in the *Lienü zhuan*); the chapter treats the difference as a substantive feature of the Han Gonggong corpus rather than as a contradiction to be resolved.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, chs. 2–3 on the substantive difference between the divine-repair and human-technical-labour distributions of the ordering-work across the *Huainanzi* and *Lienü zhuan* variants; per brief 21-gonggong.yaml variants entry on neither variant being scholarly-privileged.]

=== Yu the Great — embedded sidebar
