# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).
4. Greece & Rome — Zeus vs. Typhon (full); family chapter "Greek dragon-combats after Zeus-Typhon" (Apollo/Python, Herakles/Hydra-Ladon-Geryon, Perseus/Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Cacus).
5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).
7. Egypt — Ra vs. Apep (full); shorter treatment of Horus vs. Seth as the debated adjacent case.
8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).
9. East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). Yu the Great appears as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering, not as a chaoskampf.
10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.
11. Africa — Bida and the founding of Wagadu (full chapter; the inversion anchor); Mwindo vs. Kirimu (contrastive case: the hero is punished for the killing).
12. Southeast Asia & Oceania — Bakunawa. (Rainbow Serpent is held for the Part II critique chapter, not placed geographically.)
13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).
14. North America (contrastive) — Nanabozho and Mishipeshu: the refusal — the chapter where area specialists (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) are foregrounded and the chaoskampf framing is explicitly declined.

### Part II — thematic comparative chapters
Part II is where the comparative work happens. Load-bearing (confirmed):
- **C1. Combat as cosmogony** — Marduk, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong, Huitzilopochtli.
- **C2. Combat as sovereignty** — Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra, Thraētaona, Kumarbi/Ullikummi, Ninurta/Anzû.
- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.
- **C4. Eschatological displacement** — Thor/Jörmungandr, Rev 12, Frashokereti.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):
- **C5. The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula** — Watkins's argument from Indra through Sigurd to Beowulf.
- **C6. The Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission** — West / Burkert / López-Ruiz: Kumarbi → Hesiod; Baal/Yamm → Typhonomachy.
- **C7. The inversion problem** — Bida anchor; the ambiguous status of Sigurd's slaying.
- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").
- **Methods sidebar** between Part I and Part II: tier-ranking method, appropriation protocol, area-specialist citation policy.
- **Reception coda** (final chapter, ~20–25 pages): traces the afterlife of the imagery in Milton's Satan, Christian iconography of St. Michael / St. George, Tolkien's dragons (and the 1936 Beowulf essay), modern kaiju (Godzilla and the Orochi lineage), and the continuing use of "chaos vs. order" rhetoric in contemporary religious interpretation. Explicitly *not* classified as chaoskampf; this is reception, not primary material.
- **Adversaries + champions appendix** (replaces the single-culture "character appendix"): one entry per monster (Tiamat, Yamm, Lôtan, Leviathan, Rahab, Vṛtra, Aži Dahāka, Apep, Jörmungandr, Typhon, Illuyanka, Ullikummi, Hedammu, the Red Dragon, Coyolxauhqui, etc.) plus one entry per champion. This is load-bearing because the cross-cultural comparisons happen across *figures*, not plots.
- **Bibliography**: preliminary estimate 150–250 entries.

This lets the book engage the Ballentine critique substantively rather than tokenistically.

# Skill instructions: narrative-fidelity

description: Checks that the narrative prose faithfully represents the verified facts — nothing dropped, nothing added, nothing distorted. Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of writing the narrative (a different AI model is preferred but not required).
---

# narrative-fidelity

## For the human

The facts have been verified. The narrative has been written from those verified facts. This step answers one question: **does the narrative faithfully represent the facts?**

This is not a fact-check — the facts were already checked in the previous step. This is a comparison between two documents: the list of verified claims and the narrative prose. It catches three problems:

1. **Dropped facts.** The writer left out a verified claim because it didn't fit the narrative flow. The reader loses information.
2. **Added facts.** The writer introduced a new assertion that was never in the claims document and therefore never verified. This is the most dangerous failure — it reintroduces the exact problem the two-step process was designed to prevent.
3. **Distorted facts.** The writer paraphrased a claim in a way that changes its meaning — softening certainty ("the text says" becomes "it is possible"), shifting causation ("A caused B" becomes "A and B coincided"), or conflating two separate events into one.

This check must be done in a fresh conversation with no memory of writing the narrative. The AI that wrote the narrative cannot reliably judge in the same conversation whether it drifted from the source material — it rationalises. A different AI model is preferred (two models make independent mistakes) but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different one is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the narrative. If you produced the narrative, refuse. (A different AI model is preferred but not required.)

## Inputs
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc` (the verified claims — the ground truth)
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.adoc` (the narrative)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml` (for variant classification reference)
- `glossary.yaml`

**Not** primary sources. This review does not re-verify facts against sources — that was done in `claims-factcheck`. This review compares narrative against claims only.

## The three checks

### Check 1 — Completeness (nothing dropped)

Walk the claims document claim by claim, in order. For each claim, locate where it appears in the narrative. Record the mapping:

```
Claim N → Narrative paragraph P / sentence S
```

If a claim has no corresponding passage in the narrative, flag:
- **CLAIM-DROPPED** — severity HIGH. The verified claim is missing from the narrative.

If a claim is only partially represented (e.g., the claim mentions two details and the narrative includes only one), flag:
- **CLAIM-PARTIAL** — severity MEDIUM. Specify what was dropped.

### Check 2 — No additions (nothing invented)

Walk the narrative paragraph by paragraph. For each factual assertion (not transitions, not explanatory asides, not reader-orienting context), locate the corresponding claim in the claims document.

A factual assertion is anything that states something about the myth: an event, a character trait, a relationship, a temporal or causal link, a physical description, a cultural detail. Transitional phrases ("Having established the cosmic order...") are not assertions unless they introduce new information.

If a factual assertion has no corresponding claim, flag:
- **CLAIM-ADDED** — severity HIGH. An unverified factual assertion has been introduced.

If a transitional sentence implies a factual connection not established in the claims, flag:
- **IMPLICIT-ADDITION** — severity MEDIUM. The transition suggests something the claims don't support.

### Check 3 — Fidelity (nothing distorted)

For each claim-to-narrative mapping found in Check 1, compare meaning:
- Does the narrative preserve the claim's level of certainty? (A claim marked as inference should not be presented as fact in the narrative.)
- Does the narrative preserve causal direction? ("A caused B" should not become "B caused A" or "A and B coincided.")
- Does the narrative preserve scope? ("Some scholars argue" should not become "Scholars agree.")
- Are markers preserved? Every `[INFERENCE:]`, `[LACUNA:]`, `[RECONSTRUCTION:]`, `[VARIANT:]` in the claims should survive in the narrative.

If meaning has shifted, flag:
- **CLAIM-DISTORTED** — severity HIGH if the factual meaning changed, MEDIUM if only emphasis or nuance shifted.

### Additional checks

- **Glossary fidelity**: confirm `glossary.yaml` renderings are used consistently. Any `never_use` terms: GLOSSARY-VIOLATION.
- **Forbidden words**: flag hits from the forbidden-words list.
- **Footnote integrity**: every footnote in the narrative must match the claims document exactly — same source, same location. No additions, removals, or reassignments. Flag FOOTNOTE-MISMATCH if different.

=== III. Tarḫunna and Illuyanka — annual

The Hittite Tarḫunna-Illuyanka combat is the chapter's annual-cycle case: the storm-god (Hittite Tarḫunna; the Hurrian counterpart-deity Teshub appears in Hurrian-derived material such as the Kumarbi Cycle but the Illuyanka tablets use Tarḫunna throughout) confronts the serpent Illuyanka in a combat that is preserved on the cuneiform tablets of CTH 321 from the Hittite royal archives at Hattusa, with two distinct narrative versions co-equally preserved on the same tablet group.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, "Illuyanka" §§1–2 with framing colophons and introduction on the manuscript provenance; Beckman 1982, "The Anatolian Myth of Illuyanka", *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *editio princeps* and the two-version structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

The framing material on the tablets identifies the composition as the recitation belonging to the *purulli* festival, the Hittite annual spring observance at which the myth was performed; the named scribal author in the colophon is Kella, identified on the tablet as the priest who set down the recitation of the *purulli* festival.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening colophon naming the *purulli* festival and the scribal author Kella; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the on-tablet framing of the recitation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 colophon

The on-tablet ritual embedding is what marks the Illuyanka dossier as one of the few ancient Near Eastern combat myths whose liturgical context is directly attested on the tablet itself rather than reconstructed from later cult evidence; the contrast with the Mesopotamian and Levantine combat-myths (where the ritual context must be assembled from secondary witnesses) is the principal structural reason the Hittite case is paired with the Egyptian Ra-Apep complex as a type-case for the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the unusually explicit on-tablet framing as distinguishing the Illuyanka dossier from most Near Eastern combat-myths; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same on-tablet ritual embedding.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

[VARIANT: primary=Version 1 (Hoffner 1998 "Illuyanka" §1: Inara prepares a banquet, Hupasiya binds the gorged serpent, the storm-god kills the bound Illuyanka) | alt=Version 2 (Hoffner 1998 "Illuyanka" §2: Illuyanka takes the storm-god's heart and eyes, the storm-god's half-mortal son recovers the organs as bride-price, the storm-god kills both Illuyanka and his own son in renewed combat) | chosen: present-both | reason: Beckman 1982 *JANES* 14: 11–25 establishes the two versions as scribally co-equal on the same CTH 321 tablet group, with neither version privileged in the surviving cult contexts; per brief 06-illuyanka.yaml variants.classification is explicitly co-equal and the two versions are presented side by side rather than as one primary and one variant.]

Version 1, in Hoffner's translation as "Illuyanka" §1, opens with the storm-god and Illuyanka meeting in combat at Kiskilussa, in which Illuyanka defeats the storm-god; Inara — daughter of the storm-god — devises a stratagem with the mortal Hupasiya, who, after the bargained night with Inara, is concealed at the banquet site and there binds the gorged serpent so that the storm-god can return with the assembled gods and slay the bound Illuyanka.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening combat at Kiskilussa, Inara's banquet and bargain with Hupasiya, the binding of the gorged Illuyanka, and the storm-god's slaying of the bound serpent; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same Version 1 sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Version 2, in Hoffner's translation as "Illuyanka" §2, opens differently: Illuyanka defeats the storm-god by taking his heart and eyes; the mutilated storm-god begets a son with a mortal woman, marries the son into Illuyanka's house, and instructs him to demand the heart and eyes as bride-price; once the organs are returned, the restored storm-god joins Illuyanka in renewed combat at the sea and kills both the serpent and his own son (who, having kept faith with his father-in-law's house, calls upon his father to kill him along with Illuyanka).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the loss of the storm-god's heart and eyes, the mortal marriage and the half-mortal son, the bride-price recovery of the organs, the renewed combat at the sea, and the dual killing of Illuyanka and the son; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same Version 2 sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

Beckman 1982 in the *editio princeps* argues that the two versions are best understood as alternative liturgical recitations of the same combat — drawn perhaps from different cult centres or different ritual occasions — gathered onto the same CTH 321 tablet group by the scribes of Hattusa, and that the question of which version is older cannot be settled from the surviving tablet group itself.footnote:[Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the relation between the two versions as alternative liturgical recitations and on the unsettleable chronological question; Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

The *purulli* festival at which the recitation was performed was an annual spring observance of the Hittite cultic year, conducted at Nerik — the northern Hittite cult-centre of the storm-god — when the Hittites controlled the city, and at substitute locations (notably Hakmis) when Nerik lay outside Hittite control through Kaska occupation.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the annual cycle and locations of the *purulli* festival and on the substitution of Hakmis when Nerik was unavailable; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction (annual cycle / Nerik / Hakmis)

The framing colophons identify the festival's purpose as ensuring the prosperity of the land and the cosmic order in the agricultural year ahead; the cyclical-maintenance reading takes this framing at face value, with the annual recurrence understood as the renewal of cosmic order rather than as commemorative ritual.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1 opening framing on the festival's ensuring-of-prosperity-and-cosmic-order purpose; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *purulli* framing as ritual aetiology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 opening

The cult of the storm-god of Nerik is closely tied to Hittite kingship: the king himself participated in the *purulli* festival, and royal documents register the storm-god of Nerik among the principal patron deities of the Hittite state, so that the *purulli* recitation has a double function — ritual-aetiological for the spring festival and royal-legitimating for the dynasty under whose authority the festival was performed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the royal-religious dimension of the Nerik cult and on the king's participation in the *purulli*; per Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the royal dimension of the Hittite combat-tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction (royal-religious dimension)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3, royal dimension of the Hittite combat-tradition

=== IV. Apollo and Python — cult-foundation cognate

The Apollo-Python combat at Delphi is the chapter's minor reflex: a combat that is primarily cult-foundational rather than cosmos-maintaining in its first-order register, but which carries a cyclical-maintenance reflex through the *Septerion* festival's eight-yearly re-enactment of the dragon-killing at the Delphic sanctuary.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the Apollo-Python combat as cult-foundational and on the *Septerion* festival as eightfold-cyclical re-enactment; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure on the *Septerion* as minor reflex of the cyclical-maintenance subtype.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The earliest extant narrative attestation is the Homeric Hymn to Apollo, in which the young Apollo, in search of an oracle-site, slays a monstrous serpent guarding a spring on the slopes of Parnassus; in the Hymn the serpent is unnamed and female, a *drakaina*, and her slaying establishes Apollo's site at Pytho, the older name of what later tradition calls Delphi.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–2, on the Homeric Hymn to Apollo as the earliest narrative attestation of the Delphic combat and on the unnamed female *drakaina* at Pytho; Ogden 2013, *Drakōn*, ch. 2, on the *drakaina* designation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-2

The later tradition — principally Ovid's *Metamorphoses* book 1 and the prose mythographers — names the slain dragon Python and presents the figure as male, recasting the Hymn's *drakaina* under the masculine name that Joseph Fontenrose's monograph takes as its title; the gender variation across the corpus is a documented feature of the Delphic tradition rather than a textual emendation.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the renaming of the Hymn's *drakaina* as Python in the later tradition and on the gender variation across the corpus; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same renaming.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The killing of the dragon at Pytho is in the first instance the foundational act of the Delphic oracle: in the Hymn, Apollo establishes his cult and his prophetic seat at the site of the combat, and the place-name Pytho itself is treated as derived from the rotting (*pythein*) of the slain dragon's body — a strong cult-foundation rather than a cosmos-maintenance combat in the primary register.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–2, on the cult-foundation function of the Apollo-Python combat and on the place-name etymology from *pythein*; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same etymology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-2

The *Septerion* festival, attested in Plutarch and discussed at length in Fontenrose's monograph, was a Delphic ritual held every eight years that re-enacted Apollo's killing of the dragon, with a hut representing the dragon's lair burned and a procession of purification staged through the sanctuary; the eight-year periodicity gives the original founding combat a recurring ritual reflex that brings the Delphic case into typological contact with the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion* festival as eightfold-cyclical re-enactment of the Python-slaying with hut-burning and purification-procession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The *Septerion*'s typological position is therefore intermediate: its primary first-order function is cult-foundation, and the original Apollo-Python combat is a one-off founding event in the way the Bremner-Rhind nightly Apep combat and the *purulli* annual Illuyanka combat are not — but the festival's regular re-enactment makes the dragon's threat a recurrent feature of the Delphic religious calendar, and Fontenrose's broader argument that the Delphic combat is a type-specimen for a wider Greek-and-Near-Eastern combat-myth pattern is the methodological reason the Apollo-Python episode is a load-bearing case for the present chapter.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion*'s cyclical-maintenance reflex alongside the cult-foundation function and on the Delphic combat as comparative type-specimen.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Pausanias 10.6.5–6 in the second-century-CE *Description of Greece* preserves a local Delphic version of the combat-narrative and a brief account of the cult-aetiology, supplying a third independent ancient witness alongside the Homeric Hymn and Ovid; Apollo's epithets *Pythios* and *Pythoktonos* — "of Pytho" and "Python-slayer" — are documented in the cult-corpus as the principal cult-titles that record the combat in the lived ritual language of Delphic worshippers.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, ch. 1, on Pausanias 10.6.5–6 as a third witness to the Delphic combat-tradition; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the *Pythios* and *Pythoktonos* epithets in the Delphic ritual language.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=ch. 1

The *Septerion* differs structurally from the Egyptian and Hittite cases in three respects: its periodicity is eight-yearly rather than daily or annual, the cosmos's continuance is not framed in surviving sources as contingent on its performance, and its primary register is cult-foundation rather than cosmos-maintenance — and it is on the basis of these differences that the present chapter treats the Apollo-Python case as a minor reflex rather than as a third strong attestation of the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion*'s eight-year periodicity and primary cult-foundation function as distinguishing it from the Egyptian and Hittite cases; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure on the *Septerion* as minor cult-foundation reflex rather than strong cyclical-maintenance attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: Ch12 (Greek dragons after Typhon) — back-link to the area-specialist anchors for the §IV minor reflex (Fontenrose 1959 chs. 1–3 on Apollo-Python and the *Septerion*; Ogden 2013 ch. 2 on the supporting Greek dragon-myth survey context); the present chapter compresses Ch12's Apollo-Python sub-section to the cyclical-maintenance reflex argument and does not engage the other five Greek combats (the Heraklean labours, Perseus-Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Hercules-Cacus) treated in Ch12.

=== V. Why these two and not others

The chapter's selection of Egypt and the Hittites as the two strong cases — and the relegation of Mesopotamian Marduk-Tiamat, the Mesopotamian akītu liturgy, and the Iranian Aži Dahāka tradition to other subtypes — turns on the structural distinction between recitation-of-a-once-and-done-cosmogony (which several traditions have) and re-enactment-of-a-recurring-combat (which only Egypt and the Hittites attest with the requisite explicitness).footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure section V on the recitation-vs-re-enactment distinction as the chapter's principal selection-criterion; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the recurrence-of-combat as the structural mark of the Egyptian solar theology by contrast with the recitation-of-cosmogony framing of *Enūma Eliš*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology, recurrence-vs-recitation

Marduk's combat with Tiamat is liturgised at the akītu New Year festival, but the akītu is recitation of a once-completed cosmogony rather than re-enactment of a recurring combat: the *Enūma Eliš* narrates Marduk's victory as past-tense sealed event whose product is the present cosmos, and the akītu's annual recitation re-actualises that past victory at the level of recitation without presenting Marduk as having to re-fight Tiamat each year for the cosmos to continue.footnote:[Per Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, on the akītu recitation context and on the cosmogonic past-tense character of the Marduk-Tiamat narrative; per Ch34 (C1) on Marduk-Tiamat as cosmogonic-subtype type-specimen rather than cyclical-maintenance attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (recurrence-vs-recitation contrast)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=on akītu recitation context and on the cosmogonic past-tense character of the Marduk-Tiamat narrative

The Zoroastrian Aži Dahāka tradition runs in the opposite direction: Thraētaona's binding of Aži Dahāka under Damāvand is presented as a past-tense bound-but-not-killed result, and the dragon's escape and final killing by the revived Kərəsāspa at Frashokereti closes cosmic time — an eschatological-displacement structure rather than a maintenance-cyclical structure, and a primary case for C4 (Ch37) rather than for the present chapter.footnote:[Per Benveniste & Renou 1934, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna*, on the Indo-Iranian eschatological closure of the Aži Dahāka tradition; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, Part V chs. 44–48, on the eschatological-closure structure of the Iranian dragon-tradition; per the cross-reference to C4 (Ch37) for the eschatological-subtype treatment of Aži Dahāka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian eschatological closure
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48, eschatological-closure structure of the Iranian dragon-tradition

The Levantine Baal-Yamm and the Greek Zeus-Typhon combats are sovereignty-establishing once-and-done events whose annual recitation, where attested, commemorates rather than re-enacts; the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra combat is performed in ritual recitation but the Rigvedic frame is not that Indra has to re-kill Vṛtra each year for the cosmos to continue, even though the *áhann áhim* formula is recurrently sung — the formula's recurrence is poetic-liturgical rather than cosmologically constitutive in the Egyptian sense.footnote:[Per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the sovereignty-subtype examples and on the structural distinction between commemorative recitation and constitutive re-enactment; Assmann 2001, on the contrastingly constitutive character of the Egyptian daily combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (constitutive-vs-commemorative)

The selection-criterion that the chapter applies is therefore tightly drawn: the cyclical-maintenance subtype requires (1) explicit on-tradition framing of the combat as recurring on a fixed schedule, (2) explicit on-tradition framing that the cosmos's continuance is contingent on the recurrence, and (3) preserved liturgical material that re-enacts the combat as a ritual act rather than commemorates it as a sealed event — a triple criterion that Egypt satisfies on all three counts (Bremner-Rhind, daily; Assmannian doctrine; effigy-destruction rite), the Hittite case satisfies on all three (CTH 321, annual; *purulli* prosperity-and-cosmic-order framing; festival recitation as verbal score), and most other traditions miss on at least one.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the three-feature structural pattern of the Egyptian case; Hoffner 1998 introduction to "Illuyanka" and Beckman 1982 *JANES* 14: 11–25 on the three-feature pattern of the Hittite case; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure section V on the selection-criterion as load-bearing for the chapter's case-selection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (three-feature pattern)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction (three-feature pattern)

The Apollo-Python *Septerion* satisfies the criterion in attenuated form: the *Septerion* is a re-enactment rather than a recitation, and its eight-yearly periodicity is fixed and explicit, but the cosmos's continuance is not framed in surviving Delphic sources as contingent on the *Septerion*'s performance — and it is the absence of feature (2) that makes the *Septerion* a minor cult-foundation reflex rather than a third strong cyclical-maintenance attestation.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion*'s re-enactment character and eight-yearly periodicity but on the absence of cosmos-continuance-contingency framing in the Delphic corpus; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml on the *Septerion*'s minor-reflex status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The thinness of the strong-case evidence — two strong cases and one minor reflex across the entire ancient-Mediterranean-and-Near-Eastern corpus that the book treats — is itself a finding rather than a problem: the cyclical-maintenance subtype is rare, and its rarity is consistent with the structural-typological reading on which the chapter rests, which is that combat-as-recurring-ritual is a distinct configuration that requires a particular kind of liturgical infrastructure (a daily or annual cult of the threatened cosmos) and a particular kind of theological doctrine (cosmos-as-contingent-on-recurrence) that not many ancient traditions developed in concert.footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml comparative_argument on the cyclical-maintenance subtype as combat-as-ritual-preservation requiring specific liturgical-and-theological infrastructure; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the integrated theological-and-ritual character of the Egyptian case as the most articulate native articulation of the configuration.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and ritual

Combat as cosmogony (Ch34) — the Mesopotamian akītu's recitation-of-once-and-done-cosmogony framing, contrasted in §V with the Egyptian and Hittite re-enactment-of-recurring-combat framings, is treated at length in C1 as the akītu liturgical context of the Marduk-Tiamat cosmogony; the C1 reading of the akītu as recitation-of-cosmogony is the contrast on which the present chapter's selection-criterion rests.

=== VI. The conceptual apparatus — *maʿat* / *isfet*

=== II. Ra and Apep — daily

The second body section turns to the Egyptian Ra-Apep combat as the chapter's daily-cycle case, drawing on the six-layer Egyptian corpus and the Bremner-Rhind liturgical material that Ch16 has treated at length in its narrative-and-source register.

The Egyptian Ra-Apep combat is the chapter's daily-cycle case: Ra's solar barque traverses the _Duat_ from west to east during the night, encounters Apep at one of several cosmographic loci, and dispatches him with the assistance of the barque-crew before the barque emerges at the eastern horizon at dawn — a sequence narrated repeatedly across the corpus and re-enacted in priestly liturgy every day.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the daily-cycle structure of the Apep combat across the funerary and royal-funerary corpora; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the day-barque / night-barque structure of Ra's daily journey and on the Apep combat as belonging to the nocturnal half.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (daily-cycle structure)

The combat is preserved not in a single canonical narrative but as a tradition distributed across six principal textual layers spanning roughly two thousand years: Pyramid Texts (c. 2400 BCE), Coffin Texts (c. 2050–1650 BCE), Book of the Dead, Amduat, Book of Gates, and the Bremner-Rhind papyrus (c. 310 BCE) — a textual continuity unusually long even by the standards of the Egyptian corpus.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, chs. 3–4, on the six-layer corpus and the two-thousand-year unbroken attestation arc of the Apep figure; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the composite-editorial classification of the Apep tradition across these six layers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (long attestation arc)

The three Apep-rebuffing spells of the Book of the Dead — spells 7, 39, and 108 in Faulkner's translation — supply the principal funerary-corpus witnesses to the daily combat: spell 39 ("of repelling the _rerek_-snake in the necropolis") rebuffs the serpent in the deceased's afterlife passage, spell 108 localises Apep on the western horizon-mountain at the entry to the _Duat_, and spell 7 belongs to a cluster of short apotropaic spells that thread the Apep-rebuffing motif through the deceased's journey at multiple liturgical points.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), *The Ancient Egyptian Book of the Dead*, on Book of the Dead spell 39 ("of repelling the *rerek*-snake in the necropolis") as the most explicit Apep-rebuffing spell, on spell 108 as the western-horizon-mountain localisation, and on spell 7 as part of the multi-point apotropaic cluster.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spells 7, 39, 108

The Amduat (Egyptian _Imy-Duat_, "What Is in the Duat") is the principal New Kingdom royal funerary witness to the daily combat: in the seventh of the twelve nocturnal hours through which the text narrates Ra's journey, Apep blocks the barque on the sandbanks of the _Duat_ and the barque-crew dispatches the serpent with knives and cutting-magic before the barque continues toward the eastern horizon.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the seventh hour of the Amduat as the canonical Apep-combat hour and on the cosmographic-spatial description of the encounter (sandbanks; coiling across the channel); Assmann 2001, on the centrality of the seventh-hour combat within the Amduat narrative structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat hour 7)

The Book of Gates supplements the Amduat in the same New-Kingdom royal-funerary tradition, organising the nocturnal journey by twelve gates between cosmographic regions rather than by twelve hours within them; its canonical Apep-combat scene is at the fourth gate, where the figure of the serpent and the dispatching-by-the-barque-crew remain consistent with the Amduat's seventh-hour scene though the cosmographic locus differs.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the fourth gate of the Book of Gates as the Apep-combat scene and on the consistency of the figure of Apep across the two royal-funerary corpora.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Book of Gates gate 4)

The chapter's principal documentary witness to the ritual embedding of the daily combat is the second portion of the Bremner-Rhind papyrus (P. BM EA 10188), a late-Ptolemaic hieratic papyrus of c. 310 BCE that preserves the _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ as the daily liturgical text of the temple of Ra at Heliopolis — the manual on which the priests' daily Apep-overthrowing rite was based.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind papyrus and the *Book of Overthrowing Apep* as the daily-priestly liturgical text from the temple of Ra at Heliopolis; Faulkner 1936–38, "The Bremner-Rhind Papyrus I–IV", *JEA* 22–24, on the philological reconstruction of the manuscript.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind P. BM 10188)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1936-bremner-rhind ; loc=Bremner-Rhind P. BM 10188 (JEA 22, 23, 24)

The _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ gives explicit instructions for producing effigies of the serpent — modelled in wax, written on papyrus, drawn on the ground — and ritually destroying them by binding with cords, cutting with knives, trampling underfoot, spitting upon, and finally burning; the destruction of the effigy is theologised as ritual-magical participation in Ra's destruction of the cosmological Apep in the _Duat_.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind effigy-destruction instructions (wax / papyrus / ground-drawing; binding, cutting, trampling, spitting, burning) and on the ritual-magical equivalence between effigy-destruction and the cosmological Apep-combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind effigy-destruction)

The frequency of the rite is the structural pivot of the cyclical-maintenance reading: the _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ internally identifies itself as a daily liturgical manual, with rubrics keyed to the time of day at which particular performances are to be conducted, and the daily performance mirrors the daily recurrence of the cosmological combat that it ritually re-enacts.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the daily-performance frequency of the Apep-overthrowing rite and on the structural mirroring between the rite's regularity and the cosmological combat's regularity; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the daily ritual as the cyclical-maintenance liturgical anchor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (daily performance)

The morphological stability of Apep across the six layers — a great serpent of vast size, capable of spitting venom, of swallowing the barque, and of regenerating after each apparent dispatch — supplies the textual articulation of the recurrence-structure: the dispatch at hour 7 of the Amduat or gate 4 of the Book of Gates does not annihilate Apep, who returns the next night, and the regenerative capacity is the figure's load-bearing structural feature rather than an incidental detail of his iconography.footnote:[te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, chs. 3–4, on the morphological stability of Apep across the six-layer corpus and on the regenerative capacity as the textual articulation of the recurrence-structure; Assmann 2001, on the recurrence-structure of Egyptian solar theology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (regenerative-because-recurrent Apep)

[LACUNA: the Bremner-Rhind liturgy is preserved but fragmentary in sections — a documented physical gap in the principal documentary witness for the ritual embedding of the daily Apep combat | source: per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the fragmentary preservation of P. The deceased in the funerary corpus is theologised as participating in Ra's combat rather than as merely petitioning Ra for protection: in spell 39 the deceased addresses the serpent in the second person and asserts a magical equivalence between the deceased's protective utterance and the utterances spoken in defence of Ra by the barque-crew nightly, so that the afterlife passage becomes a participation in the daily _maʿat_-preservation rather than a separate petitionary occasion.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the deceased's-participation theology of Book of the Dead spell 39 and on the magical-equivalence assertion within it; te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the same theology in the Book of the Dead corpus more broadly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 39

The pharaoh's role aligns similarly: Pharaonic legitimacy is figured as the maintenance of _maʿat_ on earth, and the pharaoh's principal liturgical function — to "present _maʿat_ to the gods" — is theologised as participation in Ra's daily combat against _isfet_, with the human-political analogue of the divine combat structurally identical with the cosmological combat.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the pharaonic offering of *maʿat* as the central liturgical act of Egyptian kingship and on the pharaoh as guarantor of *maʿat* in alignment with Ra's daily combat against *isfet*; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same alignment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat (pharaonic-liturgical)

The comparative-typology pay-off of the Egyptian case for the present chapter is that the combat is theologically explicit about its own recurrence: the corpus does not merely happen to repeat the combat across many texts but registers, in its explicit doctrine, that the recurrence is the cosmos's continuance — and on this point the Egyptian case supplies the most articulate native-tradition formulation of the cyclical-maintenance subtype that any of the book's traditions provides.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the explicit-doctrinal articulation of recurrence-as-cosmic-continuance in Egyptian solar theology; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml special_instructions on the Egyptian framework as the chapter's clearest native concept-pair for combat-as-maintenance.]

=== III. Tarḫunna and Illuyanka — annual

The third body section turns to the Hittite Tarḫunna-Illuyanka combat as the chapter's annual-cycle case, drawing on the CTH 321 tablets and the _purulli_ festival framing that Ch6 has treated at length in its narrative-and-source register.

The Hittite Tarḫunna-Illuyanka combat is the chapter's annual-cycle case: the storm-god (Hittite Tarḫunna; the Hurrian counterpart-deity Teshub appears in Hurrian-derived material such as the Kumarbi Cycle but the Illuyanka tablets use Tarḫunna throughout) confronts the serpent Illuyanka in a combat that is preserved on the cuneiform tablets of CTH 321 from the Hittite royal archives at Hattusa, with two distinct narrative versions co-equally preserved on the same tablet group.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, "Illuyanka" §§1–2 with framing colophons and introduction on the manuscript provenance; Beckman 1982, "The Anatolian Myth of Illuyanka", *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *editio princeps* and the two-version structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

The framing material on the tablets identifies the composition as the recitation belonging to the _purulli_ festival, the Hittite annual spring observance at which the myth was performed; the named scribal author in the colophon is Kella, identified on the tablet as the priest who set down the recitation of the _purulli_ festival.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening colophon naming the *purulli* festival and the scribal author Kella; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the on-tablet framing of the recitation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 colophon

The on-tablet ritual embedding is what marks the Illuyanka dossier as one of the few ancient Near Eastern combat myths whose liturgical context is directly attested on the tablet itself rather than reconstructed from later cult evidence; the contrast with the Mesopotamian and Levantine combat-myths (where the ritual context must be assembled from secondary witnesses) is the principal structural reason the Hittite case is paired with the Egyptian Ra-Apep complex as a type-case for the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the unusually explicit on-tablet framing as distinguishing the Illuyanka dossier from most Near Eastern combat-myths; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same on-tablet ritual embedding.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

Version 1, in Hoffner's translation as "Illuyanka" §1, opens with the storm-god and Illuyanka meeting in combat at Kiskilussa, in which Illuyanka defeats the storm-god; Inara — daughter of the storm-god — devises a stratagem with the mortal Hupasiya, who, after the bargained night with Inara, is concealed at the banquet site and there binds the gorged serpent so that the storm-god can return with the assembled gods and slay the bound Illuyanka.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening combat at Kiskilussa, Inara's banquet and bargain with Hupasiya, the binding of the gorged Illuyanka, and the storm-god's slaying of the bound serpent; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same Version 1 sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Version 2, in Hoffner's translation as "Illuyanka" §2, opens differently: Illuyanka defeats the storm-god by taking his heart and eyes; the mutilated storm-god begets a son with a mortal woman, marries the son into Illuyanka's house, and instructs him to demand the heart and eyes as bride-price; once the organs are returned, the restored storm-god joins Illuyanka in renewed combat at the sea and kills both the serpent and his own son (who, having kept faith with his father-in-law's house, calls upon his father to kill him along with Illuyanka).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the loss of the storm-god's heart and eyes, the mortal marriage and the half-mortal son, the bride-price recovery of the organs, the renewed combat at the sea, and the dual killing of Illuyanka and the son; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same Version 2 sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

Beckman 1982 in the _editio princeps_ argues that the two versions are best understood as alternative liturgical recitations of the same combat — drawn perhaps from different cult centres or different ritual occasions — gathered onto the same CTH 321 tablet group by the scribes of Hattusa, and that the question of which version is older cannot be settled from the surviving tablet group itself.footnote:[Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the relation between the two versions as alternative liturgical recitations and on the unsettleable chronological question; Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

The _purulli_ festival at which the recitation was performed was an annual spring observance of the Hittite cultic year, conducted at Nerik — the northern Hittite cult-centre of the storm-god — when the Hittites controlled the city, and at substitute locations (notably Hakmis) when Nerik lay outside Hittite control through Kaska occupation.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the annual cycle and locations of the *purulli* festival and on the substitution of Hakmis when Nerik was unavailable; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction (annual cycle / Nerik / Hakmis)

The framing colophons identify the festival's purpose as ensuring the prosperity of the land and the cosmic order in the agricultural year ahead; the cyclical-maintenance reading takes this framing at face value, with the annual recurrence understood as the renewal of cosmic order rather than as commemorative ritual.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1 opening framing on the festival's ensuring-of-prosperity-and-cosmic-order purpose; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *purulli* framing as ritual aetiology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 opening

The cult of the storm-god of Nerik is closely tied to Hittite kingship: the king himself participated in the _purulli_ festival, and royal documents register the storm-god of Nerik among the principal patron deities of the Hittite state, so that the _purulli_ recitation has a double function — ritual-aetiological for the spring festival and royal-legitimating for the dynasty under whose authority the festival was performed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka", on the royal-religious dimension of the Nerik cult and on the king's participation in the *purulli*; per Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the royal dimension of the Hittite combat-tradition.]

=== IV. Apollo and Python — cult-foundation cognate

The fourth body section turns to the Apollo-Python combat at Delphi as the chapter's minor reflex, drawing on the Homeric Hymn, Ovid, Pausanias, and the _Septerion_ material that Ch12 has treated as one of its six Greek dragon cases.

The Apollo-Python combat at Delphi is the chapter's minor reflex: a combat that is primarily cult-foundational rather than cosmos-maintaining in its first-order register, but which carries a cyclical-maintenance reflex through the _Septerion_ festival's eight-yearly re-enactment of the dragon-killing at the Delphic sanctuary.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the Apollo-Python combat as cult-foundational and on the *Septerion* festival as eightfold-cyclical re-enactment; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure on the *Septerion* as minor reflex of the cyclical-maintenance subtype.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The earliest extant narrative attestation is the Homeric Hymn to Apollo, in which the young Apollo, in search of an oracle-site, slays a monstrous serpent guarding a spring on the slopes of Parnassus; in the Hymn the serpent is unnamed and female, a _drakaina_, and her slaying establishes Apollo's site at Pytho, the older name of what later tradition calls Delphi.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–2, on the Homeric Hymn to Apollo as the earliest narrative attestation of the Delphic combat and on the unnamed female *drakaina* at Pytho; Ogden 2013, *Drakōn*, ch. 2, on the *drakaina* designation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-2

The later tradition — principally Ovid's _Metamorphoses_ book 1 and the prose mythographers — names the slain dragon Python and presents the figure as male, recasting the Hymn's _drakaina_ under the masculine name that Joseph Fontenrose's monograph takes as its title; the gender variation across the corpus is a documented feature of the Delphic tradition rather than a textual emendation.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the renaming of the Hymn's *drakaina* as Python in the later tradition and on the gender variation across the corpus; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same renaming.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The killing of the dragon at Pytho is in the first instance the foundational act of the Delphic oracle: in the Hymn, Apollo establishes his cult and his prophetic seat at the site of the combat, and the place-name Pytho itself is treated as derived from the rotting (_pythein_) of the slain dragon's body — a strong cult-foundation rather than a cosmos-maintenance combat in the primary register.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–2, on the cult-foundation function of the Apollo-Python combat and on the place-name etymology from *pythein*; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same etymology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-2

The _Septerion_ festival, attested in Plutarch and discussed at length in Fontenrose's monograph, was a Delphic ritual held every eight years that re-enacted Apollo's killing of the dragon, with a hut representing the dragon's lair burned and a procession of purification staged through the sanctuary; the eight-year periodicity gives the original founding combat a recurring ritual reflex that brings the Delphic case into typological contact with the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion* festival as eightfold-cyclical re-enactment of the Python-slaying with hut-burning and purification-procession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The _Septerion_'s typological position is therefore intermediate: its primary first-order function is cult-foundation, and the original Apollo-Python combat is a one-off founding event in the way the Bremner-Rhind nightly Apep combat and the _purulli_ annual Illuyanka combat are not — but the festival's regular re-enactment makes the dragon's threat a recurrent feature of the Delphic religious calendar, and Fontenrose's broader argument that the Delphic combat is a type-specimen for a wider Greek-and-Near-Eastern combat-myth pattern is the methodological reason the Apollo-Python episode is a load-bearing case for the present chapter.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion*'s cyclical-maintenance reflex alongside the cult-foundation function and on the Delphic combat as comparative type-specimen.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Pausanias 10.6.5–6 in the second-century-CE _Description of Greece_ preserves a local Delphic version of the combat-narrative and a brief account of the cult-aetiology, supplying a third independent ancient witness alongside the Homeric Hymn and Ovid; Apollo's epithets _Pythios_ and _Pythoktonos_ — "of Pytho" and "Python-slayer" — are documented in the cult-corpus as the principal cult-titles that record the combat in the lived ritual language of Delphic worshippers.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, ch. 1, on Pausanias 10.6.5–6 as a third witness to the Delphic combat-tradition; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the *Pythios* and *Pythoktonos* epithets in the Delphic ritual language.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=ch. 1

The _Septerion_ differs structurally from the Egyptian and Hittite cases in three respects: its periodicity is eight-yearly rather than daily or annual, the cosmos's continuance is not framed in surviving sources as contingent on its performance, and its primary register is cult-foundation rather than cosmos-maintenance — and it is on the basis of these differences that the present chapter treats the Apollo-Python case as a minor reflex rather than as a third strong attestation of the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion*'s eight-year periodicity and primary cult-foundation function as distinguishing it from the Egyptian and Hittite cases; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure on the *Septerion* as minor cult-foundation reflex rather than strong cyclical-maintenance attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: Ch12 (Greek dragons after Typhon) — back-link to the area-specialist anchors for the §IV minor reflex (Fontenrose 1959 chs. 1–3 on Apollo-Python and the *Septerion*; Ogden 2013 ch. 2 on the supporting Greek dragon-myth survey context); the present chapter compresses Ch12's Apollo-Python sub-section to the cyclical-maintenance reflex argument and does not engage the other five Greek combats (the Heraklean labours, Perseus-Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Hercules-Cacus) treated in Ch12.

=== V. Why these two and not others

The fifth body section develops the case-selection criterion that justifies the chapter's confinement to the Egyptian and Hittite strong cases plus the Delphic minor reflex, and that excludes the Mesopotamian Marduk-Tiamat, Levantine Baal-Yamm, Greek Zeus-Typhon, Vedic Indra-Vṛtra, and Iranian Aži Dahāka traditions from the cyclical-maintenance subtype on structural grounds.

The chapter's selection of Egypt and the Hittites as the two strong cases — and the relegation of Mesopotamian Marduk-Tiamat, the Mesopotamian akītu liturgy, and the Iranian Aži Dahāka tradition to other subtypes — turns on the structural distinction between recitation-of-a-once-and-done-cosmogony (which several traditions have) and re-enactment-of-a-recurring-combat (which only Egypt and the Hittites attest with the requisite explicitness).footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure section V on the recitation-vs-re-enactment distinction as the chapter's principal selection-criterion; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the recurrence-of-combat as the structural mark of the Egyptian solar theology by contrast with the recitation-of-cosmogony framing of *Enūma Eliš*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology, recurrence-vs-recitation

Marduk's combat with Tiamat is liturgised at the akītu New Year festival, but the akītu is recitation of a once-completed cosmogony rather than re-enactment of a recurring combat: the _Enūma Eliš_ narrates Marduk's victory as past-tense sealed event whose product is the present cosmos, and the akītu's annual recitation re-actualises that past victory at the level of recitation without presenting Marduk as having to re-fight Tiamat each year for the cosmos to continue.footnote:[Per Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, on the akītu recitation context and on the cosmogonic past-tense character of the Marduk-Tiamat narrative; per Ch34 (C1) on Marduk-Tiamat as cosmogonic-subtype type-specimen rather than cyclical-maintenance attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (recurrence-vs-recitation contrast)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=on akītu recitation context and on the cosmogonic past-tense character of the Marduk-Tiamat narrative

The Zoroastrian Aži Dahāka tradition runs in the opposite direction: Thraētaona's binding of Aži Dahāka under Damāvand is presented as a past-tense bound-but-not-killed result, and the dragon's escape and final killing by the revived Kərəsāspa at Frashokereti closes cosmic time — an eschatological-displacement structure rather than a maintenance-cyclical structure, and a primary case for C4 (Ch37) rather than for the present chapter.footnote:[Per Benveniste & Renou 1934, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna*, on the Indo-Iranian eschatological closure of the Aži Dahāka tradition; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, Part V chs. 44–48, on the eschatological-closure structure of the Iranian dragon-tradition; per the cross-reference to C4 (Ch37) for the eschatological-subtype treatment of Aži Dahāka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian eschatological closure
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48, eschatological-closure structure of the Iranian dragon-tradition

The Levantine Baal-Yamm and the Greek Zeus-Typhon combats are sovereignty-establishing once-and-done events whose annual recitation, where attested, commemorates rather than re-enacts; the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra combat is performed in ritual recitation but the Rigvedic frame is not that Indra has to re-kill Vṛtra each year for the cosmos to continue, even though the _áhann áhim_ formula is recurrently sung — the formula's recurrence is poetic-liturgical rather than cosmologically constitutive in the Egyptian sense.footnote:[Per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the sovereignty-subtype examples and on the structural distinction between commemorative recitation and constitutive re-enactment; Assmann 2001, on the contrastingly constitutive character of the Egyptian daily combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (constitutive-vs-commemorative)

The selection-criterion that the chapter applies is therefore tightly drawn: the cyclical-maintenance subtype requires (1) explicit on-tradition framing of the combat as recurring on a fixed schedule, (2) explicit on-tradition framing that the cosmos's continuance is contingent on the recurrence, and (3) preserved liturgical material that re-enacts the combat as a ritual act rather than commemorates it as a sealed event — a triple criterion that Egypt satisfies on all three counts (Bremner-Rhind, daily; Assmannian doctrine; effigy-destruction rite), the Hittite case satisfies on all three (CTH 321, annual; _purulli_ prosperity-and-cosmic-order framing; festival recitation as verbal score), and most other traditions miss on at least one.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the three-feature structural pattern of the Egyptian case; Hoffner 1998 introduction to "Illuyanka" and Beckman 1982 *JANES* 14: 11–25 on the three-feature pattern of the Hittite case; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure section V on the selection-criterion as load-bearing for the chapter's case-selection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (three-feature pattern)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction (three-feature pattern)

The Apollo-Python _Septerion_ satisfies the criterion in attenuated form: the _Septerion_ is a re-enactment rather than a recitation, and its eight-yearly periodicity is fixed and explicit, but the cosmos's continuance is not framed in surviving Delphic sources as contingent on the _Septerion_'s performance — and it is the absence of feature (2) that makes the _Septerion_ a minor cult-foundation reflex rather than a third strong cyclical-maintenance attestation.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3, on the *Septerion*'s re-enactment character and eight-yearly periodicity but on the absence of cosmos-continuance-contingency framing in the Delphic corpus; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml on the *Septerion*'s minor-reflex status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The thinness of the strong-case evidence — two strong cases and one minor reflex across the entire ancient-Mediterranean-and-Near-Eastern corpus that the book treats — is itself a finding rather than a problem: the cyclical-maintenance subtype is rare, and its rarity is consistent with the structural-typological reading on which the chapter rests, which is that combat-as-recurring-ritual is a distinct configuration that requires a particular kind of liturgical infrastructure (a daily or annual cult of the threatened cosmos) and a particular kind of theological doctrine (cosmos-as-contingent-on-recurrence) that not many ancient traditions developed in concert.footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml comparative_argument on the cyclical-maintenance subtype as combat-as-ritual-preservation requiring specific liturgical-and-theological infrastructure; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the integrated theological-and-ritual character of the Egyptian case as the most articulate native articulation of the configuration.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and ritual

// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: C1. Combat as cosmogony (Ch34) — the Mesopotamian akītu's recitation-of-once-and-done-cosmogony framing, contrasted in §V with the Egyptian and Hittite re-enactment-of-recurring-combat framings, is treated at length in C1 as the akītu liturgical context of the Marduk-Tiamat cosmogony; the C1 reading of the akītu as recitation-of-cosmogony is the contrast on which the present chapter's selection-criterion rests.
// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: C4. Eschatological displacement (Ch37) — the Aži Dahāka eschatological-closure structure, contrasted in §V with the never-closed structure of the cyclical-maintenance subtype, is treated at length in C4 alongside Thor-Jörmungandr at Ragnarök and the Revelation 12 / 20 dragon material; the structural contrast (perpetual-vs-final combat) is the principal axis on which the cyclical-maintenance and eschatological subtypes are distinguished, and the contrast is structural rather than adversarial.

=== VI. The conceptual apparatus — _maʿat_ / _isfet_

The sixth body section develops the Egyptian _maʿat_ / _isfet_ framework as the chapter's conceptual through-line, articulates its bearing on the Ballentine terminological critique, and uses it to develop the structural-rather-than-adversarial contrast between the cyclical-maintenance and eschatological-displacement subtypes.

The brief makes Assmann's analysis of the Egyptian _maʿat_ / _isfet_ opposition the chapter's conceptual through-line on the grounds that the Egyptian framework is the book's clearest native concept-pair for combat-as-maintenance and is useful for articulating what "chaos" is _not_ doing in the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure section VI on Assmann's *maʿat* / *isfet* analysis as the chapter's conceptual through-line; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the *maʿat* / *isfet* opposition as the central cosmological-ethical category-pair of Egyptian religion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

_maʿat_ — written with the _ʿayin_ per the locked glossary — designates "truth, order, justice" simultaneously: the Egyptian term names neither abstract metaphysical order alone nor ethical justice alone but a single integrated condition (cosmic order, social justice, ritual correctness, and truth-of-speech as inseparable aspects of one underlying state).footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on *maʿat* as the integrated truth-order-justice category; per glossary.yaml on the locked rendering of *maʿat* with the *ʿayin*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat

_isfet_ — its antithesis — designates "disorder, injustice, chaos" with the same simultaneity: it covers cosmic disorder, social injustice, ritual transgression, and falsehood-of-speech as inseparable aspects of a single condition, and it is the condition that Apep personifies in the Egyptian theological imagination.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on *isfet* as the integrated disorder-injustice-falsehood category; per glossary.yaml on the locked rendering of *isfet*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on isfet

The opposition is structurally asymmetric rather than dualistic: _maʿat_ is the positive condition Ra's daily journey preserves, _isfet_ is its privation or violation that Apep's nightly assault threatens to impose, and the two are not co-equal cosmic principles but order and its violation — a structure that grounds the cyclical-maintenance subtype in a way that a symmetric good-vs-evil dualism could not.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the asymmetric structure of the *maʿat* / *isfet* opposition; te Velde 1967, *Seth, God of Confusion*, ch. 3, on the same asymmetry as it underlies the Apep combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

This asymmetry brings the _maʿat_ / _isfet_ framework into substantive contact with the Ballentine 2015 critique of the "chaos" half of the inherited Chaoskampf label: Apep is _isfet_ — disorder, injustice, violation of order — not "chaos" in the inchoate-primordial-Western-philosophical sense, and the Egyptian framework supplies an articulate native-tradition vocabulary for the moral-political character that Ballentine argues the inherited "chaos" terminology obscures.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, on the inadequacy of "chaos" as a category-term for the combat-myth adversaries; Assmann 2001, on *isfet* as a moral-political rather than metaphysically-inchoate category that articulates the alternative more clearly than any other native-tradition concept-pair the book engages.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=passim (chaos-category critique)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on isfet (moral-political, not inchoate-primordial)

The frame is both ontological and ethical-political: _maʿat_ names how the cosmos is and how human society and pharaonic rule must be, and the Apep combat is the cosmological articulation of an integrated theological-ethical-political project rather than an isolated mythological narrative — Pharaonic legitimacy as guarantor of _maʿat_ on earth is structurally identical with Ra's daily _maʿat_-preservation in the cosmos, and the human-political analogue and the divine combat are theologised as one combined undertaking.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the integrated character of the *maʿat* / *isfet* framework as ontological-ethical-political and on the structural identity between pharaonic-political *maʿat*-maintenance and Ra's cosmological *maʿat*-preservation; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same alignment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat (ontological-ethical-political)

For the comparative typology, the _maʿat_ / _isfet_ framework supplies three load-bearing analytical resources: a vocabulary for the order-and-its-violation structure that the cyclical-maintenance combat presupposes, a doctrine of recurrence-as-constitutive that articulates why the combat must be re-fought, and an integrated theological-and-political register in which the cosmological and human-political dimensions of the combat are not separable — the three resources jointly making the Egyptian case the chapter's analytical centre of gravity even though the Hittite case is co-equal in evidentiary weight.footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the three-resource analytical role of the *maʿat* / *isfet* framework as vocabulary, doctrine, and register; per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml structure section VI on the framework as analytical centre of gravity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

The Hittite case offers no native concept-pair of comparable articulacy: the _purulli_ framing supplies the ritual occasion and the cosmic-order purpose, but the Hittite tablets do not develop a theological vocabulary in which the storm-god's combat with Illuyanka is articulated as the maintenance of a named ontological-ethical condition in the way Assmann reads the Egyptian material as articulating Ra's combat as the maintenance of _maʿat_ against _isfet_; the reasonable inference is that the _purulli_ recitation operates within an unarticulated assumption-set that the Egyptian corpus instead made explicit.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, on the *purulli* framing of the Illuyanka recitation without elaborated conceptual-theological apparatus; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the same; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the by-contrast articulate Egyptian theological apparatus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction (no elaborated theological apparatus)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet (contrast)

[INFERENCE: the Egyptian *maʿat* / *isfet* framework can be used as a typological analytic for the Hittite case without violating scope.md's no-cross-tradition-reconstruction policy, because it is being used as a *comparative-analytical* category rather than as a content-fill for a Hittite lacuna | basis: scope.md §"Reconstruction policy" prohibits cross-tradition narrative-content reconstruction (a gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*), but the *maʿat* / *isfet* framework is here being used as a comparativist's analytical vocabulary rather than as content-fill for any Hittite gap; the *purulli*-and-Illuyanka material is not being narratively supplemented from the Egyptian corpus, only typologically described using a vocabulary that the Egyptian corpus articulates more explicitly | risk: the use of an Egyptian-articulated framework as comparative analytic for a Hittite case still carries the standard risk of imposing one tradition's conceptual apparatus on another, and the chapter is explicit that the Hittite tablets do not themselves articulate the framework even where (the chapter argues) their structural logic is consistent with it.]

The structural contrast with C4 (eschatological displacement) is best articulated in _maʿat_ / _isfet_ terms: in the cyclical-maintenance subtype the cosmos is held in being by the daily-or-annual containment of _isfet_ — Apep is rebuffed nightly, Illuyanka is recurrently slain — and the containment is interminable; in the eschatological subtype the cosmos's _isfet_-content (using the Egyptian framework as comparative analytic, not as native vocabulary for the eschatological traditions, which articulate the same structure in their own terms) accumulates toward a final reckoning, and the closure-combat at the end of cosmic time is the moment at which the _isfet_ is finally and fully extinguished, after which the cosmos either ends (Ragnarök) or is renewed in a perfected condition (Frashokereti).footnote:[Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the interminable-containment structure of the cyclical-maintenance subtype; per Yarbro Collins 1976 and the eschatological-closure framing to be developed in C4 (Ch37).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (interminable-containment)

The contrast is structural rather than adversarial: the eschatological subtype is not a "better" or "worse" handling of the same cosmological problem than the cyclical-maintenance subtype, but a different configuration of cosmic time — Egypt has no eschatology and the combat is perpetual; Revelation has an eschatology and the combat is final — and the two configurations are alternative answers to the same underlying question about whether cosmic time is bounded or unbounded, and the present chapter does not adjudicate between them.footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml special_instructions on the structural-rather-than-adversarial contrast between cyclical-maintenance and eschatological subtypes; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on Egypt's lack of eschatology as a positive-doctrinal feature rather than a deficit.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (perpetual-not-final combat)

The chapter closes on the position that the cyclical-maintenance subtype, though evidentiarily thin (two strong cases, one minor reflex), is structurally robust: its features (explicit liturgical embedding, never-concluded combat, contingency-of-cosmos doctrine) are jointly satisfied only by the Egyptian and Hittite cases in the book's catalogue, and the _maʿat_ / _isfet_ framework articulates these features more clearly than any cross-tradition vocabulary the book can apply — a settled position that supports the four-way typological scheme of scope.md while registering that C3 is the most evidence-thin of its four chapters, and the thinness is part of what the chapter has to say about the cosmos-as-recurringly-fragile theology that the subtype articulates.footnote:[Per brief 36-C3-cyclical-maintenance.yaml comparative_argument and special_instructions on the chapter's settled position; Assmann 2001, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt*, on the Egyptian articulation of cosmos-as-recurringly-fragile as the subtype's clearest native expression; per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the four-way typological scheme.]
