# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).
4. Greece & Rome — Zeus vs. Typhon (full); family chapter "Greek dragon-combats after Zeus-Typhon" (Apollo/Python, Herakles/Hydra-Ladon-Geryon, Perseus/Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Cacus).
5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).
7. Egypt — Ra vs. Apep (full); shorter treatment of Horus vs. Seth as the debated adjacent case.
8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).
9. East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). Yu the Great appears as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering, not as a chaoskampf.
10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.
11. Africa — Bida and the founding of Wagadu (full chapter; the inversion anchor); Mwindo vs. Kirimu (contrastive case: the hero is punished for the killing).
12. Southeast Asia & Oceania — Bakunawa. (Rainbow Serpent is held for the Part II critique chapter, not placed geographically.)
13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).
14. North America (contrastive) — Nanabozho and Mishipeshu: the refusal — the chapter where area specialists (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) are foregrounded and the chaoskampf framing is explicitly declined.

### Part II — thematic comparative chapters
Part II is where the comparative work happens. Load-bearing (confirmed):
- **C1. Combat as cosmogony** — Marduk, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong, Huitzilopochtli.
- **C2. Combat as sovereignty** — Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra, Thraētaona, Kumarbi/Ullikummi, Ninurta/Anzû.
- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.
- **C4. Eschatological displacement** — Thor/Jörmungandr, Rev 12, Frashokereti.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):
- **C5. The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula** — Watkins's argument from Indra through Sigurd to Beowulf.
- **C6. The Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission** — West / Burkert / López-Ruiz: Kumarbi → Hesiod; Baal/Yamm → Typhonomachy.
- **C7. The inversion problem** — Bida anchor; the ambiguous status of Sigurd's slaying.
- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").
- **Methods sidebar** between Part I and Part II: tier-ranking method, appropriation protocol, area-specialist citation policy.
- **Reception coda** (final chapter, ~20–25 pages): traces the afterlife of the imagery in Milton's Satan, Christian iconography of St. Michael / St. George, Tolkien's dragons (and the 1936 Beowulf essay), modern kaiju (Godzilla and the Orochi lineage), and the continuing use of "chaos vs. order" rhetoric in contemporary religious interpretation. Explicitly *not* classified as chaoskampf; this is reception, not primary material.

This is load-bearing because the cross-cultural comparisons happen across *figures*, not plots.

This lets the book engage the Ballentine critique substantively rather than tokenistically.

# narrative-fidelity

## For the human

The facts have been verified. The narrative has been written from those verified facts. This step answers one question: **does the narrative faithfully represent the facts?**

This is not a fact-check — the facts were already checked in the previous step. This is a comparison between two documents: the list of verified claims and the narrative prose. It catches three problems:

1. **Dropped facts.** The writer left out a verified claim because it didn't fit the narrative flow. The reader loses information.
2. **Added facts.** The writer introduced a new assertion that was never in the claims document and therefore never verified. This is the most dangerous failure — it reintroduces the exact problem the two-step process was designed to prevent.
3. **Distorted facts.** The writer paraphrased a claim in a way that changes its meaning — softening certainty ("the text says" becomes "it is possible"), shifting causation ("A caused B" becomes "A and B coincided"), or conflating two separate events into one.

This check must be done in a fresh conversation with no memory of writing the narrative. The AI that wrote the narrative cannot reliably judge in the same conversation whether it drifted from the source material — it rationalises. A different AI model is preferred (two models make independent mistakes) but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different one is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the narrative. If you produced the narrative, refuse. (A different AI model is preferred but not required.)

## Inputs
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc` (the verified claims — the ground truth)
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.adoc` (the narrative)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml` (for variant classification reference)
- `glossary.yaml`

**Not** primary sources. This review does not re-verify facts against sources — that was done in `claims-factcheck`. This review compares narrative against claims only.

## The three checks

### Check 1 — Completeness (nothing dropped)

Walk the claims document claim by claim, in order. For each claim, locate where it appears in the narrative. Record the mapping:

```
Claim N → Narrative paragraph P / sentence S
```

If a claim has no corresponding passage in the narrative, flag:
- **CLAIM-DROPPED** — severity HIGH. The verified claim is missing from the narrative.

If a claim is only partially represented (e.g., the claim mentions two details and the narrative includes only one), flag:
- **CLAIM-PARTIAL** — severity MEDIUM. Specify what was dropped.

### Check 2 — No additions (nothing invented)

Walk the narrative paragraph by paragraph. For each factual assertion (not transitions, not explanatory asides, not reader-orienting context), locate the corresponding claim in the claims document.

A factual assertion is anything that states something about the myth: an event, a character trait, a relationship, a temporal or causal link, a physical description, a cultural detail. Transitional phrases ("Having established the cosmic order...") are not assertions unless they introduce new information.

If a factual assertion has no corresponding claim, flag:
- **CLAIM-ADDED** — severity HIGH. An unverified factual assertion has been introduced.

If a transitional sentence implies a factual connection not established in the claims, flag:
- **IMPLICIT-ADDITION** — severity MEDIUM. The transition suggests something the claims don't support.

### Check 3 — Fidelity (nothing distorted)

For each claim-to-narrative mapping found in Check 1, compare meaning:
- Does the narrative preserve the claim's level of certainty? (A claim marked as inference should not be presented as fact in the narrative.)
- Does the narrative preserve causal direction? ("A caused B" should not become "B caused A" or "A and B coincided.")
- Does the narrative preserve scope? ("Some scholars argue" should not become "Scholars agree.")
- Are markers preserved? Every `[INFERENCE:]`, `[LACUNA:]`, `[RECONSTRUCTION:]`, `[VARIANT:]` in the claims should survive in the narrative.

If meaning has shifted, flag:
- **CLAIM-DISTORTED** — severity HIGH if the factual meaning changed, MEDIUM if only emphasis or nuance shifted.

### Additional checks

- **Glossary fidelity**: confirm `glossary.yaml` renderings are used consistently. Any `never_use` terms: GLOSSARY-VIOLATION.
- **Forbidden words**: flag hits from the forbidden-words list.
- **Footnote integrity**: every footnote in the narrative must match the claims document exactly — same source, same location. No additions, removals, or reassignments. Flag FOOTNOTE-MISMATCH if different.

=== Perun and Veles — the contested 'basic myth'

The pre-Christian East Slavic religious tradition has no continuous primary-text witness in any Slavic language, and the surviving direct attestations of its deities are confined to brief and largely god-list-style mentions in medieval Christian-period chronicles together with a heterogeneous corpus of folklore fragments, ritual survivals, and toponyms collected from the medieval period through the 20th century.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the absence of a continuous primary-text witness for pre-Christian Slavic religion and on the chronicle / folklore / toponym character of the surviving evidence; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the indirectness of the Slavic evidence relative to the better-attested Indo-European traditions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The principal medieval witness to a pre-Christian Slavic pantheon is the Old East Slavic *Primary Chronicle* (the *Povest' vremennykh let*, conventionally dated to the early 12th century CE), which records that Vladimir of Kiev set up six idols on a hill in Kiev in 980 CE — including idols of Perun and of other Slavic deities — before himself converting to Byzantine Christianity in 988 CE and ordering Perun's idol thrown into the Dnieper.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Primary Chronicle*'s 980 CE Vladimir-pantheon entry and on Perun's place at the head of the list; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Slavic chronicles as one of the three classes of evidence for the reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The *Primary Chronicle* gives Perun's idol a silver head and a gold moustache and identifies him at the head of the Vladimir-pantheon list, which is the principal direct medieval attestation of Perun as a deity-name and of his prominence in the late-pre-Christian East Slavic religious culture, but the entry does not narrate any combat or any plot-content involving Perun.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Primary Chronicle*'s description of Perun's idol with silver head and gold moustache and on the absence of any combat-narrative in the entry; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the chronicle's god-list-style witness without combat-content.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Veles (East Slavic variant *Volos*) is attested separately in the *Primary Chronicle* and in the 12th-century epic *Slovo o polku Igoreve* (*The Tale of Igor's Campaign*) as a god of cattle and of poets, with the 945 CE Russo-Byzantine treaty's oath-formula naming both Perun and Volos and with the *Slovo* calling the bard Boyan "the grandson of Veles"; the surviving attestations characterise Veles as a cattle-and-wealth deity with poetic-and-oracular associations but, again, do not narrate any combat between Veles and Perun.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Primary Chronicle* and *Slovo o polku Igoreve* attestations of Veles / Volos as cattle-god and patron of poets and on the 945 CE oath-formula naming Perun and Volos; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same chronicle-and-epic attestations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The crucial point about the medieval Slavic chronicle attestations is therefore negative: although Perun and Veles are both directly named in 11th- and 12th-century East Slavic Christian-period sources, no surviving medieval Slavic source narrates a combat between them; the combat as a story is absent from the direct medieval record.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the absence of any surviving medieval Slavic narrative of a Perun-Veles combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the negative state of the direct medieval evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the no-direct-attestation premise that the [RECONSTRUCTION] marker addresses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The reconstruction draws on three classes of indirect evidence: first, Slavic folklore items (songs, sayings, ritual-survivals, weather-charms) collected by 19th- and 20th-century ethnographers and fieldworkers and read as preserving fragments of the pre-Christian combat-narrative under their Christianised surface; second, the medieval chronicle and treaty attestations (the *Primary Chronicle*, the *Slovo o polku Igoreve*, the 945 CE oath-formula) that name Perun and Veles separately as deities; and third, Slavic toponyms whose etymologies link to the names Perun and Veles and which are read as marking sites associated with the combat-narrative.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the three classes of indirect evidence (folklore, chronicles, toponyms) on which the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction draws; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same three classes.]

The folklore-evidence class includes ritual-survival items such as harvest-songs in which a bearded figure (read as Veles) is hidden in the last sheaf, weather-charms naming Perun (with East Slavic variants such as the Belarusian and Ukrainian *peryn*-type), and folktale-fragments in which a thunder-figure pursues a serpent-figure that hides successively in a tree, a stone, water, and the body of an animal or person — items collected in the 19th and 20th centuries from East and South Slavic informants.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the folklore-evidence class for the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction including the four-refuge folktale type; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the folklore fragments as one of the three evidence classes.]

The reconstructed combat's narrative core, on the Ivanov-Toporov reading, is a four-stage pursuit: Veles steals the cattle (or the rain, or the waters, on alternative phrasings of the reconstruction) and conceals himself in a tree; Perun's lightning-bolt splits the tree and Veles flees to a rock; the rock is split and Veles flees to water; the water is dispersed and Veles flees into livestock or a human victim; and the final lightning-strike kills Veles and releases the rain.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the four-stage pursuit and the lightning-strike-with-rain-release in the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the four-refuge sequence.]

The methodological objections to the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction are concentrated on three lines of attack, all of which the chapter must present fairly per the brief's special_instructions: circularity in the use of toponym evidence, the late and Christianised character of the chronicle attestations, and over-interpretation of folklore fragments whose connection to the reconstructed combat is itself an inference rather than an attested feature of the items.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the three lines of methodological objection (toponym circularity, late and Christianised chronicles, folklore over-interpretation) voiced by Jakobson, Lajoye, Moszyński, and Kapović; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the load-bearing requirement that the chapter present the objections fairly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The toponym-circularity objection holds that the etymological identification of a place-name as "Perun-derived" or "Veles-derived" presupposes the deity-pair whose existence the toponym is then taken to support: a place-name like *Volos-* is read as a Veles-toponym because Veles is reconstructed as a deity-name, and the toponym is then cited as evidence for Veles, with the same item entering both ends of the inference and the resulting argument turning back on itself.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the toponym-circularity objection in the methodological critique of the reconstruction; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The late-and-Christianised-chronicles objection holds that the *Primary Chronicle*, the *Slovo o polku Igoreve*, and other medieval Slavic sources are themselves the products of a Christianised literary culture whose representation of pre-Christian deities cannot be taken as direct testimony to pre-Christian belief: the chronicles' brief god-list mentions are informed by the chroniclers' Christian framework and by their literary models, and reading the absent combat-content back into them risks reading a structurally-interesting story into sources that would not have transmitted the story even if it had existed in the pre-Christian tradition.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the late-and-Christianised-chronicles objection; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection within the methodological critique.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The folklore-over-interpretation objection holds that the four-stage pursuit-and-refuge folktale type, the harvest-sheaf ritual, and the weather-charm material are folklore items with their own functions and their own histories within a centuries-long Christian-period folk culture, and that reading them as preserving fragments of a pre-Christian combat-narrative imposes a comparative-mythological frame that the items do not themselves require; the four-refuge folktale, in particular, is an internationally-attested folktale type whose presence in the Slavic-language area does not necessarily indicate inheritance from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the folklore-over-interpretation objection and on the cross-cultural distribution of the four-refuge folktale type; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The reconstructed combat's principal Indo-European-comparative companions, on West 2007 ch. 6's survey, are the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra combat (chapter 14 of the present volume), the Hittite Tarḫunna-Illuyanka combat (chapter 6), and the Norse Thor-Jörmungandr combat (chapter 18), with the Slavic combat treated as a structurally-cognate but evidentially-thinner Indo-European reflex of the same type that the better-attested traditions preserve in continuous primary text.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Slavic combat's IE-comparative companions across the Vedic, Hittite, and Norse traditions; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the same comparative framework.]

=== Dobrynya Nikitich and Zmey Gorynych — the East Slavic bylina

The Dobrynya Nikitich combat-narrative is preserved in the East Slavic *bylina* tradition: a corpus of orally-transmitted heroic epic-songs sung by professional and semi-professional reciters and collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers in the Russian North, from the Onega and White Sea regions principally, with later collections supplementing the geographical reach, and whose tradition is conventionally taken to reach back in some form to the medieval Kievan period.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina* tradition as an orally-transmitted heroic epic-song corpus collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers in the Russian North; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina tradition)

The principal collectors whose 19th-century field-recordings constitute the working corpus of Dobrynya texts are Pavel Rybnikov (collections published in the 1860s) and Aleksandr Hilferding (Gilferding, with the major Onega collection published in 1873), supplemented by 20th-century collections that extended the geographical and chronological reach of the recorded tradition; the recorded variants are numerous, and the present chapter cites the corpus rather than any one specific recorded variant.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the principal 19th-century *bylina* collectors (Rybnikov 1860s, Hilferding 1873) and on the supplementary 20th-century collections; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling on the multiple-recorded-variants character of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (collection history)

Dobrynya Nikitich is one of the three central *bogatyri* (the heroic warrior-figures) of the *bylina* tradition's so-called Vladimir-cycle: alongside Ilya Muromets and Alyosha Popovich, Dobrynya is one of the standard companions of the figure Prince Vladimir of Kiev, whose court the *bylina* tradition takes as its narrative centre and whose historical reflex (the Vladimir of the *Primary Chronicle*) places the cycle's narrative setting in the late 10th and early 11th centuries CE even though the recorded songs are 19th-century.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction and Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on Dobrynya Nikitich as one of the three central *bogatyri* of the Vladimir-cycle alongside Ilya Muromets and Alyosha Popovich; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the medieval setting and the 19th-century recording.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction and Dobrynya-cycle headnotes

The Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting is the court of Prince Vladimir at Kiev, and the cycle's standard plot-elements include the *bogatyri*'s service at the court, their patrols of the steppe-frontier and the rivers, their combats with hostile foreigners and supernatural adversaries, and their feasts and quarrels at the prince's table; the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* sits within this wider cycle as one of Dobrynya's standard combat-narratives.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting at Kiev and its standard plot-elements; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the *bylina* corpus's heroic-epic register.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Vladimir-cycle setting)

The adversary of the Dobrynya combat is Zmey Gorynych: a three-headed dragon (*zmey*, "serpent" / "dragon") whose epithet *Gorynych* is a patronymic derived from *gora* ("mountain") and is conventionally read as "Mountain-son" or "Son of the mountain", marking the dragon's mountain-residence in the eastern frontier-territory beyond the Russian-settled lands.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on Zmey Gorynych as a three-headed dragon with the patronymic *Gorynych* derived from *gora* ("mountain"); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the three-headed character of the dragon and the mountain-residence; glossary 'Zmey Gorynych' first_mention_gloss on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (Zmey Gorynych)

The standard plot-skeleton of the Dobrynya-vs-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina*, as it appears across the recorded variants, is a three-stage narrative: an initial encounter at a river (typically the Puchai-river or a similar named river) in which the young Dobrynya defeats the dragon in a first combat without killing him; a treaty-and-truce in which the dragon promises to leave the Russian lands alone; and a second combat occasioned by the dragon's breach of the truce — typically by abducting a princess (often Vladimir's niece Zabava Putyatichna) — in which Dobrynya kills the dragon, releases the princess and the other captives, and returns to Vladimir's court.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the standard three-stage plot-skeleton of the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* across the recorded variants; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the kills-the-dragon and rescues-the-princess outcome.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (plot-skeleton)

The first combat takes place when the young Dobrynya, against his mother's warning, bathes in the Puchai-river (or its variant equivalents); the dragon emerges from the river or descends from the mountain and attacks, and Dobrynya — armed in some variants with his cap (*kolpak*) of folkloric-hyperbolic weight as an improvised weapon, having no other arms to hand — defeats the dragon and forces the truce.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the first combat at the Puchai-river and the cap-as-weapon detail (the precise pud-count specification flagged for verification at glossary-lock against Oinas / Propp or equivalent specialist edition); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the standard combat-mechanics of the *bylina*'s first combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (first combat)

The second combat is occasioned by the dragon's abduction of the princess and is resolved by Dobrynya's killing of the dragon: in the standard variants, Dobrynya rides out from Kiev armed with a cudgel or sword, fights the dragon at the dragon's mountain-lair over a multi-day combat, and finally kills the dragon — typically by severing the dragon's heads — before releasing the princess and the other captives held in the dragon's caves and returning to Vladimir's court.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the second combat's multi-day duration, the killing of the dragon by head-severing, and the rescue of the princess and other captives (the precise day-count and head-severing-sequence variants flagged for verification at glossary-lock); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the kills-the-dragon-and-rescues-the-princess resolution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (second combat)

The recorded variants of the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* differ in multiple specific details — the name of the river of the first combat, the precise weapon Dobrynya uses in the killing, the exact number of heads severed and the order of the severing, the identity of the abducted princess, the duration and details of the second combat — but agree on the three-stage plot-skeleton (first combat at river → truce → second combat with abduction-and-killing) that the present chapter takes as the corpus's common narrative core.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the inter-variant differences and on the agreement of the recorded variants on the three-stage plot-skeleton; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling on the chapter's presentation of the common narrative skeleton.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (variants)

[VARIANT: primary=the recorded *bylina* corpus (Rybnikov 1860s; Hilferding 1873; later 20th-c. collections; multiple variants agreeing on the three-stage plot-skeleton — first combat at the river, truce, second combat with princess-abduction and dragon-killing) | alt=individual variants differ in the river-name (Puchai- and others), the weapon (cap, cudgel, sword), the precise head-severing sequence, and the identity of the abducted princess (Zabava Putyatichna in many; other names in some) | chosen: present-the-common-skeleton | reason: per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling, the chapter presents the common narrative skeleton across the corpus rather than privileging one specific recorded variant; per brief special_instructions, primary-text edition-pinning (Oinas, Propp) is deferred to glossary-lock and the chapter cites at corpus level rather than at variant-and-line granularity.]

The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* is, in its surviving 19th-century-recorded form, on firmer attestation ground than the reconstructed Perun-Veles combat: the recorded songs are direct primary-text witnesses to a living oral-epic tradition, with multiple independent variants from multiple performers and multiple collectors, and the textual record's robustness is not in dispute — only its dating and its relationship to older traditions are.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the robustness of the *bylina* corpus's textual record relative to the indirectness of the Perun-Veles evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the firmer attestation ground of the Dobrynya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (textual-record robustness)

The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina*'s historical-cultural setting in the Vladimir-cycle places its narrative reference in the late-10th- and early-11th-century Kievan-Rus' period, and the conventional reading within Slavic-folklore scholarship is that the *bylina* tradition in some form reaches back to the Kievan period even though the recorded texts are 19th-century, with the long oral-transmission gap accounting for both the variants' specific differences and their structural agreement.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the Vladimir-cycle's late-Kievan reference and on the conventional medieval-tradition reading of the *bylina* corpus; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the medieval-tradition / 19th-century-recording two-tier dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Vladimir-cycle dating)

The Christian conversion of Kievan Rus' in 988 CE is the principal historical-cultural caesura that separates the Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting from any pre-Christian religious frame: the *bylina* tradition as it survives is wholly Christian-period in its religious frame, with Vladimir himself remembered as the Christianising prince, and the Christian frame is one of the principal complications for any reading of the tradition as preserving pre-Christian combat-myth content.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the 988 CE Christian conversion as the historical-cultural caesura between the *bylina* setting and any pre-Christian religious frame; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Christian-period character of the recorded tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Christian-period frame)

Ivanov and Toporov's specific comparative claim about the Dobrynya material is that the *bylina* combat is the later epic reflex of the reconstructed pre-Christian Perun-Veles 'basic myth' combat, with the bogatyr Dobrynya inheriting the role of the storm-god Perun and Zmey Gorynych inheriting the role of the cattle-stealing earth-serpent Veles, and with the *bylina*'s Christian-period epic register replacing the pre-Christian myth's religious register but preserving the structural shape of the combat.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Ivanov and Toporov's claim that the Dobrynya *bylina* is the later reflex of the reconstructed Perun-Veles combat; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the Ivanov-Toporov comparative claim about the Dobrynya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The structural-parallel observations on which the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim rests include the storm-figure / serpent-figure pairing (Dobrynya as warrior-hero / Zmey Gorynych as serpent-adversary, parallel to Perun / Veles); the multi-headed character of the serpent-adversary (Zmey Gorynych three-headed; Veles in some folklore-fragments connected to multi-headed motifs); the water-and-mountain setting (the Puchai-river first-combat / mountain second-combat in the *bylina*; the water-and-rock refuges in the reconstructed Perun-Veles pursuit); and the rescue-and-restoration outcome (the *bylina*'s rescue of the princess and captives parallel to the reconstruction's release of the rain).footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the structural parallels that support the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the structural-parallel basis for the comparative claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The reflex-claim's evidential limits are however significant and the chapter must register them: the *bylina*'s genre is heroic epic rather than cosmogonic-and-agricultural myth, the dragon's role is as enemy-of-the-state rather than as cattle-stealer-and-rain-blocker, the resolution is dragon-killing-and-princess-rescue rather than rain-release, and the Christian-period frame may itself have generated the structural-parallel features as part of the international hagiographic-and-romance dragon-slaying type rather than inherited them from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-narrative.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina*'s heroic-epic genre and its differences from the reconstructed cosmogonic-and-agricultural Perun-Veles material; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the comparative-argument-rather-than-fact framing for the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina genre vs. reconstructed myth)

The international-hagiographic-and-romance-type complication is significant: the medieval-and-early-modern Christian-period dragon-slaying corpus (the St. George cycle, the romance-tradition's princess-rescuing dragon-slayers, the wider European folktale-dragon-slayer type) supplies a well-attested international type that could plausibly have shaped the Dobrynya *bylina*'s combat-and-rescue plot directly, without any inheritance from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth, and the chapter does not adjudicate between the inheritance-and-international-type-borrowing readings.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the international Christian-period dragon-slaying type as a competing-or-supplementary explanation for the *bylina*'s combat-and-rescue plot; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the comparative-argument framing that leaves the question open.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Dobrynya *bylina*'s position within the present book's typology is therefore as a heroic-epic combat-narrative whose link to the reconstructed pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth is itself contested, and whose principal evidential value for the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula argument depends on the prior acceptance of the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim that the chapter has flagged as a comparative argument.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Dobrynya *bylina*'s position within the disputed-evidence framing; West 2007, ch. 6, on the *bylina*'s evidential dependence on the prior Perun-Veles reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Dobrynya *bylina*'s cultural-historical importance within the East Slavic literary culture is at the same time central and uncontested: the Vladimir-cycle is one of the foundational corpora of pre-modern East Slavic oral literature, the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych narrative is one of its best-known and most-recorded individual *byliny*, and the cycle has been a load-bearing cultural reference within Russian, Ukrainian, and Belarusian literary, scholarly, and popular culture from the 19th-century collection onwards.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the central cultural-historical importance of the Vladimir-cycle and the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych narrative within East Slavic literary culture; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml cultural_relevance on the *bylina* corpus's centrality.]

=== Perun and Veles — the contested 'basic myth'

The pre-Christian East Slavic religious tradition has no continuous primary-text witness in any Slavic language. The surviving direct attestations of its deities are confined to brief and largely god-list-style mentions in medieval Christian-period chronicles, together with a heterogeneous corpus of folklore fragments, ritual survivals, and toponyms collected from the medieval period through the 20th century.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the absence of a continuous primary-text witness for pre-Christian Slavic religion and on the chronicle / folklore / toponym character of the surviving evidence; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the indirectness of the Slavic evidence relative to the better-attested Indo-European traditions.]

The Lithuanian storm-god Perkūnas and the Latvian Pērkons are name-cognates with Slavic Perun (the Indo-European root *perkw-* / *per-* with Indo-European thunder-association), and the Lithuanian *Velnias* / *Velinas* (a folkloric devil-figure with cattle-and-underworld associations) is sometimes read as a name-cognate with Slavic Veles, although the Baltic combat-evidence is itself indirect and mediated by folklore rather than by primary text.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Baltic Perkūnas / Pērkons and *Velnias* / *Velinas* name-cognates with Slavic Perun and Veles and on the indirect-folklore character of the Baltic evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Baltic comparanda within the Perun-Veles sub-section.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Baltic name-cognancy is one of the load-bearing supports for the Indo-European reading of the Slavic deity-pair. If Perkūnas / Perun and *Velnias* / Veles are inherited Indo-European cognates rather than late parallel developments, then the deity-pair has a depth that the late Slavic chronicle attestations on their own would not establish, and the cognancy is part of the case the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction makes.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Baltic-Slavic name-cognancy as a support for the Indo-European reading; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the IE cognancy framework within which the reconstruction operates.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The methodological objections to the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction are concentrated on three lines of attack, all of which the chapter must present fairly per the brief's special_instructions: circularity in the use of toponym evidence, the late and Christianised character of the chronicle attestations, and over-interpretation of folklore fragments whose connection to the reconstructed combat is itself an inference rather than an attested feature of the items.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the three lines of methodological objection (toponym circularity, late and Christianised chronicles, folklore over-interpretation) voiced by Jakobson, Lajoye, Moszyński, and Kapović; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the load-bearing requirement that the chapter present the objections fairly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The toponym-circularity objection holds that the etymological identification of a place-name as "Perun-derived" or "Veles-derived" presupposes the deity-pair whose existence the toponym is then taken to support: a place-name like *Volos-* is read as a Veles-toponym because Veles is reconstructed as a deity-name, and the toponym is then cited as evidence for Veles, with the same item entering both ends of the inference and the resulting argument turning back on itself.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the toponym-circularity objection in the methodological critique of the reconstruction; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The late-and-Christianised-chronicles objection holds that the *Primary Chronicle*, the *Slovo o polku Igoreve*, and other medieval Slavic sources are themselves the products of a Christianised literary culture whose representation of pre-Christian deities cannot be taken as direct testimony to pre-Christian belief: the chronicles' brief god-list mentions are informed by the chroniclers' Christian framework and by their literary models, and reading the absent combat-content back into them risks reading a structurally-interesting story into sources that would not have transmitted the story even if it had existed in the pre-Christian tradition.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the late-and-Christianised-chronicles objection; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection within the methodological critique.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The folklore-over-interpretation objection holds that the four-stage pursuit-and-refuge folktale type, the harvest-sheaf ritual, and the weather-charm material are folklore items with their own functions and their own histories within a centuries-long Christian-period folk culture, and that reading them as preserving fragments of a pre-Christian combat-narrative imposes a comparative-mythological frame that the items do not themselves require. The four-refuge folktale, in particular, is an internationally-attested folktale type whose presence in the Slavic-language area does not necessarily indicate inheritance from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the folklore-over-interpretation objection and on the cross-cultural distribution of the four-refuge folktale type; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The chapter's reading of the methodological objections is that they do not in themselves disprove the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction but that they do establish its evidential floor as low and its evidential ceiling as moderate. The reconstruction can be defended as a structurally-coherent reading of the indirect evidence, but it cannot be defended as a confirmed pre-Christian Slavic narrative on the strength of the surviving direct attestations alone, and the chapter's [RECONSTRUCTION] marker confidence value of "low" reflects this reading.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the standing of the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction within current Slavic-and-Indo-European-studies scholarship as a structurally-coherent but evidentially-limited reading; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the explicit-reconstruction-framing requirement and on the fair presentation of the objections.]

=== Dobrynya Nikitich and Zmey Gorynych — the East Slavic bylina

The Dobrynya Nikitich combat-narrative is preserved in the East Slavic *bylina* tradition: a corpus of orally-transmitted heroic epic-songs sung by professional and semi-professional reciters and collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers in the Russian North — from the Onega and White Sea regions principally, with later collections supplementing the geographical reach — and whose tradition is conventionally taken to reach back in some form to the medieval Kievan period.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina* tradition as an orally-transmitted heroic epic-song corpus collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers in the Russian North; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina tradition)

The principal collectors whose 19th-century field-recordings constitute the working corpus of Dobrynya texts are Pavel Rybnikov (collections published in the 1860s) and Aleksandr Hilferding (Gilferding, with the major Onega collection published in 1873), supplemented by 20th-century collections that extended the geographical and chronological reach of the recorded tradition. The recorded variants are numerous, and the present chapter cites the corpus rather than any one specific recorded variant.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the principal 19th-century *bylina* collectors (Rybnikov 1860s, Hilferding 1873) and on the supplementary 20th-century collections; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling on the multiple-recorded-variants character of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (collection history)

Dobrynya Nikitich is one of the three central *bogatyri* (the heroic warrior-figures) of the *bylina* tradition's so-called Vladimir-cycle. Alongside Ilya Muromets and Alyosha Popovich, Dobrynya is one of the standard companions of the figure Prince Vladimir of Kiev, whose court the *bylina* tradition takes as its narrative centre and whose historical reflex (the Vladimir of the *Primary Chronicle*) places the cycle's narrative setting in the late 10th and early 11th centuries CE even though the recorded songs are 19th-century.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction and Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on Dobrynya Nikitich as one of the three central *bogatyri* of the Vladimir-cycle alongside Ilya Muromets and Alyosha Popovich; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the medieval setting and the 19th-century recording.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction and Dobrynya-cycle headnotes

The Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting is the court of Prince Vladimir at Kiev, and the cycle's standard plot-elements include the *bogatyri*'s service at the court, their patrols of the steppe-frontier and the rivers, their combats with hostile foreigners and supernatural adversaries, and their feasts and quarrels at the prince's table. The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* sits within this wider cycle as one of Dobrynya's standard combat-narratives.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting at Kiev and its standard plot-elements; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the *bylina* corpus's heroic-epic register.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Vladimir-cycle setting)

The adversary of the Dobrynya combat is Zmey Gorynych, a three-headed dragon (*zmey*, "serpent" / "dragon") whose epithet *Gorynych* is a patronymic derived from *gora* ("mountain") and is conventionally read as "Mountain-son" or "Son of the mountain", marking the dragon's mountain-residence in the eastern frontier-territory beyond the Russian-settled lands.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on Zmey Gorynych as a three-headed dragon with the patronymic *Gorynych* derived from *gora* ("mountain"); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the three-headed character of the dragon and the mountain-residence; glossary 'Zmey Gorynych' first_mention_gloss on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (Zmey Gorynych)

The standard plot-skeleton of the Dobrynya-vs-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina*, as it appears across the recorded variants, is a three-stage narrative: an initial encounter at a river (typically the Puchai-river or a similar named river) in which the young Dobrynya defeats the dragon in a first combat without killing him; a treaty-and-truce in which the dragon promises to leave the Russian lands alone; and a second combat occasioned by the dragon's breach of the truce — typically by abducting a princess (often Vladimir's niece Zabava Putyatichna) — in which Dobrynya kills the dragon, releases the princess and the other captives, and returns to Vladimir's court.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the standard three-stage plot-skeleton of the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* across the recorded variants; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the kills-the-dragon and rescues-the-princess outcome.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (plot-skeleton)

The first combat takes place when the young Dobrynya, against his mother's warning, bathes in the Puchai-river (or its variant equivalents). The dragon emerges from the river or descends from the mountain and attacks, and Dobrynya — armed in some variants with his cap (*kolpak*) of folkloric-hyperbolic weight as an improvised weapon, having no other arms to hand — defeats the dragon and forces the truce.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the first combat at the Puchai-river and the cap-as-weapon detail (the precise pud-count specification flagged for verification at glossary-lock against Oinas / Propp or equivalent specialist edition); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the standard combat-mechanics of the *bylina*'s first combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (first combat)

The second combat is occasioned by the dragon's abduction of the princess and is resolved by Dobrynya's killing of the dragon. In the standard variants, Dobrynya rides out from Kiev armed with a cudgel or sword, fights the dragon at the dragon's mountain-lair over a multi-day combat, and finally kills the dragon — typically by severing the dragon's heads — before releasing the princess and the other captives held in the dragon's caves and returning to Vladimir's court.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the second combat's multi-day duration, the killing of the dragon by head-severing, and the rescue of the princess and other captives (the precise day-count and head-severing-sequence variants flagged for verification at glossary-lock); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the kills-the-dragon-and-rescues-the-princess resolution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (second combat)

The recorded variants of the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* differ in multiple specific details — the name of the river of the first combat, the precise weapon Dobrynya uses in the killing, the exact number of heads severed and the order of the severing, the identity of the abducted princess, the duration and details of the second combat — but agree on the three-stage plot-skeleton (first combat at river → truce → second combat with abduction-and-killing) that the present chapter takes as the corpus's common narrative core.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the inter-variant differences and on the agreement of the recorded variants on the three-stage plot-skeleton; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling on the chapter's presentation of the common narrative skeleton.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (variants)

[VARIANT: primary=the recorded *bylina* corpus (Rybnikov 1860s; Hilferding 1873; later 20th-c. collections; multiple variants agreeing on the three-stage plot-skeleton — first combat at the river, truce, second combat with princess-abduction and dragon-killing) | alt=individual variants differ in the river-name (Puchai- and others), the weapon (cap, cudgel, sword), the precise head-severing sequence, and the identity of the abducted princess (Zabava Putyatichna in many; other names in some) | chosen: present-the-common-skeleton | reason: per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling, the chapter presents the common narrative skeleton across the corpus rather than privileging one specific recorded variant; per brief special_instructions, primary-text edition-pinning (Oinas, Propp) is deferred to glossary-lock and the chapter cites at corpus level rather than at variant-and-line granularity.]

The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* is, in its surviving 19th-century-recorded form, on firmer attestation ground than the reconstructed Perun-Veles combat. The recorded songs are direct primary-text witnesses to a living oral-epic tradition, with multiple independent variants from multiple performers and multiple collectors, and the textual record's robustness is not in dispute — only its dating and its relationship to older traditions are.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the robustness of the *bylina* corpus's textual record relative to the indirectness of the Perun-Veles evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the firmer attestation ground of the Dobrynya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (textual-record robustness)

The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina*'s historical-cultural setting in the Vladimir-cycle places its narrative reference in the late-10th- and early-11th-century Kievan-Rus' period. The conventional reading within Slavic-folklore scholarship is that the *bylina* tradition in some form reaches back to the Kievan period even though the recorded texts are 19th-century, with the long oral-transmission gap accounting for both the variants' specific differences and their structural agreement.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the Vladimir-cycle's late-Kievan reference and on the conventional medieval-tradition reading of the *bylina* corpus; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the medieval-tradition / 19th-century-recording two-tier dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Vladimir-cycle dating)

The Christian conversion of Kievan Rus' in 988 CE is the principal historical-cultural caesura that separates the Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting from any pre-Christian religious frame. The *bylina* tradition as it survives is wholly Christian-period in its religious frame, with Vladimir himself remembered as the Christianising prince, and the Christian frame is one of the principal complications for any reading of the tradition as preserving pre-Christian combat-myth content.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the 988 CE Christian conversion as the historical-cultural caesura between the *bylina* setting and any pre-Christian religious frame; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Christian-period character of the recorded tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Christian-period frame)

Ivanov and Toporov's specific comparative claim about the Dobrynya material is that the *bylina* combat is the later epic reflex of the reconstructed pre-Christian Perun-Veles 'basic myth' combat, with the *bogatyr* Dobrynya inheriting the role of the storm-god Perun and Zmey Gorynych inheriting the role of the cattle-stealing earth-serpent Veles, and with the *bylina*'s Christian-period epic register replacing the pre-Christian myth's religious register but preserving the structural shape of the combat.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Ivanov and Toporov's claim that the Dobrynya *bylina* is the later reflex of the reconstructed Perun-Veles combat; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the Ivanov-Toporov comparative claim about the Dobrynya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

[INFERENCE: the Ivanov-Toporov claim that the Dobrynya *bylina* is a later reflex of the reconstructed pre-Christian Perun-Veles combat is itself a comparative argument rather than a directly attested feature of the Slavic textual record; the recorded *byliny* do not name Perun or Veles, do not invoke the deity-pair, and do not present themselves as preserving older mythological material, and the reflex-claim is therefore made on structural-comparative grounds (storm-figure-pursues-serpent / cattle-and-frontier-themes / dragon-with-multiple-heads) by reading the Christian-period epic against the reconstructed pre-Christian myth that the chapter has already framed as contested. | basis: West 2007 ch. 6 surveys the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim as a comparative argument; the *bylina* texts themselves (Forsyth 1987 ch. 2's overview of the corpus) make no reference to Perun or Veles; the reflex-claim is doubly mediated, depending both on the contested pre-Christian reconstruction and on the structural reading of the Christian-period epic. | risk: the reflex-claim's evidential weight is therefore sensitive to two prior questions — whether the Perun-Veles reconstruction itself holds, and whether structural-comparative reading is sufficient warrant for inheritance-claims of this kind — and the chapter presents the claim as a comparative argument within Ivanov and Toporov's framework rather than as a fact about the *bylina* tradition's textual history.]

The structural-parallel observations on which the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim rests include the storm-figure / serpent-figure pairing (Dobrynya as warrior-hero / Zmey Gorynych as serpent-adversary, parallel to Perun / Veles); the multi-headed character of the serpent-adversary (Zmey Gorynych three-headed; Veles in some folklore-fragments connected to multi-headed motifs); the water-and-mountain setting (the Puchai-river first-combat / mountain second-combat in the *bylina*; the water-and-rock refuges in the reconstructed Perun-Veles pursuit); and the rescue-and-restoration outcome (the *bylina*'s rescue of the princess and captives parallel to the reconstruction's release of the rain).footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the structural parallels that support the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the structural-parallel basis for the comparative claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The reflex-claim's evidential limits are however significant, and the chapter must register them. The *bylina*'s genre is heroic epic rather than cosmogonic-and-agricultural myth, the dragon's role is as enemy-of-the-state rather than as cattle-stealer-and-rain-blocker, the resolution is dragon-killing-and-princess-rescue rather than rain-release, and the Christian-period frame may itself have generated the structural-parallel features as part of the international hagiographic-and-romance dragon-slaying type rather than inherited them from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-narrative.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina*'s heroic-epic genre and its differences from the reconstructed cosmogonic-and-agricultural Perun-Veles material; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the comparative-argument-rather-than-fact framing for the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina genre vs. reconstructed myth)

The international-hagiographic-and-romance-type complication is significant. The medieval-and-early-modern Christian-period dragon-slaying corpus — the St. George cycle, the romance-tradition's princess-rescuing dragon-slayers, the wider European folktale-dragon-slayer type — supplies a well-attested international type that could plausibly have shaped the Dobrynya *bylina*'s combat-and-rescue plot directly, without any inheritance from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth, and the chapter does not adjudicate between the inheritance-and-international-type-borrowing readings.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the international Christian-period dragon-slaying type as a competing-or-supplementary explanation for the *bylina*'s combat-and-rescue plot; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the comparative-argument framing that leaves the question open.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Dobrynya *bylina*'s position within the present book's typology is therefore as a heroic-epic combat-narrative whose link to the reconstructed pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth is itself contested, and whose principal evidential value for the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula argument depends on the prior acceptance of the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim that the chapter has flagged as a comparative argument.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Dobrynya *bylina*'s position within the disputed-evidence framing; West 2007, ch. 6, on the *bylina*'s evidential dependence on the prior Perun-Veles reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Dobrynya *bylina*'s cultural-historical importance within the East Slavic literary culture is at the same time central and uncontested. The Vladimir-cycle is one of the foundational corpora of pre-modern East Slavic oral literature, the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych narrative is one of its best-known and most-recorded individual *byliny*, and the cycle has been a load-bearing cultural reference within Russian, Ukrainian, and Belarusian literary, scholarly, and popular culture from the 19th-century collection onwards.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the central cultural-historical importance of the Vladimir-cycle and the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych narrative within East Slavic literary culture; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml cultural_relevance on the *bylina* corpus's centrality.]

=== Family pattern and forward links

The two sub-cases treated in the chapter — Perun-Veles (reconstructed) and Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych (recorded) — sit at fundamentally different evidential levels and contribute to the present book's comparative argument in fundamentally different ways. Perun-Veles contributes as contested reconstruction whose evidential weight depends on the reader's acceptance of the Ivanov-Toporov methodology, and Dobrynya contributes as attested heroic epic whose link to the reconstruction is itself a comparative argument rather than an attested fact.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the asymmetric evidential levels of the two sub-cases and on their differing contributions to the comparative argument; West 2007, ch. 6, on the disputed-evidence character of the Slavic material.]

The chapter's joint contribution to the present book is therefore a case study in the limits of mythological reconstruction. The Slavic-and-Baltic combat-corpus shows what an Indo-European combat-tradition looks like when the surviving direct attestations are sparse, when the reconstruction is methodologically charged, and when the link between the reconstructed myth and the surviving oral epic is itself a comparative argument rather than a documented inheritance.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the chapter's case-study contribution to Chapter 33 (methods sidebar) and to Part II C5 (IE formula chapter); West 2007, ch. 6, on the Slavic material's case-study character within the wider IE comparative argument.]
