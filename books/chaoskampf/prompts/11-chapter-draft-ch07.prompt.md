# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).
4. Greece & Rome — Zeus vs. Typhon (full); family chapter "Greek dragon-combats after Zeus-Typhon" (Apollo/Python, Herakles/Hydra-Ladon-Geryon, Perseus/Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Cacus).
5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).
7. Egypt — Ra vs. Apep (full); shorter treatment of Horus vs. Seth as the debated adjacent case.
8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).
9. East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). Yu the Great appears as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering, not as a chaoskampf.
10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.
11. Africa — Bida and the founding of Wagadu (full chapter; the inversion anchor); Mwindo vs. Kirimu (contrastive case: the hero is punished for the killing).
12. Southeast Asia & Oceania — Bakunawa. (Rainbow Serpent is held for the Part II critique chapter, not placed geographically.)
13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).
14. North America (contrastive) — Nanabozho and Mishipeshu: the refusal — the chapter where area specialists (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) are foregrounded and the chaoskampf framing is explicitly declined.

### Part II — thematic comparative chapters
Part II is where the comparative work happens. Load-bearing (confirmed):
- **C1. Combat as cosmogony** — Marduk, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong, Huitzilopochtli.
- **C2. Combat as sovereignty** — Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra, Thraētaona, Kumarbi/Ullikummi, Ninurta/Anzû.
- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.
- **C4. Eschatological displacement** — Thor/Jörmungandr, Rev 12, Frashokereti.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):
- **C5. The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula** — Watkins's argument from Indra through Sigurd to Beowulf.
- **C6. The Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission** — West / Burkert / López-Ruiz: Kumarbi → Hesiod; Baal/Yamm → Typhonomachy.
- **C7. The inversion problem** — Bida anchor; the ambiguous status of Sigurd's slaying.
- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").
- **Methods sidebar** between Part I and Part II: tier-ranking method, appropriation protocol, area-specialist citation policy.
- **Reception coda** (final chapter, ~20–25 pages): traces the afterlife of the imagery in Milton's Satan, Christian iconography of St. Michael / St. George, Tolkien's dragons (and the 1936 Beowulf essay), modern kaiju (Godzilla and the Orochi lineage), and the continuing use of "chaos vs. order" rhetoric in contemporary religious interpretation. Explicitly *not* classified as chaoskampf; this is reception, not primary material.
- **Adversaries + champions appendix** (replaces the single-culture "character appendix"): one entry per monster (Tiamat, Yamm, Lôtan, Leviathan, Rahab, Vṛtra, Aži Dahāka, Apep, Jörmungandr, Typhon, Illuyanka, Ullikummi, Hedammu, the Red Dragon, Coyolxauhqui, etc.) plus one entry per champion. This is load-bearing because the cross-cultural comparisons happen across *figures*, not plots.

Body prose uses "combat myth" as the default, reserving "Chaoskampf" for quotations, discussions of Gunkel's term, and the Part I and Part II passages where the specificity matters. This lets the book engage the Ballentine critique substantively rather than tokenistically.

# Skill instructions: chapter-draft

---
name: chapter-draft
description: Transforms the verified facts into Asimov-style narrative prose. The AI's only job is writing — the facts are already checked. No new facts may be introduced. Every verified claim must appear in the narrative, and nothing unverified may be added.
---

# chapter-draft

## For the human

This is the creative stage — and it is much safer now that the facts are already locked. The AI is not simultaneously researching, citing, and writing. It is only writing. The factual skeleton has already been written and verified. The AI's job is to turn a list of numbered, checked facts into a narrative that reads like Asimov at his best.

The main risk is no longer inventing facts (those are locked) but **drift during the prose transformation**: dropping a verified fact because it doesn't fit the narrative flow, softening a claim until its meaning changes, or inserting a "helpful" detail that was never in the verified facts. The next step (`narrative-fidelity`) will catch these, but the writing AI should work to prevent them.

Importantly, the writing AI does **not** receive the original source texts. It only receives the verified claims document. This is deliberate: giving it the sources would tempt it to "improve" the chapter with additional details that were never fact-checked.

## Hard rules
1. **Every factual assertion in the narrative must correspond to a claim in the approved claims document.** No new facts, no new citations, no "I happen to know" additions.
2. **Every claim in the approved document must appear in the narrative.** Do not silently drop claims because they are inconvenient for the prose flow. If a claim is genuinely irrelevant, flag it rather than omitting it.
3. Use exactly the English renderings locked in `glossary.yaml`.
4. All markers from the claims document survive into the narrative. Do not paraphrase them away.
5. Citations carry over from the claims document. Do not add, remove, or reassign footnotes.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc` (fact-checked, human-reviewed claims)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml` (for variant classification, target length, special instructions)

**Not** primary source translations. The drafter does not need the sources — the claims document already contains everything verified. Providing sources would tempt the drafter to "improve" claims with additional details.

## Style: the Asimov method

Treat the reader as intelligent and uninformed. Gloss proper nouns with functional roles, not poetic epithets. Short paragraphs, one idea each. Plain modern English. Where the source is ambiguous, say so. Where scholars disagree, name it briefly. Use etymology and geography when they illuminate a specific point, never as mandatory ornament.

### Exemplar passage

> The Sumerians believed that before anything else existed, there was a primeval sea. They called her Nammu, and she seems to have been thought of as a kind of cosmic mother — though the texts that survive are maddeningly brief on this point. From Nammu came heaven and earth, conceived of as a single mountain with the sky (An) on top and the earth (Ki) below. An and Ki were the first divine couple, and their union produced Enlil, the god of the air.
>
> Enlil then did something that requires explanation. He separated his father An from his mother Ki, pushing the sky upward and leaving the earth below. This is a motif found in many mythologies — the violent separation of sky and earth by a younger god — but the Sumerian version is unusually matter-of-fact about it. There is no battle, no castration, no cosmic drama. Enlil simply separates them, and the world as we know it comes into being.

### Forbidden words

Do not use, in this register: *shimmering, ancient* (as ornament), *mystical, unfathomable, primordial* (as ornament), *ineffable, sacred* (as filler), *mighty, fearsome, dread, eternal, boundless, infinite, veiled, shrouded*. Avoid openings like "Long ago...", "In the time before time...", "It is said that...". These mark imitation-sacred prose, not Asimov.

## Variant handling (from brief)

- If `variants.classification` is **single-prevalent**: present the prevalent version in the body. Put alternate versions in AsciiDoc footnotes.
- If **co-equal**: present the variants inline in the body, each introduced with its source ("One tradition, preserved in [source], describes... Another, in [source], gives a different account...").

## Narrative transitions

The claims document has no connective tissue — that is your job. You may add:
- **Transitional sentences** that connect one claim to the next ("Having established the cosmic order, the narrative turns to...")
- **Orienting context** that helps the reader follow the story ("At this point in the text, the setting shifts to...")
- **Explanatory asides** that help the reader understand why something matters ("This is worth pausing on, because...")

These are **not** factual claims — they are prose scaffolding. They must not introduce new information about the myth. If a transition requires a factual bridge, that bridge should already be in the claims document as an `[INFERENCE:]` marker.

## Length

Per brief's `target_length_words`, driven by source volume. If you find yourself padding, stop and return the shorter version with a note.

## Source overview opening

Every chapter begins with a short prose passage (2–5 paragraphs) that orients the reader in the physical evidence before the story begins. This passage is built from the `=== Source overview` claims in the approved claims document and covers:

- **What we have**: the tablets, fragments, or manuscripts — named by museum number or corpus ID where possible — and where they were found.
- **What condition it is in**: complete, fragmentary, or composite. Where the major gaps are.
- **How we read it**: which critical edition or translation the retelling follows.

This passage sets the Asimov register immediately: the reader understands they are about to hear a story reconstructed from physical artifacts, not received from some mystical tradition. It is the scholarly equivalent of "here is the evidence, now here is the story it tells."

The source overview is separated from the narrative body by a blank line or a section transition (e.g., "The story, as the tablets preserve it, goes as follows."). It is not a dry bibliography — it is narrative prose about artifacts, written in the same Asimov register as the rest of the chapter.

=== Setting and the Ugaritic divine assembly

The Ugaritic divine world depicted in the Cycle is presided over by El, the chief god and patriarchal head of the divine assembly, whose name is the common Northwest Semitic word for "god" and whose figure stands at the apex of the pantheon as father and judge.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on El and the divine assembly; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on El's role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

El's residence is described in the Cycle as situated at the Source of the Two Rivers, an idealised cosmic location at the meeting of the upper and lower waters, where the divine council convenes around him.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on El's residence at the Source of the Two Rivers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The combat-champion of the Cycle is Baʿlu, the Ugaritic storm-god whose by-name Haddu (hd) marks him as a regional reflex of the wider Northwest Semitic storm-god type; the chapter follows the locked glossary in using the Ugaritic spelling Baʿlu / Haddu at this first mention and then defaulting to Baal in body prose.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Baʿlu / Haddu in Ugaritic and the storm-god type.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

Baal's parentage in the Cycle is the source of one of the Ugaritic religion's persistent textual cruxes: he is repeatedly named in the tablets as son of Dagan rather than son of El, although El is the head of the assembly to which Baal also belongs.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Baal's filiation as son of Dagan.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

Smith treats the bn dgn ("son of Dagan") epithet as a stable Ugaritic formula whose theological implications he discusses at length, without trying to harmonise it into a single genealogical model with El's fatherhood.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the bn dgn formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The principal antagonist of the Cycle's first movement is Yamm, whose name is the Ugaritic common noun for "sea" and whose status is that of a god among the assembly rather than an inchoate primordial force; Yamm is also titled Judge River (ṯpṭ nhr) in the parallel epithetic formulae.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Yamm and the Yamm / Judge River epithetic pair.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The narrative of the Cycle additionally features Mot, whose name is the Ugaritic common noun for "death," as Baal's adversary in the second half of the Cycle on tablets 5 and 6.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Mot's role within the wider Cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The craftsman-god of the Cycle is Kothar-wa-Khasis, a divine smith and artificer whose name is a fixed dual epithet meaning roughly "skilful-and-clever"; the chapter follows the locked glossary in giving the full name Kothar-wa-Khasis at first mention and using Kothar thereafter where the short form is appropriate.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Kothar-wa-Khasis as craftsman-god.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

Baal's principal divine ally and combat-companion in the Cycle is ʿAnat, his sister and a warrior-goddess in her own right, whose epithets and combat-actions are extensively narrated on tablets 3 and 4.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on ʿAnat as warrior-goddess and Baal's ally.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

The wider divine assembly named in the Cycle includes Athtart, Athirat (the consort of El and mother of the gods), Shapsh (the sun-goddess and divine messenger), and the craftsman-pair's location at Memphis and Crete in the geographical apparatus of Kothar's residences.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the wider Ugaritic pantheon attested in the Cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The seat of Baal's eventual kingship after his defeat of Yamm is Ṣapānu, the mountain identified in modern geography as Jebel al-Aqra on the Syrian–Turkish border, also known in later Hebrew Bible reception as Mount Zaphon; the chapter follows the locked glossary in using the Ugaritic Ṣapānu at first mention.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Ṣapānu as Baal's mountain; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the geography of Ṣapānu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The Cycle's broader cast positions Baal among El's sons and clients in a divine court whose decisions are made in council under El's presidency; this council form, rather than a sole-creator monotheism or an unbounded primordial chaos, is the political vocabulary in which the Cycle's combats unfold.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the divine-council form of the Ugaritic pantheon; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the council in the palace-building movement.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The Cycle is, in this institutional setting, primarily a narrative about the institution and contest of divine kingship within the assembly, rather than a cosmogony in the Mesopotamian sense; Smith and Smith & Pitard argue this point repeatedly across both volumes, and it is the framework in which every claim that follows must be read.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on kingship as the Cycle's primary subject; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the kingship reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The Cycle does not present a creation-of-the-world narrative as its frame: the world, the gods, and the divine offices are already in place when the surviving tablets begin, and the Cycle's narrative work is the redistribution of office and authority within an existing pantheon rather than the establishment of cosmos from primordial materials.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the absence of a Cycle-frame cosmogony; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The category in which the Cycle's combats are intelligible is therefore the divine-court politics of the assembly: who is granted kingship, by whom, with what title-and-residence, and against what challenge — a vocabulary that is closer to the Late Bronze Age inter-state diplomatic register than to the cosmogonic register of Enūma Eliš or Lugal-e.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the diplomatic register of the Cycle; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The Late Bronze Age city of Ugarit was itself a vassal-kingdom within the wider Hittite imperial sphere, and Smith and Smith & Pitard observe that the diplomatic and tribute vocabulary of the surviving Ugaritic letter-corpus colours the Cycle's depiction of the assembly's negotiations among its high gods.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Ugarit's geopolitical position and its register-mark on the Cycle; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

=== Movement I — Baal vs. Yamm (KTU 1.1–1.2)

[LACUNA: KTU 1.1 throughout — the opening tablet of the Cycle is badly damaged, and the sequence of events prior to Baal's full emergence as combat-champion is partially reconstructed | source: Smith 1994, Vol. I, edition and commentary on KTU 1.1 | scholarly_reconstruction: Smith reconstructs continuity from internal cross-references in KTU 1.2 and from thematic parallels in KTU 1.3–1.4, while flagging the speculative character of much of the surface narrative on this tablet]

The narrative begins, in the surviving text, with the assembly of the gods convened at El's residence, where the order of business is the elevation of one of El's sons to a position of divine kingship over the assembly.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.1 commentary on the assembly and the kingship motion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.1 commentary

The figure El initially proposes — and to whom the assembly initially defers — is Yamm, who is named on the tablets as El's beloved (mdd il) and is granted by El the title of king and the standing of an authorised divine sovereign within the assembly.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.1 commentary on Yamm's elevation; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the on-text status of Yamm's elevation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.1 commentary

El's proposal, in the surviving lines of KTU 1.1, includes the renaming of Yamm — the assembly is invited to confer on Yamm a new throne-name befitting the kingship, in a passage Smith comments on at length as one of the Cycle's clearest investiture markers.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.1 commentary on the renaming-and-investiture passage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.1 commentary

The on-text fact that El initially confirms Yamm in a position of legitimate divine kingship is the textual basis for Ballentine's reading that Yamm is a rival sovereign within the assembly rather than an outside chaos-figure; this reading is developed below in the dedicated Gunkel-Ballentine section.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on Yamm as rival sovereign rather than chaos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The tablets describe a banquet and an exchange of speeches at the assembly in which the divine offices and prerogatives associated with the kingship are distributed and debated; the Cycle's opening movement is thus framed as a political-juridical event in the divine court before it becomes a combat.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.1 commentary on the assembly's juridical opening.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.1 commentary

Yamm sends two messengers to the assembly with a demand framed in the diplomatic language of inter-state correspondence: that Baal be handed over to him as a tribute or hostage, an act that would establish Yamm's superordinate kingship over Baal and the assembly.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 i, on the messenger episode and the demand.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 i

The messengers' speech is delivered with the formal markers of vassalage and the assembly's initial response is recorded in the surviving lines of KTU 1.2 i as one of consternation, with several gods bowing their heads at the messengers' approach.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 i, on the assembly's response to Yamm's messengers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 i

Baal himself responds with defiance: against the assembly's apparent willingness to accede to Yamm's demand, Baal refuses the hand-over and rebukes the assembly for its readiness to capitulate.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 i commentary on Baal's defiant response.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 i commentary

Baal's rebuke is paired in the tablet text with a physical gesture against the messengers themselves — a striking-action that ʿAnat and Athtart restrain — and the Cycle's combat is thereby announced in the assembly's chamber before it is fought in the open.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 i commentary on the messengers and the restraining-action. The identity of the restraining figures is one of the tablet's interpretive cruxes: an alternative reading in the secondary literature attributes the restraint to El or Athirat rather than to ʿAnat and Athtart.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 i commentary

The combat between Baal and Yamm is thus precipitated not by an external invasion of cosmic order but by Baal's refusal to be handed over within the diplomatic frame Yamm has set; the narrative cause of the combat is intra-assembly political conflict over the kingship.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 i commentary on the political framing of the combat; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the political-frame reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 i commentary

Baal seeks out the craftsman-god Kothar-wa-Khasis, whose forge is the source of the divine weapons of the assembly, to commission the implements with which he will fight.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on Baal's commissioning of Kothar's weapons.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

Kothar forges for Baal not one weapon but two: a pair of named maces — Yagrush ("Driver") and Ayyamur ("Chaser") — each given a proper name in the tablets and each accompanied by a poetic incantation describing its function in the imminent combat.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the maces Yagrush and Ayyamur.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The naming and pairing of the two maces is a load-bearing detail in the Ugaritic text: the weapons are not anonymous instruments of force but named partners in the combat, addressed by Baal in second-person speech and instructed in their work.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the named-mace pair as a Ugaritic literary feature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

Yagrush and Ayyamur are commanded to leap from Baal's hand and strike Yamm in the prescribed places: Yagrush at Yamm's shoulders, Ayyamur at Yamm's skull, in a sequence the tablets articulate as a paired deployment.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the targeted deployment of the two maces.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

Kothar's accompanying speech to Baal, recorded in the surviving lines of KTU 1.2 iv, addresses the maces in the second person and instructs them in their work — a speech-act in which the maces are personified as agents under Baal's command rather than as inert weapons.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on Kothar's speech and the personification of the maces.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The first mace's strike does not finish the combat: Yamm staggers but recovers, and only the second mace's blow brings him down, in a two-stage combat pattern that is one of the Cycle's distinctive narrative features.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the two-stage combat sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The two-stage combat pattern — the first weapon insufficient, the second weapon decisive — is a structural feature shared with the Mesopotamian Anzû Epic, where Ninurta's first arrows fail and a renewed assault with new instruments follows; the parallel is structural-typological and is recorded as a comparative note rather than a transmission claim.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the two-stage combat as a recurring Late Bronze Age combat-narrative feature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The combat itself is depicted in formulaic combat-poetry that emphasises the physical impact of the maces and the collapse of Yamm to the ground; the verbal repertoire is the Ugaritic combat-formula register that recurs in Baal's later confrontations within the Cycle.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the combat-poetry register.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

After the combat-blow, the tablets describe Baal as scattering Yamm — the verb-and-object pair the text uses is the standard Ugaritic verb of dispersal applied to the defeated rival — and pronouncing Yamm's defeat in his own first-person voice.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the post-combat scattering and the victor's pronouncement.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The defeat of Yamm is not narrated as Yamm's annihilation: the Ugaritic verbs and the surrounding poetry cohere with a defeat-and-subjugation reading rather than a destruction-and-replacement one, and Smith comments that this is consistent with the political-rivalry frame of the surrounding tablets.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the defeat-not-destruction reading; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the same point.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The goddess Athtart is named in the surviving lines of KTU 1.2 iv as intervening at the moment of Yamm's defeat with a rebuke or warning to Baal regarding the disposition of the defeated rival, an episode whose precise sense is one of the tablet's interpretive cruxes.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on Athtart's intervention.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

After Yamm's defeat the assembly's earlier deference to Yamm's kingship is overturned, and the surviving tablet text moves toward the proclamation of Baal as king — the institutional consequence of the combat is the transfer of the kingship that El had initially conferred on Yamm.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the post-combat transfer of kingship; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the kingship-as-Cycle-spine reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The proclamation acclaims Baal as king with a formula the Cycle returns to in subsequent passages — Baal "shall be king" (ymlk) — that fixes the institutional outcome of the combat in the same juridical register in which the kingship was earlier conferred on Yamm.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the ymlk acclamation formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

The formula's reuse — first for Yamm, then for Baal — is one of the Cycle's principal lexical demonstrations that the kingship at issue is the same office, contested between two rival sovereigns, rather than two different categories of rule.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary on the formula's reuse; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the rival-sovereignty reading the formula supports.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

[INFERENCE: the structure of Movement I — initial royal grant by El to Yamm, demand for Baal's hand-over, refusal, two-mace combat, defeat, and assembly-level transfer of the kingship — exhibits a consistent juridical-political pattern in which the combat does sovereignty-establishing work rather than cosmogonic work | basis: Smith 1994 Vol. I introduction's treatment of the Cycle as primarily about kingship, Ballentine 2015 ch. 2 on the on-text status of Yamm's elevation, and the absence of any cosmogonic creation-act in the surviving combat-aftermath | risk: the inference is structural rather than verbatim; portions of KTU 1.1 are broken and any reading of the Cycle's overall shape depends on the reconstructed tablet order, which is itself a scholarly convention rather than an ancient datum.]

The combat is not associated, in the surviving Ugaritic text, with the creation of the world: there is no creation-from-the-corpse motif of the kind that follows Marduk's defeat of Tiamat in Enūma Eliš, and the post-combat narrative moves directly into the institutional matter of Baal's enthronement.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the absence of cosmogonic creation in the Baal-Yamm aftermath; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The combat is also not associated, in the Ugaritic text itself, with a seven-headed-serpent description of Yamm: the seven-headed serpent named in the Cycle is Lôtan, whose connection to Yamm is a separate textual question discussed below in the dedicated Lôtan section.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the distinct treatment of Yamm and Lôtan in the Cycle; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The Movement I narrative thus hands over to the next movement of the Cycle the question of how Baal's newly secured kingship is to be institutionalised — a question that the palace-building cycle of KTU 1.3–1.4 takes up directly.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, KTU 1.2 iv commentary, on the hand-over to the palace-building movement; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the structural transition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=KTU 1.2 iv commentary

=== Movement II — Palace-building and divine kingship (KTU 1.3–1.4)

The second movement of the Cycle, preserved across the better-attested tablets KTU 1.3 and 1.4, narrates the construction of a palace for Baal on the heights of Ṣapānu, an act that institutionalises the kingship his combat-victory has won.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 commentary on the palace-building cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 commentary

The opening of KTU 1.3 finds Baal already victorious and presiding over a banquet in which he and the divine assembly celebrate his combat-success, with the goddess ʿAnat in attendance and engaged in her own cycle of combat-actions.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 ii commentary on the post-combat banquet and ʿAnat's actions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 ii commentary

KTU 1.3 ii preserves a famously gory scene of ʿAnat slaughtering at her shrine: she is described in the tablet wading in blood up to her thighs, hanging severed heads at her belt and severed hands at her girdle, in a portrait of the warrior-goddess at the height of her destructive power.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 ii commentary on ʿAnat's combat-scene.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 ii commentary

The ʿAnat slaughter scene is not directly continuous with the Baal-Yamm combat in the surviving text and its precise referent — whether enemies of Baal, human soldiery, or members of a defeated divine faction — is one of the textual cruxes Smith and Pitard discuss at length in their commentary.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 ii commentary on the interpretive crux of the slaughter scene.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 ii commentary

In the tablet's continuation Baal sends messengers to ʿAnat with instructions to set aside her combat-actions and to assist in the project of securing him a palace, marking the structural transition from combat to institution-building.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 iii commentary on Baal's message to ʿAnat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 iii commentary

Within ʿAnat's combat speech recorded on tablet 3 — column iii — appears the Cycle's most extended catalogue of the warrior-goddess's prior victories, in which she lists the adversaries she has personally defeated; this passage is the immediate context of one of the Cycle's references to Lôtan, discussed below in the dedicated Lôtan section.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 iii commentary on ʿAnat's catalogue of victories.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 iii commentary

ʿAnat travels to El's residence at the Source of the Two Rivers to petition for the palace on Baal's behalf, her petition framed by the surviving lines of KTU 1.3 v as an aggressive demand backed by the threat of physical violence against El himself.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 v commentary on ʿAnat's petition and threat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 v commentary

El's response in this passage is recorded in formulaic blessing-language that does not yet grant the palace, and the tablet preserves a back-and-forth in which the petition is repeated and refined across multiple speeches of the divine assembly.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 v commentary on El's initial response.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 v commentary

The petition for Baal's palace is presented in the tablets as a matter of justice: Baal alone among the high gods of the assembly lacks a residence of his own, and the construction of the palace is framed as the rectification of an institutional anomaly within the divine court.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 v commentary on the palace-as-justice frame; KTU 1.4 iv–v commentary on the same theme.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 v commentary

The petition continues across KTU 1.4, where Athirat — El's consort and the senior matron of the assembly — is brought into the negotiation as the figure whose intercession with El is necessary to secure the palace's authorisation.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 i–iii commentary on Athirat's intercession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 i-iii commentary

The tablets describe a sequence of gift-presentations and formal speeches in which Baal and ʿAnat solicit Athirat's support, with Athirat's eventual assent providing the social-political bridge across which El's authorisation can be secured.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 i–iii commentary on the gift-and-speech sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 i-iii commentary

Athirat then journeys to El at the Source of the Two Rivers and conveys the petition, and El's authorising speech in KTU 1.4 iv–v grants the palace and the materials necessary for its construction.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 iv–v commentary on Athirat's audience and El's authorisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 iv-v commentary

The architect-builder of the palace is Kothar-wa-Khasis, the same craftsman-god who forged Yagrush and Ayyamur for the Yamm combat: the palace and the maces are continuous expressions of the same divine-craftsmanship register within the Cycle's narrative economy.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 v–vi commentary on Kothar's architectural commission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 v-vi commentary

Baal commissions the palace in cedar and silver and gold, and the construction proceeds across a sequence of furnaces and craft-actions narrated in KTU 1.4 vi as a feat of sustained metallurgical and architectural labour rather than as instantaneous divine fiat.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 vi commentary on the materials and the construction sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 vi commentary

A famous textual crux of the palace-building movement is the disagreement between Baal and Kothar over whether the palace should be built with a window: Baal initially refuses a window, Kothar insists on it, and Baal ultimately concedes — a passage Smith and Pitard discuss as bearing on the connection between Baal's storm-god function and the architectural form of his residence.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 v–vi commentary on the window-of-the-palace debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 v-vi commentary

When the window is finally opened, Baal's voice — the storm-god's thunder — sounds through it, in a gesture that links the palace's architectural form to Baal's meteorological prerogative as the sovereign whose storm and rain mark the seasons.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 vii commentary on the opening of the window and Baal's thunder.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 vii commentary

The window-debate has been read by Smith and Pitard as bearing on the relation between divine sovereignty and openness-to-the-world: Baal's initial reluctance to permit a window in the palace is, on this reading, a fortified-residence instinct, while Kothar's insistence makes the palace a site from which the storm-god can act upon the world below.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 v–vi commentary on the sovereignty-and-openness reading of the window-debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 v-vi commentary

The completed palace is the seat of Baal's institutional kingship: from Ṣapānu Baal exercises the prerogatives of a royal sovereign, presiding over banquets, dispatching messengers, and asserting authority over the natural and political order subordinate to him.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 vii–viii commentary on Baal's enthronement at Ṣapānu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 vii-viii commentary

Baal's residence at Ṣapānu is the Cycle's principal geographical anchor: the mountain functions in the surviving text as the storm-god's locus and the cult-site whose name will remain associated with the storm-god's presence in the wider Northwest Semitic religious vocabulary.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Ṣapānu as the Cycle's geographical anchor; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 viii commentary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

A banquet inaugurating the palace marks Baal's enthronement: the assembly is convened, the gods are feasted, and the new sovereign's authority is recognised in the same banqueting and gift-exchange register that opened the Cycle's earlier movements.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 vi–vii commentary on the inaugural banquet.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 vi-vii commentary

Baal's voice from the opened window declares his sovereignty over the surrounding territory and over the rival kings of the cosmos, in a speech the Cycle records on KTU 1.4 vii as the formal articulation of his royal claim from the palace.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.4 vii commentary on Baal's enthronement-speech from the window.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.4 vii commentary

The palace-building movement institutes Baal's kingship in the same juridical register that opened the Cycle: the assembly grants the palace in proper form, El authorises through Athirat, Kothar builds, and the result is a sovereignty exercised through palace-residence and divine-court ceremony rather than through cosmogonic creation.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the palace-building movement as kingship-institution; Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the kingship-as-spine reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

The institutional reading of Movement II — palace-building as the formalisation of a kingship the combat has won — is the load-bearing argument of Smith and Pitard's volume-II introduction and is the framework within which the chapter presents the tablets' content.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the palace-as-kingship reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

Movement II contains no combat with a serpentine adversary in its surviving text: the actions of the movement are construction, intercession, and ceremony, not combat, and the chapter presents this as a structural fact rather than as a gap to be filled.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3–1.4 commentary on the absence of combat in the palace-building movement.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3-1.4 commentary

The transition from Movement II to the surviving fragments of KTU 1.5 closes the palace-building cycle and opens onto the Mot material; it also frames, in the immediate context of KTU 1.5 i, the brief reference to Lôtan that the next section addresses.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the structural transition into KTU 1.5; Smith 1994 Vol. I introduction on the same transition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

=== The Lôtan passage (KTU 1.5 i 1–3) — retrospective allusion

[LACUNA: KTU 1.5 i 1–3 — the Lôtan reference is a retrospective boast embedded in Mot's speech to Baal; the narrative of the Lôtan combat itself is not preserved in the extant Cycle | source: Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II commentary on KTU 1.5 i 1–3 | scholarly_reconstruction: none — the chapter treats Lôtan as an allusion to a previously-completed combat that is not narrated in the surviving tablets, and explicitly does not import a Baal-Lôtan combat narrative from Hebrew Bible or other parallels]

Lôtan is named in the Cycle as a serpent (bṯn) of formidable proportions, given the epithetic descriptions "fleeing" (brḥ) and "twisting" (ʿqltn) and identified as a seven-headed adversary; the chapter follows the locked glossary in spelling the name Lôtan with the circumflex.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on Lôtan's epithets and seven-headed form.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

The Ugaritic spelling ltn is conventionally vocalised Lôtan in modern scholarly usage, on the basis of the Northwest Semitic vocalisation pattern reconstructed from Hebrew lwytn (Leviathan) and from internal Ugaritic evidence.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the vocalisation of ltn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

The principal preserved reference to Lôtan within the Cycle stands at KTU 1.5 i 1–3, where the words form part of a speech delivered by Mot to Baal at the opening of the second half of the Cycle.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the placement of the Lôtan reference within Mot's speech.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

The grammar and pragmatics of the passage are those of a retrospective taunt rather than a narrative report: Mot, addressing Baal, recalls Lôtan's defeat as an event of the past whose recurrence — this time at Mot's own hands against Baal — is now threatened.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the retrospective-taunt pragmatics of the passage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

The Mot speech at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 is therefore the principal textual evidence the Cycle preserves for a combat between Baal and Lôtan: the combat is named in passing as a victory Baal has achieved, but the narrative of that combat is not preserved on any surviving tablet of the Baal Cycle.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the absence of an explicit Baal-Lôtan combat narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

A second, briefer Lôtan-related claim appears in ʿAnat's catalogue of her own victories on KTU 1.3 iii, where the warrior-goddess lists among the adversaries she has personally crushed a seven-headed serpent and other monstrous figures; whether this passage refers to the same Lôtan as KTU 1.5 i 1–3 or to a parallel adversary is a question Smith and Pitard discuss at length in their commentary.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 iii commentary on ʿAnat's catalogue and the seven-headed serpent.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 iii commentary

Smith and Pitard treat ʿAnat's catalogue as evidence that the slaying of monstrous serpentine adversaries was a topos with circulation in the Ugaritic divine biography of Baal and ʿAnat, even where the specific narrative of any given combat is not preserved at length.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.3 iii commentary on the topos of monstrous-serpent victory.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.3 iii commentary

The chapter's policy on the Lôtan material follows the brief: Lôtan is treated as named, formally described, and located within Baal's prior victories by reference, but the combat itself is not narrativised, because no Baal-Lôtan combat narrative is preserved in the extant Cycle.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the absence of a narrated Baal-Lôtan combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

The chapter also follows the brief in declining to import a Baal-Lôtan combat narrative from Hebrew Bible material (Psalm 74, Isaiah 27, Job 41) or from Mesopotamian parallels: the Hebrew Bible attestations of Yahweh's combat with Leviathan are part of the reception history of the Ugaritic material, not its supplement, and are reserved for the Hebrew Bible chapter (Ch. 8) and for Day 1985's separate treatment.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on the Leviathan / Lôtan continuity; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the methodological separation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Lôtan passage at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 has nonetheless become, in the wider scholarly literature, the central textual hinge of the Ugaritic-to-Hebrew-Bible Leviathan argument: the linguistic cognancy ltn ↔ lwytn and the shared seven-headedness make the passage the load-bearing piece of evidence for the continuity Day 1985 develops at length.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on the cognancy ltn ↔ lwytn and the seven-headed-serpent topos; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the Hebrew-Bible reception.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The chapter therefore presents Lôtan's seven heads, the locked Hebrew-Bible connection, and the linguistic cognancy as on-text Ugaritic facts, while explicitly reserving the Yahweh-vs-Leviathan / Yahweh-vs-Rahab combat-narratives of the Hebrew Bible for the next chapter (Ch. 8) of this volume.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on the Hebrew Bible reception; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the methodological boundary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Lôtan reference also has a Hebrew-Bible cross-reference to tehôm — the primordial deep of Genesis 1:2 — that Day 1985 develops within the wider Yahweh-and-the-sea complex; the chapter notes the cross-reference at glossary level and reserves substantive treatment for Ch. 8.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on tehôm within the Yahweh-and-the-sea complex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Hebrew Bible's cognate term Tannīn (with macron) — a sea-monster figure named in passages such as Isaiah 27:1 alongside Leviathan — is recorded by Day 1985 as a third Northwest Semitic monstrous-figure name with Ugaritic background; like the Leviathan and tehôm cross-references, the chapter notes the cognancy and reserves substantive Hebrew Bible treatment for Ch. 8.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on the Tannīn cognate within the Yahweh-and-the-sea complex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

[VARIANT: primary=Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II commentary on KTU 1.5 i 1–3 (treats the Lôtan reference as a retrospective allusion within Mot's speech, with no preserved Baal-Lôtan combat narrative) | alt=earlier scholarship that treated KTU 1.3 iii and KTU 1.5 i 1–3 as portions of a single, partially-preserved Baal-and-ʿAnat combat-with-Lôtan narrative | chosen: primary | reason: the Smith and Pitard reading is the current critical-edition consensus and is the framing the chapter brief explicitly mandates; the older harmonising reading is recorded as an alternative but not adopted as the chapter's position.]

The chapter's handling of the Lôtan material is therefore deliberately minimal: the passage is named, described, located, and acknowledged as the textual hinge of a major reception-history argument, while the combat itself is not narrated, because the surviving tablets do not narrate it.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i 1–3 commentary on the chapter's narrative reticence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 commentary

=== Mot context (KTU 1.5–1.6)

The second half of the Baal Cycle, preserved on tablets 5 and 6, narrates the confrontation between Baal and Mot — the god of death — and is the context in which the Lôtan reference at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 is embedded.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the Baal-Mot material as the second half of the Cycle; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The Baal-Mot narrative tells of Baal's descent into the underworld at Mot's command, his death, and his eventual revival through ʿAnat's actions against Mot, in a cycle of death-and-revival that is structurally distinct from the Yamm combat.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the Baal-Mot death-and-revival cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The chapter's policy on the Mot material follows the brief: the Mot cycle is acknowledged as the narrative context for the Lôtan reference and as part of the wider Baal Cycle, but it is not itself a chaoskampf and is not narrativised as one.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the methodological status of the Mot material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

The Baal-Mot encounter is a confrontation between two members of the divine assembly over the prerogatives of life, death, and seasonal succession; the Cycle's treatment of Mot is in the register of cosmic-political negotiation between gods rather than combat with a serpentine adversary.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the Baal-Mot material as cosmic-political rather than combat-of-the-Yamm-type; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

The chapter therefore notes the Baal-Mot material's place within the Cycle and its structural distinctness from the Baal-Yamm combat, but defers any extended treatment to specialist literature on Ugaritic religion and the agricultural-calendar reading of the Cycle.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the agricultural-calendar reading of the Cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

The Baal-Mot material is also the immediate setting in which the Lôtan reference is delivered: it is Mot's taunt, in the opening lines of KTU 1.5 i, that introduces the named figure Lôtan into the Cycle's surviving textual record.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, KTU 1.5 i commentary on the Mot-speech setting of the Lôtan reference.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i commentary

The agricultural-calendar reading of the Baal-Mot material — Baal's death-and-revival as a mythic articulation of the seasonal alternation between the wet productive season and the dry season — has wide currency in the secondary literature, but Smith and Pitard caution that the Cycle's text does not present it as a calendrical-ritual aetiology in the same explicit way that, for example, the Hittite *purulli* recitations frame the Illuyanka combat.footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the cautious treatment of the agricultural-calendar reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=Vol. II introduction

=== The Gunkel-Ballentine debate

The Baal-Yamm combat at KTU 1.1–1.2 is the principal Ugaritic test-case for one of the central terminological debates of the wider Chaoskampf scholarship: whether the combat-myth corpus should be read as a struggle between divine order and primordial chaos (the Gunkel framing), or as a contest of rival sovereignties within an established divine assembly (the Ballentine framing).footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on Baal-Yamm as the central rival-sovereignty test-case; Gunkel 1895, Teil I as the historical anchor of the chaos-vs-order framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

Hermann Gunkel's 1895 *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* established the Chaoskampf category by reading the Hebrew Bible's combat-imagery as the survival of an older Mesopotamian creation-and-chaos myth; Gunkel did not have access to the Ugaritic tablets, which were not excavated until the 1920s, but his framing of the category dominated all subsequent treatment of what would become the Baal-Yamm material.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, on the Chaoskampf category; Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the historiographical position of Gunkel before the Ugaritic finds.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The standard mid-twentieth-century reading of Baal-Yamm — built up across the work of scholars from Cassuto to Day — extended Gunkel's chaos-vs-order framing to the Ugaritic material, treating Yamm as a personification of primordial chaotic sea against which Baal's combat secures cosmic order.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3, on the Yamm-as-chaos reading within the Gunkelian tradition; Wakeman 1973, on Baal-Yamm as combat-with-the-monster.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Debra Scoggins Ballentine's 2015 *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* argues, against this dominant reading, that Yamm is on the Ugaritic text presented as a legitimate divine sovereign within the assembly — beloved of El, granted the kingship by El, and engaged with as a rival rather than as an outside chaotic force.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2, on Yamm as legitimate divine sovereign within the assembly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

Ballentine's argument turns on three on-text observations: that Yamm's name is the common noun for "sea" without any cosmogonic primordiality marker, that El initially confirms Yamm's kingship within the assembly, and that the post-combat narrative is the institution of Baal's kingship rather than the creation of the world.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2, on the three on-text observations grounding the rival-sovereignty reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

Ballentine adds a fourth observation: Yamm's epithetic pair Yamm / Judge River (ym / ṯpṭ nhr) gives him a juridical title — Judge — that is incompatible with a primordial-chaos category and is exactly the kind of office an alternative sovereign within an established assembly would hold.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the Yamm / Judge River epithetic pair as a juridical title.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

Smith and Pitard, in their critical-edition introductions, present the Baal Cycle as primarily about kingship rather than cosmogony — a position Smith develops at length in the volume-I introduction and Smith and Pitard reinforce in the volume-II introduction — and Ballentine's reading aligns substantively with this kingship-as-spine framing.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on the kingship-as-Cycle-subject reading; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the same; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the alignment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-1994-baal-i ; loc=Vol. I introduction

Forsyth, writing earlier than Ballentine and within the broadly Gunkelian historiographical tradition, nevertheless registers in chapter 3 of *The Old Enemy* that the Ugaritic combat is closer to a sovereignty contest than to the cosmogonic-chaos framing that the Mesopotamian comparison would suggest, anticipating part of what Ballentine systematises in 2015.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on the Ugaritic combat's sovereignty register.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

The Ballentine critique does not require the abandonment of the Chaoskampf vocabulary as such, but it does require that the category be tightened: where the combat does sovereignty-establishing rather than cosmogonic work, "rival-sovereignty combat" is on Ballentine's argument the more accurate description.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the proposed terminological tightening.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The chapter retains the term "the adversary" as the neutral category designation for Yamm and Lôtan within the body, in line with the locked glossary's policy that "the adversary" is the default cross-chapter term and that "chaos-figure" is banned because it begs the very question Ballentine raises.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2; book glossary v1-locked 2026-04-24 entry on "the adversary".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The Baal-Yamm material is the clearest test case in the corpus for this terminological question: every component the Gunkel framing assigns to the chaos role — the personified sea, the named seven-headed serpent, the storm-god's combat-victory — is in the Ugaritic text framed by an explicit divine-court politics in which the contest is over kingship, not over the existence of order itself.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on Baal-Yamm as the load-bearing test case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The chapter takes the position, following the brief, that the rival-sovereignty reading is the on-text reading of the Ugaritic Cycle, and that the cosmogonic-chaos reading is a Hebrew-Bible-mediated extension of the category that may apply to some later receptions but does not apply to the Baal-Yamm narrative as the Cycle itself preserves it.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2; Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The Day 1985 reading, while remaining the principal twentieth-century treatment of the Hebrew Bible's continuity with the Ugaritic material, can be substantively preserved within Ballentine's framework on the linguistic and narrative-cognancy front (ltn ↔ lwytn; the seven-headed serpent topos), even where the cosmogonic-chaos category is not adopted as the structuring frame for the Ugaritic material itself.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on linguistic and narrative cognancy; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the compatibility of the cognancy material with the rival-sovereignty reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Baal-Yamm test case thus serves a double function in the wider book: as the primary Ugaritic warrant for Day's continuity argument with the Hebrew Bible (Ch. 8), and as the primary Ugaritic warrant for Ballentine's terminological tightening of the Chaoskampf category (Part II C2).footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3; Ballentine 2015, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The chapter therefore engages the Gunkel-Ballentine debate substantively rather than tokenistically: Yamm is treated as a rival sovereign within the assembly throughout the body, the rival-sovereignty reading is foregrounded over the chaos-vs-order reading, and Part II C2 is flagged as the dedicated comparative chapter where the Baal-Yamm test case is generalised across Zeus-Typhon, Indra-Vṛtra, and the other sovereignty-combat materials.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2 on the Baal-Yamm test case for sovereignty-combat; cross-reference to Part II C2 of this volume.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The retention of the Chaoskampf term in the chapter title and headings is in line with the book's locked terminology policy, in which "Chaoskampf" remains the search-term and reader-expectation handle while "combat myth" is the body-prose default and the rival-sovereignty reading is the substantive position adopted for Baal-Yamm specifically.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 2; Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 2

The Wakeman 1973 treatment of Baal-Yamm and Leviathan, while pre-Ballentine and within the Gunkelian tradition, retains its value for the chapter on the comparative-imagery side: Wakeman's catalogue of monstrous-adversary depictions is preserved as a reference-resource even where the cosmogonic-chaos framing she works within is not adopted.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, on the comparative-imagery catalogue of the monstrous adversary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=full volume comparative-imagery treatment

=== Reception and forward links

The Ugaritic Baal Cycle's reception in the Hebrew Bible is the subject of John Day's 1985 monograph and is the principal reception arc the chapter forwards to Ch. 8 of this volume — the chapter on Yahweh, Leviathan, and Rahab.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on the Hebrew Bible reception; cross-reference to Ch. 8 of this volume.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Day argues that the Hebrew Bible's depictions of Yahweh's combat with Leviathan (Psalm 74:13–14; Job 41), with Rahab (Psalm 89:9–10; Isaiah 51:9–10), and with the sea (Psalm 74:13; Habakkuk 3:8) preserve, with linguistic and motivic specificity, the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm and Baal-Lôtan material, and that the Hebrew Bible passages are best read as the Israelite reception of this Canaanite mythological inheritance.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3, on the Hebrew Bible passages and their Ugaritic precedents.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The linguistic cognancy ltn ↔ lwytn (Lôtan ↔ Leviathan) is the most specific lexical evidence for Day's continuity argument: the names are reflexes of the same Northwest Semitic root, and the seven-headed-serpent topos travels intact from the Ugaritic to the Hebrew Bible side of the chain.footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–3 on the ltn ↔ lwytn cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-3

A second body of reception material — the Greek and Phoenician transmission of the storm-god-vs-sea pattern through Levantine intermediaries — is treated by West and Burkert as part of a broader Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission complex that brings the Baal-Yamm material into proximity with the Hesiodic Zeus-vs-Typhon combat.footnote:[West 1997, on the Phoenician-mediated transmission; Burkert 1992, on the Orientalising transmission complex; cross-reference to Ch. 11 and Part II C6 of this volume.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=on the Phoenician-mediated transmission

The Greek-transmission material is reserved for Ch. 11 (Zeus vs. Typhon) and for Part II C6 (the Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chapter), and is flagged here only as forward link rather than developed substantively.footnote:[West 1997; Burkert 1992; cross-reference to Ch. 11 and Part II C6.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=Orientalising-transmission references

A third body of reception material — the structural continuity between the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm material and the Mesopotamian Marduk-Tiamat material — is recorded in Forsyth's chapter 3 and in the wider comparative literature on combat-myth, and is treated typologically rather than as evidence of direct historical transmission between the two corpora.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on Ugaritic-Mesopotamian structural continuity; Lambert 2013 on Marduk-Tiamat as the Mesopotamian comparison.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3
