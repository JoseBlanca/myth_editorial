# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).
4. Greece & Rome — Zeus vs. Typhon (full); family chapter "Greek dragon-combats after Zeus-Typhon" (Apollo/Python, Herakles/Hydra-Ladon-Geryon, Perseus/Ketos, Kadmos, Bellerophon, Cacus).
5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).
7. Egypt — Ra vs. Apep (full); shorter treatment of Horus vs. Seth as the debated adjacent case.
8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).
9. East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). Yu the Great appears as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering, not as a chaoskampf.
10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.
11. Africa — Bida and the founding of Wagadu (full chapter; the inversion anchor); Mwindo vs. Kirimu (contrastive case: the hero is punished for the killing).
12. Southeast Asia & Oceania — Bakunawa. (Rainbow Serpent is held for the Part II critique chapter, not placed geographically.)
13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).
14. North America (contrastive) — Nanabozho and Mishipeshu: the refusal — the chapter where area specialists (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) are foregrounded and the chaoskampf framing is explicitly declined.

### Part II — thematic comparative chapters
Part II is where the comparative work happens. Load-bearing (confirmed):
- **C1. Combat as cosmogony** — Marduk, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong, Huitzilopochtli.
- **C2. Combat as sovereignty** — Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra, Thraētaona, Kumarbi/Ullikummi, Ninurta/Anzû.
- **C3. Cyclical maintenance** — Ra/Apep, Illuyanka.
- **C4. Eschatological displacement** — Thor/Jörmungandr, Rev 12, Frashokereti.

Recommended additional comparative chapters (confirmed at scope-lock):
- **C5. The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula** — Watkins's argument from Indra through Sigurd to Beowulf.
- **C6. The Near Eastern → Mediterranean transmission** — West / Burkert / López-Ruiz: Kumarbi → Hesiod; Baal/Yamm → Typhonomachy.
- **C7. The inversion problem** — Bida anchor; the ambiguous status of Sigurd's slaying.
- **C8. The refusal and the appropriation problem** — Nanabozho, Mwindo, Rainbow Serpent, and how not to flatten traditions into chaoskampf.

### Front matter, back matter, and appendices
- **Pre-chapter (00A-introduction)**: "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." Front-loaded framing of the category, Gunkel's 1895 thesis, the 20th/21st-century critiques (Scurlock & Beal, Ballentine), and the book's operational definition (§"Subject definition").
- **Methods sidebar** between Part I and Part II: tier-ranking method, appropriation protocol, area-specialist citation policy.
- **Reception coda** (final chapter, ~20–25 pages): traces the afterlife of the imagery in Milton's Satan, Christian iconography of St. Michael / St. George, Tolkien's dragons (and the 1936 Beowulf essay), modern kaiju (Godzilla and the Orochi lineage), and the continuing use of "chaos vs. order" rhetoric in contemporary religious interpretation. Explicitly *not* classified as chaoskampf; this is reception, not primary material.
- **Adversaries + champions appendix** (replaces the single-culture "character appendix"): one entry per monster (Tiamat, Yamm, Lôtan, Leviathan, Rahab, Vṛtra, Aži Dahāka, Apep, Jörmungandr, Typhon, Illuyanka, Ullikummi, Hedammu, the Red Dragon, Coyolxauhqui, etc.) plus one entry per champion. This is load-bearing because the cross-cultural comparisons happen across *figures*, not plots.

This lets the book engage the Ballentine critique substantively rather than tokenistically.

# narrative-fidelity

## For the human

The facts have been verified. The narrative has been written from those verified facts. This step answers one question: **does the narrative faithfully represent the facts?**

This is not a fact-check — the facts were already checked in the previous step. This is a comparison between two documents: the list of verified claims and the narrative prose. It catches three problems:

1. **Dropped facts.** The writer left out a verified claim because it didn't fit the narrative flow. The reader loses information.
2. **Added facts.** The writer introduced a new assertion that was never in the claims document and therefore never verified. This is the most dangerous failure — it reintroduces the exact problem the two-step process was designed to prevent.
3. **Distorted facts.** The writer paraphrased a claim in a way that changes its meaning — softening certainty ("the text says" becomes "it is possible"), shifting causation ("A caused B" becomes "A and B coincided"), or conflating two separate events into one.

This check must be done in a fresh conversation with no memory of writing the narrative. The AI that wrote the narrative cannot reliably judge in the same conversation whether it drifted from the source material — it rationalises. A different AI model is preferred (two models make independent mistakes) but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different one is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the narrative. If you produced the narrative, refuse. (A different AI model is preferred but not required.)

## Inputs
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc` (the verified claims — the ground truth)
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.adoc` (the narrative)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml` (for variant classification reference)
- `glossary.yaml`

**Not** primary sources. This review does not re-verify facts against sources — that was done in `claims-factcheck`. This review compares narrative against claims only.

## The three checks

### Check 1 — Completeness (nothing dropped)

Walk the claims document claim by claim, in order. For each claim, locate where it appears in the narrative. Record the mapping:

```
Claim N → Narrative paragraph P / sentence S
```

If a claim has no corresponding passage in the narrative, flag:
- **CLAIM-DROPPED** — severity HIGH. The verified claim is missing from the narrative.

If a claim is only partially represented (e.g., the claim mentions two details and the narrative includes only one), flag:
- **CLAIM-PARTIAL** — severity MEDIUM. Specify what was dropped.

### Check 2 — No additions (nothing invented)

Walk the narrative paragraph by paragraph. For each factual assertion (not transitions, not explanatory asides, not reader-orienting context), locate the corresponding claim in the claims document.

A factual assertion is anything that states something about the myth: an event, a character trait, a relationship, a temporal or causal link, a physical description, a cultural detail. Transitional phrases ("Having established the cosmic order...") are not assertions unless they introduce new information.

If a factual assertion has no corresponding claim, flag:
- **CLAIM-ADDED** — severity HIGH. An unverified factual assertion has been introduced.

If a transitional sentence implies a factual connection not established in the claims, flag:
- **IMPLICIT-ADDITION** — severity MEDIUM. The transition suggests something the claims don't support.

### Check 3 — Fidelity (nothing distorted)

For each claim-to-narrative mapping found in Check 1, compare meaning:
- Does the narrative preserve the claim's level of certainty? (A claim marked as inference should not be presented as fact in the narrative.)
- Does the narrative preserve causal direction? ("A caused B" should not become "B caused A" or "A and B coincided.")
- Does the narrative preserve scope? ("Some scholars argue" should not become "Scholars agree.")
- Are markers preserved? Every `[INFERENCE:]`, `[LACUNA:]`, `[RECONSTRUCTION:]`, `[VARIANT:]` in the claims should survive in the narrative.

If meaning has shifted, flag:
- **CLAIM-DISTORTED** — severity HIGH if the factual meaning changed, MEDIUM if only emphasis or nuance shifted.

### Additional checks

- **Glossary fidelity**: confirm `glossary.yaml` renderings are used consistently. Any `never_use` terms: GLOSSARY-VIOLATION.
- **Forbidden words**: flag hits from the forbidden-words list.
- **Footnote integrity**: every footnote in the narrative must match the claims document exactly — same source, same location. No additions, removals, or reassignments. Flag FOOTNOTE-MISMATCH if different.

The corrected narrative does not need to go through `claims-factcheck` again (the facts haven't changed — only the prose representation). But it should go through `narrative-fidelity` again to confirm the fixes resolved the findings.

=== Sigurd vs. Fáfnir — strongest philological evidence

The Sigurd-Fáfnir narrative is preserved principally in the Eddic poem *Fáfnismál* in the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE), one of three closely-grouped poems of the Sigurd-cycle in the Codex (with *Reginsmál* preceding and *Sigrdrífumál* following in the manuscript order), and is also attested in the prose *Völsunga saga* (c. 1270 CE) and in the Middle High German *Nibelungenlied* (c. 1200 CE) as the related Germanic witness with Sigurd in the German form Siegfried.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* and the surrounding Codex Regius poems (*Reginsmál* preceding, *Sigrdrífumál* following) on Sigurd; *Völsunga saga* c. 1270 CE; *Nibelungenlied* c. 1200 CE as the related Middle High German Siegfried witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the related-Germanic-witness status of the Nibelungenlied.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

Sigurd is the son of Sigmund of the Völsung lineage, raised in the household of the smith Regin after his father's death, and the *Fáfnismál* presents him as a young hero on the threshold of his first major heroic deed, the killing of the dragon Fáfnir on the heath of Gnitaheiðr.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* and the surrounding Codex Regius poems (with *Reginsmál* on Sigurd's upbringing and Regin's role); on the Völsung lineage and Gnitaheiðr setting.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Reginsmál; Fáfnismál

Fáfnir is a dwarf transformed into a dragon by his guardianship of the cursed gold of Andvari, gold that Loki had earlier extorted from the dwarf Andvari to pay a wergild to Hreidmar (Fáfnir's and Regin's father) for the killing of Otr, and the curse on the gold is the load-bearing motif that drives the Sigurd-cycle from the killing of the dragon to Sigurd's own death.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* and *Fáfnismál*, on the cursed gold of Andvari, Loki's extortion of the hoard, the wergild for Otr, Fáfnir's transformation into a dragon, and the curse-driven trajectory of the cycle; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the cursed-gold and Sigurd-death motifs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Reginsmál; Fáfnismál

Regin, Fáfnir's brother and the smith who fosters Sigurd, forges the sword Gram from the fragments of Sigmund's broken sword and incites Sigurd to ambush Fáfnir on the heath, and the *Fáfnismál* opens with Sigurd at the moment of the killing-stroke, having dug a pit on the dragon's path to the water and lying in wait below.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* on the forging of Gram from Sigmund's sword-fragments and Regin's incitement; *Fáfnismál* opening on the pit-and-ambush mechanism.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál opening

The killing-stroke itself is delivered with the sword Gram from the pit: Sigurd thrusts upward into Fáfnir's underbelly as the dragon passes over the pit on his way to the water, and the *Fáfnismál* presents the killing-stroke as the central act around which the poem's dialogue is organised rather than as an extended combat-narrative.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* on Sigurd's upward thrust with Gram from the pit and on the structure of the poem as dialogue around the central killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

The post-killing dialogue between Sigurd and the dying Fáfnir occupies the bulk of the *Fáfnismál*: Fáfnir, mortally wounded, asks Sigurd's name, warns him of the curse on the gold, and supplies cosmological-and-genealogical lore in the wisdom-dialogue register that the *Vafþrúðnismál* and *Grímnismál* establish elsewhere in the Eddic corpus.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál*, on the post-killing wisdom-dialogue between Sigurd and Fáfnir, including the curse-warning and the cosmological-and-genealogical exchange; on the Eddic wisdom-dialogue register paralleled in *Vafþrúðnismál* and *Grímnismál*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál stanzas (post-killing dialogue)

After Fáfnir's death Regin requests that Sigurd roast the dragon's heart, and when Sigurd touches the cooking heart with his finger and licks the burned blood from his finger he gains understanding of the speech of birds, and the nuthatches in the surrounding trees warn him that Regin plans to kill him for the treasure.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* on the heart-roasting, the blood-tasting, the finger-burn, the bird-speech understanding, and the nuthatches' warning; on the resulting threat from Regin.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál (heart-roasting and bird-speech)

Sigurd, warned by the birds, kills Regin in turn — beheading him with Gram — and then takes Fáfnir's hoard onto his horse Grani, with the *Fáfnismál* ending on Sigurd in possession of the cursed gold and on the way to the next stage of the cycle (his encounter with the valkyrie Sigrdrífa / Brynhild in the *Sigrdrífumál*).footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* concluding stanzas and *Sigrdrífumál* opening, on Sigurd's killing of Regin, the loading of the hoard onto Grani, and the bridge to the Sigrdrífa / Brynhild encounter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál concluding stanzas; Sigrdrífumál opening

The Sigurd-cycle's outcome is ambiguous in a way that distinguishes it from the cosmogonic and sovereignty-establishment subtypes of the combat myth: Sigurd kills the dragon and gains the hoard, but the curse on the gold drives the long-arc tragedy of the cycle (Brynhild's death, the Burgundian disasters, Sigurd's own death at the hands of Gunnar's brother Gutthorm) and the killing therefore destroys rather than secures the hero's prosperity.footnote:[Larrington 2014, on the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle from *Fáfnismál* through *Sigrdrífumál* and the Burgundian poems to Sigurd's death; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the ambiguous-outcome reading and on the forward-link to Part II C7 (inversion problem).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-cycle long arc

[INFERENCE: the reading of the Sigurd-cycle's outcome as ambiguous — the killing brings the hero the treasure but also the curse that will destroy him — is the chapter's editorial reading of the long-arc tragedy of the cycle, drawn from Larrington 2014 on the Codex Regius poems and from Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the diction of the killing, and is presented in the chapter as the principal forward-link to Part II C7 of the present book (the inversion problem); the *Fáfnismál* itself does not in so many words frame the killing as destroying-rather-than-securing prosperity, but the curse-warning of Fáfnir to Sigurd and the long-arc trajectory of the surrounding Eddic poems supply the basis for the inference. | basis: Larrington 2014 on the curse-driven trajectory of the Sigurd-cycle including Fáfnir's curse-warning at *Fáfnismál*; Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the philological diction of the killing; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the C7-inversion forward-link | risk: the framing as "ambiguous outcome" is a comparative-typological reading rather than a direct attestation, and the *Fáfnismál* presents the killing as a heroic deed in its own right within the poem's own register.]

The Sigurd-cycle is also attested in the prose *Völsunga saga* (c. 1270 CE), which gives a continuous-narrative version of the cycle including the Fáfnir-killing episode, and the prose witness is treated by Old Norse scholarship as preserving older oral-tradition material alongside the Eddic poetic witnesses; the present chapter cites the *Völsunga saga* as a parallel witness rather than as the primary text for the *Fáfnismál* episode itself.footnote:[*Völsunga saga* c. 1270 CE as continuous-narrative parallel witness to the Sigurd-cycle including the Fáfnir-killing episode; Larrington 2014 on the Eddic-and-prose witness relationship.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-cycle parallel witnesses

The Middle High German *Nibelungenlied* (c. 1200 CE) is the related Germanic witness to the Sigurd-cycle, with Sigurd in the German form Siegfried and the dragon-killing transposed into a brief retrospective narrative episode rather than treated as a focal scene of the poem; the *Nibelungenlied* is cited in the present chapter at corpus level as the related Germanic witness rather than as a primary text for the Fáfnir-episode.footnote:[*Nibelungenlied* c. 1200 CE on Siegfried's dragon-killing in retrospective narrative; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the related-Germanic-witness status of the Nibelungenlied as parallel to the Old Norse Sigurd material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd / Siegfried Germanic parallel

In Watkins's reading in *How to Kill a Dragon* chapter 42, the Eddic *Fáfnismál* diction of Sigurd's killing is a direct reflex of the reconstructed Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula: the verb of killing (Old Norse *vega*, "to kill in combat"), its subject (the named hero), and its object (the named dragon) instantiate in Old Norse the same syntactic-and-semantic structure that Vedic *áhann áhim* ("he slew the serpent") instantiates in Vedic Sanskrit and that Hittite *kuenta . . . illuyanka* ("he killed the serpent") instantiates in Hittite.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on the *Fáfnismál* diction of Sigurd's killing as a direct reflex of the reconstructed IE HERO SLAY SERPENT formula; Vedic *áhann áhim* (RV 1.32) and Hittite *kuenta . . . illuyanka* (CTH 321) as the comparative philological exhibits of the same formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42

Watkins's argument in chapter 42 is that the *Fáfnismál* preserves the IE poetic formula at the level of the verbal diction of the killing-stroke itself, not merely at the level of the structural-typological pattern, and that the Norse sub-case is therefore among the strongest philological evidence in the post-Iranian, post-Vedic, post-Anatolian corpus for the reconstructed formula's status as Indo-European poetic inheritance rather than as accidental cross-tradition convergence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on the philological strength of the Norse evidence as inheritance rather than convergence; on the *Fáfnismál* diction of the killing-stroke as the load-bearing locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42

The Sigurd-Fáfnir episode is also one of the iconographically densest in the post-Vedic IE corpus, with several extant Scandinavian rune-stones and rock carvings from the 11th and 12th centuries CE depicting Sigurd's killing of Fáfnir (the Ramsund carving in Södermanland c. 1030 CE is the principal-and-best-preserved witness), and the iconographic dossier supplements the Eddic and prose textual witnesses as a third register of attestation alongside the verbal-narrative and the philological-formulaic.footnote:[Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the Sigurd-Fáfnir iconographic tradition as a register of attestation alongside the Eddic and prose witnesses; Ramsund carving (Södermanland c. 1030 CE) as principal Scandinavian witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the iconographic dossier as a third register of attestation; specific stone-count to be pinned at glossary-lock against a rune-stone-specialist source.]

=== Beowulf vs. Grendel and the dragon

The Old English poem *Beowulf* survives in a single manuscript witness — London, British Library, Cotton Vitellius A.xv (the Nowell Codex), c. 1000 CE — and is conventionally assigned to a composition-period bracketed somewhere in the 7th to 10th centuries CE, with the dragon-episode forming the third and final movement of the poem after the Grendel-episode and the Grendel's-mother-episode that dominate the poem's first two movements.footnote:[*Beowulf*, single-manuscript witness London BL Cotton Vitellius A.xv (Nowell Codex), c. 1000 CE; composition 7th–10th c. CE in standard scholarly bracketing; three-movement structure (Grendel / Grendel's mother / dragon) with the dragon-episode as third and final movement; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the manuscript and composition-dating conventions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf is a Geatish hero — a member of the Gēatas, a southern Scandinavian people — who in the poem's first movement travels to the Danish court of king Hrothgar to defend the hall Heorot from the man-eating monster Grendel, the descendant of Cain who has been killing Hrothgar's warriors at Heorot for twelve years.footnote:[*Beowulf*, the Geatish-arrival passage (Beowulf's introduction and Geatish identity from approximately line 194 onwards in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering), with Grendel's introduction, his descent from Cain, and his twelve-year depredations at Heorot narrated at lines 86–193 in the same line-numbering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf kills Grendel by tearing off his arm in a wrestling-match in the dark hall of Heorot, refusing weapons because Grendel uses none, and Grendel flees mortally wounded to die in the fenland-mere where his mother also dwells; the killing-encounter is at lines 710–836 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 710–836 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on the wrestling-match killing of Grendel, the arm-tearing, the weaponless contest, and Grendel's flight to the fen-mere.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The next night Grendel's mother attacks Heorot to avenge her son, and Beowulf descends into the underwater hall of the fen-mere to kill her with a giant-forged sword that he finds in the underwater hall itself; the encounter is at lines 1251–1887 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 1251–1887 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on Grendel's mother's revenge-attack, Beowulf's descent into the fen-mere, the underwater-hall combat, and the giant-forged sword.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The poem's third and final movement is set fifty years after the Grendel and Grendel's-mother encounters, with Beowulf now an old king of the Gēatas, when a treasure-hoarding dragon — the *draca* — that has been sleeping under an ancient burial-mound is roused by a thief who steals a single cup from the hoard and begins flying out by night to burn the Geatish countryside in revenge; the dragon-episode is at lines 2200–3182 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 2200–3182 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on the fifty-year interval, the aged-king status, the thief-and-cup motif, the burial-mound location of the hoard, the dragon's nocturnal raids, and the third-movement framing; Watkins 1995 Part V on the dragon-slaying philological diction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf, now an old king, takes a small band of retainers to confront the dragon at the burial-mound; in the combat the dragon's fire is too much for his shield and his retainers flee except for the young Wiglaf, who stays with the king and helps him land the killing-blow on the dragon, but Beowulf is mortally wounded by the dragon's bite and dies in Wiglaf's arms after killing the dragon.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 2200–3182 (the dragon-episode), with the principal narrative beats at lines 2510–2820 (the combat itself, Wiglaf's loyalty, the killing of the dragon, and Beowulf's death-wound); Watkins 1995, Part V, on the IE-reflex reading of the dragon-killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The aged-king status of Beowulf at the dragon-episode is the principal narrative-thematic distinction between the *Beowulf* dragon-combat and the standard heroic-initiation dragon-combat of the Sigurd-Fáfnir type: Beowulf has already done his major-hero deeds in the first two movements of the poem, and the dragon-combat is end-of-life and death-bringing rather than heroic-initiatory and life-establishing.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 2200–2820 on the aged-king status; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the contrast between aged-king dragon-combat and heroic-initiation dragon-combat patterns; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the contrast.]

=== Lugh vs. Balor — the Second Battle of Mag Tuired

The Lugh-Balor narrative is preserved principally in the Middle Irish prose narrative *Cath Maige Tuired* ("The Battle of Mag Tuired"), transmitted in manuscripts from the 12th to the 16th century CE and presenting the Second Battle of Mag Tuired as the decisive combat between the Tuatha Dé Danann (the Irish gods-and-ancestors) and the Fomorians (a monstrous-and-oppressive people associated with the sea and with tyranny over the Tuatha Dé).footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired*, manuscripts 12th–16th c. CE; standard critical edition Gray 1982 (Irish Texts Society) — to be pinned at glossary-lock per inventory.approved.yaml identifier_missing flag; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Tuatha Dé Danann / Fomorians combat-axis; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the Second Battle of Mag Tuired as the decisive Tuatha-Dé combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Lugh is a young warrior-hero of the Tuatha Dé Danann, the son of Cian (a Tuatha Dé) and of Ethniu (the daughter of the Fomorian Balor), and his hybrid lineage — Tuatha Dé father and Fomorian mother — is the load-bearing genealogical motif of the *Cath Maige Tuired* narrative since it positions Lugh as the only figure who can kill his Fomorian grandfather Balor and end the Fomorian tyranny.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Lugh's hybrid lineage (Cian / Ethniu / Balor) and on the kill-the-grandfather motif; West 2007 ch. 6 on the same lineage as the IE-warrior structural-genealogical pattern; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the hybrid-lineage motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Balor is the king (or principal warrior) of the Fomorians and is named in the *Cath Maige Tuired* as "Balor of the Evil Eye" (Balor Birug-derc, "Balor of the piercing eye") because of his single enormous destructive eye that can incinerate any host on which it is opened, and the tactical problem of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired is the problem of how to bring the eye down before it is opened on the Tuatha Dé host.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Balor's title "Balor of the Evil Eye" / Balor Birug-derc; on the destructive eye and the tactical-narrative problem it poses; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the evil-eye motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The killing-stroke at the Second Battle of Mag Tuired is delivered by Lugh with a sling-stone that strikes Balor's eye at the moment of the eye's opening, driving the eye through the back of Balor's head so that its destructive gaze is turned on the Fomorian host behind Balor and breaks their order; the *Cath Maige Tuired* narrates the killing-stroke as the decisive moment of the battle that ends the Fomorian tyranny and inaugurates Tuatha Dé Danann rule.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Lugh's sling-stone killing of Balor, on the eye-driven-through-the-head reversal, on the destruction of the Fomorian host by the redirected eye, and on the inauguration of Tuatha Dé rule; West 2007 ch. 6 on the same narrative-structural pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The *Cath Maige Tuired* narrative is structured around the wider battle-context of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired, in which the Tuatha Dé and the Fomorians fight a decisive engagement on the plain of Mag Tuired (in modern County Sligo), with the Lugh-Balor encounter as the decisive single-combat at the centre of the wider battle and with Lugh's status as battle-leader of the Tuatha Dé established by the prelude of the prose narrative.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on the wider battle-context, on the plain of Mag Tuired (County Sligo) as the setting, and on Lugh's battle-leader role; West 2007 ch. 6 on the centred-single-combat structure of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer narrative.]

The Lugh-Balor encounter is read by Indo-European comparativists — principally following Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments and West's 2007 synthesis — as a Celtic reflex of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern, with Lugh in the warrior-pole and Balor in the morphologically-distinct adversary-pole; the Celtic reflex is morphologically distinctive in two respects, namely that Balor is not a serpentine adversary but a one-eyed giant, and that the killing-weapon is a projectile (sling-stone) rather than a sword or thunderbolt.footnote:[West 2007 ch. 6 on the Celtic reflex of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern; on the morphologically-distinctive features of the Lugh-Balor encounter (one-eyed-giant adversary; sling-stone killing-weapon); brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the same morphological distinctiveness.]

=== The Red Dragon and the White Dragon — Welsh combat

The Welsh Red-and-White Dragon narrative is preserved in two principal primary witnesses with distinct framings: the Latin *Historia Brittonum* (the Nennius-recension, conventionally dated c. 828 CE) gives a politicised version in which the two dragons are interpreted as the Britons and the Saxons, and the Middle Welsh prose tale *Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (manuscripts from c. 1350 CE onward) preserves an older, less politicised version in which the combat is an annual cosmological-and-territorial disturbance to be ended by the king Lludd at the urging of his brother Llefelys.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* c. 828 CE (Nennius recension) on the Britons-vs-Saxons political reading; *Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (manuscripts White Book of Rhydderch c. 1350 CE; Red Book of Hergest c. 1382–1410 CE) on the annual-disturbance reading; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the two-recension distinction; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the two-witness framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Historia Brittonum* the combat-narrative is set within the Vortigern-and-Ambrosius episode: Vortigern, the British king, attempts to build a fortress at Dinas Emrys, but the foundations sink each night; Ambrosius (the boy-prophet who is, in the narrative, the figure who later becomes Merlin in the post-Geoffrey-of-Monmouth tradition) reveals that beneath the foundation-site is a hidden underground pool in which a red dragon and a white dragon fight, and the political-allegorical reading is that the red dragon is the Britons and the white dragon is the Saxons.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* on the Vortigern-Ambrosius episode at Dinas Emrys, on the foundation-sinking, on the underground pool, on the red-dragon-vs-white-dragon combat, and on the Britons-vs-Saxons political-allegorical reading; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

The political-allegorical reading of *Historia Brittonum* casts the Welsh Red-and-White Dragon combat as an early-medieval prophetic-and-political narrative — the red Welsh dragon will eventually defeat the white Saxon dragon, and the political-allegorical frame is load-bearing for the early-medieval recension of the narrative — and the red-dragon symbol enters the long-arc British (and specifically Welsh) political iconography from this *Historia Brittonum* moment onward.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* on the political-allegorical frame and on the prophetic outcome; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the long-arc Welsh-political iconographic reception of the red dragon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Lludd and Llefelys* the combat-narrative is one of three plagues that afflict the Island of Britain in the time of king Lludd: the first plague is the Coraniaid (a magical foreign people who hear all conversations), the second plague is the dragon-combat (an annual scream that destroys crops, terrifies pregnant women, and devastates the realm), and the third plague is a magical thief who steals the king's stores; Lludd, on his brother Llefelys's advice, addresses each of the three plagues in turn.footnote:[*Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* on the three-plague structure (Coraniaid; dragon-combat; magical thief); on Lludd's address to each plague on his brother Llefelys's advice; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the three-plague structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Lludd and Llefelys* the dragon-combat is presented as an annual cosmological-and-territorial disturbance: every May Eve the two dragons rise from beneath the centre of Britain and fight in the air with a scream that shakes the realm and destroys crops, and the resolution prescribed by Llefelys is to find the centre of the Island, dig a pit, fill the pit with mead, lure the two dragons into the pit, capture them when they are drunk on the mead, and bury them in a stone chest at Dinas Emrys.footnote:[*Lludd and Llefelys* on the May-Eve annual disturbance, on the central location of the combat, on the mead-pit-and-burial resolution, and on the Dinas Emrys burial-site; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the resolution-mechanism.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

[VARIANT: primary=*Historia Brittonum* (red-and-white-dragon political allegory; Britons-vs-Saxons reading; the dragons fight beneath Dinas Emrys and Vortigern's fortress sinks; the prophetic-political resolution) | alt=*Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (annual May-Eve disturbance; the dragons are buried at Dinas Emrys after being lured into a mead-pit; cosmological-territorial resolution rather than political-allegorical) | chosen: present-both | reason: the two recensions present two distinct framings of the same underlying combat-tradition, with *Historia Brittonum* the political-allegorical reading of c. 828 CE and *Lludd and Llefelys* the older non-political reading of the *Mabinogion* tradition; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the two-witness side-by-side presentation.]

=== Batraz and the Nart combats — Ossetic

The Batraz narrative is preserved in the Ossetic Nart saga corpus, an oral-epic tradition recorded in the 19th and 20th centuries CE from Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen narrators across the North Caucasus, with the principal scholarly anchor Georges Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments and the principal IE-comparative apparatus West 2007's chapter 6.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus, 19th–20th c. CE oral-epic recordings from Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen narrators; Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments as the principal scholarly precedent for the IE-comparative reading; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Scytho-Alanic IE warrior / dragon-slayer reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Narts are the heroic figures of the North Caucasian epic tradition — a category-class of warrior-figures who occupy the epic narrative-space and who are the principal characters of the Ossetic Nart saga corpus and of the cognate Circassian and Chechen Nart traditions — with Batraz one of the principal Nart warrior-figures in the Ossetic version of the corpus.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on the Narts as heroic figures of the North Caucasian epic tradition; West 2007 ch. 6 on Batraz as a principal Ossetic Nart warrior-figure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Batraz is distinguished within the Ossetic Nart corpus by his steel body — he is born from a tumour or boil on the back of his father Khamits, plunges himself into a furnace, and emerges with a body of steel — and the steel-body motif is the principal physical-and-narrative attribute that distinguishes Batraz from other Narts within the corpus.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on Batraz's steel-body origin (born from Khamits's back; furnace-tempering; steel-body status); West 2007 ch. 6 on the steel-body motif as IE-warrior structural attribute.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Batraz fights monstrous giants and dragons across multiple episodes of the Nart corpus: against the giant Mukara, against various dragons-and-monstrous-figures, and against opposing Nart-clans within the Ossetic tradition; the Nart corpus is not a single continuous narrative but a cluster of episodic combats, with each combat-episode preserving the warrior-pole of the IE pattern in distinctive Ossetic morphology.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on Batraz's combats against the giant Mukara and against various other giant and dragon adversaries within the corpus; West 2007 ch. 6 on the episodic-cluster structure of the Nart corpus and on the warrior-pole preservation; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the episodic-cluster structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

In Dumézil's twentieth-century reading, Batraz is the Scytho-Alanic Indo-European reflex of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern — preserved within the Ossetic-and-cognate Nart traditions as the indigenous-and-uninterrupted continuation of an ancient Iranian-Indo-European warrior-figure type — and the steel-body motif is read by Dumézil as a distinctively-developed feature of the warrior-pole within the Scytho-Alanic stratum.footnote:[West 2007 ch. 6 on Dumézil's reading of Batraz as Scytho-Alanic IE warrior / dragon-slayer reflex; on the Ossetic-and-cognate Nart traditions as the uninterrupted Scytho-Alanic continuation; on the steel-body motif as Scytho-Alanic distinctive development.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen Nart corpora preserve multiple recorded variants of the Batraz combats across narrators, with the Ossetic-narrator variants forming the principal corpus for the Dumézilian IE-comparative reading and the Circassian-and-Chechen variants providing comparative-and-control material whose narrative-detail differences are the subject of twentieth- and twenty-first-century North Caucasian folkloric and philological scholarship.footnote:[Ossetic, Circassian, and Chechen Nart corpora on the multiple-narrator variant structure; West 2007 ch. 6 on the variant-comparative methodological framing; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the multiple-narrator-variant structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

=== Sigurd vs. Fáfnir — strongest philological evidence

The Sigurd-Fáfnir narrative is preserved principally in the Eddic poem *Fáfnismál* in the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE), one of three closely-grouped poems of the Sigurd-cycle in the Codex (with *Reginsmál* preceding and *Sigrdrífumál* following in the manuscript order), and is also attested in the prose *Völsunga saga* (c. 1270 CE) and in the Middle High German *Nibelungenlied* (c. 1200 CE) as the related Germanic witness with Sigurd in the German form Siegfried.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* and the surrounding Codex Regius poems (*Reginsmál* preceding, *Sigrdrífumál* following) on Sigurd; *Völsunga saga* c. 1270 CE; *Nibelungenlied* c. 1200 CE as the related Middle High German Siegfried witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the related-Germanic-witness status of the Nibelungenlied.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

Sigurd is the son of Sigmund of the Völsung lineage, raised in the household of the smith Regin after his father's death, and the *Fáfnismál* presents him as a young hero on the threshold of his first major heroic deed, the killing of the dragon Fáfnir on the heath of Gnitaheiðr.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* and the surrounding Codex Regius poems (with *Reginsmál* on Sigurd's upbringing and Regin's role); on the Völsung lineage and Gnitaheiðr setting.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Reginsmál; Fáfnismál

Fáfnir is a dwarf transformed into a dragon by his guardianship of the cursed gold of Andvari, gold that Loki had earlier extorted from the dwarf Andvari to pay a wergild to Hreidmar (Fáfnir's and Regin's father) for the killing of Otr, and the curse on the gold is the load-bearing motif that drives the Sigurd-cycle from the killing of the dragon to Sigurd's own death.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* and *Fáfnismál*, on the cursed gold of Andvari, Loki's extortion of the hoard, the wergild for Otr, Fáfnir's transformation into a dragon, and the curse-driven trajectory of the cycle; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the cursed-gold and Sigurd-death motifs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Reginsmál; Fáfnismál

Regin, Fáfnir's brother and the smith who fosters Sigurd, forges the sword Gram from the fragments of Sigmund's broken sword and incites Sigurd to ambush Fáfnir on the heath, and the *Fáfnismál* opens with Sigurd at the moment of the killing-stroke, having dug a pit on the dragon's path to the water and lying in wait below.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* on the forging of Gram from Sigmund's sword-fragments and Regin's incitement; *Fáfnismál* opening on the pit-and-ambush mechanism.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál opening

The killing-stroke itself is delivered with the sword Gram from the pit: Sigurd thrusts upward into Fáfnir's underbelly as the dragon passes over the pit on his way to the water, and the *Fáfnismál* presents the killing-stroke as the central act around which the poem's dialogue is organised rather than as an extended combat-narrative.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* on Sigurd's upward thrust with Gram from the pit and on the structure of the poem as dialogue around the central killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

The post-killing dialogue between Sigurd and the dying Fáfnir occupies the bulk of the *Fáfnismál*: Fáfnir, mortally wounded, asks Sigurd's name, warns him of the curse on the gold, and supplies cosmological-and-genealogical lore in the wisdom-dialogue register that the *Vafþrúðnismál* and *Grímnismál* establish elsewhere in the Eddic corpus.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál*, on the post-killing wisdom-dialogue between Sigurd and Fáfnir, including the curse-warning and the cosmological-and-genealogical exchange; on the Eddic wisdom-dialogue register paralleled in *Vafþrúðnismál* and *Grímnismál*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál stanzas (post-killing dialogue)

After Fáfnir's death Regin requests that Sigurd roast the dragon's heart, and when Sigurd touches the cooking heart with his finger and licks the burned blood from his finger he gains understanding of the speech of birds, and the nuthatches in the surrounding trees warn him that Regin plans to kill him for the treasure.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* on the heart-roasting, the blood-tasting, the finger-burn, the bird-speech understanding, and the nuthatches' warning; on the resulting threat from Regin.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál (heart-roasting and bird-speech)

Sigurd, warned by the birds, kills Regin in turn — beheading him with Gram — and then takes Fáfnir's hoard onto his horse Grani, with the *Fáfnismál* ending on Sigurd in possession of the cursed gold and on the way to the next stage of the cycle (his encounter with the valkyrie Sigrdrífa / Brynhild in the *Sigrdrífumál*).footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* concluding stanzas and *Sigrdrífumál* opening, on Sigurd's killing of Regin, the loading of the hoard onto Grani, and the bridge to the Sigrdrífa / Brynhild encounter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál concluding stanzas; Sigrdrífumál opening

The Sigurd-cycle's outcome is ambiguous in a way that distinguishes it from the cosmogonic and sovereignty-establishment subtypes of the combat myth: Sigurd kills the dragon and gains the hoard, but the curse on the gold drives the long-arc tragedy of the cycle (Brynhild's death, the Burgundian disasters, Sigurd's own death at the hands of Gunnar's brother Gutthorm) and the killing therefore destroys rather than secures the hero's prosperity.footnote:[Larrington 2014, on the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle from *Fáfnismál* through *Sigrdrífumál* and the Burgundian poems to Sigurd's death; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the ambiguous-outcome reading and on the forward-link to Part II C7 (inversion problem).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-cycle long arc

[INFERENCE: the reading of the Sigurd-cycle's outcome as ambiguous — the killing brings the hero the treasure but also the curse that will destroy him — is the chapter's editorial reading of the long-arc tragedy of the cycle, drawn from Larrington 2014 on the Codex Regius poems and from Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the diction of the killing, and is presented in the chapter as the principal forward-link to Part II C7 of the present book (the inversion problem); the *Fáfnismál* itself does not in so many words frame the killing as destroying-rather-than-securing prosperity, but the curse-warning of Fáfnir to Sigurd and the long-arc trajectory of the surrounding Eddic poems supply the basis for the inference. | basis: Larrington 2014 on the curse-driven trajectory of the Sigurd-cycle including Fáfnir's curse-warning at *Fáfnismál*; Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the philological diction of the killing; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the C7-inversion forward-link | risk: the framing as "ambiguous outcome" is a comparative-typological reading rather than a direct attestation, and the *Fáfnismál* presents the killing as a heroic deed in its own right within the poem's own register.]

The Sigurd-cycle is also attested in the prose *Völsunga saga* (c. 1270 CE), which gives a continuous-narrative version of the cycle including the Fáfnir-killing episode, and the prose witness is treated by Old Norse scholarship as preserving older oral-tradition material alongside the Eddic poetic witnesses; the present chapter cites the *Völsunga saga* as a parallel witness rather than as the primary text for the *Fáfnismál* episode itself.footnote:[*Völsunga saga* c. 1270 CE as continuous-narrative parallel witness to the Sigurd-cycle including the Fáfnir-killing episode; Larrington 2014 on the Eddic-and-prose witness relationship.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-cycle parallel witnesses

The Middle High German *Nibelungenlied* (c. 1200 CE) is the related Germanic witness to the Sigurd-cycle, with Sigurd in the German form Siegfried and the dragon-killing transposed into a brief retrospective narrative episode rather than treated as a focal scene of the poem; the *Nibelungenlied* is cited in the present chapter at corpus level as the related Germanic witness rather than as a primary text for the Fáfnir-episode.footnote:[*Nibelungenlied* c. 1200 CE on Siegfried's dragon-killing in retrospective narrative; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the related-Germanic-witness status of the Nibelungenlied as parallel to the Old Norse Sigurd material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd / Siegfried Germanic parallel

In Watkins's reading in *How to Kill a Dragon* chapter 42, the Eddic *Fáfnismál* diction of Sigurd's killing is a direct reflex of the reconstructed Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula: the verb of killing (Old Norse *vega*, "to kill in combat"), its subject (the named hero), and its object (the named dragon) instantiate in Old Norse the same syntactic-and-semantic structure that Vedic *áhann áhim* ("he slew the serpent") instantiates in Vedic Sanskrit and that Hittite *kuenta . . . illuyanka* ("he killed the serpent") instantiates in Hittite.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on the *Fáfnismál* diction of Sigurd's killing as a direct reflex of the reconstructed IE HERO SLAY SERPENT formula; Vedic *áhann áhim* (RV 1.32) and Hittite *kuenta . . . illuyanka* (CTH 321) as the comparative philological exhibits of the same formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42

Watkins's argument in chapter 42 is that the *Fáfnismál* preserves the IE poetic formula at the level of the verbal diction of the killing-stroke itself, not merely at the level of the structural-typological pattern, and that the Norse sub-case is therefore among the strongest philological evidence in the post-Iranian, post-Vedic, post-Anatolian corpus for the reconstructed formula's status as Indo-European poetic inheritance rather than as accidental cross-tradition convergence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on the philological strength of the Norse evidence as inheritance rather than convergence; on the *Fáfnismál* diction of the killing-stroke as the load-bearing locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42

The Sigurd-Fáfnir episode is also one of the iconographically densest in the post-Vedic IE corpus, with several extant Scandinavian rune-stones and rock carvings from the 11th and 12th centuries CE depicting Sigurd's killing of Fáfnir (the Ramsund carving in Södermanland c. 1030 CE is the principal-and-best-preserved witness), and the iconographic dossier supplements the Eddic and prose textual witnesses as a third register of attestation alongside the verbal-narrative and the philological-formulaic.footnote:[Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the Sigurd-Fáfnir iconographic tradition as a register of attestation alongside the Eddic and prose witnesses; Ramsund carving (Södermanland c. 1030 CE) as principal Scandinavian witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the iconographic dossier as a third register of attestation; specific stone-count to be pinned at glossary-lock against a rune-stone-specialist source.]

=== Beowulf vs. Grendel and the dragon

The Old English poem *Beowulf* survives in a single manuscript witness — London, British Library, Cotton Vitellius A.xv (the Nowell Codex), c. 1000 CE — and is conventionally assigned to a composition-period bracketed somewhere in the 7th to 10th centuries CE, with the dragon-episode forming the third and final movement of the poem after the Grendel-episode and the Grendel's-mother-episode that dominate the poem's first two movements.footnote:[*Beowulf*, single-manuscript witness London BL Cotton Vitellius A.xv (Nowell Codex), c. 1000 CE; composition 7th–10th c. CE in standard scholarly bracketing; three-movement structure (Grendel / Grendel's mother / dragon) with the dragon-episode as third and final movement; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the manuscript and composition-dating conventions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf is a Geatish hero — a member of the Gēatas, a southern Scandinavian people — who in the poem's first movement travels to the Danish court of king Hrothgar to defend the hall Heorot from the man-eating monster Grendel, the descendant of Cain who has been killing Hrothgar's warriors at Heorot for twelve years.footnote:[*Beowulf*, the Geatish-arrival passage (Beowulf's introduction and Geatish identity from approximately line 194 onwards in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering), with Grendel's introduction, his descent from Cain, and his twelve-year depredations at Heorot narrated at lines 86–193 in the same line-numbering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf kills Grendel by tearing off his arm in a wrestling-match in the dark hall of Heorot, refusing weapons because Grendel uses none, and Grendel flees mortally wounded to die in the fenland-mere where his mother also dwells; the killing-encounter is at lines 710–836 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 710–836 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on the wrestling-match killing of Grendel, the arm-tearing, the weaponless contest, and Grendel's flight to the fen-mere.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The next night Grendel's mother attacks Heorot to avenge her son, and Beowulf descends into the underwater hall of the fen-mere to kill her with a giant-forged sword that he finds in the underwater hall itself; the encounter is at lines 1251–1887 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 1251–1887 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on Grendel's mother's revenge-attack, Beowulf's descent into the fen-mere, the underwater-hall combat, and the giant-forged sword.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The poem's third and final movement is set fifty years after the Grendel and Grendel's-mother encounters, with Beowulf now an old king of the Gēatas, when a treasure-hoarding dragon — the *draca* — that has been sleeping under an ancient burial-mound is roused by a thief who steals a single cup from the hoard and begins flying out by night to burn the Geatish countryside in revenge; the dragon-episode is at lines 2200–3182 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 2200–3182 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on the fifty-year interval, the aged-king status, the thief-and-cup motif, the burial-mound location of the hoard, the dragon's nocturnal raids, and the third-movement framing; Watkins 1995 Part V on the dragon-slaying philological diction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf, now an old king, takes a small band of retainers to confront the dragon at the burial-mound; in the combat the dragon's fire is too much for his shield and his retainers flee except for the young Wiglaf, who stays with the king and helps him land the killing-blow on the dragon, but Beowulf is mortally wounded by the dragon's bite and dies in Wiglaf's arms after killing the dragon.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 2200–3182 (the dragon-episode), with the principal narrative beats at lines 2510–2820 (the combat itself, Wiglaf's loyalty, the killing of the dragon, and Beowulf's death-wound); Watkins 1995, Part V, on the IE-reflex reading of the dragon-killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The aged-king status of Beowulf at the dragon-episode is the principal narrative-thematic distinction between the *Beowulf* dragon-combat and the standard heroic-initiation dragon-combat of the Sigurd-Fáfnir type: Beowulf has already done his major-hero deeds in the first two movements of the poem, and the dragon-combat is end-of-life and death-bringing rather than heroic-initiatory and life-establishing.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 2200–2820 on the aged-king status; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the contrast between aged-king dragon-combat and heroic-initiation dragon-combat patterns; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the contrast.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

=== Lugh vs. Balor — the Second Battle of Mag Tuired

The Lugh-Balor narrative is preserved principally in the Middle Irish prose narrative *Cath Maige Tuired* ("The Battle of Mag Tuired"), transmitted in manuscripts from the 12th to the 16th century CE and presenting the Second Battle of Mag Tuired as the decisive combat between the Tuatha Dé Danann (the Irish gods-and-ancestors) and the Fomorians (a monstrous-and-oppressive people associated with the sea and with tyranny over the Tuatha Dé).footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired*, manuscripts 12th–16th c. CE; standard critical edition Gray 1982 (Irish Texts Society) — to be pinned at glossary-lock per inventory.approved.yaml identifier_missing flag; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Tuatha Dé Danann / Fomorians combat-axis; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the Second Battle of Mag Tuired as the decisive Tuatha-Dé combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Lugh is a young warrior-hero of the Tuatha Dé Danann, the son of Cian (a Tuatha Dé) and of Ethniu (the daughter of the Fomorian Balor), and his hybrid lineage — Tuatha Dé father and Fomorian mother — is the load-bearing genealogical motif of the *Cath Maige Tuired* narrative since it positions Lugh as the only figure who can kill his Fomorian grandfather Balor and end the Fomorian tyranny.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Lugh's hybrid lineage (Cian / Ethniu / Balor) and on the kill-the-grandfather motif; West 2007 ch. 6 on the same lineage as the IE-warrior structural-genealogical pattern; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the hybrid-lineage motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Balor is the king (or principal warrior) of the Fomorians and is named in the *Cath Maige Tuired* as "Balor of the Evil Eye" (Balor Birug-derc, "Balor of the piercing eye") because of his single enormous destructive eye that can incinerate any host on which it is opened, and the tactical problem of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired is the problem of how to bring the eye down before it is opened on the Tuatha Dé host.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Balor's title "Balor of the Evil Eye" / Balor Birug-derc; on the destructive eye and the tactical-narrative problem it poses; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the evil-eye motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The killing-stroke at the Second Battle of Mag Tuired is delivered by Lugh with a sling-stone that strikes Balor's eye at the moment of the eye's opening, driving the eye through the back of Balor's head so that its destructive gaze is turned on the Fomorian host behind Balor and breaks their order; the *Cath Maige Tuired* narrates the killing-stroke as the decisive moment of the battle that ends the Fomorian tyranny and inaugurates Tuatha Dé Danann rule.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Lugh's sling-stone killing of Balor, on the eye-driven-through-the-head reversal, on the destruction of the Fomorian host by the redirected eye, and on the inauguration of Tuatha Dé rule; West 2007 ch. 6 on the same narrative-structural pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The *Cath Maige Tuired* narrative is structured around the wider battle-context of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired, in which the Tuatha Dé and the Fomorians fight a decisive engagement on the plain of Mag Tuired (in modern County Sligo), with the Lugh-Balor encounter as the decisive single-combat at the centre of the wider battle and with Lugh's status as battle-leader of the Tuatha Dé established by the prelude of the prose narrative.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on the wider battle-context, on the plain of Mag Tuired (County Sligo) as the setting, and on Lugh's battle-leader role; West 2007 ch. 6 on the centred-single-combat structure of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer narrative.]

The Lugh-Balor encounter is read by Indo-European comparativists — principally following Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments and West's 2007 synthesis — as a Celtic reflex of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern, with Lugh in the warrior-pole and Balor in the morphologically-distinct adversary-pole; the Celtic reflex is morphologically distinctive in two respects, namely that Balor is not a serpentine adversary but a one-eyed giant, and that the killing-weapon is a projectile (sling-stone) rather than a sword or thunderbolt.footnote:[West 2007 ch. 6 on the Celtic reflex of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern; on the morphologically-distinctive features of the Lugh-Balor encounter (one-eyed-giant adversary; sling-stone killing-weapon); brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the same morphological distinctiveness.]

=== The Red Dragon and the White Dragon — Welsh combat

The Welsh Red-and-White Dragon narrative is preserved in two principal primary witnesses with distinct framings: the Latin *Historia Brittonum* (the Nennius-recension, conventionally dated c. 828 CE) gives a politicised version in which the two dragons are interpreted as the Britons and the Saxons, and the Middle Welsh prose tale *Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (manuscripts from c. 1350 CE onward) preserves an older, less politicised version in which the combat is an annual cosmological-and-territorial disturbance to be ended by the king Lludd at the urging of his brother Llefelys.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* c. 828 CE (Nennius recension) on the Britons-vs-Saxons political reading; *Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (manuscripts White Book of Rhydderch c. 1350 CE; Red Book of Hergest c. 1382–1410 CE) on the annual-disturbance reading; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the two-recension distinction; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the two-witness framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Historia Brittonum* the combat-narrative is set within the Vortigern-and-Ambrosius episode: Vortigern, the British king, attempts to build a fortress at Dinas Emrys, but the foundations sink each night; Ambrosius (the boy-prophet who is, in the narrative, the figure who later becomes Merlin in the post-Geoffrey-of-Monmouth tradition) reveals that beneath the foundation-site is a hidden underground pool in which a red dragon and a white dragon fight, and the political-allegorical reading is that the red dragon is the Britons and the white dragon is the Saxons.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* on the Vortigern-Ambrosius episode at Dinas Emrys, on the foundation-sinking, on the underground pool, on the red-dragon-vs-white-dragon combat, and on the Britons-vs-Saxons political-allegorical reading; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

The political-allegorical reading of *Historia Brittonum* casts the Welsh Red-and-White Dragon combat as an early-medieval prophetic-and-political narrative — the red Welsh dragon will eventually defeat the white Saxon dragon, and the political-allegorical frame is load-bearing for the early-medieval recension of the narrative — and the red-dragon symbol enters the long-arc British (and specifically Welsh) political iconography from this *Historia Brittonum* moment onward.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* on the political-allegorical frame and on the prophetic outcome; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the long-arc Welsh-political iconographic reception of the red dragon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Lludd and Llefelys* the combat-narrative is one of three plagues that afflict the Island of Britain in the time of king Lludd: the first plague is the Coraniaid (a magical foreign people who hear all conversations), the second plague is the dragon-combat (an annual scream that destroys crops, terrifies pregnant women, and devastates the realm), and the third plague is a magical thief who steals the king's stores; Lludd, on his brother Llefelys's advice, addresses each of the three plagues in turn.footnote:[*Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* on the three-plague structure (Coraniaid; dragon-combat; magical thief); on Lludd's address to each plague on his brother Llefelys's advice; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the three-plague structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Lludd and Llefelys* the dragon-combat is presented as an annual cosmological-and-territorial disturbance: every May Eve the two dragons rise from beneath the centre of Britain and fight in the air with a scream that shakes the realm and destroys crops, and the resolution prescribed by Llefelys is to find the centre of the Island, dig a pit, fill the pit with mead, lure the two dragons into the pit, capture them when they are drunk on the mead, and bury them in a stone chest at Dinas Emrys.footnote:[*Lludd and Llefelys* on the May-Eve annual disturbance, on the central location of the combat, on the mead-pit-and-burial resolution, and on the Dinas Emrys burial-site; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the resolution-mechanism.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

[VARIANT: primary=*Historia Brittonum* (red-and-white-dragon political allegory; Britons-vs-Saxons reading; the dragons fight beneath Dinas Emrys and Vortigern's fortress sinks; the prophetic-political resolution) | alt=*Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (annual May-Eve disturbance; the dragons are buried at Dinas Emrys after being lured into a mead-pit; cosmological-territorial resolution rather than political-allegorical) | chosen: present-both | reason: the two recensions present two distinct framings of the same underlying combat-tradition, with *Historia Brittonum* the political-allegorical reading of c. 828 CE and *Lludd and Llefelys* the older non-political reading of the *Mabinogion* tradition; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the two-witness side-by-side presentation.]

=== Batraz and the Nart combats — Ossetic

The Batraz narrative is preserved in the Ossetic Nart saga corpus, an oral-epic tradition recorded in the 19th and 20th centuries CE from Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen narrators across the North Caucasus, with the principal scholarly anchor Georges Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments and the principal IE-comparative apparatus West 2007's chapter 6.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus, 19th–20th c. CE oral-epic recordings from Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen narrators; Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments as the principal scholarly precedent for the IE-comparative reading; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Scytho-Alanic IE warrior / dragon-slayer reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Narts are the heroic figures of the North Caucasian epic tradition — a category-class of warrior-figures who occupy the epic narrative-space and who are the principal characters of the Ossetic Nart saga corpus and of the cognate Circassian and Chechen Nart traditions — with Batraz one of the principal Nart warrior-figures in the Ossetic version of the corpus.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on the Narts as heroic figures of the North Caucasian epic tradition; West 2007 ch. 6 on Batraz as a principal Ossetic Nart warrior-figure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Batraz is distinguished within the Ossetic Nart corpus by his steel body — he is born from a tumour or boil on the back of his father Khamits, plunges himself into a furnace, and emerges with a body of steel — and the steel-body motif is the principal physical-and-narrative attribute that distinguishes Batraz from other Narts within the corpus.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on Batraz's steel-body origin (born from Khamits's back; furnace-tempering; steel-body status); West 2007 ch. 6 on the steel-body motif as IE-warrior structural attribute.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Batraz fights monstrous giants and dragons across multiple episodes of the Nart corpus: against the giant Mukara, against various dragons-and-monstrous-figures, and against opposing Nart-clans within the Ossetic tradition; the Nart corpus is not a single continuous narrative but a cluster of episodic combats, with each combat-episode preserving the warrior-pole of the IE pattern in distinctive Ossetic morphology.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on Batraz's combats against the giant Mukara and against various other giant and dragon adversaries within the corpus; West 2007 ch. 6 on the episodic-cluster structure of the Nart corpus and on the warrior-pole preservation; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the episodic-cluster structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

In Dumézil's twentieth-century reading, Batraz is the Scytho-Alanic Indo-European reflex of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern — preserved within the Ossetic-and-cognate Nart traditions as the indigenous-and-uninterrupted continuation of an ancient Iranian-Indo-European warrior-figure type — and the steel-body motif is read by Dumézil as a distinctively-developed feature of the warrior-pole within the Scytho-Alanic stratum.footnote:[West 2007 ch. 6 on Dumézil's reading of Batraz as Scytho-Alanic IE warrior / dragon-slayer reflex; on the Ossetic-and-cognate Nart traditions as the uninterrupted Scytho-Alanic continuation; on the steel-body motif as Scytho-Alanic distinctive development.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen Nart corpora preserve multiple recorded variants of the Batraz combats across narrators, with the Ossetic-narrator variants forming the principal corpus for the Dumézilian IE-comparative reading and the Circassian-and-Chechen variants providing comparative-and-control material whose narrative-detail differences are the subject of twentieth- and twenty-first-century North Caucasian folkloric and philological scholarship.footnote:[Ossetic, Circassian, and Chechen Nart corpora on the multiple-narrator variant structure; West 2007 ch. 6 on the variant-comparative methodological framing; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the multiple-narrator-variant structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6
