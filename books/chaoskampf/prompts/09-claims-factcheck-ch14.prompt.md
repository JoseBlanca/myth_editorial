# Scope: Chaoskampf Across Cultures

## Book type
**Cross-cultural comparison.** Unlike the other books in this project (one-culture-per-book), this book's subject is a *motif family* — the combat-with-chaos myth — traced across ~15 ancient traditions. The comparison is the point, not a capstone. The `comparative` stage of the pipeline is therefore the engine of the book rather than the final chapter.

## Subject definition
"Chaoskampf" here means: a narrative in which a sovereign or would-be sovereign deity (or heroic champion) defeats a serpentine / monstrous adversary, and that combat does one or more of the following order-making kinds of work:
1. **Cosmogonic** — the cosmos is established from or after the combat (e.g., Marduk/Tiamat, Ninurta/Asag, Gonggong).
2. **Kingship / sovereignty** — the victor's rule over gods and/or humans is established (Baal/Yamm, Zeus/Typhon, Indra/Vṛtra).
3. **Cyclical maintenance** — the combat is re-enacted, preserving an order that is always under threat (Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival).
4. **Eschatological** — the combat closes cosmic time (Thor/Jörmungandr at Ragnarök, Rev 12, Frashokereti).

This working definition is operational, not doctrinal. The book will explicitly flag that the category "Chaoskampf" is contested in current scholarship (see §"Terminological position" below).

## Terminological position
The term *Chaoskampf* was coined by Hermann Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895). It has been critiqued in successive waves, most consequentially in:
- *Creation and Chaos: A Reconsideration of Hermann Gunkel's Chaoskampf Hypothesis*, ed. JoAnn Scurlock & Richard Beal (Eisenbrauns, 2013).
- Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues "chaos" is the wrong word — adversaries are better read as agents of an alternative divine power structure.

The book will include a **dedicated introductory chapter** titled provisionally "What Chaoskampf is, and why the word is contested." The term is retained because it is what readers will search for and what scholars still (if uneasily) use, but the book acknowledges the critique and adopts "combat myth" as an interchangeable term where precision matters.

## Period
- Earliest in-scope primary source: Sumerian *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), Old Babylonian copies of Ur III originals — c. 2000 BCE.
- Latest in-scope primary source: Zoroastrian *Bundahišn*, late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction — c. 8th–9th c. CE.
- Secondary (scholarly) literature is contemporary (1895–present).

Post-medieval reworkings (Milton's Satan, Tolkien's Smaug, etc.) are explicitly **out of scope as primary material** but may appear briefly in the concluding chapter as reception history.

## Languages of primary sources
Sumerian, Akkadian, Hurrian, Hittite, Ugaritic, Biblical Hebrew, Koine Greek, Classical Greek, Latin, Vedic Sanskrit, Classical Sanskrit, Avestan, Middle Persian (Pahlavi), Old Egyptian (and later Egyptian through Demotic), Old Norse (Eddic + skaldic), Old English, Classical Nahuatl, Quechua, Classical Chinese, Old Japanese (*man'yōgana*), Classical Armenian, Old Church Slavonic / later East Slavic, Old Irish, Middle Welsh, Old Finnish (Kalevala-era runes), and oral-tradition records in Nyanga, Soninke, Visayan/Bikol, and Mapudungun.

The book is **translation-mediated**. The author does not read most of these languages. The source-whitelist strategy (see §"Source strategy") requires two things for each language: (1) a single scholar-standard translation that will be cited first, and (2) at least one secondary-scholarship cross-check. Where a translation is disputed, the chapter will flag the variant and footnote both readings.

## In-scope catalog

**CORE (15 stories)** — full chapters, each treating the primary text(s), the combat narrative in plain-English retelling, and the scholarly debate. See `catalog.yaml` for the complete list with key scholars.

**RELATED (~30 stories)** — shorter treatments. See `catalog.yaml`. Two options for format (see DECIDE_SCOPE.md §1):
- (A) Each gets an abbreviated chapter (2–5 pages).
- (B) Grouped into "family" chapters by tradition or subtype (e.g., "Greek dragon-slayings after Zeus-Typhon"; "Indo-European reflexes of Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT*"; "Mesopotamian minor combats").

**PROVISIONAL / Methodological** — Rainbow Serpent as the dedicated critique case; Yu the Great as a sidebar contrasting hydraulic-ordering with combat-ordering.

**CONTRASTIVE / Inversion** — dedicated chapters for:
- **The inversion (Bida)** — where slaying the serpent *destroys* prosperity rather than establishing it.
- **The refusal (Nanabozho / Mishipeshu)** — where area specialists explicitly reject the chaos-vs-order mapping, and the Thunderer / Underwater-manitouk dialectic is necessary rather than adversarial.

**EXCLUDED** — see `catalog.yaml` §"exclude".

## Inclusion / exclusion criteria (the operational test)
A story is in CORE if it meets **all** of:
1. The adversary is a serpentine / monstrous figure.
2. The combat is explicitly attested in a primary source (not a modern reconstruction alone).
3. The combat does cosmogonic, kingship, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work as identified by *area specialists*, not only by pattern-matching comparativists.
4. The scholarly corpus treating it is robust enough that we can cite multiple independent area-specialist readings.

A story is RELATED if it meets (1)–(2) but (3) is weaker, or if (3) holds only under a comparativist reading that area specialists qualify. Apollo/Python and Susanoo/Orochi were moved here for this reason.

A story is PROVISIONAL / CRITIQUE if area specialists explicitly reject the chaoskampf framing (Rainbow Serpent, Yu/Xiangliu), or if the combat is absent or inverted (Nanabozho, Bida handled in dedicated chapters with the right framing).

## Appropriation protocol
Given scholarly warnings about forcing Near Eastern / Indo-European templates onto non-Eurasian traditions (explicitly flagged for Rainbow Serpent, Nanabozho, Mwindo in the research notes), every RELATED chapter on a non-Eurasian tradition must:
1. Cite at least two area specialists **by name** in the first factual pass.
2. Record any specialist objection to the chaoskampf framing in an `[APPROPRIATION-FLAG:]` marker, which the `comparative` chapter must address rather than paper over.
3. Open with the tradition's own framing (*maʿat*/*isfet*, *vṛ-*/"obstruction," *wak'a* succession, etc.) before invoking the comparative category.

## Excluded adjacent motifs (contamination risks)
A cross-cultural book does not exclude neighboring *cultures* (that would defeat its purpose); it excludes *adjacent motifs* that readers and lazy comparativists conflate with chaoskampf. The book explicitly excludes:
1. **Dismemberment cosmogonies without combat** (Ymir, Puruṣa, Pangu, arguably Cipactli) — these are order-making but not combat-order-making. Cipactli is kept in RELATED precisely to mark this boundary, not ignore it.
2. **Hero-initiation monster fights** (Fionn vs. Aillen, most of Herakles's non-Hydra labours outside the serpent-combat corpus) — order-making for the hero, not for the cosmos.
3. **Inter-species enmity / folkloric monsters** (Garuḍa vs. nāgas, Welsh Afanc, Sedna) — no cosmogonic or sovereignty function.
4. **Trickster-demiurge tales without serpent-adversary** (Māui fishing the land).
5. **Storm-god theophanies without a dragon combat** (most Yoruba Ṣàngó material, Korean foundation myths).
6. **Post-medieval literary reworkings** as primary sources (see §"Period").

## Reconstruction policy
Because every chapter is a different tradition with a different textual situation, reconstruction is handled **per chapter** in the brief, not book-globally. The governing principles are:
1. **No cross-tradition reconstruction.** A gap in the *Hymiskviða* may not be filled from the *Baal Cycle*, even if structurally analogous.
2. **In-tradition reconstruction** must specify the donor source and period-proximity tolerance (e.g., "any Rigvedic hymn; never post-Vedic").
3. All reconstructions carry `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` markers naming the donor source.
4. For fragmentary cases (*Marduk vs. Labbu*, *Song of Hedammu*, some vishap material), the chapter explicitly states the fragmentary status and presents both the securely attested material and the reconstructed narrative separately.

## Variant presentation policy
- When one variant dominates a tradition's scholarly corpus (e.g., Snorri's version of the Thor fishing episode), it is presented in the body with minor variants in footnotes.
- When variants are roughly co-equal within a tradition (the two Illuyanka versions on the same tablet group; the Homeric Hymn vs. Ovidian Apollo/Python), they are presented side by side with source attributions.
- Cross-tradition comparison is never performed inline in a story-chapter; it is confined to the `comparative` material (see §"Comparative pipeline shape").

## Book structure (locked 2026-04-23)

### Part I — story chapters, geographic clusters
Part I is organised geographically, in a sequence that respects the known Near-Eastern → Mediterranean transmission chain (West, Burkert, López-Ruiz) and then moves outward. Within each cluster, CORE stories get full chapters; closely related RELATED stories are bundled into "family" chapters rather than spun out as individual mini-chapters.

Provisional geographic clusters:
1. Mesopotamia — Ninurta/Asag, Ninurta/Anzû, Marduk/Tiamat (full chapters each); family chapter on minor Mesopotamian combats (Marduk/Labbu).
2. Anatolia & Levant — Kumarbi Cycle / Ullikummi, Illuyanka, Baal/Yamm-Lotan (full chapters each).
3. Hebrew Bible & Early Christianity — Yahweh vs. Leviathan/Rahab (full); Revelation 12 Red Dragon (full); family chapter on Christian reception (St. George / Michael lineage, Ogden 2021).

5. Iran — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (full); Frashokereti as its eschatological pendant.
6. India — Indra vs. Vṛtra (full); family chapter on Vedic and post-Vedic reflexes (Trita Āptya; Krishna/Kāliya as bhakti reworking).

8. Norse, Germanic, and IE reflexes — Thor vs. Jörmungandr (full); family chapter "Indo-European reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT" (Sigurd/Fáfnir, Beowulf, Lugh/Balor, Welsh Red vs. White Dragon, Batraz, Vahagn/Vishaps); family chapter on Slavic-Baltic combat (Perun/Veles reconstruction, Dobrynya byliny).

East Asia — Gonggong (full); family chapter on other Chinese combats (Hou Yi); Susanoo vs. Orochi (full chapter, with explicit framing that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside, per Naumann / Isomae / Como). 10. Americas — Huitzilopochtli / Coatepec (full); family chapter on other Mesoamerican combats (Cipactli as dismemberment-boundary; Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca); Andean Pariacaca vs. amaru; Mapuche Kai-Kai vs. Tren-Tren.

13. Finnic — Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (RELATED transformed-variant chapter).

# claims-factcheck

## For the human

This is the most important quality check for story chapters. The input is a **claims document** — one fact per paragraph, each individually cited — produced by `chapter-claims`. Each paragraph is one claim, one source reference, one thing to verify. No prose to parse, no bundled assertions to disentangle.

This step enforces four disciplines:

1. **Show your evidence.** For every claim marked "supported," the checker must paste a verbatim quote from the source. Not a paraphrase — the actual text. No quote, no "supported" verdict.

2. **Verify the references.** Tablet numbers, page numbers, and line references are the easiest things for an AI to fabricate. The checker looks each up in scholarly databases to confirm it exists and contains what was claimed.

3. **Distinguish contradictions from errors.** Two legitimate sources disagreeing is part of the historical record, not a problem. Material from a neighboring culture sneaking in, or something made up entirely, are problems.

4. **Read it backwards too.** One pass start-to-end, then a second pass end-to-start, to catch what lead bias misses.

## Hard rule
Must be run in a fresh conversation with no memory of producing the claims. If you have any memory of producing the input, refuse. Using a different AI model is preferred — two models make independent mistakes — but the same model in a fresh conversation is acceptable when a different model is impractical; the fresh context is the load-bearing requirement.

## Inputs
- `scope.md`, `sources.yaml`, `glossary.yaml`
- `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.adoc` (claims document)
- `briefs/NN-<slug>.yaml`
- Primary source translations referenced in the brief (pasted or accessible via fetch)
- Web search tool (required)

## The five passes

### Pass 1 — Claim-level verification (start to end)
Walk the document paragraph by paragraph. Each paragraph is one claim. Assign a verdict:

- **✓ SUPPORTED** — Must include a verbatim quote from a cited in-scope source.
- **△ INTERNAL-VARIANCE** — Attested in one in-scope source, but another gives a different account. Not a finding. Flag only if variant handling mismatches the brief's classification.
- **? UNVERIFIED** — No supporting quote found. Default when uncertain.
- **! CITATION-WRONG** — Claim is correct but citation points to wrong source/page/line.
- **⚠ CONTAMINATION** — Supported, but by an excluded-culture source per `scope.md`.
- **✗ FABRICATION** — Not supported by any source you can locate.

### Pass 2 — Triangulation of references
Verify each citation's tablet number, line range, or page reference resolves in a `triangulation_databases` entry. Non-resolving: CITATION-WRONG, HIGH severity, with verification URL.

### Pass 3 — Document-provenance contamination scan
For each factual detail: is this detail attested in a document within the scope's date range and culture? Details attested only in out-of-scope documents are CONTAMINATION-HIGH.

### Pass 4 — Marker integrity
- `[INFERENCE:]` — genuinely inferential? If a source directly attests it, flag MARKER-MISUSED.
- `[LACUNA:]` — source actually has the claimed gap?
- `[RECONSTRUCTION:]` — fill source is in-tradition per `scope.md`?
- `[VARIANT:]` — both quoted phrases present in cited sources?
- **Silent bridges** — uncited claims not under a marker. Flag SILENT-BRIDGE, HIGH.

### Pass 5 — Reverse-order second pass
Walk from last claim to first. Add any new findings.

**Glossary**: check consistency. **Style**: not checked — there is no prose yet.

## Verdict rules

Apply in order (first match wins):
- **MAJOR-REVISE**: ≥ 3 HIGH findings, or any contamination, or triangulation failures indicating fabricated references.
- **REVISE**: 1–2 HIGH, or > 3 MEDIUM, or any silent bridge.
- **PASS**: zero HIGH, zero silent bridges, ≤ 3 MEDIUM.

## Honesty self-check
Answer in `meta`:
1. Did I open every cited primary source, or skim? Name any I skimmed.
2. Did I triangulate every reference, or assume some? Name any assumed.
3. For every ✓, did I paste a real quote or pattern-match? Downgrade any pattern-matched.
4. Did I distinguish internal variance from contamination?
5. Am I the same model that produced the claims? If yes, this audit is invalid.

## Human review protocol
Review HIGH findings (~10 minutes), skim MEDIUM. Apply accepted fixes to produce `chapters/NN-<slug>.claims.approved.adoc`. Run `post-human-normalize` before proceeding to `chapter-draft`.

The eight Rigvedic hymns that constitute the working Vṛtra-combat corpus for the chapter are RV 1.32 (the primary and most concentrated Vṛtra hymn, fifteen verses), with RV 1.80, 2.11, 2.12, 4.18, 5.32, 6.17, 8.96, and 10.113 supplying co-witness material in which the combat is alluded to and partially narrated.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml, sources.primary, listing the eight Vṛtra hymns; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the same eight hymns as the principal Vṛtra-combat corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32; 1.80; 2.11–12; 4.18; 5.32; 6.17; 8.96; 10.113

The Rigveda is orally preserved with extreme philological precision through a transmission tradition (the _śākhā_ schools and the elaborate phonetic-and-mnemonic apparatus of recitation) that is generally regarded as preserving the text more faithfully than any comparable ancient corpus, and there are no philological lacunae in the Vṛtra-combat hymns of the kind that affect, for instance, the Hittite Illuyanka tablets.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction, on the oral preservation and philological reliability of the Rigvedic corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction

The lacuna in the Vṛtra-combat material is therefore narrative rather than philological: the Rigveda never tells the Vṛtra-combat as a continuous story, and the combat must be reconstructed from the alluding ritual hymns by reading them together as a hymn-cycle.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml, lacunae section, on the narrative-not-philological lacuna in the Vṛtra material; Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction, on the ritual-allusive (rather than narrative-continuous) genre of the Rigvedic hymns.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction

=== The hymn-cycle method

The Rigveda is a corpus of liturgical hymns addressed to the gods in the context of the soma sacrifice and other Vedic rituals, and its primary genre is the praise-and-invocation hymn, not the continuous narrative epic.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction to volume 1, on the liturgical-and-ritual genre of the Rigvedic hymns.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=volume 1 introduction

The Vṛtra-combat narrative is therefore not present in the Rigveda as a continuous story: each Rigvedic hymn that addresses Indra alludes to the combat, draws a single image from it, or invokes the combat as warrant for a present petition, but no single hymn tells the combat as a self-contained narrative from start to finish.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction and notes to RV 1.32, on the ritual-allusive mode of Rigvedic Indra-hymns and the absence of a single continuous Vṛtra-combat narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction; RV 1.32 notes

The chapter therefore reconstructs the Vṛtra-combat by reading the eight principal Vṛtra-hymns together as a hymn-cycle, with RV 1.32 supplying the most concentrated narrative spine and the other seven hymns supplying co-witness motifs, ritual frames, and cosmogonic consequences that the spine-text does not develop.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml, on the hymn-cycle method as the chapter's reconstructive procedure; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 1.32 as the most concentrated Vṛtra hymn and on the wider Vṛtra-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and supplementary hymns

[INFERENCE: the chapter's hymn-cycle method — reading the eight Vṛtra-hymns together as a single reconstructive corpus — is itself a methodological claim about how to read the Rigveda, distinct from any specific philological or comparative claim within the corpus; the method is well-supported by Jamison and Brereton 2014's editorial practice and by Watkins 1995's comparative procedure, but the chapter flags it explicitly because the resulting "Vṛtra-combat narrative" is a synthesis of allusive ritual hymns rather than a translation of a single ancient text. | source: Jamison and Brereton 2014 (editorial practice); Watkins 1995 (comparative procedure); brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml | scope: methodological claim about the hymn-cycle reconstruction; flagged for transparency.]

The hymn-cycle method is the contrastive case to the Mesopotamian, Hittite, and Ugaritic combat-narratives treated earlier in the book: where *Enūma Eliš*, the Anzû Epic, and the Baal Cycle are continuous narrative compositions, the Rigvedic Vṛtra-combat exists only as a distributed corpus of ritual allusions whose narrative coherence is editorially reconstructed.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the genre-difference between the Rigvedic ritual hymns and the continuous narrative epics of the ancient Near East; Lambert 2013 and Watkins 1995 on the contrast.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction

The hymn-cycle method has the consequence that variant detail across the eight hymns is to be treated as variation within a single ritual-poetic tradition rather than as variation across distinct narrative recensions: each hymn presents its own selection of motifs and its own ritual emphasis, and apparent inconsistency at the level of motif (the count of Indra's enemies, the precise sequence of pre-combat events, the exact cosmogonic consequences) is constitutive of the genre rather than evidence of textual corruption.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction and notes to the Indra-hymns, on the variation among Indra-hymns as constitutive of the Rigvedic ritual genre.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction; Indra-hymn notes

The hymn-cycle method also has the consequence that no single Rigvedic hymn supplies all of the combat's cosmogonic effects: the separation of heaven and earth, the release of the waters, the production of dawn, and the foundation of Indra's kingship are distributed across the eight hymns, and the combat's full cosmogonic-functional profile is itself a synthetic reconstruction.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the distribution of cosmogonic effects across the Vṛtra-hymns; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same distribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=multiple Vṛtra-hymns

Watkins's reconstruction of the HERO SLAY SERPENT poetic formula in *How to Kill a Dragon* chapters 41 to 48 proceeds by exactly the same hymn-cycle method, drawing the formula's attestations from across multiple Rigvedic hymns and from the Avestan, Hittite, and Greek comparanda rather than from any single continuous text.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the hymn-cycle (and cross-language) method used to reconstruct the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The hymn-cycle method also distinguishes the Rigvedic Vṛtra-combat from the post-Vedic brahminicide reframing of the same combat in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa and the Mahābhārata, both of which are continuous narrative texts in a way that the Rigvedic hymns are not, and which therefore confront the comparative reader with a different genre of evidence and a different mode of reconstruction.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the genre-difference between the Rigvedic ritual hymns and the post-Vedic prose Brāhmaṇa and epic Mahābhārata; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same genre-difference.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction

The hymn-cycle method is the chapter's principal methodological commitment, and the chapter signposts the method explicitly at the head of each Movement so that the reader can distinguish the editorial work of synthesis from the philological reportage of what each Rigvedic hymn individually says.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml, on the hymn-cycle method as a load-bearing methodological commitment of the chapter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=editorial procedure

The chapter's working synthesis of the Vṛtra-combat narrative is therefore an "as-reconstructed" text in the same sense in which Watkins's HERO SLAY SERPENT formula is an "as-reconstructed" Indo-European poetic line: it is a scholarly object built from the surviving evidence by explicit and methodologically disciplined synthesis.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the "as-reconstructed" status of the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the corresponding status of the Vṛtra-combat narrative reconstructed from the Rigvedic hymn-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

=== Setting and Vedic religious context

Indra (Devanagari इन्द्र) is the storm-god of the Rigveda and the most frequently invoked deity in the entire Rigvedic corpus, addressed in roughly a quarter of the more than a thousand hymns of the Rigveda.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, general introduction, on Indra as the most frequently invoked deity in the Rigvedic corpus and on the share of the corpus addressed to him.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=general introduction

Indra is the chief of the Vedic gods in the Rigveda's working theology: a warrior-king who leads the gods in combat against their adversaries, who has the highest share of the soma offering at the sacrifice, and whose Vṛtra-victory is repeatedly invoked as the warrant for his sovereign status among the gods.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction and Indra-hymn headnotes, on Indra as warrior-king and chief of the Vedic gods, with the Vṛtra-victory as warrant for his kingship.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction; Indra-hymn headnotes

The Rigvedic ritual setting is the soma sacrifice (Sanskrit _yajña_ centred on the pressing of the _soma_ plant), in which a ritual drink (and deified plant) is pressed, filtered, mixed with milk and other ingredients, and offered to the gods, with Indra as its primary recipient.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction, on the soma sacrifice as the principal Rigvedic ritual setting and on Indra as primary recipient of the _soma_-offering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction

_Soma_ (Sanskrit सोम) is the ritual drink that fortifies Indra for the Vṛtra-combat in the Rigvedic mytho-ritual logic: in multiple Vṛtra-hymns, Indra's drinking of the pressed _soma_ at the sacrifice is the immediate prelude to his going out to combat the serpent-adversary.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnotes to RV 1.32 and to the supplementary Vṛtra-hymns, on the _soma_-drinking as the immediate prelude to the Vṛtra-combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 headnote and supplementary hymn headnotes

Tvaṣṭṛ (Sanskrit त्वष्टृ) is the divine artisan of the Vedic pantheon, the smith-and-shaper-god whose function in the Rigveda is the manufacture of divine implements and forms, and whose principal narrative role in the Vṛtra-cycle is the forging of Indra's _vajra_.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Tvaṣṭṛ as the divine artisan of the Vedic pantheon and the forger of Indra's _vajra_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Tvaṣṭṛ entries in introduction and hymn-notes

The _vajra_ (Sanskrit वज्र) is Indra's thunderbolt-mace, the divine weapon forged by Tvaṣṭṛ, with which Indra strikes the killing blow against Vṛtra in the Rigvedic combat-narrative.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the _vajra_ as Indra's thunderbolt-mace and as the weapon of the Vṛtra-killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and supplementary hymns; _vajra_ glossary

The _vajra_ is the cosmic combat-weapon of the Rigveda: not merely an instrument of Indra's personal armament but the implement through which the lightning-strike of the storm-god is mythologised as a forged divine artefact, and through which the combat acquires its concrete material image.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the _vajra_ as cosmic combat-weapon and forged image of the lightning-strike; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the cognancy of the _vajra_-image with the Indo-European storm-god's weapon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=_vajra_ entries

The Vedic pantheon's structure is broadly tripartite — sovereign-priestly gods (Varuṇa, Mitra), warrior gods (Indra and the Maruts), and producer-fertility gods (the Aśvins, Pūṣan, and others) — and Indra's position within this structure is firmly the warrior tier, with the Vṛtra-combat as the principal warrior-tier act of the Rigvedic mythology.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction, on the broad tripartite structure of the Vedic pantheon and on Indra's warrior-tier position within it.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction

The storm-god / waters complex of the Rigveda — in which the storm-god's combat with the obstructing serpent releases the waters and produces rain — is the Vedic instance of a wider Indo-European pattern that West's chapter 6 ("Storm and Stream") treats across the Vedic, Avestan, Hittite, Greek, Norse, and Italic comparanda together.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6 ("Storm and Stream"), on the Indo-European storm-god / waters complex including the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra instance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Vṛtra (Sanskrit वृत्र) is the serpent-adversary of Indra in the Rigveda, an obstructing figure whose name derives from the Sanskrit verbal root _vṛ-_ "to obstruct, to enclose, to cover", and whose narrative function is the obstruction of the cosmic waters that Indra's combat must release.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the etymology of Vṛtra from Sanskrit _vṛ-_ "to obstruct"; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Vṛtra as the obstructing-and-enclosing serpent of the Rigveda.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=etymological discussion

The name Vṛtra is therefore semantically transparent in its own language: Vṛtra is "the obstructor", "the enclosure", and the lexical fact that the adversary's proper name is built on the verbal root for what the adversary does is itself a feature of the Rigvedic combat-vocabulary that Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph treats in detail.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the lexical transparency of the name Vṛtra and on the integration of name and function in the Rigvedic combat-vocabulary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=etymology and naming

Vṛtra is also called _ahi_ (Sanskrit अहि, "serpent") in the Rigvedic combat-vocabulary, and the word _ahi_ is the load-bearing term for "serpent" in the áhann áhim formula that Watkins reconstructs as the Indo-European poetic line of combat-myth.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on _ahi_ as the load-bearing serpent-term of the áhann áhim formula; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the use of _ahi_ as a designation for Vṛtra in the Rigvedic Vṛtra-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

Dānu (Sanskrit दानु) is Vṛtra's mother in the Rigvedic combat-narrative, a feminine figure associated with the waters and the danāva ("descendants of Dānu") demonic class, and whose presence over the body of the slain Vṛtra is one of the named motifs of the RV 1.32 narrative.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Dānu as Vṛtra's mother and on the Dānu-and-Vṛtra-body motif in RV 1.32; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Dānu in the Indo-Iranian combat-myth corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 notes; Dānu entries

The Vedic combat is repeatedly invoked at the soma sacrifice as a ritual replay of divine history: the offering of _soma_ to Indra at every major Vedic sacrifice is mythologically the same action that fortified Indra for the Vṛtra-combat, and the ritual-aetiological function of the combat is one of the chapter's load-bearing function-claims.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, introduction and Indra-hymn notes, on the ritual-aetiological function of the Vṛtra-combat in the soma sacrifice.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction; Indra-hymn notes

=== Movement I — Rigveda 1.32 as primary witness

Rigveda 1.32 is the primary Vṛtra-hymn of the Rigvedic corpus, a fifteen-verse hymn from the first _maṇḍala_ of the Rigveda, attributed in the traditional indices to the seer Hiraṇyastūpa Āṅgirasa, and used by Jamison and Brereton 2014 as the principal narrative-spine text for the Vṛtra-combat.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 1.32, on the hymn's fifteen-verse length, its attribution, and its position as the primary Vṛtra-hymn of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 headnote

The opening verse of RV 1.32 announces the theme in the first-line poetic formula that Watkins identifies as a Vedic instance of the inherited Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT line: "I shall now proclaim the manly deeds of Indra, those that he, the wielder of the _vajra_, did first" — with the principal verb of the killing (_áhan_, "he slew") and its object (_áhim_, "the serpent") falling in the second verse, in the formula áhann áhim.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and headnote of RV 1.32.1–2, on the opening proclamation and the áhann áhim line; Watkins 1995, ch. 41, on the same opening as the Vedic instance of the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.1-2

RV 1.32.1 names Indra as the wielder of the _vajra_ and frames the hymn as a recital of his "manly deeds" (Sanskrit _vīryà_), with the Vṛtra-killing positioned in the same verse as the chief and first of those deeds.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and headnote of RV 1.32.1, on Indra as the _vajra_-wielder and the Vṛtra-killing as the chief and first of his manly deeds.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.1

RV 1.32.2 develops the killing in a sequence of paratactic lines: he slew the serpent, he split the mountain (Sanskrit _parvata_), he released the waters, and he opened a channel for the seven rivers — four cosmogonic-effect verbs in a single tightly compressed verse.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and headnote of RV 1.32.2, on the four cosmogonic-effect verbs (slew, split, released, opened) in the second verse.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2

The seven rivers (Sanskrit _saptá síndhavaḥ_) of RV 1.32.2 are the released waters of the combat in the Rigvedic geographical-and-cosmological imagination, and the conjunction of the Vṛtra-killing with the opening of the seven-river channel is one of the central motifs of the Vṛtra-corpus.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.2, on the seven rivers and on the channel-opening motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2 and notes

The forging of the _vajra_ by Tvaṣṭṛ is recorded in RV 1.32.2 itself, with Tvaṣṭṛ named as the maker of the weapon Indra wields in the killing: the divine artisan supplies the implement, and the implement is recorded in the same compressed combat-verse as the killing it accomplishes.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.2, on Tvaṣṭṛ as the maker of the _vajra_ in the second verse of the hymn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2

RV 1.32.3 to 1.32.4 narrate Indra's drinking of the _soma_ at the sacrifice and his subsequent setting-out to the combat: the offered _soma_ fortifies Indra, the storm-god grasps the _vajra_, and the combat-narrative is articulated with its ritual prelude inside the hymn itself.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.3–4, on the _soma_-drinking and the going-out to combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.3-4

RV 1.32.5 describes Vṛtra in mountainous-and-mountain-comparable terms: the serpent-adversary is "lying" or "sprawled" on the slopes, with a body that is treated in the hymn's imagery as itself a mountain or a mountain-form.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.5, on Vṛtra's mountainous form and on the body-as-mountain imagery.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.5

The mountain-imagery of Vṛtra has the consequence that the splitting (Sanskrit _bhid-_) of the mountain in RV 1.32.2 is at once the splitting of the obstructing serpent's body and the splitting of the cosmic obstacle that holds back the waters — a single verbal action that is simultaneously a killing and a cosmogonic act.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.2 and 1.32.5, on the dual reading of the mountain-splitting; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same dual reading in the comparative context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2 and 1.32.5

RV 1.32.5 also stages Vṛtra's challenge to Indra in the form of an attack: the serpent strikes at Indra, and the combat is therefore reciprocal rather than a one-sided dispatch.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.5, on Vṛtra's strike at Indra and the reciprocal-combat framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.5

RV 1.32.6 reports the killing-blow: Indra strikes Vṛtra with the _vajra_, the serpent falls, and the cosmic waters that Vṛtra was withholding begin their downward course toward the human world.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.6, on the killing-blow with the _vajra_ and the release of the cosmic waters.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.6

RV 1.32.7 develops the body-and-water tableau: the slain Vṛtra lies broken across the channel, and the released waters flow over and past his fallen body — an image in which the dead serpent is visually integrated into the watercourse he had been blocking.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.7, on the body-and-water tableau and on the visual integration of the slain Vṛtra into the watercourse.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.7

RV 1.32.8 names Vṛtra's mother Dānu and stages her over the body of her slain son: Dānu lies above Vṛtra "as a cow above her calf", and the maternal-grief image is one of the most distinctive of the RV 1.32 narrative.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.8, on Dānu over the body of Vṛtra and on the cow-and-calf simile.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.8

The Dānu-over-Vṛtra image has the consequence that the slain serpent's body in RV 1.32 is mourned within the hymn itself: the Rigvedic combat-narrative does not present the killing as morally one-sided, and the maternal-grief verse acknowledges the cost of the victory in a way that parallels (without depending on) the Mesopotamian and Greek tradition's similar acknowledgments.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.8, on the maternal-grief image and on the Rigvedic acknowledgment of the cost of the victory.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.8

RV 1.32.10 records the released waters surging forth in a many-rivered cascade: the waters move outward and downward from the place of the killing, and the hymn's water-language treats the post-combat watercourses as the immediate cosmogonic consequence of the killing.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.10, on the post-combat water-cascade and the cosmogonic-consequence framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.10

RV 1.32.11 to 1.32.12 develop the cosmogonic-consequence list: with Vṛtra slain, Indra is credited with separating heaven and earth (Sanskrit _dyaus_ and _pṛthivī_), making fast the firmament, and producing the conditions under which the dawn (Sanskrit _uṣas_) appears.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.11–12, on the heaven-and-earth separation, the firmament, and the production of dawn as cosmogonic consequences of the Vṛtra-killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.11-12

The triple cosmogonic effect of the Vṛtra-killing — separation of heaven and earth, release of the waters, and production of dawn — is the principal evidence-base for the chapter's claim that the Vedic combat is cosmologically active in a strong sense, and the three effects together are what the brief calls the combat's "cosmogonic work" within the Rigvedic theology.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml, on the triple cosmogonic effect of the Vṛtra-killing; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the same triple effect in RV 1.32.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.11-12 and surrounding verses

RV 1.32.13 to 1.32.14 stage Indra's wider sovereignty: with Vṛtra slain and the waters released, Indra is acclaimed as the sovereign of the gods, and the hymn moves from the combat-narrative into a brief summary of Indra's other manly deeds and his accepted kingship.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.13–14, on Indra's wider sovereignty and on the kingship-summary at the close of the combat-narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.13-14

RV 1.32.15 closes the hymn with the standard Rigvedic ritual envelope: a request for Indra's continued help and prosperity, addressed by the priestly speaker on behalf of the worshipping community, in the formulaic language of the ritual genre.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.15, on the closing ritual envelope and on the formulaic petition-language of the Rigvedic Indra-hymns.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.15

The fifteen-verse arc of RV 1.32 is therefore: announcement (vv. 1–2), ritual prelude (vv. 3–4), Vṛtra's form and challenge (v. 5), the killing-blow (v. 6), body-and-water tableau (vv. 7–8 with the Dānu image), water-release (v. 10), cosmogonic consequences (vv. 11–12), kingship-summary (vv. 13–14), and ritual closure (v. 15).footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and headnote of RV 1.32, on the fifteen-verse arc of the hymn and on the structural sequence outlined in the present claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 headnote and full hymn

The internal arc of RV 1.32 is therefore the closest the Rigvedic corpus comes to a continuous Vṛtra-combat narrative: even within this most-concentrated hymn, however, the narrative is broken by the ritual-allusive frame at the opening and the petition-frame at the close, and the combat itself occupies only roughly verses 2 to 12 of a fifteen-verse hymn.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and headnote of RV 1.32, on the ritual-allusive framing of the combat-narrative within the fifteen-verse hymn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 (full)

RV 1.32 supplies the principal motifs of the Vṛtra-combat — the announcement-line áhann áhim, the Tvaṣṭṛ-forged _vajra_, the _soma_-prelude, the mountain-form of Vṛtra, the killing-blow, the Dānu-over-Vṛtra grief-image, the seven-rivers release, the heaven-earth separation, the dawn-production, and the kingship-acclamation — and the supplementary hymn-cycle adds further motifs and ritual emphases without contradicting this central spine.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32 and headnotes to the supplementary Vṛtra-hymns, on the motifs supplied by RV 1.32 and on the consistency of the supplementary hymn-cycle with the central spine.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and supplementary hymns

RV 1.32 is therefore the chapter's primary witness for the Rigvedic Vṛtra-combat, and the chapter cites this hymn at verse-and-line granularity for specific narrative moments and at hymn-headnote level for the hymn's overall structure and editorial framing.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and headnote of RV 1.32, on the hymn as the chapter's primary witness; per brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the citation discipline.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32

The combat in RV 1.32 has no body-of-Vṛtra-as-cosmos episode parallel to Marduk's splitting of Tiamat into sky and earth in *Enūma Eliš* IV: the cosmos already exists in the Rigvedic mytho-ritual frame, and Indra's killing reorders it (separating heaven and earth, releasing waters, producing dawn) without physically fashioning it from the slain serpent's body.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32, on the absence of a body-of-Vṛtra-as-cosmos episode; Lambert 2013, on the Marduk-Tiamat body-as-cosmos episode in *Enūma Eliš* IV as the Mesopotamian comparand.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32

The absence of body-as-cosmos in RV 1.32 is the textual backbone of the chapter's "cosmologically active but not cosmogonic in the Mesopotamian sense" claim: Indra's combat does cosmogonic work without doing cosmogony of the Marduk-Tiamat type, and the textual fact that no such episode appears in the primary spine-hymn is the load-bearing evidence.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32, on the absence of a body-as-cosmos episode; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the "cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic" framing; Lambert 2013, on the Mesopotamian comparand.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32

The combat in RV 1.32 also shows none of the post-Vedic brahminicide motifs — Vṛtra is not a Brahmin, Indra does not require expiation for the killing, and the killing is celebrated as Indra's chief manly deed without moral complication beyond the brief Dānu-over-Vṛtra acknowledgment of the mother's grief.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32, on the absence of the brahminicide-motif and on the celebration of the killing as Indra's chief manly deed.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32

The textual absence of brahminicide in RV 1.32 is the load-bearing evidence for the chapter's insistence (per brief) that the Rigvedic Vṛtra-combat must not be merged with the post-Vedic Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa and Mahābhārata reframing in which Vṛtra is reframed as a Brahmin and Indra's killing becomes a sin requiring expiation.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32, on the absence of the brahminicide-motif; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions on the no-merge rule; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same distinction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32

=== Movement II — supplementary hymn-cycle (1.80, 2.11–12, 4.18, 5.32, 6.17, 8.96, 10.113)

RV 1.80 is a Vṛtra-hymn of the first _maṇḍala_ that frames the killing as the central act of Indra's career, and that supplies the strophic refrain "by your manliness alone, O Indra" that places the Vṛtra-killing alongside Indra's other deeds as the principal warrant for his sovereignty.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 1.80, on the strophic refrain and on the Vṛtra-killing as warrant for Indra's sovereignty.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.80

RV 1.80 also supplies independent attestation of the áhann áhim formula: the verb of slaying and the serpent-object recur in the hymn, and Watkins's reconstruction of the formula is built from this and similar attestations across the Vṛtra-hymn corpus rather than from RV 1.32 alone.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on RV 1.80 as a co-witness for the áhann áhim formula; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the formula's recurrence across the Vṛtra-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

RV 2.11 is the principal Vṛtra-hymn of the second _maṇḍala_, and its distinctive contribution to the corpus is the staging of the gods' acclamation of Indra after the killing: the hymn presents the wider divine community as recognising and celebrating Indra's victory, and the kingship-acclamation is articulated through the gods' speech as well as through the priestly speaker.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 2.11, on the gods' acclamation of Indra and on the kingship-articulation by divine speech.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 2.11

RV 2.12 is the immediately following hymn in the same _maṇḍala_ and is one of the most often-quoted of the Indra-hymns: each of its strophes ends in the refrain "he, O peoples, is Indra" (Sanskrit _sá janāsa índraḥ_), and the hymn's mode is a sequence of identifying-deeds whose function is to establish the Vṛtra-slayer's identity through accumulated narrative-fact.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 2.12, on the strophic refrain and on the identifying-deeds mode of the hymn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 2.12

RV 2.12's accumulating-deeds mode includes the Vṛtra-killing, the splitting of the mountain, the release of the waters, and the production of dawn as parallel identifying acts in successive strophes, so that the four cosmogonic-effect motifs of RV 1.32.2 are distributed across multiple verses of the second-_maṇḍala_ hymn.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 2.12, on the distribution of the four cosmogonic-effect motifs across the strophes of the hymn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 2.12

RV 4.18 is the principal "embryonic Indra" hymn of the corpus, in which the divine narrator and Indra in dialogue stage the unusual motif of Indra's birth: the hymn presents Indra as having forced his way out of his mother's womb sideways, against the natural mode of birth, and presents this irregular birth as anticipating the irregular and combat-decisive character of his later career.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 4.18, on the embryonic-Indra motif and on the irregular-birth-as-anticipation framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 4.18

RV 4.18 contributes to the Vṛtra-corpus the biographical pre-history of the combat: the hymn implies that Indra is in some sense a combat-figure before he is born, and the combat-with-Vṛtra is then the appropriate mature performance of an irregularity that was already present in his birth.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 4.18, on the implied biographical pre-history and on the combat as the mature performance of birth-irregularity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 4.18

RV 5.32 is a Vṛtra-hymn of the fifth _maṇḍala_ that emphasises the obstruction-and-release narrative element: the hymn dwells on Vṛtra's withholding of the waters and on Indra's piercing-and-opening action that brings the waters down to the human world.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 5.32, on the obstruction-and-release emphasis and on the piercing-and-opening combat-language.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 5.32

RV 6.17 is a Vṛtra-hymn of the sixth _maṇḍala_ that emphasises the cosmic scale of the combat: the hymn frames Indra's combat as performed at the cosmic boundary between the upper and lower worlds, and treats the killing-and-water-release as the action that makes the cosmos's three-tiered structure functionally operative.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 6.17, on the cosmic-scale framing and on the three-tiered cosmos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 6.17

RV 8.96 is a Vṛtra-hymn of the eighth _maṇḍala_ that presents the slaying as the central event of Indra's career in the most concentrated form of any Indra-hymn outside RV 1.32: the hymn names Indra repeatedly as "Vṛtra-slayer" (Sanskrit _vṛtrahán_) and treats the epithet as Indra's principal cult-title.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 8.96, on the centrality of the slaying and on _vṛtrahán_ as Indra's principal cult-title.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 8.96

The cult-title _vṛtrahán_ ("Vṛtra-slayer", "obstruction-slayer") is the Vedic counterpart of the Avestan divine name Vərəθraγna (Middle Persian Wahrām, New Persian Bahrām), and the philological cognancy of the two cult-titles is one of the principal exhibits of Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the philological cognancy of Vedic _vṛtrahán_ and Avestan Vərəθraγna; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same cognancy as a load-bearing case for the Indo-Iranian combat-poetic phase.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Vṛtrahán/Vərəθraγna discussion

RV 10.113 is a Vṛtra-hymn of the tenth _maṇḍala_ that develops the cosmogonic dimension of the combat more explicitly than any other hymn in the corpus: the hymn integrates the Vṛtra-killing into a wider cosmogonic narrative in which the heavens, the waters, the dawn, and Indra's kingship are treated as the joint outputs of the combat.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 10.113, on the cosmogonic-integration of the Vṛtra-killing and on the joint cosmogonic outputs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.113

RV 10.113's cosmogonic emphasis is the principal late-corpus reflex of the Vṛtra-combat's cosmologically active character: the tenth-_maṇḍala_ hymn shows the cosmogonic reading already at work within the Rigvedic corpus, and the chapter's "cosmologically active but not cosmogonic in the Mesopotamian sense" claim is built on RV 10.113 alongside RV 1.32.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnote and translation of RV 10.113, on the cosmogonic-emphasis as a late-corpus reflex; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.113

The supplementary hymn-cycle therefore distributes seven distinct emphases across seven hymns: 1.80 (refrain-and-warrant), 2.11 (gods' acclamation), 2.12 (identifying-deeds with the _sá janāsa índraḥ_ refrain), 4.18 (embryonic-Indra biography), 5.32 (obstruction-and-release), 6.17 (cosmic-scale combat), 8.96 (cult-title _vṛtrahán_), and 10.113 (cosmogonic-integration).footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnotes and translations of RV 1.80, 2.11, 2.12, 4.18, 5.32, 6.17, 8.96, and 10.113, on the seven distinct emphases of the supplementary hymn-cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.80; 2.11; 2.12; 4.18; 5.32; 6.17; 8.96; 10.113

The supplementary hymn-cycle does not contradict RV 1.32 at any load-bearing motif: the eight hymns together present a consistent picture of the Vṛtra-combat in which RV 1.32 supplies the narrative spine and the seven supplementary hymns supply emphases that are mutually compatible with the spine and with one another.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnotes to RV 1.32 and to the seven supplementary Vṛtra-hymns, on the mutual consistency of the spine and the supplementary corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and supplementary hymns

The supplementary hymn-cycle does, however, vary in motif-selection: each hymn selects a sub-set of the central narrative motifs and develops one or two of them at length, so that no single supplementary hymn covers all the motifs of RV 1.32, and the combined corpus is the only place where the full motif-set is jointly attested.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the motif-selection variation across the supplementary hymn-cycle and on the jointly attested motif-set.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=supplementary hymns

The supplementary hymn-cycle includes occasional motifs absent from RV 1.32: the "embryonic Indra" of RV 4.18 has no parallel in RV 1.32, and the explicit gods'-acclamation tableau of RV 2.11 is similarly more developed in the supplementary corpus than in the spine-hymn.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnotes to RV 4.18 and 2.11, on motifs developed in the supplementary corpus that are absent or less developed in RV 1.32.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 4.18 and 2.11

The supplementary hymn-cycle therefore enriches the RV 1.32 narrative spine in three principal ways: it widens the cosmogonic frame (10.113), it deepens the biographical pre-history (4.18), and it externalises the kingship-acclamation through the gods' speech (2.11), with each enrichment located in a distinct hymn.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, headnotes to RV 10.113, 4.18, and 2.11, on the three principal modes of enrichment supplied by the supplementary hymn-cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.113; 4.18; 2.11

The eight-hymn corpus is the working evidentiary base for the chapter's reconstruction of the Vṛtra-combat, and the chapter cites the eight hymns at hymn level for general points about the corpus and at verse level for specific narrative moments.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the eight-hymn working corpus; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the eight hymns as the working evidentiary base for the chapter's reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=eight Vṛtra-hymns

Indra's other principal combat-narratives in the Rigveda — most importantly the Vala myth, in which Indra and the Aṅgiras priests open the Vala cave and release the captured cattle and the dawn — are conceptually related but textually distinct from the Vṛtra-combat, and the chapter does not merge the two combats while flagging the structural family-resemblance that scholars including Watkins read across them.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Vala-myth as a structurally related but textually distinct Indra-combat; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the structural family-resemblance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Indra-corpus generally; Vala-myth notes

=== Movement III — The Watkins formula in Vedic form

The áhann áhim formula — Sanskrit "he slew the serpent" — is the Vedic instance of what Calvert Watkins's *How to Kill a Dragon* reconstructs as the inherited Indo-European poetic line of combat-myth: subject (the hero), verb (a verb of killing), and object (the serpent), in a fixed lexical-and-syntactic arrangement preserved across multiple Indo-European traditions.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the áhann áhim formula as the Vedic instance of the reconstructed Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The áhann áhim formula is attested at RV 1.32.1 (and in the second verse, depending on the editorial division of the line) as the announcing-line of the Vṛtra-hymn, and Watkins's monograph treats this attestation as the most concentrated and most recoverable Vedic instance of the inherited Indo-European poetic structure.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 41, on RV 1.32.1 (and 1.32.2) as the principal Vedic attestation of the áhann áhim formula; Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation of RV 1.32.1–2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 41

The áhann áhim formula's structural elements are: the subject (the hero, here Indra), an aorist verb of killing (Sanskrit _áhan_, "he slew"), and the serpent-object (Sanskrit _áhim_, "the serpent" — accusative of _ahi_), with phonetic and rhythmic features (the figura etymologica of _áhan_ and _áhim_, the alliterative repetition of the initial vowel) that Watkins treats as the formula's poetic-marker.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 41, on the structural elements of the áhann áhim formula and on the phonetic-and-rhythmic features Watkins identifies as the formula's poetic-marker.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 41

The figura etymologica of _áhan_ and _áhim_ (the verb of slaying and the serpent-object built on the same phonetic shape) is one of the load-bearing features of the formula in Watkins's reconstruction: the etymological figure is preserved in Vedic and recoverable, with appropriate sound-changes, in the comparable formulae of the other Indo-European traditions.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 41, on the figura etymologica of _áhan_ and _áhim_ and on its preservation across the Indo-European comparanda.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 41

Watkins's argument is that the áhann áhim formula is not a Vedic poetic invention but the Vedic reflex of an inherited Indo-European combat-line, with comparable formulae attested in Hittite (Tarḫunna's killing of the serpent Illuyanka in the *kuenta... illuyanka* line), in Greek (Apollo's killing of Python and the wider serpent-killing diction), in Norse (Thor's killing of Jörmungandr), and in Iranian (the Avestan Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat-language).footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the cross-language attestation of the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula in Vedic, Hittite, Greek, Norse, and Iranian.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula in Watkins's reconstruction is therefore not a content-claim about a single inherited story but a formal-poetic claim about an inherited line of combat-poetic diction whose continuity across the daughter languages is documented at the level of words, sounds, and syntactic arrangement.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the formal-poetic (rather than content-narrative) status of the reconstructed HERO SLAY SERPENT formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

Indra/Vṛtra is the anchor case for Watkins's formula: it is the most concentrated Indo-European attestation of the HERO SLAY SERPENT line in a single text (RV 1.32.1–2), it is the most fully contextualised by a continuous combat-narrative within the same hymn, and it is the most often invoked across Watkins's chapters 41 to 48 as the comparison against which the other daughter-language attestations are read.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on Indra/Vṛtra as the anchor case for the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula and as the central comparison for the other Indo-European attestations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The cross-cultural reflexes of the áhann áhim formula are deferred in the present book to Part II C5, the comparative chapter on HERO SLAY SERPENT, where the Hittite Illuyanka, Greek Apollo-Python, Norse Thor-Jörmungandr, and Iranian Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka attestations are treated together with the Vedic line; the present chapter introduces the formula in its Vedic form only.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the deferral of cross-cultural reflexes to Part II C5; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the comparative architecture of the formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The áhann áhim formula's status in the Rigvedic corpus is itself widely attested: the formula and its near-variants recur in multiple Vṛtra-hymns and in further Indra-hymns, so that the formula is a piece of Rigvedic poetic-ritual diction in active use across the corpus rather than a fossilised line preserved in one location.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the wider attestation of the áhann áhim formula and its variants across the Rigvedic Indra-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The formula's wide attestation in active Vedic poetry is one of the principal reasons Watkins treats it as the strongest available evidence for the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT line: in Vedic the formula is not merely surviving but actively productive, in a way that makes its inherited status more visible than in some of the other daughter-language attestations.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the actively productive status of the formula in Vedic and on its evidentiary weight in the reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The áhann áhim formula and the cult-title _vṛtrahán_ ("obstruction-slayer" / "Vṛtra-slayer") together constitute the principal Vedic combat-vocabulary of the chapter: the formula is the act-line of the combat, and the cult-title is the agent's resulting designation, and the two together define Indra's identity as a combat-figure in the Vedic tradition.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the formula and the cult-title as the principal Vedic combat-vocabulary; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on _vṛtrahán_ as agent-designation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

Indra's formula and cult-title also define the chapter's principal forward-link to Part II C5: the comparative chapter on HERO SLAY SERPENT will treat the áhann áhim formula and the _vṛtrahán_ cult-title alongside the Hittite, Greek, Norse, and Iranian comparanda, and the present chapter is the principal anchor for what the comparative chapter inherits in Vedic form.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the forward-link to Part II C5; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the comparative architecture.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

Watkins's reconstruction is itself a scholarly synthesis in the same sense as the chapter's hymn-cycle reconstruction of the Vṛtra-combat narrative: a methodologically explicit recovery of an inherited form from a distributed body of attestations, with the recovery's procedure and limits acknowledged at every step.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the explicit methodological character of the HERO SLAY SERPENT reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The áhann áhim formula is therefore introduced in the present chapter at hymn-and-verse level (RV 1.32.1–2), with brief reference to its wider Vedic attestation, and with the cross-cultural reflexes deferred to Part II C5 in line with the chapter brief's instruction to "keep brief here".footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions on keeping the cross-cultural reflexes brief in the present chapter; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the comparative architecture deferred to Part II C5.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The Watkins formula's relationship to the chapter's wider argument is twofold: at the level of poetics, the formula identifies the Vedic Vṛtra-combat as a recognisable instance of an inherited Indo-European poetic diction; at the level of comparative method, the formula identifies which features of the Vedic combat are inherited and which are Vedic innovations, and so disciplines the comparative treatment to follow.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the formula's twin role in identifying the inherited poetic diction and in disciplining the comparative method.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

=== The cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic reading

The Rigvedic Vṛtra-combat does cosmogonic work in three explicit and named ways within the eight-hymn corpus: it separates heaven and earth (most explicitly in RV 1.32.11–12 and in RV 10.113), it releases the cosmic waters (the seven rivers of RV 1.32.2 and the obstruction-and-release narrative of RV 5.32), and it produces dawn (the dawn-after-Vṛtra-killing of the RV 1.32 cosmogonic-consequence verses).footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the three named cosmogonic effects of the Vṛtra-combat in RV 1.32 and RV 10.113; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the same triple effect.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2; 1.32.11-12; 5.32; 10.113

The combat is therefore cosmologically active in a strong sense: the Rigvedic cosmos as we encounter it in the post-combat hymns is a cosmos that the Vṛtra-killing has made — heaven and earth are separated by the killing, the rivers are running because of the killing, and the dawn rises because of the killing.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the strong-sense cosmological activity of the Vṛtra-killing in the Rigvedic cosmology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and 10.113

The combat is, however, not cosmogonic in the Mesopotamian sense: there is no body-of-Vṛtra-as-cosmos episode parallel to *Enūma Eliš* IV's splitting of Tiamat into the upper and lower halves of the world, and Indra's combat reorders an already-existing cosmos rather than physically fabricating the cosmos out of the slain serpent's body.footnote:[Lambert 2013, on the body-of-Tiamat-as-cosmos episode in *Enūma Eliš* IV; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the absence of such an episode in the Rigvedic Vṛtra-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=*Enūma Eliš* IV

The Marduk-Tiamat episode and the Indra-Vṛtra episode therefore differ at a structural rather than incidental level: the Mesopotamian combat is the founding moment of the cosmos itself, whereas the Vedic combat is the founding moment of the cosmos's functional operation (its waters, its day-night cycle, its sky-earth separation).footnote:[Lambert 2013, on the founding-moment-of-the-cosmos status of the Marduk-Tiamat combat; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the founding-moment-of-the-cosmos's-operation status of the Indra-Vṛtra combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=*Enūma Eliš* IV; Jamison and Brereton 2014 RV 1.32

[INFERENCE: the chapter's "cosmologically active but not cosmogonic in the Mesopotamian sense" framing is the chapter's editorial synthesis of the textual evidence, drawing on Jamison and Brereton 2014's translations and notes on the Vṛtra-corpus and on Lambert 2013's edition of *Enūma Eliš*; the framing is well-supported by the textual evidence on both sides but is itself a typological claim that no single source articulates in this exact form. | source: Jamison and Brereton 2014; Lambert 2013; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions | scope: typological-framing claim for Part II C1, flagged for transparency.]

The cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic framing has the consequence that the Vedic combat fits naturally into a typology of "combat that does cosmogonic work without doing cosmogony of the body-as-cosmos type", a typology that the Part II C1 comparative chapter on cosmogonic combats develops at length and to which the present chapter is the principal Vedic anchor.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions on the cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic distinction as load-bearing for Part II C1; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Vedic combat's place within such a typology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and 10.113

The cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic framing also distinguishes the Vedic combat from the Hittite Kumarbi-Ullikummi combat, the Greek Zeus-Typhon combat, and the Norse Thor-Jörmungandr combat in different specific ways: the Hittite combat is a sovereignty-succession contest in which the cosmos is already established, the Greek combat is a sovereignty-confirmation contest after Zeus's accession, and the Norse combat is an eschatological contest at the world's end.footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the structural distinctions among the Hittite, Greek, Norse, and Vedic combats; West 2007, ch. 6, on the same distinctions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48

The Vedic combat is closer than any of these to a cosmologically active combat in the strong sense — closer than the Hittite, the Greek, or the Norse — without crossing the line to cosmogonic-from-the-body in the Mesopotamian sense, and the chapter's typological position for Indra-Vṛtra in Part II C1 is therefore "cosmologically active, not cosmogonic from the body".footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the typological positioning; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the strong-sense cosmological activity; Lambert 2013, on the body-cosmogonic comparison.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32

The release of the waters in RV 1.32.2 is the closest the Rigvedic combat comes to a body-and-cosmos episode: the released waters flow over and past Vṛtra's body, and the body of the slain serpent is visible in the watercourse as the watercourse runs.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.2 and 1.32.7, on the released waters running over the body of Vṛtra.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2 and 1.32.7

The body-in-the-watercourse image is not, however, a body-as-cosmos image: Vṛtra's body remains a discrete dead body that the rivers run past, and the rivers themselves are not made of Vṛtra's body but flow from the cosmic source that Vṛtra had been obstructing.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, translation and notes of RV 1.32.2, 1.32.7, and 1.32.10, on the released waters as flowing from a pre-existing cosmic source rather than being made of Vṛtra's body.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.2; 1.32.7; 1.32.10

The Vedic body-and-watercourse image is therefore a "mountain-form-and-water" tableau in which the obstructing serpent is recognisably mountain-shaped (RV 1.32.5) and is split (RV 1.32.2), with the waters flowing past the body rather than being made of the body — a characteristic Indo-European storm-god / waters tableau that West's chapter 6 treats as a recurrent type within the wider IE corpus.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the storm-god / waters tableau in the Indo-European corpus; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Vedic instance in RV 1.32.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic distinction is sharpest at the level of what the slain adversary's body becomes: in *Enūma Eliš* IV the body becomes the cosmos, in RV 1.32 the body remains a body and the cosmos is what the killing reorders rather than what the body is fabricated into.footnote:[Lambert 2013, on the body-becomes-cosmos episode in *Enūma Eliš* IV; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the body-remains-body and cosmos-reordered framing in RV 1.32.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=*Enūma Eliš* IV

The cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic distinction is also load-bearing for the chapter's comparative pairing of the Vedic combat with the Iranian Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat treated in chapter 13 of the present volume: the Iranian combat similarly reorders an existing cosmos (containing the rebound serpent under the mountain) rather than fabricating the cosmos from the serpent's body, and the Indo-Iranian comparand confirms the cosmologically-active-but-not-body-cosmogonic shape as the inherited Indo-Iranian pattern.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the Indo-Iranian comparand with chapter 13; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the structural similarity of the Indo-Iranian combats; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian discussion

The triple cosmogonic effect — heaven-earth separation, water-release, dawn-production — is therefore in the chapter's reading the diagnostic feature of the Vedic combat's cosmologically active character, and the absence of body-as-cosmos is the diagnostic feature of its non-Mesopotamian-cosmogonic character.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the triple cosmogonic effect; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the diagnostic features.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and 10.113

The Vedic combat is also distinct from the Mesopotamian Marduk-Tiamat combat at the level of pre-existing cosmos: the Vedic combat presupposes an already-existing world with rivers, mountains, and gods, in which Vṛtra is an obstruction that must be removed; the Mesopotamian combat presupposes only the primordial waters out of which Marduk's victory creates the cosmos.footnote:[Lambert 2013, on the primordial-waters-only setting of the Mesopotamian combat; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the already-existing-world setting of the Vedic combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=*Enūma Eliš* I-IV

The Vedic combat's already-existing-world setting is one of the chapter's principal reasons for resisting the older comparative literature's tendency to assimilate the Vedic combat to the Mesopotamian: the two combats are doing structurally different work in their respective cosmologies, and the comparative treatment must preserve the difference.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions on resisting Mesopotamian assimilation; West 2007, ch. 6, on the structural difference at the level of pre-existing cosmos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The cosmological-but-not-cosmogonic reading is therefore the chapter's principal typological contribution to Part II C1 and the principal warrant for distinguishing the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra combat from the Mesopotamian Marduk-Tiamat combat without denying the structural family-resemblance that has made the comparison perennially attractive.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the typological contribution; Jamison and Brereton 2014; Lambert 2013; West 2007 ch. 6.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 and 10.113

The structural family-resemblance between Indra-Vṛtra and Marduk-Tiamat — splitting of an obstructing-or-monstrous adversary by the storm-god, release of waters, ordering of the cosmos — is real, but the transmission route is not demonstrable, and West 2007 treats the parallel as structural-typological rather than as evidence of historical transmission.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the structural-typological character of the Indra-Marduk parallel and on the absence of demonstrable transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Vedic combat's cosmologically active character is also the basis of its ritual-aetiological function in the soma sacrifice: the released waters of the killing become the rains that fertilise the cultivated world, and the soma sacrifice's annual recurrence is the ritual replay of a cosmogonic-functional episode that has already once occurred.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the ritual-aetiological function of the cosmologically active combat in the soma sacrifice.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=introduction; Indra-hymn notes

=== The post-Vedic brahminicide reframing

The post-Vedic Vedic-Brahmin literature reframes the Vṛtra-combat in a sequence of texts whose principal exemplars are the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* 1.6.3–4 (a late-Vedic prose ritual-theological text) and the *Mahābhārata* Udyogaparvan 9–18 (an early-classical Sanskrit epic), and in this reframing Vṛtra is presented as a Brahmin and Indra's killing of him is a sin of brahminicide that requires expiation.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the post-Vedic brahminicide reframing of the Vṛtra-combat in the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* and the *Mahābhārata*; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the same reframing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* 1.6.3–4 develops a ritual-theological narrative in which Vṛtra is a priestly figure (and so a Brahmin in the post-Vedic varṇa terminology), and in which Indra's killing of him generates ritual pollution that must be addressed by expiatory ritual rather than celebrated as an unmixed warrior triumph.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* 1.6.3–4 narrative as a ritual-theological reframing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Mahābhārata* Udyogaparvan 9–18 develops the brahminicide-and-expiation narrative more extensively: Vṛtra is presented as a virtuous Brahmin sage, Indra's killing of him is staged with explicit moral discomfort, and the narrative-frame includes an expiation sequence that addresses the karmic and ritual consequences of the killing.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the *Mahābhārata* Udyogaparvan 9–18 narrative as the extended brahminicide-and-expiation reframing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The post-Vedic reframing has the consequence that the Vṛtra-figure of the *Mahābhārata* and the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* is a different narrative entity from the Vṛtra-figure of the Rigveda: the Rigvedic Vṛtra is an obstructing-and-mountain-form serpent whose killing is celebrated as Indra's chief manly deed, and the post-Vedic Vṛtra is a virtuous Brahmin whose killing is morally complicated and ritually polluting.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the entity-difference between the Rigvedic Vṛtra and the post-Vedic Vṛtra; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the no-merge rule.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

[VARIANT: primary=Rigvedic combat (cosmological, ritual-invocation, water-release; eight-hymn Vṛtra-corpus) | alt=post-Vedic brahminicide reframing (Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa 1.6.3–4; Mahābhārata Udyogaparvan 9–18) | chosen=Rigvedic-primary, post-Vedic-as-clearly-marked-later-transformation per brief special_instructions | reason=the brief mandates that the Rigvedic combat be treated as the chapter's primary object and the post-Vedic reframing as a clearly-marked later transformation that must not be merged with the Rigvedic combat; the chapter accordingly gives the Rigvedic combat its full eight-hymn treatment and the post-Vedic reframing a clearly bounded sub-section.]

The post-Vedic reframing is itself a piece of evidence for the cultural centrality of the Vṛtra-combat in the long Indian tradition: the combat is important enough that the post-Vedic ritual-theological tradition reworks it to fit its own moral and ritual concerns, and the brahminicide reframing is a creative transformation of an inherited combat-narrative rather than a contradiction of it.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the cultural centrality of the Vṛtra-combat as evidenced by its post-Vedic reworking.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The post-Vedic reframing is, however, not the chapter's focus, and the chapter treats it within a clearly-bounded sub-section so that the comparative reader can see the reframing as a distinct evidentiary layer rather than as part of the Rigvedic combat itself.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions on the clearly-bounded sub-section and the no-merge rule; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same boundary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The post-Vedic reframing has its own forward-link in the present book to chapter 15 (the Vedic family chapter), where the longer arc of post-Rigvedic Indian dragon-combat material — including the bhakti-period Krishna-Kāliya combat — is treated alongside the Brāhmaṇa and *Mahābhārata* reflexes.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the forward-link to chapter 15; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same long arc.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The post-Vedic reframing also marks a transition in the moral status of the combat-killing within Indian thought: what is in the Rigveda an unmixed warrior triumph becomes in the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* and the *Mahābhārata* a morally complicated act that requires expiation, and this transition tracks a wider development in classical Indian ethics about the moral status of killing as such.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the moral-status transition in the post-Vedic reframing of the combat-killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The post-Vedic reframing must therefore not be merged with the Rigvedic combat at the level of either narrative content or moral framing: the two are different texts with different doctrinal assumptions, and conflating them would distort both the Rigvedic combat (by reading back later moral concern) and the post-Vedic reframing (by stripping it of its critical engagement with the inherited narrative).footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml special_instructions on the no-merge rule; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the doctrinal-assumption difference.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The chapter's treatment of the post-Vedic reframing is therefore brief, clearly bounded, and forward-linked: it acknowledges the reframing's existence and importance, identifies its principal textual loci (the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* 1.6.3–4 and the *Mahābhārata* Udyogaparvan 9–18), notes the Vṛtra-as-Brahmin transformation, and forwards the wider reception-history to chapter 15 and to the chapter's comparative hooks.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the brief-and-bounded treatment and the forward-link; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the principal textual loci.]

=== Indo-Iranian cognancy and forward links

Vedic _ahi_ ("serpent") and Avestan _aži_ ("serpent") are philologically the same word: the two terms are regular phonological reflexes of a common Indo-Iranian *_ajhi-_ in their respective languages, and the cognancy is one of the load-bearing exhibits of Indo-Iranian historical linguistics.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the cognancy of Vedic _ahi_ and Avestan _aži_; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same cognancy as evidence for the Indo-Iranian phase of the combat-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian phonology and lexicon

The cognancy of _ahi_ and _aži_ has the consequence that the principal serpent-term of the Vedic Vṛtra-combat (the _áhim_ of the áhann áhim formula) and the principal serpent-term of the Avestan Aži Dahāka combat (the _aži_ of the Avestan combat-language) are linguistically the same word, and that the Indo-Iranian combat-myth corpus inherits a shared serpent-vocabulary at the language-historical level.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the shared serpent-vocabulary of the Indo-Iranian combat-myth corpus; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the áhann áhim and Avestan combat-language.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian phonology and lexicon

Trita Āptya (Sanskrit त्रित आप्त्य) is the older Vedic combat-figure whose Avestan cognate is Thraētaona, the Avestan slayer of Aži Dahāka treated in chapter 13 of the present volume, and the Vedic-Avestan cognancy of the two figures is one of the principal exhibits of Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph and of Watkins's *How to Kill a Dragon*.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Trita Āptya-Thraētaona cognancy; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same cognancy and on its position in the Indo-European combat-poetic family; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Trita Āptya in the Rigvedic corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

Trita Āptya appears in the Rigveda as a combat-figure who is in places associated with the Vṛtra-killing — sometimes as a junior partner of Indra in the killing, sometimes as a standalone slayer of a similar adversary — and the Vedic textual situation is the clearest evidence that the Trita-figure is the older Indo-Iranian combat-figure whom Indra's emergence has partially displaced.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Trita Āptya as the older Indo-Iranian combat-figure partially displaced by Indra; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Trita Āptya material in the Rigvedic corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The Trita Āptya / Thraētaona cognancy is therefore the chapter's principal Indo-Iranian forward-link, and it is also the chapter's principal back-link to chapter 13 (the Iranian Aži Dahāka chapter), where Thraētaona's combat is treated in its Avestan and post-Avestan forms.footnote:[Brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the Trita-Thraētaona cognancy as forward-and-back-link; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the cognancy itself.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The Vedic _vṛtrahán_ ("Vṛtra-slayer") and the Avestan Vərəθraγna (the divine name of the cult-figure of victory, anglicized Verethragna) are the second principal Indo-Iranian cognancy of the chapter: the two terms are philologically the same compound (an obstruction-noun plus a slaying-suffix) in the two languages, and Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph treats the cognancy as the principal evidence for an inherited Indo-Iranian combat-cult.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vedic _vṛtrahán_ / Avestan Vərəθraγna cognancy; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Vṛtrahán/Vərəθraγna discussion

The two Indo-Iranian cognancies — _ahi_ / _aži_ at the lexical level and _vṛtrahán_ / Vərəθraγna at the cult-title level — together establish the Indo-Iranian phase of the combat-myth as a documented language-historical fact rather than as a purely structural-typological hypothesis: the Vedic and Avestan witnesses share a vocabulary, not just a story-shape.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the two Indo-Iranian cognancies as documented language-historical evidence; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the documented-vocabulary status of the Indo-Iranian combat-myth.]
