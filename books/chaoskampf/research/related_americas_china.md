# Chaoskampf Research: Americas and China (Related / Provisional Cases)

Research notes compiled 2026-04-23 for the mythology book on combat-with-chaos stories across cultures. Focus: four candidate stories from the Americas and East Asia that are frequently brought into comparative chaoskampf discussions but whose area specialists often resist the comparative frame.

---

## 1. Cipactli (Aztec / Nahua)

### What the story tells
Cipactli ("crocodile / caiman / spiny lizard" in Nahuatl) is the primordial sea-monster from which the earth is made. In the best-preserved version (the *Histoyre du Mechique*, a 16th-century French manuscript translating a lost Nahuatl/Spanish original by fray Andrés de Olmos, c. 1543), before creation there is only dark primordial water containing Cipactli, a vast crocodilian/fish/toad hybrid covered in mouths at every joint. Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl descend as serpents; Tezcatlipoca uses his own foot as bait, loses it to Cipactli's jaws, and together the two gods seize the monster, pull her apart, and fashion the cosmos from her dismembered body: head becomes the heavens (in some variants the thirteen upper levels), tail the underworld, middle the earth; hair becomes trees and herbs, skin grass and flowers, eyes wells and caverns, mouth rivers, nose valleys, shoulders mountains. The *Leyenda de los Soles* (part of the Codex Chimalpopoca, late 16th c., Nahuatl) and the *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas* give shorter, partly overlapping variants. A key variant problem: Cipactli is often conflated/identified with **Tlaltecuhtli** (the "earth-lord", depicted as a squatting toad-crocodile), and the dismemberment story appears attached to either figure.

### Primary textual attestation and transmission problems
- **Histoyre du Mechique** (Thevet MS, c. 1553 French translation): the single fullest narrative; Thevet translates a now-lost Spanish text by the Franciscan Andrés de Olmos (c. 1543), who in turn worked from Nahua informants in the Chalco/Tenochtitlan area. Three layers of mediation (Nahua oral tradition -> Olmos in Castilian -> Thevet's French) before any surviving written form.
- **Leyenda de los Soles** (1558, part of the *Codex Chimalpopoca*): Nahuatl text, possibly compiled by disciples of Sahagún; the physical MS is lost but transcriptions survive. More cosmogonic-historical than narrative.
- **Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas** (c. 1530s–40s): anonymous Spanish gloss of a lost pictorial MS.
- All three are post-Conquest, post-1521, written under Franciscan supervision. No pre-contact pictorial source preserves the full dismemberment narrative; the iconographic tradition (Tlaltecuhtli reliefs, e.g. the 2006 Templo Mayor monolith excavated by Leonardo López Luján) confirms an earth-monster theme but not the specific narrative shape.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Guilhem Olivier** (*Mockeries and Metamorphoses of an Aztec God: Tezcatlipoca*, Univ. Press of Colorado, 2003): treats the Tezcatlipoca–Cipactli episode as a foundational sacrificial-creation myth centered on Tezcatlipoca's lost foot, not as combat-cosmogony in the Near-Eastern sense. The emphasis is on bait-sacrifice and transformation, not heroic duel.
- **Alfredo López Austin** (*Tamoanchan, Tlalocan*; *Los mitos del tlacuache*): frames Mesoamerican cosmogony through the "núcleo duro" (hard nucleus) of Mesoamerican worldview — dual male/female earth, layered cosmos, cyclical destruction/renewal. Dismemberment here is a *creation-by-sacrifice* pattern (shared with Nanahuatzin/Fifth Sun, with maize-from-bones), not chaos-combat. López Austin explicitly resists importing Eurasian templates.
- **Miguel León-Portilla** (*La filosofía náhuatl*, *Aztec Thought and Culture*): reads the creation narratives philosophically in terms of Ometeotl and the dual principle; does not use "chaoskampf" as an organizing category.
- **Doris Heyden**: her published work focuses on caves, Teotihuacan, and Aztec symbolism; I did not find an explicit Heyden treatment of Cipactli-as-chaoskampf in search results — noting uncertainty.
- Comparative/general sources (Wikipedia, popular mythology websites, and the Birmingham MRes thesis by Rosanna Rackley, *Kingship, Struggle, and Creation: The Story of Chaoskampf*) do list Cipactli as a chaoskampf analogue, but area specialists do not.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- The chaoskampf label is a 19th-century German (Gunkel) category coined for Enūma Eliš and the Hebrew Bible; even within Near-Eastern studies (Tsumura, Watson, Ballentine) its overuse is now criticized. Applying it to Mesoamerica compounds the problem: Mesoamerican cosmogony centers on cyclical suns, sacrificial dismemberment, and dual/complementary (not adversarial) principles. Cipactli is not a chaos-opponent in the Tiamat/Leviathan sense — she is the *substance* of the earth-to-be, and her "killing" is a productive sacrifice, closer to Ymir (Norse) or Puruṣa (Vedic) than to Marduk-vs-Tiamat.
- López Austin and Olivier both insist on reading Mesoamerican myth from internal categories (teixiptla, sacrifice, cempohualli, tonalli) rather than through imported combat-myth grids.

### Verdict
- **RELATED (dismemberment-cosmogony, not chaoskampf)**
- Confidence: **HIGH**
- The Cipactli narrative is a dismemberment cosmogony in the Ymir/Puruṣa family: a primordial body is pulled apart to form the world. There is a brief trickery/capture (the foot-as-bait), but no sustained combat, no storm-god-vs-sea-dragon typology, and no restoration-of-order-against-chaos structure. Area specialists (Olivier, López Austin) do not frame it as chaoskampf; only comparativist/popular sources do.

## 2. Yu the Great and Xiangliu (Chinese)

### What the story tells
The Chinese "Great Flood" cycle is a composite. In the *Shujing / Book of Documents* (Canon of Yao, Canon of Shun, "Yugong"/Tribute of Yu), the flood appears as a cosmic-political crisis: Yao appoints Gun (Yu's father) who tries to dam the waters and fails; his son **Yu** succeeds by dredging channels and guiding the water to the sea, and on that merit founds the Xia dynasty. There is no monster combat in this version — the action is hydraulic engineering plus ritual-political ordering.

The monster layer comes from the *Shanhaijing / Classic of Mountains and Seas* (compiled Warring States to Han, c. 4th c. BCE – 2nd c. CE). In **Haiwai beijing** (juan 8, "Classic of Regions Beyond the Seas: North") and **Dahuang beijing** (juan 17, "Great Wilderness: North"), the nine-headed serpent **Xiangliu** (variant *Xiangyao* 相繇) is described as a minister of the water-god **Gonggong**; Yu kills him, but the serpent's blood/stench poisons the land so that nothing grows and Yu must build a terrace/altar (the "Terrace of the Many Emperors"). The *Huainanzi* (2nd c. BCE, Liu An) attributes the flood itself to Gonggong, who after losing a cosmic struggle with Zhuanxu rams Mount Buzhou and tilts the heavens (this tilt-of-heaven episode is distinct from the Yu-Xiangliu killing and is better understood as a pillar-of-heaven etiology, not chaoskampf proper).

Key variants: the Xiangliu-slayer is sometimes Yu, sometimes the fire-god **Zhurong**. Gonggong's adversary is variably Zhuanxu, Zhurong, or Nüwa (who then patches the sky with five-colored stones). Yu's heroism is almost always hydraulic, not martial.

### Primary textual attestation and transmission problems
- **Shujing** ("Yugong", "Canons"): traditionally pre-Confucian but textually heterogeneous; modern sinology dates the received *Yugong* to the Warring States or early Han. This is the Yu-as-engineer layer.
- **Shanhaijing** juan 8 and 17: textually heterogeneous compilation, 4th c. BCE – 2nd c. CE, with the *Dahuangjing* section generally considered latest. Transmitted text heavily glossed by Guo Pu (3rd c. CE).
- **Huainanzi** (c. 139 BCE): Han synthesis, Daoist-leaning, written at the court of Liu An; includes the Gonggong-hits-Mount-Buzhou cosmogony and the Nüwa sky-patching.
- **Tianwen** ("Heavenly Questions", *Chuci*, c. 3rd c. BCE): alludes to Gun/Yu flood and to Gonggong.
- All primary sources are literate, elite, post-Zhou, and rework much older (likely Shang/Xia?) oral materials that are no longer directly recoverable; the Shang-era material Sarah Allan tries to reconstruct is pre-textual and speculative.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Mark Edward Lewis**, *The Flood Myths of Early China* (SUNY, 2006): the standard monograph. Lewis reads the flood cycle as a foundational myth of *social order* — the flood is linked to criminality (Gonggong, Gun) and the taming of it establishes the lineage, the married couple, the state, even the human body. The model is *ordering* and *boundary-making*, not heroic dragon-slaying. Lewis does not frame Yu as a chaoskampf hero.
- **Anne Birrell**, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction* (Johns Hopkins, 1993): catalogues Yu and Xiangliu under flood myths and hero myths, uses comparative categories more freely than other sinologists, and does discuss a "combat myth" type that touches Xiangliu — she is the most comparativist of the specialists on this question, but still distinguishes Yu's dredging from true combat-cosmogony.
- **Sarah Allan**, *The Shape of the Turtle* (SUNY, 1991): reconstructs Shang cosmology centered on a squared-earth / domed-sky schema with the Yellow River as a key axis; she does not use chaoskampf and treats Yu's flood-taming as part of the sun/ten-suns and dynastic legitimation complex. (Her turtle-cosmos thesis is contested.)
- **Rémi Mathieu**, *Étude sur la mythologie et l'ethnologie de la Chine ancienne* (Paris, 1983) and annotated French translation of the *Shanhaijing* (*Le Livre des monts et des mers*): philological/ethnological treatment; records Xiangliu as a chthonic water-monster, minister of Gonggong, slain by Yu, without forcing the chaoskampf label.
- **Kwang-chih Chang**: archaeological-anthropological work on Shang; his "Asian-American shamanism" comparative thesis is broad but does not apply chaoskampf to Yu. No findable Chang treatment of the Yu-Xiangliu material as chaos-combat.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- Chinese dragon mythology is fundamentally different from Near-Eastern/Indo-European dragon mythology: the *long* 龍 is usually benevolent, rain-bringing, and imperial; monstrous water-serpents like Xiangliu and the *jiaolong* are marked *exceptions*, not the norm. Imposing the Indo-European storm-god-vs-serpent template risks misreading the whole category.
- The Yu narrative is centrally about **hydraulic engineering and moral-political order** — dredging vs. damming, guidance vs. confrontation. Yu's virtue is persistence (he famously passes his own home three times without entering); this is a sage-king myth, not a combat myth.
- Xiangliu-slaying is real but marginal within the Yu corpus: the *Shujing* does not mention it; it is a *Shanhaijing* episode. Treating it as central distorts the primary ancient framing.
- Lewis in particular cautions against reading Chinese flood myths through Near-Eastern cosmogonic lenses; his analytic framework is drawn from the internal Chinese concerns of lineage, body, and state.

### Verdict
- **PROVISIONAL / RELATED (composite case)**
- Confidence: **MEDIUM-HIGH**
- The **Yu-tames-flood** main storyline is *not* chaoskampf — it is a hydraulic ordering myth that sinologists (Lewis, Allan, Mathieu) explicitly read through non-combat categories. The **Yu-kills-Xiangliu** episode in *Shanhaijing* is a genuine nine-headed-serpent-slaying motif and can reasonably be compared to chaoskampf as a subordinate component, but it is not the cosmogonic center of the Chinese flood cycle and area specialists treat it as a localized *Shanhaijing* monster-lore rather than a creation-combat. Include only with careful framing.

## 3. Pariacaca vs. Amaru (Andean / Huarochirí)

### What the story tells
The *Huarochirí Manuscript* (c. 1598–1608, Classical Quechua) centers its middle chapters on **Pariacaca**, a high-mountain rain/storm/lightning huaca who is born from five eggs on Mt. Condor Coto and hatches as five falcons that become five brothers (Pariacaca is simultaneously one and five). The central conflict is Pariacaca vs. **Huallallo Carhuincho** (or Wallallu Qarwinchu), an older fire-eating huaca — a man-eating fire god of the pre-Pariacaca dispensation — fought across several chapters (most densely **chapters 8 and 9** in the Salomon/Urioste English division: "How Paria Caca ascended" / "How Paria Caca, having accomplished all this, began to ordain his own cult"; chapter 16 later returns to Huallallo's final banishment).

Pariacaca's weapon is hail, rain and lightning; he floods Huallallo's fire-country, turning it into a lagoon, and drives Huallallo east to the Antis (Amazonian lowlands). The **amaru** appears as Huallallo's summoned ally: a vast two-headed serpent that emerges from a mountain to fight Pariacaca. Pariacaca petrifies / transfixes the amaru with his golden staff (the petrified amaru is identified with a landscape feature — a standard Huarochirí pattern where huaca bodies become mountains, lagoons, stones). The amaru is thus a subordinate antagonist, not the cosmic adversary; Huallallo is the real rival.

Note on the word *amaru*: in broader Andean usage (including Inca imperial iconography) the *amaru* is a two-headed cosmic serpent, linked with water, pachacuti (world-turn), and the lower world *Ukhu Pacha* — it is ambivalent (fertility and catastrophe), not purely adversarial.

### Primary textual attestation and transmission problems
- **Huarochirí Manuscript** (Biblioteca Nacional de España, MS 3169, ff. 64r–114r): c. 1598–1608, written in Classical Quechua, anonymous (plausibly the Andean ladino Cristóbal Choque Casa or a collaborator of Francisco de Ávila). Compiled at the behest of **Francisco de Ávila**, parish priest of San Damián and notorious "extirpator of idolatries", who used it as intelligence for his anti-huaca campaigns. It is the *only* sustained pre-dictionary Quechua prose source for native Andean mythology.
- Deep mediation paradox: the text is a colonial product, gathered to destroy the tradition it records, yet it speaks largely from inside Andean categories. Salomon calls it "a testament of ancient and colonial Andean religion" — *and* colonial.
- No pre-contact Andean writing system; all comparanda (Inca oral sources in Spanish chroniclers — Cieza, Betanzos, Molina, Guaman Poma) are similarly post-Conquest.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Frank Salomon and George Urioste** (translation introduction, *The Huarochirí Manuscript*, U. Texas Press, 1991): frame the Pariacaca-Huallallo sequence as a *huaca-succession* narrative — an older fire/drought regime displaced by a newer water/rain regime — rooted in the Andean logic of paired, competing *huacas* and local ecological rivalries (highland pastoralists vs. lowland/fire peoples). They do **not** use chaoskampf; they emphasize local Andean categories (huaca, camac, pacha). The amaru episode is treated as one of many huaca-combats within this regional theater.
- **Gerald Taylor** (French/Spanish edition, *Ritos y tradiciones de Huarochirí*, IEP/IFEA, multiple editions since 1987): philological translation and commentary; reads the Pariacaca cycle linguistically and ethnologically, stressing the specificity of Andean religious vocabulary. No chaoskampf framing.
- **Carolyn Dean** (*A Culture of Stone*, Duke, 2010): works on Inca dualism, *saya* division, and the agency of lithic huacas; her framework is Andean complementary opposition (*yanantin*) rather than chaos-vs-order.
- **Bruce Mannheim** (*The Language of the Inka since the European Invasion*, U. Texas Press, 1991; and joint work with Guillermo Salas Carreño): works on Quechua linguistics and on huacas as relational persons; emphasizes internal Andean semantic categories. No findable Mannheim chaoskampf framing.
- **Tom Zuidema**, **Gary Urton** (astro-cosmological tradition): read Andean myth through ceque systems, dualism, astronomy; their frame is structural and specifically Andean, not combat-cosmogonic.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- Andean religion is organized around **huacas** (sacred persons/places) and paired **moieties** (hanan/hurin, upper/lower; hanan pacha / kay pacha / ukhu pacha), not around a primordial chaos-vs-order combat. Pariacaca-vs-Huallallo is a *huaca succession* (ecological and calendrical — rain defeats fire, the cultivating water-regime supplants the scorched-earth regime), not a cosmogonic first-combat.
- The amaru is a *summoned ally* in the Huarochirí episode, not the primary adversary. Treating it as a chaoskampf sea-serpent mistakes a subordinate monster-helper for the cosmic antagonist.
- The *amaru* more broadly (Inca imperial contexts) is ambivalent — feared but also a regal totem — and linked to *pachacuti* (world-turn). *Pachacuti* is not "chaos" in the Gunkel sense: it is cyclic transformation, not a defeated foe.
- Salomon and Urioste's introduction repeatedly warns against reading Andean religion through European monotheist or classical categories; the Huarochirí cycle is structured by Andean *pacha* (space-time), *camay* (animating essence), and huaca genealogy.

### Verdict
- **RELATED (huaca-succession with a monster-helper, not chaoskampf)**
- Confidence: **MEDIUM-HIGH**
- Pariacaca's defeat of Huallallo (with the amaru as Huallallo's summoned ally, petrified by Pariacaca's staff) is a genuine storm-god-vs-serpent moment and *superficially* resembles chaoskampf, but Andeanists (Salomon/Urioste, Taylor, Dean, Mannheim) uniformly read it within local Andean categories of huaca succession, moiety opposition, and ecological displacement, not as a cosmogonic first-combat. The amaru here is an ally-monster, not a primordial chaos-serpent. Usable as a comparative analogue with heavy framing; do not present as a straightforward chaoskampf.

## 4. Nanabozho and Mishipeshu / Mishibijiw (Ojibwe / Anishinaabe)

### What the story tells
Two interlocking Anishinaabe narrative complexes bear on this case:

1. **The Great Flood / Earth-Diver cycle**: Nanabozho (also *Waynaboozhoo*, *Wenebojo*, *Nanabush*) is the Anishinaabe culture-hero/trickster/transformer. In the widespread flood story, his nephew (or brother, in some tellings — a young wolf named *Maheengun*) is drowned by the **underwater manitouk**, led by **Mishipeshu/Mishibijiw** (the Great Lynx / Underwater Panther). Nanabozho, grieving and enraged, finds and wounds (or kills) the chief underwater being; the underwater manitouk retaliate with a cosmic flood. Nanabozho survives floating on a log with a handful of animals, sends diver after diver (Loon, Beaver, Otter, finally **Muskrat** who succeeds) to bring up a speck of earth from the drowned world, and re-creates the world on **Turtle**'s back — Turtle Island. In some versions, the underwater manitouk later give Nanabozho the **Midewiwin** (medicine society) as reparation.

2. **The Thunderers vs. Underwater Manitouk dialectic**: at a larger cosmological level, *Animikiig* (Thunderbirds, upper-world sky beings) and the *Mishibizhiw*/underwater manitouk are perennial antagonists; thunder-storms are enactments of this rivalry. Nanabozho stands in for / sides with the upper-world/human side in this dialectic. Petroglyphs (Agawa Rock, Lake Superior) and wampum depict Mishipeshu with a spiky back, horned-cat body, and serpentine tail.

### Primary textual attestation and transmission problems
- No pre-contact written sources; Anishinaabe traditions are oral. The stories surface in:
  - **Jesuit Relations** and early French sources (17th c.) with garbled outsider framings.
  - **Henry Rowe Schoolcraft**'s 19th-century collections (mediated through his Ojibwe wife Jane Johnston Schoolcraft) — source of Longfellow's "Hiawatha", and therefore already at a remove from Anishinaabe cadence.
  - **William Warren** (*History of the Ojibway People*, 1885), Ojibwe-Anishinaabe himself.
  - 20th-century anthropologists: A. Irving **Hallowell** (*Ojibwa Ontology, Behavior, and World View*, 1960 — the classic source for "other-than-human persons"), Ruth Landes, Victor Barnouw.
  - Contemporary Anishinaabe authors: **Basil Johnston** (Wasauksing), **Edward Benton-Banai**, **Gerald Vizenor** (White Earth), **Leanne Betasamosake Simpson**.
- "Mishipeshu" pictography (Agawa Rock) is pre-contact material culture but does not give a fixed narrative.
- The flood / Earth-Diver story has dozens of tellings, varying by community (Ojibwe, Odawa, Potawatomi, Mississauga), by storyteller, and by context (public vs. ceremonial).

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Basil Johnston** (*Ojibway Heritage*, 1976; *The Manitous*, 1995): presents Nanabush and the underwater manitouk inside the Anishinaabe framework of *manidoog* (other-than-human persons) and the four orders of creation; the Mishipeshu episodes are moral-ecological (respect, reciprocity, consequence), not chaos-combat. Johnston emphasizes that Anishinaabe stories work through **relational personhood**, not good-vs-evil cosmogony.
- **Theresa S. Smith**, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg: Thunderers and Water Monsters in the Traditional Ojibwe Life-World* (U. of Idaho Press, 1995; reprinted Nebraska): the single most important scholarly treatment. Smith reads the Thunderbird / Mishipeshu relationship as a **dialectic** — a productive tension that structures the Anishinaabe lived world, not a battle to be won. Each pole is necessary; humans navigate between them through dreams, ceremony, and offerings. This is explicitly *not* a chaoskampf frame.
- **Gerald Vizenor** (*Trickster Discourse*, 1989; *Manifest Manners*, 1994): attacks the entire anthropological apparatus that tries to fit Nanabozho into cross-cultural "trickster" or "combat-myth" grids. His "trickster discourse" insists on the playful, survivance-oriented, elusive nature of Anishinaabe narrative; to pin Nanabozho-vs-Mishipeshu on a chaoskampf template is precisely the sort of academic capture he resists.
- **Chad Uran** (White Earth Anishinaabe, American Indian Studies, UW; *Transmotion* contributor): works on language, sovereignty, and narrative; part of the broader Native studies insistence that Anishinaabe stories be read within Anishinaabe epistemology, not through imported mythological typologies. (I did not find a specific Uran essay on Mishipeshu-as-chaoskampf; noting that the broader critique aligns with Vizenor/Simpson/Doerfler.)
- **A. Irving Hallowell**'s "Ojibwa Ontology, Behavior, and World View" (in *Culture in History*, ed. Diamond, 1960) remains foundational: his "other-than-human persons" framework rules out the chaos-vs-order binary from the start — Mishipeshu is a *person*, potentially dangerous, potentially allied, not an abstraction of chaos.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- Ojibwe specialists **explicitly warn** against mapping trickster-monster dynamics onto chaoskampf. The Anishinaabe cosmos is not binary (order vs. chaos) but *multi-polar and relational* — four directions, four orders of creation, upper/lower beings all persons with whom one negotiates. Reading Mishipeshu as "chaos" and Nanabozho (or Thunderbirds) as "order" imports a Mesopotamian-biblical dualism alien to Anishinaabe thought.
- The flood is not caused by a chaos-monster attacking order; it is *retaliation* for Nanabozho's own killing of the underwater being's relative — a reciprocity/vengeance sequence inside a network of persons. The moral economy is kinship and balance, not cosmic good-vs-evil.
- Mishipeshu is also a *giver* (copper, medicine, the Midewiwin); treating him purely as an adversary distorts the tradition.
- Vizenor, Simpson, and other Anishinaabe scholars warn that academic comparative-mythology categories (including "trickster" itself, as applied to Nanabozho) are colonial abstractions; "chaoskampf" would compound the harm.
- Recording-history warning: much 19th-century Schoolcraft/Longfellow material deforms the tradition; use Anishinaabe-authored sources (Johnston, Benton-Banai, Simpson) and careful ethnographers (Hallowell, Smith) in preference.

### Verdict
- **EXCLUDE from core chaoskampf; RELATED only as a distinct cosmological pattern**
- Confidence: **HIGH**
- Nanabozho vs. Mishipeshu is a flood-vengeance / Earth-Diver / dialectical-persons cycle, not a chaoskampf. Anishinaabe specialists (Johnston, Smith, Vizenor) and the underlying Anishinaabe framework (Hallowell's other-than-human persons; Thunderers/Underwater-beings as a necessary dialectic, not a defeated chaos) explicitly resist the chaos-vs-order mapping. Including it as chaoskampf would be an appropriative category mistake; it belongs, if anywhere in the book, as a *contrastive case* that shows what chaoskampf is *not*.

## Cross-case summary

| Story | Verdict | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| Cipactli | RELATED (dismemberment-cosmogony, not combat) | HIGH |
| Yu / Xiangliu | PROVISIONAL (Yu-flood: not; Xiangliu-slaying: marginal analogue) | MEDIUM-HIGH |
| Pariacaca / amaru | RELATED (huaca-succession, ally-monster) | MEDIUM-HIGH |
| Nanabozho / Mishipeshu | EXCLUDE from core; contrastive case only | HIGH |

Overall finding: all four are frequently pulled into popular chaoskampf lists, but area specialists in every case either reject or heavily qualify the label. A responsible mythology book should either exclude them, or include them as *contrastive / related* cases that illuminate the limits of the chaoskampf category.
