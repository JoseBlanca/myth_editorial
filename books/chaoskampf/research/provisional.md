# Provisional / flagged stories — Chaoskampf research

Decision-grade review of four stories flagged as doubtful for inclusion in the Chaoskampf book. For each: what the story is, how it is attested, whether serious scholars treat it as chaoskampf, an explicit appropriation warning, and a verdict.

---

## 1. Mucalinda (Buddhist)

### What the story actually tells
Mucalinda (Pāli: Mucalinda / Muccalinda; Sanskrit: Mucilinda) is the nāga-king who, in the week after the Buddha's awakening, emerges from his pool beside the Bodhi-manda when an unseasonable seven-day storm breaks. He coils his body seven times around the seated, meditating Buddha and spreads his hood above the Buddha's head to shield him "from cold and heat, from gadflies, mosquitoes, wind and sun, and the touch of creeping things." When the sky clears, Mucalinda resumes human form and pays homage. The episode is a PROTECTION narrative, not a combat narrative: no blow is struck, no adversary is defeated, and the serpent is a devotee, not an opponent.

### Primary textual / ethnographic attestation
- Vinaya Piṭaka, Mahāvagga I.3 (Khandhaka 1, ch. 3): the canonical Pāli setting immediately after enlightenment. Rhys Davids & Oldenberg's 1881 Sacred Books of the East translation is the standard English source.
- Udāna 2.1 ("Mucalindasutta") in the Khuddaka-Nikāya — a short parallel telling; translations by John D. Ireland (Access to Insight) and Bhikkhu Ānandajoti (SuttaCentral).
- Nidānakathā (introduction to the Jātaka); Lalitavistara and Mahāvastu preserve Sanskritic Mahāyāna parallels with variations (e.g. the hood count, the pool).
- Iconographically, the nāga-enthroned Buddha is heavily attested from roughly the 2nd century CE onward in South and Southeast Asia (Amarāvatī, then Khmer, Mon, Dvāravatī, and especially Angkorian material; see Robert L. Brown's work and Paul Mus, *Barabudur*, for the South-/Southeast-Asian iconography).

### Is this chaoskampf at all?
No serious Buddhist-studies scholar treats the Mucalinda episode as chaoskampf. The dominant scholarly frame is ASSIMILATION of a pre-Buddhist local serpent/water cult into the Buddhist hagiography, with the nāga becoming a devotee and protector (Robert DeCaroli, *Haunting the Buddha: Indian Popular Religions and the Formation of Buddhism*, Oxford University Press, 2004; John Strong, *The Buddha: A Short Biography*, 2001, and *Relics of the Buddha*, 2004). Compare also Robert L. Brown's treatment of the nāga-enthroned Buddha and Vidya Dehejia on early narrative reliefs, neither of whom frame it as combat. Shaw (*Buddhist Landscapes in Central India*, 2007) and Daud Ali's work on landscape cults similarly describe nāgas as LOCAL tutelary water-deities being incorporated, not subdued in cosmic battle. The narrative inverts the chaoskampf template: the serpent SHELTERS the hero.

### Appropriation warning
This is one of the clearest cases where a Near-Eastern "hero vs. serpent" grid would actively misread the source. Pāli and Sanskrit nāgas are not Leviathan-types. They are ambivalent water-spirits, often patrons and ancestors (royal lineages in Funan, Pallavas, etc. claim nāga descent). Reading Mucalinda as a "tamed chaos-monster" imposes a conquest frame that the Buddhist tradition precisely does not use — it uses a conversion/protection frame. Including Mucalinda under chaoskampf would be a textbook case of forcing a non-combat Asian serpent into a Near-Eastern template.

### Verdict
- **EXCLUDE**
- Confidence: **HIGH**
- Rationale: The Vinaya Mahāvagga I.3 narrative contains no combat, no defeat, and no chaos-adversary; the nāga is a devotee-protector. DeCaroli (2004) and Strong (2001, 2004) frame Buddhist nāga episodes as local-cult assimilation, not chaoskampf. Including Mucalinda would require inventing a conflict the text does not contain and would exemplify exactly the Near-Eastern-template distortion the book should avoid. If the author wants a Buddhist serpent-combat, Vajrapāṇi vs. nāgas or Padmasambhava's subjugation narratives are the correct comparanda — not Mucalinda.

## 2. Antaboga (Balinese / Javanese)

### What the story actually tells
Antaboga (also Anantaboga; Sang Hyang Anantaboga) is the primordial world-serpent of Javanese, Sundanese and Balinese cosmology. In the Balinese creation frame, "in the beginning of time, only the world snake Antaboga existed"; through meditation (tapa) he brings into being Bedawang (Bedawang Nala), the cosmic turtle on whose shell the world rests, with two further nāgas (Basuki, Anantabhoga) coiled on the shell and the Black Stone serving as the lid of the underworld. When Bedawang shifts, earthquakes and volcanic eruptions follow. In the Javanese wayang cycle Antaboga is the ruler of Saptapratala (the seventh layer of the underworld), husband of Dewi Supreti, father of Dewi Nagagini (who marries Bhima, producing the hero Antareja). His name derives from Old Javanese / Sanskrit Ananta-bhoga, "endless enjoyment / endless food," and he is explicitly identified in the tradition with the Hindu Śeṣa / Ananta — the cosmic supporting serpent on which Viṣṇu reclines.

### Primary textual / ethnographic attestation
- Old Javanese / Kawi textual corpus: Tantu Panggĕlaran (14th–16th c.), Wawacan Sulanjana (Sundanese), and assorted wayang lakon (shadow-play scripts), particularly Lakon Bima Suci and the Antareja/Antasena cycles.
- C. Hooykaas, *Religion in Bali* (Leiden: Brill, 1973) and his earlier Āgama Tīrtha / Kawi-Balinese text editions remain the Indonesianist reference on Balinese cosmology and serpent-figures.
- P.J. Zoetmulder, *Kalangwan: A Survey of Old Javanese Literature* (1974) and the *Old Javanese-English Dictionary* (1982) give philological grounding for Anantabhoga in Kawi literature.
- R. Goris (*Prasasti Bali*, 1954) and Hedi Hinzler's work on Balinese iconography document Antaboga / Bedawang in temple art, palm-leaf manuscripts, and kamasan painting.
- James Fox and the Leiden school treat the Indonesian world-turtle/world-serpent motif as a syncretic blend of indigenous Austronesian cosmology with Indic Śeṣa-Ananta iconography.

### Is this chaoskampf at all?
No. Indonesianist scholarship (Hooykaas, Zoetmulder, Goris, Hinzler, Fox) frames Antaboga strictly as a COSMOGONIC SUPPORT / foundational serpent — closer to the Indic Śeṣa-Ananta type than to Tiamat / Illuyanka / Leviathan. There is no extant episode in which a god, hero, or culture-bringer slays, wounds, binds, or defeats Antaboga. The serpent is not chaos; he is the still, meditating foundation of order. When earthquakes occur through Bedawang's motion, the serpent is the stabiliser of the pair, not the destabiliser. The wayang narratives (Bima visiting the underworld, the marriage of Bhima and Nagagini) treat Antaboga as a revered father-in-law figure, not an adversary. No scholar I can identify — Hooykaas, Zoetmulder, Goris, Pigeaud, Rassers, Stephen Lansing, Margaret Wiener — discusses Antaboga under a chaoskampf / combat-myth rubric.

### Appropriation warning
Antaboga is ripe for misreading because the silhouette (giant primordial serpent + world) superficially resembles Tiamat or Jörmungandr. But the South/Southeast Asian theological grammar is the opposite: Śeṣa-type serpents UPHOLD cosmos, they do not oppose it; they are meditators and hosts, not monsters to be slain. Forcing Antaboga into a Near-Eastern combat template erases the Indic cosmogonic-support category and imposes a conflict that the Javanese-Balinese sources do not narrate. It would also flatten the specifically Indonesian syncretism — indigenous cosmic-turtle cosmology fused with imported Ananta — into a generic "dragon to be beaten."

### Verdict
- **EXCLUDE** (optionally: mention in a contrast / typology box as a non-combat cosmic serpent)
- Confidence: **HIGH**
- Rationale: There is no attested combat episode in Antaboga's Javanese-Balinese repertoire; he is a Śeṣa-type foundational serpent, not a chaos-adversary. Hooykaas (1973), Zoetmulder (1974, 1982) and the Leiden Indonesianist tradition frame him cosmogonically, not agonistically. Including him under chaoskampf would be a straight category error and would exemplify the Near-Eastern-template distortion. If he appears in the book at all, it should be as a CONTRAST case — "serpents that hold up the world and are not fought."

## 3. Whowie (southeastern Aboriginal Australia)

### What the story actually tells
The Whowie is described as a roughly 20-foot (6–7 metre), six-legged, goanna-/lizard-like monster with a huge frog-like head, living in a cave on the banks of the Murray River in the Riverina district. The story: the Whowie emerges at night and devours whole family groups encamped along the river; the "water-rat people" finally coordinate the neighbouring tribes, who stack firewood at the mouth of his cave, smoke him out over seven days, and kill him with spears and waddies as he staggers blinded into the open. The narrative is aetiological/placebased (it explains the Riverina sandhills as tracks of his trampling) and a community-coordination tale; it is NOT a cosmogony, does not inaugurate world-order, and does not involve a sky-god or culture-hero deity.

### Primary textual / ethnographic attestation
- David Unaipon (Ngarrindjeri author), *Legendary Tales of the Australian Aborigines*, manuscript 1924–25, held by the State Library of New South Wales. This is the original source.
- The story was published — without acknowledgement of Unaipon — in W. Ramsay Smith, *Myths and Legends of the Australian Aboriginals* (London: Harrap, 1930), pp. 147–151. For nearly 70 years the Whowie was cited only via Ramsay Smith.
- Unaipon's authorship was restored by Stephen Muecke and Adam Shoemaker, eds., *Legendary Tales of the Australian Aborigines* (Melbourne University Press, 2001 / 2006).
- IMPORTANT ATTRIBUTION CORRECTION: the Whowie is often repeated as "Wiradjuri" on wiki-type sources, but the only canonical recorded source is Unaipon (Ngarrindjeri, lower Murray, SA), who collected tales under University of Adelaide contract across multiple SA peoples. The story is set in the Riverina, which overlaps Wiradjuri, Yorta Yorta, and Wemba Wemba country, but the textual provenance is Unaipon, not direct Wiradjuri ethnography.
- Karl Brandt's 2021 *Caves Australia* piece proposing Punyelroo Cave as a "lair" is speculative geography, not ethnography.
- No substantive treatment in Luise Hercus, Aldo Massola (*Bunjil's Cave*, 1968; *Journey to Aboriginal Victoria*, 1969), R.M. and Catherine Berndt (*The Speaking Land*, 1989), or Howard Morphy that I can find. The Whowie is effectively a single-informant, single-text item.

### Is this chaoskampf at all?
No. Deborah Bird Rose's framing of Aboriginal Dreaming (*Dingo Makes Us Human*, 1992; *Nourishing Terrains*, 1996; *Reports from a Wild Country*, 2004) emphasises that most Aboriginal narratives operate in a Country-specific, relational, ancestor-grounded frame rather than a cosmic-combat frame. Howard Morphy's Yolngu ethnography (*Ancestral Connections*, 1991; *Aboriginal Art*, 1998) and Francesca Merlan's central Australian work similarly foreground place and ancestral agency over hero-vs-chaos schemes. The Whowie is classified in the ethnographic and folklore literature as a dangerous-creature / "nightmare" / place-spirit narrative, comparable to the Bunyip or Yara-ma-yha-who — cautionary and aetiological. Lynne Kelly's comparative work on monsters (*The Memory Code*, 2016) treats such creatures as mnemonic-landscape devices, not cosmic adversaries. No Aboriginal-studies scholar appears to read the Whowie episode as a cosmogonic combat. The protagonists are human communities, not a thunder- or sky-god; the outcome is local safety, not world-order.

### Appropriation warning
Including the Whowie under chaoskampf would be a doubled distortion: (a) the textual provenance is a single Ngarrindjeri author whose authorship was itself appropriated by a white ethnographer in 1930 — repackaging the story a second time under a European comparative-mythology template replicates that colonial gesture; (b) the tale has no cosmic-ordering content and becomes chaoskampf only if one elides its local, place-bound, community-scale meaning. It is precisely the kind of narrative Aboriginal-studies scholars (Rose, Morphy, Merlan, Keen) have warned against forcing into universalising Western grids.

### Verdict
- **EXCLUDE**
- Confidence: **HIGH**
- Rationale: The Whowie is a local dangerous-creature narrative attested in a single source (Unaipon, via Ramsay Smith 1930), with no cosmogonic content and no scholarly treatment as chaoskampf. Its inclusion would double a colonial appropriation already performed once by Ramsay Smith, and would flatten exactly the place-specific, ancestral frame that Deborah Bird Rose, Howard Morphy and Francesca Merlan have argued Aboriginal narratives must be read within. If the book needs an Aboriginal "monster" comparandum to illustrate what chaoskampf is NOT, the Whowie is a clean negative example.

## 4. Rainbow Serpent (pan-Aboriginal Australian — with caveats)

### What the story actually tells
There is no single "Rainbow Serpent story." The English label conceals a family of distinct, regionally specific ancestral beings with DIFFERENT names, genders, domains, and narratives. Any responsible treatment must keep these separate. Major regional figures (not exhaustive):

- **Yingarna (Western Arnhem Land, Kuninjku / Kunwinjku)** — PRIMORDIAL FEMALE ancestral serpent; mother-creator from whose body the clans and languages emerge. Core frame: GENERATIVE / COSMOGONIC. Not combat.
- **Ngalyod (Western Arnhem Land, Kuninjku)** — transformer / "Rainbow Serpent" proper; often daughter/offspring of Yingarna. Associated with wet-season waters, conception spirits, and "swallowing" or transforming those who break Law. Frame: LAW-ENFORCER / TRANSFORMER, with destructive episodes but within a Law-punishment schema, not a cosmogonic combat.
- **Wanambi / Wanampi (Western Desert: Pitjantjatjara, Yankunytjatjara, Anangu)** — water-snake tied to specific rockholes and soaks; releases or withholds water; dangerous if approached improperly. Frame: PLACE-GUARDIAN, water-custodian. Not combat.
- **Wagyl / Waugal / Waakal (Noongar, Whadjuk, southwest WA)** — creator-serpent who shaped the Swan (Derbal Yirragan) and Canning rivers and the Darling Scarp during the Nyitting. Frame: LANDSCAPE CREATOR / life-giver. Not combat. Tom Bennell and other Noongar Elders describe the Wagyl as sustainer of water country.
- **Borlung (eastern Arnhem Land), Julunggul / Julanggul (Yolŋu, linked to Wawilak sisters), Kurrichalpongo, Mindi (western Victoria), Taipan (Cape York, Wik), Galeru, etc.** — each a distinct being with its own Law, country, and song-cycle.

The Wawilak / Djang'kawu cycles (Yolŋu, Arnhem Land) do involve a serpent (Yulunggul/Julunggul) swallowing the sisters and inaugurating ritual; this comes closest to a "conflict" frame but is read by Stanner, Warner, and Morphy as a ritual-initiation and Law narrative, not a chaos-combat.

### Primary textual / ethnographic attestation
- A.R. Radcliffe-Brown, "The Rainbow-Serpent Myth of Australia," *Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute* 56 (1926): 19–25 — the paper that COINED the pan-Australian label and the foundational critique-target.
- W. Lloyd Warner, *A Black Civilization* (1937) — Wawilak / Yulunggul.
- W.E.H. Stanner, *On Aboriginal Religion* (Oceania Monographs, 1959–63).
- Catherine H. and Ronald M. Berndt, *The World of the First Australians* (1964; rev. 1988) and *The Speaking Land* (1989).
- Kenneth Maddock, *The Australian Aborigines: A Portrait of Their Society* (1972) and his essays on Rainbow Serpent variants.
- Luke Taylor, *Seeing the Inside: Bark Painting in Western Arnhem Land* (1996) — Kuninjku Ngalyod iconography.
- Paul S.C. Taçon, Meredith Wilson and Christopher Chippindale, "Birth of the Rainbow Serpent in Arnhem Land rock art and oral history," *Archaeology in Oceania* 31 (1996): 103–124 — rock-art evidence going back 6,000–8,000 years, with the Ribboned Pipefish as a proposed morphological prototype.
- Howard Morphy, *Ancestral Connections* (1991); Francesca Merlan, *Caging the Rainbow* (1998) — the central critical texts on lumping / appropriation.
- Ian Keen, *Knowledge and Secrecy in an Aboriginal Religion* (1994) on Yolŋu; *Aboriginal Economy and Society* (2004).
- Deborah Bird Rose, *Nourishing Terrains* (1996).
- Vicki Grieves and Aboriginal artist commentary (e.g. Anderson 2001, "Rejecting the Rainbow Serpent," *TAJA*) on the contested politics of the label.

### Is this chaoskampf at all?
Mostly NO, with a partial exception that still does not fit cleanly. The scholarly consensus, following Radcliffe-Brown's own framing, is that the Rainbow Serpent family is primarily about creation, water, fertility, Law, and transformation — not about a hero-god defeating a chaos-adversary. Punishment episodes (Ngalyod swallowing Law-breakers; Wagyl drying country if disrespected) are LAW-ENFORCEMENT, not Tiamat-style cosmic combat: the serpent is the enforcer of order, not the enemy of order.

Critiques of pan-Australian "lumping" are now the dominant position:
- Francesca Merlan (*Caging the Rainbow*, 1998) explicitly argues that the "Rainbow Serpent" label is a colonial-anthropological cage that flattens local ancestral distinctions.
- Howard Morphy (*Ancestral Connections*, 1991; *Aboriginal Art*, 1998) treats Yolŋu serpent-ancestors as specific to clan estates and ritual cycles.
- Ian Keen (*Knowledge and Secrecy*, 1994) shows the internal differentiation of Yolŋu cosmology against pan-Australian glosses.
- Deborah Bird Rose warns against reading Aboriginal ancestral beings through universalising Western frames.

Taçon, Wilson & Chippindale (1996) treat the rock-art Rainbow Serpent as an ANCESTRAL/transformational being, not a chaos-monster. No serious Australianist scholar argues for a chaoskampf reading. The only route into a combat frame would be via Yulunggul-swallowing-the-Wawilak-sisters, and Warner and Stanner explicitly read that as ritual-inauguration.

### Appropriation warning
This is THE case where the distortion risk is highest. The term "Rainbow Serpent" was itself coined by Radcliffe-Brown (1926) lumping multiple distinct traditions. Almost a century of critique (Merlan, Morphy, Keen, Rose, Taçon) has pushed against that lumping. Slotting "the Rainbow Serpent" into a Near-Eastern chaoskampf grid compounds two mistakes: (1) the pan-Aboriginal homogenisation Radcliffe-Brown introduced, (2) forcing a non-combat life-giver into a combat-myth template. Several contemporary Aboriginal artists and commentators (e.g. the case documented by Anderson 2001) have explicitly rejected the pan-Aboriginal "Rainbow Serpent" as New-Age appropriation. A chaoskampf framing would amplify exactly what Indigenous and Australianist scholars have been trying to dismantle.

### Verdict
- **Keep as PROVISIONAL** — only if handled with extreme care, as a CRITIQUE / CONTRAST case. Do NOT include as a straightforward chaoskampf comparandum.
- Confidence: **MEDIUM-HIGH** on the judgement (the scholarly record is clear that it is not chaoskampf); **HIGH** on the warning against naive inclusion.
- Rationale: Radcliffe-Brown's 1926 pan-Australian "Rainbow Serpent" label has been systematically critiqued by Morphy (1991), Keen (1994), Merlan (1998), and Rose (1996) as a colonial lumping that erases the distinct ancestral beings of Yingarna, Ngalyod, Wanambi, Wagyl, Yulunggul, etc. None of these are read by specialists as chaos-combat figures; they are creators, Law-enforcers, and water/landscape ancestors. If the book includes the Rainbow Serpent, it should be in a dedicated methodological section explaining why universalising categories such as chaoskampf misfire here — citing the regional variants separately, foregrounding the Merlan/Morphy critique, and explicitly flagging the appropriation history. Otherwise, exclude.
