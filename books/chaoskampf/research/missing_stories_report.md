### Ninurta vs. Asag (Lugal-e) — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Sumerian / Mesopotamian (late 3rd millennium BCE composition, copies into OB).
- **Summary:** In the epic *Lugal-e*, the warrior-god Ninurta marches to the mountain lands and battles the demon Asag (Asakku), who has fathered stone offspring. Ninurta's talking mace Sharur aids him; after slaying Asag, Ninurta uses the body/stones to pile a mountain ridge (the *hursag*) that rechannels the rivers, establishing irrigation and the ordered agricultural regime of Sumer. It is an archetypal cosmogonic combat: the hero's victory physically institutes the post-conflict ordered world.
- **Scholarship:** Jacobsen, *The Treasures of Darkness* (1976) treats it as a defining Sumerian combat-cosmogony; van Dijk edited and translated *LUGAL UD ME-LÁM-bi NIR-GÁL* (1983); Wakeman, *God's Battle with the Monster* (1973) lists Ninurta-Asag in her chaoskampf typology; Forsyth, *The Old Enemy* (1987) uses it as an early combat-myth exemplar; Annus, *The God Ninurta* (2002). Gunkel's original frame anticipated this by treating Mesopotamian monster-combats as the backdrop for biblical chaoskampf.
- **Tier:** CORE (arguably the oldest attested full-length combat-cosmogony we have; ranks beside Enuma Elish as a Mesopotamian anchor).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Ninurta vs. Anzû — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Sumerian / Akkadian (Old Babylonian *Anzu* epic, with Sumerian antecedents).
- **Summary:** The lion-headed storm-bird Anzû steals the Tablet of Destinies from Enlil, overturning cosmic order; Ninurta (or in Sumerian Ningirsu) pursues, shoots Anzû with arrows, and restores the tablet. Though Anzû is not a serpent, the structure is hero-god vs. chaos-beast whose theft threatens the ordering of decrees themselves.
- **Scholarship:** Foster, *Before the Muses* (3rd ed. 2005) gives translation + commentary; Annus, *The Standard Babylonian Epic of Anzu* (2001, SAACT 3); Vanstiphout treats it as royal/combat ideology; Wakeman (1973) and Batto, *Slaying the Dragon* (1992) include Anzû among Mesopotamian combat-myth analogues for Marduk-Tiamat.
- **Tier:** CORE (it is the direct Mesopotamian literary ancestor of the Marduk-Tiamat pattern and antedates Enuma Elish).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Song of Ullikummi / Kumarbi Cycle — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Hurrian, transmitted in Hittite (Late Bronze Age).
- **Summary:** The Kumarbi Cycle narrates succession struggles among the gods: Kumarbi castrates Anu, is then supplanted by the storm-god Teshub. To dethrone Teshub he engenders monstrous offspring — most famously Ullikummi, a diorite giant who grows from the shoulder of the titan Upelluri and threatens heaven. With Ea's copper saw (the primordial heaven-earth cleaver) Teshub's allies cripple Ullikummi; the storm-god prevails. The cycle also includes Hedammu (a voracious sea-dragon) and the Song of the Sea.
- **Scholarship:** Güterbock, "The Song of Ullikummi" (*JCS* 1951–1952) is the foundational edition; Hoffner, *Hittite Myths* (2nd ed. 1998); Beckman; López-Ruiz, *When the Gods Were Born* (2010) — directly links Kumarbi Cycle to Hesiodic *Theogony* and the Typhon episode. West, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth* (2007) and *The East Face of Helicon* (1997) use Kumarbi Cycle as a direct Near Eastern source for Greek combat-succession pattern. Ogden, *Drakon* (2013) discusses Hedammu as dragon-adversary.
- **Tier:** CORE (treated by most comparativists as the Near Eastern bridge that shaped the Greek succession/combat pattern).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### YHWH vs. Leviathan / Rahab / Yam — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Ancient Israel / Hebrew Bible.
- **Summary:** Hebrew poetry (Psalm 74:12–17; Psalm 89:9–11; Isaiah 27:1; Isaiah 51:9–10; Job 26:12–13; Job 41; Habakkuk 3) preserves combat-myth fragments in which YHWH crushes the heads of Leviathan (Ugaritic Lotan), pierces Rahab, and rebukes Yam. The imagery is largely cosmogonic or exodus-political rather than a standalone narrative, but it is the classic Gunkelian chaoskampf corpus.
- **Scholarship:** Gunkel, *Schöpfung und Chaos* (1895) — foundational; Day, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* (CUP 1985); Wakeman, *God's Battle with the Monster* (1973); Batto, *Slaying the Dragon* (1992); Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP 2015); Cross, *Canaanite Myth and Hebrew Epic* (1973).
- **Tier:** CORE. This corpus is distinct from Baal-Yamm/Lotan (West Semitic polytheistic) and from Revelation's Red Dragon (apocalyptic); it is the pivotal monotheistic transformation of the motif and cannot be folded into either.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Thraetaona / Fereydun vs. Aži Dahāka — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Iranian (Avestan, then Middle Persian and *Shahnameh*).
- **Summary:** In the Avesta (Yašt 5, 9, 14, 15, 17) Thraētaona son of Āthwya defeats the three-headed, six-eyed dragon Aži Dahāka — not by killing but by binding him on Mount Damavand, whence he will break free at the end of days. In the *Shahnameh* the hero is Fereydun, the dragon becomes the tyrant Zahhāk. The eschatological binding is shared with Greek Typhon.
- **Scholarship:** Benveniste & Renou, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna* (1934) is the canonical Indo-Iranian comparative; Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995) Chapter 38 reconstructs the formula *HERO SLEW (*gʷhen-) SERPENT*, with Thraētaona as a flagship case; Skjærvø writings on Zoroastrianism; West, *IE Poetry and Myth* (2007); Lincoln, *Priests, Warriors, and Cattle* (1981).
- **Tier:** CORE (one of Watkins's anchor cases for the reconstructed PIE combat formula).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Trita Āptya and the three-headed serpent — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Vedic (Rig Veda 10.8, 10.99; Avestan cognate Θrita).
- **Summary:** Trita Āptya, "Trita of the Waters," is a shadowy Vedic figure older than Indra who slays a three-headed, six-eyed serpent (Viśvarūpa, son of Tvaṣṭṛ) in RV 10.8.8. In the Avesta, Θrita is the father of Thraētaona. The layer is pre-Indra: Indra in RV 10.8 is said to have slain the monster "with Trita's help." Watkins reconstructs this as the earliest attested Indo-Iranian stratum of the dragon-slaying formula.
- **Scholarship:** Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995) — central chapter; Dumézil on the three sins of the warrior (*Heur et malheur du guerrier*, 1969) and *Mythe et épopée* (1968–1973); Oberlies, *Die Religion des Ṛgveda*; West, *IE Poetry and Myth* (2007).
- **Tier:** RELATED (distinct Indo-Iranian layer that the book must mention alongside Indra-Vṛtra, but narratively thin).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Krishna vs. Kāliya — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Hindu / Puranic (Bhāgavata Purāṇa 10.15–17, Harivamśa, Viṣṇu Purāṇa).
- **Summary:** The young Krishna subdues the poisonous multi-headed nāga Kāliya who has fouled a pool in the Yamunā, dancing on his hoods until the serpent submits; Krishna spares his life but exiles him. The motif reproduces the Indra-Vṛtra pattern in later bhakti form: storm/divine hero subdues water-serpent and restores life-giving waters.
- **Scholarship:** Wendy Doniger O'Flaherty, *Hindu Myths* (1975) discusses the reflex of Indra-Vṛtra; Charlotte Vaudeville, *Myths, Saints and Legends in Medieval India* (1996); John Stratton Hawley, *Krishna, the Butter Thief* (1983); Vogel, *Indian Serpent-Lore* (1926); Witzel, *Origins of the World's Mythologies* (2012) treats it as a later stratum continuing the Vedic motif.
- **Tier:** RELATED (a secondary, taming-rather-than-killing variant; directly acknowledged in scholarship as reflex of the core Indra pattern).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Gonggong vs. Zhurong / Nüwa's repair — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Chinese (Han-era systematizations of earlier material: *Huainanzi*, *Lüshi Chunqiu*, *Liezi*).
- **Summary:** The water-god Gonggong fights Zhurong (fire) or Zhuanxu; losing, he smashes Mount Buzhou, a cosmic pillar, causing the sky to tilt and floods to break loose. Nüwa smelts five-coloured stones to patch heaven, severs a turtle's legs to prop the sky, and slays the black dragon to save Ji Province. This is a proper combat-with-chaos cosmogony with disorder, divine combat, and re-ordering.
- **Scholarship:** Mark Edward Lewis, *The Flood Myths of Early China* (SUNY 2006) is the standard monograph and explicitly discusses chaoskampf affinities; Anne Birrell, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction* (1993); Derk Bodde in Kramer ed., *Mythologies of the Ancient World* (1961); Witzel (2012) places it in Laurasian mythology. This is materially different from Yu-Xiangliu (which is on your list) because it is a primordial cosmogonic combat rather than a flood-control heroic cycle.
- **Tier:** CORE or high-RELATED (one of the two Chinese chaoskampf-proper cases; stronger cosmogonic fit than Yu).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Beowulf vs. Grendel and the Dragon — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Old English / Germanic.
- **Summary:** Beowulf kills the Cain-descended Grendel and his mother, and years later dies slaying a hoard-keeping dragon that has burned his kingdom. The two monster-combats bracket the poem; scholars from Tolkien onward read them as Germanic instances of the combat-myth pattern rather than mere folktale.
- **Scholarship:** J.R.R. Tolkien, "Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics" (1936) — canonical; Fontenrose, *Python* (1959) explicitly includes Beowulf-Grendel/dragon in his cross-cultural combat-myth typology; Andy Orchard, *Pride and Prodigies* (1995); Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995) Chapter 42 analyses Beowulf-dragon with the IE formula; Ogden, *Drakon* (2013) cites Beowulf.
- **Tier:** RELATED (late Germanic literary reflex; Watkins treats it as preserving IE formula, Fontenrose as structurally a combat myth).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Sigurd/Siegfried vs. Fáfnir — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Norse / Germanic (*Völsunga saga*, *Fáfnismál*, *Nibelungenlied*).
- **Summary:** Sigurd, advised by Regin and Odin, digs a pit and stabs the dwarf-turned-dragon Fáfnir from below as he crawls to water; he drinks dragon-blood and eats the heart, gaining bird-speech. The combat is an intra-Germanic doublet of Thor-Jörmungandr but framed as heroic rather than cosmic.
- **Scholarship:** Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995) Ch. 42 reads Sigurd-Fáfnir as a canonical IE dragon-slaying formula realization; Lionarons, *The Medieval Dragon* (1998); Ogden, *Drakon* (2013); Margaret Clunies Ross, *Prolonged Echoes* (1994); Fontenrose (1959) treats it within the combat-myth family.
- **Tier:** RELATED (heroic rather than cosmogonic, but one of Watkins's core IE exemplars).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Herakles vs. the Hydra (and Ladon, Geryon) — INCLUDE (Hydra primarily)
- **Culture:** Greek.
- **Summary:** Herakles kills the many-headed Lernaean Hydra, whose heads regrow until Iolaos cauterizes them; he then shoots Ladon guarding the Hesperides' apples and steals Geryon's cattle after killing the three-bodied giant and his two-headed dog Orthros. Fontenrose treats Hydra as a direct structural reflex of Python, and Geryon-Cacus as the IE cattle-recovery combat (see Cacus).
- **Scholarship:** Fontenrose, *Python* (1959) Ch. XI–XII integrates Hydra, Ladon, Geryon into the combat-myth typology; Ogden, *Drakon* (2013) and *Drakōn: Dragon Myth and Serpent Cult* chapters on Hydra and Ladon; Watkins (1995) uses the Geryon cattle-theft as a Greek instance of the IE "hero slew serpent, recovered cattle/waters" formula; Burkert, *Structure and History in Greek Mythology* (1979).
- **Tier:** RELATED (heroic-labour variant of the Greek pattern already represented by Apollo-Python).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Perseus vs. Ketos (and Medusa) — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Greek.
- **Summary:** Perseus rescues Andromeda from the sea-monster Ketos sent by Poseidon, and separately beheads the gorgon Medusa. The Andromeda rescue is the archetype of the later Saint George scheme.
- **Scholarship:** Fontenrose, *Python* (1959) Ch. XI "Perseus and Kadmos" treats both as combat-myth variants; Ogden, *Perseus* (Routledge 2008) and *Drakon* (2013); Kingsley Hopkinson discussions. Frazer and earlier solar-myth scholarship.
- **Tier:** RELATED.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Kadmos vs. the dragon of Ares — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Greek (Boeotian foundation myth).
- **Summary:** Kadmos slays the serpent guarding the spring of Ares at Thebes; he sows its teeth, from which armed men spring. The myth fuses dragon-combat with foundation-cosmogony (Thebes) and autochthony.
- **Scholarship:** Fontenrose, *Python* (1959) Ch. XI "Perseus and Kadmos" — textbook treatment; Ogden, *Drakon* (2013); Vian, *Les origines de Thèbes: Cadmos et les Spartes* (1963).
- **Tier:** RELATED.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Bellerophon vs. Chimaera — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Greek (*Iliad* 6; Hesiod; Pindar).
- **Summary:** Riding Pegasus, Bellerophon spears the lion-goat-serpent Chimaera, child of Typhon and Echidna. The hybrid monster and aerial combat are distinctive.
- **Scholarship:** Fontenrose, *Python* (1959); Ogden, *Drakon* (2013); Watkins (1995) mentions Bellerophon as another Greek hero-dragon combat; West, *IE Poetry and Myth* (2007).
- **Tier:** RELATED.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Cacus vs. Herakles / Hercules — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Roman (Vergil *Aeneid* 8; Livy 1; Propertius, Ovid).
- **Summary:** The fire-breathing monster Cacus steals cattle from Hercules at the future site of Rome; Hercules discovers the theft, strangles Cacus in his cave. The episode founds the Ara Maxima cult on the Aventine.
- **Scholarship:** Dumézil, *Mythe et épopée* (1968–73) reads Cacus as the Latin continuation of the IE three-headed-monster / cattle-recovery myth (cf. Trita, Thraetaona, Indra-Viśvarūpa, Geryon); Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995) treats it as a flagship Italic reflex; Bremmer, *Roman Myth and Mythography* (1987).
- **Tier:** RELATED.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### St. George vs. the dragon — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Christian hagiography (medieval, drawing on Greek Andromeda pattern; *Legenda Aurea* c. 1260).
- **Summary:** George rescues the king's daughter from a dragon demanding human tribute outside a city (Silene/Cyrene/Beirut in variant traditions), spears the dragon, converts the population.
- **Scholarship:** Samantha Riches, *St George: Hero, Martyr and Myth* (2000); Ogden, *The Dragon in the West* (OUP 2021); Jonathan Good, *The Cult of Saint George in Medieval England* (2009). Ogden explicitly traces the chaoskampf lineage forward from Apollo-Python and Perseus-Ketos into Christian dragon-slayer hagiography.
- **Tier:** RELATED (Christianized late reflex; useful for reception history).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Michael vs. the Dragon (Revelation 12) — INCLUDE as companion to Revelation's Red Dragon
- **Culture:** Early Christian apocalyptic.
- **Summary:** Revelation 12 depicts Michael and his angels warring in heaven against the great dragon (identified as the ancient serpent, the devil, Satan), casting him down to earth. This is structurally distinct from the Red Dragon scene in the rest of Revelation 12–13 because it is a heavenly battle that explicitly echoes the primordial combat, whereas the Red Dragon vs. the Woman/child is an earthly threat scene.
- **Scholarship:** Forsyth, *The Old Enemy* (1987) treats Rev 12 as the culmination of the biblical combat-myth trajectory; Adela Yarbro Collins, *The Combat Myth in the Book of Revelation* (1976) is the standard monograph explicitly framing Revelation around the combat myth; Aune, *Revelation* (WBC, 1997–98).
- **Tier:** RELATED (treat alongside Red Dragon, not duplicate — Yarbro Collins's book makes the case that Rev as a whole is *the* Christian combat-myth reworking).
- **Confidence:** HIGH. Author's existing "Revelation's Red Dragon" entry should be expanded to cover Rev 12 explicitly and cite Yarbro Collins.

### Lugh vs. Balor / Tuatha Dé vs. Fomorians — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Irish / Celtic (*Cath Maige Tuired*, "The Second Battle of Mag Tuired", c. 11th-century MS of older material).
- **Summary:** The Tuatha Dé Danann under Lugh defeat the Fomorians, demonic sea- and chaos-powers, at the Second Battle of Mag Tuired. Lugh kills his grandfather Balor by slinging a stone into his destructive eye. The combat founds the ordered Irish divine world against the chaotic Fomorian "other."
- **Scholarship:** Proinsias Mac Cana, *Celtic Mythology* (1970); Georges Dumézil, *Mythe et épopée* I–III (1968–73) sets Lugh-Balor inside the IE tripartite scheme and treats it as a combat-myth variant; Elizabeth Gray ed./trans. *Cath Maige Tuired* (Irish Texts Society, 1982); John Carey, various papers; Watkins (1995) references Celtic reflexes of the IE combat formula; Ogden, *Drakon* (2013) discusses Celtic dragon material.
- **Tier:** RELATED (Celtic reflex of order-vs.-chaos god-vs.-primordial-others; not strictly a dragon combat but scholarship routinely includes it in the typology).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Huitzilopochtli vs. Coyolxauhqui (Coatepec) — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Aztec / Mexica.
- **Summary:** On Mount Coatepec ("Serpent Mountain"), Huitzilopochtli is born fully armed from Coatlicue, and instantly slays his sister Coyolxauhqui and the 400 Centzon Huitznahua who were about to murder their mother. He dismembers Coyolxauhqui and hurls her down the mountain. This is re-enacted architecturally at the Templo Mayor, whose pyramid *is* Coatepec, and ritually in mass sacrifices.
- **Scholarship:** Eduardo Matos Moctezuma, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs* (1988) and *Life and Death in the Templo Mayor* (1995); David Carrasco, *City of Sacrifice* (1999); Johanna Broda in *The Great Temple of Tenochtitlan* (1987); Miguel León-Portilla. Cecelia Klein and others read Coatepec as an Aztec chaoskampf/cosmogonic combat that daily re-founds solar and political order. Witzel (2012) lists Mesoamerica among his "Laurasian" combat-mythologies.
- **Tier:** CORE for Mesoamerica (Cipactli is already present, but Coatepec is arguably the more literally combat-structured case).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Hou Yi (Yi the Archer) vs. the Ten Suns and monsters — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Chinese (*Huainanzi*, *Shan Hai Jing*, *Chuci*).
- **Summary:** The divine archer Yi is sent by Emperor Yao (or Di Jun) to shoot down nine of ten scorching suns and then to dispatch six monsters plaguing the world: Zaochi, Jiuying (a nine-headed water/fire monster), Dafeng (a giant wind-bird), Fengxi (a giant boar), Xiushe (a giant python), and Yayu. Several of these — especially Jiuying and Xiushe the giant serpent — are structurally chaos-monsters against which the hero champions cosmic order.
- **Scholarship:** Anne Birrell, *Chinese Mythology* (1993) and *Chinese Myths* (2000) treat Hou Yi as a combat-order hero; Mark Edward Lewis, *The Flood Myths of Early China* (2006); Sarah Allan, *The Shape of the Turtle* (1991); Riccardo Fracasso writings on *Shan Hai Jing*; Witzel (2012). Solar-myth and cosmic-ordering elements align with chaoskampf scholarship.
- **Tier:** RELATED.
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH.

### Māui fishing up the land / slowing the sun — EXCLUDE as chaoskampf proper
- The Polynesian Māui tradition is a hero/trickster creation of ordered land (Te Ika-a-Māui), but it is not structured as combat with a chaos-monster. There is no adversarial dragon or primordial opponent subdued by killing. Māui's death by Hine-nui-te-pō is the closest to combat, and even that is a failed trickster intrusion rather than a classical chaoskampf. Scholars (Buck, *The Coming of the Maori*, 1949; Reed's *Māori Myths*; Witzel 2012) treat Māui as trickster-demiurge, not combat hero. EXCLUDE — but author could note Māui briefly as a case where the pattern is ABSENT.

### Dobrynya Nikitich vs. Zmey Gorynych — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** East Slavic (Russian byliny, attested from 17th–19th century but reflecting older material).
- **Summary:** The bogatyr Dobrynya Nikitich fights the three-headed (or twelve-trunked) fire-breathing dragon Zmey Gorynych at the Puchai River, using a "hat of the Greek land" as improvised weapon; the dragon begs mercy, breaks the truce by abducting Prince Vladimir's niece, and is finally killed after a three-day duel.
- **Scholarship:** Felix Oinas, *Heroic Epic and Saga* (1978) and essays on Russian byliny; Roman Jakobson, "The Slavic God Veles and his Indo-European Cognates" (1969); Alex Alexander, *Bylina and Fairy Tale* (1973); Watkins (1995) mentions Slavic reflexes of the IE dragon-slaying formula. Ivanov & Toporov reconstructed the Slavic "basic myth" (Perun vs. Veles) — Dobrynya-Zmey is its humanized epic reflex.
- **Tier:** RELATED (epic/heroic reflex of the Slavic combat-myth already represented by Perun-Veles).
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Humbaba / Huwawa (Gilgamesh) — INCLUDE with a caveat
- **Culture:** Sumerian / Akkadian (*Gilgamesh and Huwawa* A/B; *Epic of Gilgamesh* Tablets II–V).
- **Summary:** Gilgamesh and Enkidu travel to the Cedar Forest, defeat its monstrous guardian Humbaba/Huwawa (granted his seven protective *melammu*-auras by Enlil), and cut down the cedars. Later they kill the Bull of Heaven sent by Ishtar.
- **Scholarship:** Forsyth, *The Old Enemy* (1987) — Chapter 1 explicitly treats the Humbaba combat as an early combat-myth exemplar; Andrew George, *The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic* (2003); Tzvi Abusch on Gilgamesh; Jeffrey Tigay, *The Evolution of the Gilgamesh Epic* (1982). Debate: some scholars (e.g., George) resist labeling it chaoskampf because Humbaba is Enlil's agent, not a chaos principle; others (Forsyth, Forsyth's followers) treat it as one of the prototypes.
- **Tier:** RELATED (heroic monster-combat without a clear chaos-cosmos pole; Forsyth explicitly includes).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM. Worth discussing because Forsyth put it on the map; flag the caveat.

### Bull of Heaven — EXCLUDE (as standalone)
- The Bull of Heaven episode is part of Gilgamesh and is best treated inside the Humbaba/Gilgamesh discussion. Ishtar is the antagonist, not a chaos principle; the bull is a punitive instrument. EXCLUDE as a separate entry; fold into Humbaba note.

### Marduk vs. Labbu — INCLUDE briefly
- **Culture:** Akkadian (fragmentary *Labbu Myth*).
- **Summary:** A sea-dragon Labbu, "raging fifty bēru long," rises from the sea to destroy humanity; Tishpak (in surviving tablets) — not Marduk; some scholars have argued the god is Nergal or another — is commissioned to slay it. Older scholarship conflated this with the Marduk-Tiamat battle. The text is too fragmentary to be certain.
- **Scholarship:** Wakeman (1973) discusses Labbu among Mesopotamian sea-dragon combats; Lambert, *Babylonian Creation Myths* (2013); Heidel, *The Babylonian Genesis* (1951); Foster, *Before the Muses* (2005).
- **Tier:** RELATED (brief note — Mesopotamian sea-dragon combat alongside the main cases).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM.

### Nergal / Erra — EXCLUDE
- The *Erra Epic* describes Erra (plague/destruction god) unleashing chaos on Babylon and eventually relenting. It is a god-of-destruction-runs-amok poem, not a chaoskampf; the structure is inverse (order-god is persuaded to restrain himself, no monster). Scholars (Cagni's edition; Foster) do not typically classify it as combat-myth. EXCLUDE.

### Rāma vs. Rāvaṇa — EXCLUDE (with caveat)
- The *Rāmāyaṇa* is a heroic epic, not a chaoskampf. Rāvaṇa is a ten-headed *rākṣasa* king, not a primordial chaos-serpent; the conflict is dynastic/moral, not cosmogonic. While some scholars (Goldman, *The Rāmāyaṇa of Vālmīki*, 1984–) note echoes of Indra-Vṛtra in Rāma's martial imagery, mainstream comparativists (Witzel, Brockington, *The Sanskrit Epics*) do not treat it as combat-myth. EXCLUDE — mention as a heroic genre that *replaced* cosmogonic combat in later Hindu narrative.

### Garuḍa vs. nāgas — EXCLUDE with caveat
- Garuḍa's eternal enmity with nāgas (Mahābhārata Ādi Parva) is hostility between species, not a cosmic combat establishing order. Garuḍa is not a storm/sovereign god, and the nāgas are enslaved kin of his mother's sister, not chaos monsters. Scholars (Vogel, *Indian Serpent-Lore*, 1926; Williams, *The Two-Headed Deer*) treat the pattern as avian-ophidian opposition, not chaoskampf. EXCLUDE — but note as iconographic sibling of dragon-slayer scheme.

### Mapuche Kai Kai vs. Tren Tren — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Mapuche (Chile/Argentina).
- **Summary:** The water-serpent Kai-Kai Vilu raises the sea to drown humanity; the earth-serpent Tren-Tren Vilu lifts the land and people higher. The two serpents fight; Tren-Tren wins partially, leaving the archipelago of Chiloé. This is a cosmogonic serpent-combat that founds present geography and the Mapuche people.
- **Scholarship:** Louis Faron, *Hawks of the Sun* (1964); Martín Alonqueo, *Instituciones Religiosas del Pueblo Mapuche* (1979); Rolf Foerster, *Introducción a la religiosidad mapuche* (1995); Tom Dillehay writings on Mapuche cosmology. It is a rare two-serpent combat cosmogony (no slayer-hero) that is routinely compared to deluge combat myths.
- **Tier:** RELATED (South American cosmogonic serpent-combat — structurally distinct and valuable; currently missing from the catalog). This complements Pariacaca-Amaru in the Andean section.
- **Confidence:** HIGH.

### Batraz (Nart Sagas) — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Ossetian / North Caucasian (Nart sagas, Scytho-Sarmatian-Alanic core).
- **Summary:** The steel-bodied warrior Batraz, forged and tempered, fights and kills various dragons, giants, and chthonic enemies; his final death (sword thrown into the sea) is a famous IE combat-cycle close.
- **Scholarship:** Georges Dumézil, *Légendes sur les Nartes* (1930) and subsequent work — he treated Batraz as an archaic IE warrior-type and direct analogue of the Scytho-Alanic reflex of the storm-god combat; John Colarusso, *Nart Sagas from the Caucasus* (2002 Princeton); Anna Chaudhri and others in *Nart Sagas from the Caucasus*. Batraz's dragon-kills are part of the wider IE dragon-slaying substratum.
- **Tier:** RELATED (additional IE reflex, bridging Iranian and Caucasian material; helps round out Watkins's map).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH.

### Welsh Red vs. White Dragon (Lludd and Llefelys / Historia Brittonum) — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Brittonic / Welsh (*Historia Brittonum* c. 9th c.; *Cyfranc Lludd a Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion*).
- **Summary:** Two dragons — the native Red and the invading White — scream annually, blighting land and people. King Lludd traps them in a pit of mead, buries them at Dinas Emrys; in Nennius's version Merlin later interprets them as Britons and Saxons. The "Red Dragon of Wales" descends from this motif.
- **Scholarship:** Patrick Sims-Williams, *Celtic Inscribed Stones* and various; Rachel Bromwich, *Trioedd Ynys Prydein*; John Carey; Ogden, *The Dragon in the West* (2021) treats Welsh dragons as part of the reception of the combat-myth pattern. Watkins (1995) notes Celtic reflexes.
- **Tier:** RELATED (Celtic narrative survival of combat-myth imagery, heavily politicized).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH.

### Fionn vs. Aillen — EXCLUDE
- Fionn mac Cumhaill's defeat of Aillén mac Midhna, the fire-breathing Otherworld musician who burns Tara each Samhain, is a hero-against-supernatural-fire-pourer story but structurally a Mabinogion-style Otherworld threat, not a chaoskampf. Scholarship (Nagy, *The Wisdom of the Outlaw*, 1985) treats it as initiation rather than combat-cosmogony. EXCLUDE.

### Welsh Afanc — EXCLUDE
- The Afanc is a regional water-monster trapped by various heroes (Hu Gadarn, Peredur) in late folk material; it is not a cosmogonic or sovereign-establishing combat. EXCLUDE.

### Horus vs. Seth — INCLUDE with nuance
- **Culture:** Egyptian (Pyramid Texts onward; *Contendings of Horus and Seth* P. Chester Beatty I, c. 1160 BCE).
- **Summary:** Horus and Seth contest the kingship of Egypt after Osiris's murder by Seth; their combats (spearings, testicle- and eye-mutilation, legal tribunal) end with Horus confirmed as legitimate king. Egyptologists debate whether this is chaoskampf: Seth is not purely chaos (he also defends Ra's barque against Apep), and the conflict is dynastic/judicial, not cosmogonic.
- **Scholarship:** Te Velde, *Seth, God of Confusion* (1967) — classical treatment, explicitly discusses Seth's ambiguous chaos status; Assmann, *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt* (2001); Wilkinson, *The Complete Gods and Goddesses of Ancient Egypt* (2003). Gunkel and Wakeman did NOT include Horus-Seth in chaoskampf proper; they reserved that label for Ra-Apep, which is already in the catalog.
- **Tier:** RELATED (include as a "domesticated" Egyptian variant alongside Ra-Apep, with the caveat that most scholarship treats Horus-Seth as dynastic/judicial rather than cosmogonic combat).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM. Worth a short section noting the debate.

### Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca — INCLUDE
- **Culture:** Aztec / Mesoamerican (Historia de los Mexicanos por sus Pinturas; Codex Vaticanus A; Leyenda de los Soles).
- **Summary:** The brothers alternate as suns across the Five Suns cosmogony, each destroying the other's creation. The first cycle's combat is already represented by Cipactli (they jointly tear her apart to make earth and sky), but the repeated agonistic dualism across cosmic ages is itself a distinctive Mesoamerican chaoskampf variant.
- **Scholarship:** Miguel León-Portilla, *Aztec Thought and Culture* (1963); Guilhem Olivier, *Mockeries and Metamorphoses of an Aztec God: Tezcatlipoca* (2003); David Carrasco, *Quetzalcoatl and the Irony of Empire* (1982); Alfredo López Austin, *The Myths of the Opossum* (1993) and *Tamoanchan, Tlalocan* (1997).
- **Tier:** RELATED (co-treatment with Cipactli; Carrasco and López Austin explicitly read the Quetzalcoatl/Tezcatlipoca dualism as Mesoamerican cosmic-order combat).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH.

### Thunderbird vs. Whale (Pacific Northwest) — INCLUDE (provisional)
- **Culture:** Quileute, Makah, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida, Tlingit and related.
- **Summary:** The Thunderbird (*ḰwuḰwaxaweʔ*, etc.) battles the monstrous Whale who preys on other whales and starves the people; their fight causes storms and earthquakes. Independent scholarship has linked versions to the 1700 Cascadia megathrust earthquake/tsunami memory.
- **Scholarship:** Ruth Ludwin et al., "Dating the 1700 Cascadia Earthquake: Great Coastal Earthquakes in Native Stories" (*Seismological Research Letters* 2005); Alan McMillan and Ian Hutchinson on coastal Indigenous oral tradition; Franz Boas's early ethnography. Combat-myth scholarship (Witzel 2012) includes Pacific Northwest monster-combats among his comparative data.
- **Tier:** PROVISIONAL (dramatic combat-with-sea-monster but scholars typically frame it as geomythology rather than chaoskampf proper).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM.

### Sedna — EXCLUDE
- Sedna's myth (her fingers cut off by her father, sinking, becoming mother of sea-mammals) is a primordial dismemberment-cosmogony, not a combat myth. No hero-god defeats her; shamans propitiate her. Not chaoskampf. EXCLUDE.

### Erlik (Turkic-Mongolian) — INCLUDE (provisional / related)
- **Culture:** Altaic, Siberian Turkic, Mongolian (shamanic material collected by 19th–20th c. ethnographers: Radloff, Potapov, Anokhin).
- **Summary:** In Altaic creation myths Ülgen (sky-god) and Erlik cooperate, then quarrel over the creation; Erlik is cast down and becomes lord of the underworld. Variants include a primordial water-diving motif and a heavenly combat that establishes the upper/lower world split. This is a binary-creation, not a single dragon-slaying, but it is structurally an order-vs.-chaos agonic cosmogony.
- **Scholarship:** Uno Harva, *Die religiösen Vorstellungen der altaischen Völker* (1938); Mircea Eliade, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* (1951/1964) — Eliade explicitly treats Altaic dualistic creation as a variant of the cosmogonic combat pattern; Jean-Paul Roux, *La religion des Turcs et des Mongols* (1984); Walter Heissig, *The Religions of Mongolia* (1980).
- **Tier:** RELATED (brother-deity cosmogonic dualism; worth including as a Central Asian variant alongside the Slavic Perun-Veles).
- **Confidence:** MEDIUM.

### Yoruba Ṣàngó — EXCLUDE (with brief note)
- Ṣàngó is the Yoruba storm/thunder *òrìṣà*, which makes him a functional analogue of Indra/Thor, but there is no canonical combat-with-a-chaos-monster narrative at the center of his cult. Scholars (Barber, *I Could Speak Until Tomorrow*; Pemberton) emphasize Ṣàngó's royal and ethical dimensions. EXCLUDE as chaoskampf, but worth a short "storm-god without dragon" comparative footnote. Mwindo already represents the African sub-Saharan chapter.

### Korean / Vietnamese dragon-slaying — MOSTLY EXCLUDE
- Korean mythology has *imugi* and *yong* (rise-to-dragon) tales and foundation myths (Dangun, Jumong) but no canonical storm-god-vs.-sea-serpent chaoskampf. Vietnamese Lạc Long Quân subdues sea and mountain monsters (Ngư Tinh the fish-demon, Hồ Tinh, Mộc Tinh) — this *does* approach chaoskampf. CONDITIONAL INCLUDE (see next).

### Lạc Long Quân (Vietnam) — INCLUDE (provisional)
- **Culture:** Vietnamese (Lạc Việt foundation myth, *Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái* c. 14th c.).
- **Summary:** The dragon-lord Lạc Long Quân subdues three monsters — Ngư Tinh (the giant fish-serpent), Hồ Tinh (nine-tailed fox), Mộc Tinh (tree-demon) — before marrying the mountain-fairy Âu Cơ and fathering the Vietnamese people.
- **Scholarship:** Keith Taylor, *The Birth of Vietnam* (1983); Nguyễn Đổng Chi's folklore collections; Olga Dror, *Cult, Culture, and Authority: Princess Liễu Hạnh in Vietnamese History*. Not widely discussed in comparative chaoskampf literature.
- **Tier:** PROVISIONAL.
- **Confidence:** LOW-MEDIUM.

### Adela Yarbro Collins / Revelation 12 — already merged above.

### Witzel "Laurasian" flood-and-combat survey — CONTEXT NOTE
- Michael Witzel, *The Origins of the World's Mythologies* (OUP 2012) proposes a "Laurasian" mythic corpus in which dragon-combat, flood, and world-ordering are linked across Eurasia, North Africa, and the Americas, with a separate "Gondwana" layer. This is controversial (Witzel's reconstructive ambition is criticized, e.g., by Thompson, d'Huy) but it is the most recent serious attempt to systematize the worldwide combat-myth distribution. The book should flag Witzel's framework and the critical response.
