# Chaoskampf Research Notes: Four Non-ANE Core/Related Candidates

Scope: focused academic notes on four dragon-combat narratives frequently compared to the Near Eastern Chaoskampf cycle. Each note weighs specialist scholarship against the comparative-mythology tradition and issues a tier recommendation.

---

## 1. Indra vs. Vṛtra (Vedic; Rigveda)

### What the story tells
The paradigmatic act of Indra is the slaying of the serpent-demon Vṛtra ("the Obstructor, Encloser"), who lies coiled on or within the mountain and withholds the cosmic waters. Fortified by soma and wielding the vajra forged by Tvaṣṭar, Indra strikes Vṛtra, splits the mountain/belly, and releases the waters, which rush forth like "bellowing milk-cows." The blow simultaneously props heaven and earth apart and produces sun, sky, and dawn (RV 1.32.4; 1.32.15). The hymn is unusually vivid: Vṛtra falls "like a tree-trunk split by an axe" (1.32.5), his mother Dānu lies over him (1.32.9), and, strikingly, Indra himself flees "across ninety-nine rivers" (1.32.14) terrified of an avenger — a detail later Indian sources attempt to explain away. Dozens of other RV hymns allude to the deed (especially RV 1.80, 2.11, 2.12, 4.18, 5.32, 6.17, 8.96, 10.113); the formulaic phrase *áhann áhim* ("he slew the serpent") is the recurring signature.

Cosmological/theological work: the act is simultaneously (a) a cosmogony — the separation of heaven and earth and release of ordered flow — and (b) a kingship myth — Indra's warrior-deed validates his sovereignty over the devas. Divergent later versions (Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa 1.6.3, Mahābhārata Udyoga-parvan) reframe Vṛtra as a Brahmin, adding a guilt-of-brahminicide layer not present in the Rigveda.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Rigveda** (oral composition c. 1500–1200 BCE; written transmission vastly later). Vedic Sanskrit. Preservation is exceptional due to the oral śākhā tradition.
- **Key editions / translations**: Stephanie Jamison & Joel Brereton, *The Rigveda: The Earliest Religious Poetry of India*, 3 vols., Oxford 2014 (current scholarly standard, English); Karl Friedrich Geldner, *Der Rig-Veda*, 4 vols., Harvard Oriental Series 33–36, 1951 (German, still philologically central); Louis Renou, *Études védiques et pāṇinéennes*, 17 vols., 1955–69 (French, exhaustive commentary); Michael Witzel & Toshifumi Gotō, *Rig-Veda: Das heilige Wissen*, 2007/2013/2022 (new German translation in progress). For Anglophone use, Jamison–Brereton is the current default; older Griffith (1889) and H. H. Wilson (1866) are still cited but dated.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
Essentially unanimous that Indra/Vṛtra is Chaoskampf in its strongest form. The key authorities:
- **Benveniste & Renou, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna* (Cahiers de la Société Asiatique III, 1934)**: foundational Indo-Iranian study; demonstrated that Vṛtra's core function is the "obstruction of the waters" (*vṛ-* "to enclose, obstruct"), and linked the Vedic figure to the Avestan Verethragna.
- **Arthur Macdonell, *Vedic Mythology* (1897)** and **E. W. Hopkins** already placed it squarely in the "dragon-slayer" category.
- **Calvert Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* (Oxford 1995)** makes Indra/Vṛtra the starting-point of his reconstructed Indo-European formula "HERO SLAY SERPENT" (*gʷhen- + *h₁ógʷhi-), tracing the signature across Vedic, Avestan, Hittite, Greek, Celtic, Germanic and Armenian evidence.
- **Stephanie Jamison, *The Ravenous Hyenas and the Wounded Sun* (1991)** and her RV introduction treat the myth as simultaneously cosmogonic and royal.
- **Michael Witzel**, *Origins of the World's Mythologies* (2012), reads it within his "Laurasian" mythological macro-scheme but accepts the chaoskampf character.
No serious dissent exists on the chaoskampf label itself; what *is* debated is (a) whether the "release of waters" is primarily meteorological (monsoon), hydrological (glacial melt / Indus floods — Witzel), or cosmogonic (Jamison), and (b) the relative age of the "three-headed" doublet (Viśvarūpa/Triśiras) tradition versus the Vṛtra story proper.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Central to every comparative treatment of the motif:
- Gunkel (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895) invoked Indra/Vṛtra as a principal Indo-Iranian parallel to Marduk/Tiamat and YHWH/Leviathan.
- Forsyth, *The Old Enemy* (Princeton 1987), treats it as part of the combat-myth matrix that feeds into Satan-figures.
- Wakeman, *God's Battle with the Monster* (Brill 1973), uses it as an Indo-European witness to the pattern.
- Day, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* (Cambridge 1985), Watkins (1995), West (*Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, 2007), Lincoln (*Priests, Warriors, and Cattle*, 1981; "The Indo-European Cattle-Raiding Myth," 1976), and Ogden (*Drakōn*, Oxford 2013) all treat it as prototypical. Watkins's "HERO SLAY SERPENT" formula was in fact designed around it.

### Verdict
- **Tier: CORE**
- **Confidence: HIGH**
- Indra/Vṛtra is one of the two or three canonical exemplars (with Marduk/Tiamat and Baʿal/Yamm) of the chaoskampf pattern; it combines cosmogony, kingship validation, release-of-waters, and the *gʷhen-* formula in a single archaic attestation. No specialist dissents.

## 2. Ra vs. Apep / Apophis (Egyptian)

### What the story tells
Apep (Gk. Apophis) is a gigantic chaos serpent who each night attacks the solar barque as Ra traverses the Duat (underworld). The sun-god and his crew — variously Seth, Mehen, Isis, Bastet, Shu — wound, coil, dismember, and bind the serpent so that Ra can emerge at dawn. The combat is eternal and quotidian rather than cosmogonic-one-time: every sunrise is a re-won victory over Apep, every sunset renews the threat. Apep embodies *isfet* (disorder, violence, non-being); Ra embodies and enforces *maʿat* (cosmic/social order). Specific episodes: Book of the Dead spells 7, 39, 108; Coffin Texts spells 160, 414 (Apep's "evil eye" and hypnotic gaze); Amduat hour 7 (Apep bound by Isis's magic, dismembered by Mehen's knives); Book of Gates hours 5 and 11 (Apep bound, sliced); Bremner-Rhind Papyrus (Papyrus BM 10188, 4th c. BCE), "The Book of Overthrowing Apep" — a liturgical manual for daily execration rituals in the Amun temple at Karnak. Pottery images of a solar boat opposing a large serpent appear as early as Naqada I (c. 4000–3550 BCE) and may prefigure the later narrative.

Cosmological/theological work: Apep has no moment of origin within the ordered world — in one tradition he is spat from Neith's primeval waters, in another he predates everything. His defeat is not *creatio*; it is *maintenance*. This distinguishes the Egyptian pattern sharply from the Marduk/Tiamat model.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Pyramid Texts** (Old Kingdom, c. 2400–2300 BCE): earliest textual allusions.
- **Coffin Texts** (Middle Kingdom, c. 2100–1800 BCE).
- **Book of the Dead / Book of Going Forth by Day** (New Kingdom onward, c. 1550 BCE–Roman period), hieratic/hieroglyphic on papyrus.
- **Amduat**, **Book of Gates**, **Book of Caverns** (New Kingdom royal tombs, c. 1500–1100 BCE).
- **Bremner-Rhind Papyrus** (British Museum EA 10188, c. 310 BCE): *The Book of Overthrowing Apep*.
- **Key editions / translations**: R. O. Faulkner, *The Ancient Egyptian Book of the Dead* (1972, rev. 1985); Faulkner, *The Ancient Egyptian Coffin Texts*, 3 vols. (Aris & Phillips 1973–78); Faulkner, "The Bremner-Rhind Papyrus" series, *JEA* 22–24, 1936–38; Erik Hornung, *Das Amduat* (1963–67) and *Das Buch von den Pforten des Jenseits* (1979–80); Joris F. Borghouts, *Ancient Egyptian Magical Texts* (Brill 1978) and "The Evil Eye of Apophis," *JEA* 59 (1973); T. G. Allen, *The Egyptian Book of the Dead* (1974).

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
Egyptologists overwhelmingly treat Apep as the Egyptian chaos-figure *par excellence*, but with a strong structural caveat: Egyptian cosmology is cyclical rather than one-and-done, so Apep is a "permanent opponent" rather than a "primordial monster slain at creation."
- **Herman te Velde, *Seth, God of Confusion* (Brill 1967, 2nd ed. 1977)**: Seth as Apep's slayer at the prow of the barque; the clearest articulation of the *maʿat*–*isfet* polarity.
- **Jan Assmann, *Ägyptische Sonnenhymnen in thebanischen Gräbern* (1983)** and *The Search for God in Ancient Egypt* (2001): argues Apep is the ontological opposite of *maʿat*, not a mere creature; the nightly combat is the ritualized enactment of cosmic order.
- **Erik Hornung, *Conceptions of God in Ancient Egypt* (1982)** and his *Amduat / Book of Gates* editions: frames the combat as the engine of solar regeneration.
- **J. F. Borghouts** on execration rituals: Apep spells are performative magic, not narrative cosmogony.
- **Mary K. Wakeman, *God's Battle with the Monster* (1973)** includes Apep as an instance of the general pattern.
Dissent/qualification: some Egyptologists (notably John Baines, and implicitly Assmann) caution that the category "Chaoskampf" is a Gunkel-derived imposition; the Egyptian pattern is better described as cyclical maintenance of *maʿat* against *isfet* rather than a creation-through-combat myth. In this sense the Ra/Apep case is a **structurally atypical** chaoskampf — arguably the *paradigm* of one subtype (cyclical/maintenance) rather than the Marduk subtype (cosmogonic/one-time).

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Gunkel, Forsyth, Wakeman, and Day all list Ra/Apep as a core parallel. Watkins's strictly Indo-European "HERO SLAY SERPENT" formula does not apply (Egyptian is Afroasiatic), but the narrative/structural match to Near Eastern chaoskampf is strong. West (2007) acknowledges Apep as structurally analogous without claiming genetic linkage. Ogden (*Drakōn*, 2013) treats Apep as a Mediterranean-adjacent serpent combat paradigm relevant to Greek dragon traditions.

### Verdict
- **Tier: CORE**
- **Confidence: HIGH**
- Apep is the most fully elaborated chaos-serpent in any ancient tradition, with a dedicated liturgical literature. The caveat is that the Egyptian pattern is cyclical/maintenance-type rather than cosmogonic — which, far from disqualifying it, distinguishes it as the canonical non-Semitic subtype. Any chaoskampf book that omits it is incomplete.

## 3. Thor vs. Jörmungandr (Norse; Prose Edda, Poetic Edda, skaldic poetry)

### What the story tells
Jörmungandr ("Huge Monster"), the *Miðgarðsormr* ("World-Serpent"), is one of Loki's three monstrous children (with Fenrir and Hel). Odin casts the serpent into the encircling ocean, where it grows to girdle Miðgarðr, biting its own tail — a cosmic *ouroboros* whose grip paradoxically holds the world together. Thor and Jörmungandr meet three times:
1. At Útgarðaloki's hall, Thor unknowingly tries to lift the serpent (disguised as a cat); he only manages to raise one paw, revealing his and the serpent's near-equal power (*Gylfaginning* 46–47).
2. In the **fishing episode** (the most iconographically dense), Thor rows out with the giant Hymir, baits his line with an ox-head (taken from Hymir's prize bull), hooks the serpent, and hauls it up; either Hymir cuts the line (so *Hymiskviða* st. 23–24 in the Poetic Edda, and *Gylfaginning* 48) or Thor strikes off its head (implied in Úlfr Uggason's *Húsdrápa* st. 3–6, c. 983, and in skaldic kennings by Bragi Boddason, Eysteinn Valdason, Gamli gnævaðarskáld; Snorri notes "some say" Thor killed it). This is the most depicted Norse myth in stone — Altuna, Hørdum, Gosforth, and Ardre VIII stones, 9th–11th c.
3. At **Ragnarök** (*Völuspá* 56; *Gylfaginning* 51): Thor kills the serpent with Mjǫllnir, then staggers nine paces and dies from its venom. This is the only attested combat in which the serpent actually dies.

Cosmological/theological work: Jörmungandr is both a hostile outsider (he will destroy the world) and a constitutive boundary (his coils *are* the world's edge). His death coincides with cosmic dissolution.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Skaldic poetry** (c. 850–1000 CE): Bragi Boddason, *Ragnarsdrápa* (partial); Úlfr Uggason, *Húsdrápa* (c. 983, fragmentary; ekphrasis of carved panels); Eysteinn Valdason; Gamli gnævaðarskáld; Ǫlvir hnúfa. These are the *earliest* and textually thinnest attestations.
- **Poetic Edda** (compiled late 13th c., Codex Regius GKS 2365 4to): *Vǫluspá*, *Hymiskviða*.
- **Prose Edda** (c. 1220) by Snorri Sturluson: *Gylfaginning* 34, 46–48, 51.
- **Iconography**: Altuna runestone (Sweden, 11th c.), Hørdum stone (Denmark, 8th–11th c.), Gosforth Cross / "fishing stone" (Cumbria, 10th c.), Ardre VIII (Gotland, 8th–11th c.).
- **Key editions / translations**: Gustav Neckel & Hans Kuhn, *Edda: Die Lieder des Codex Regius* (1962); Ursula Dronke, *The Poetic Edda*, 3 vols. (Oxford 1969–2011); Carolyne Larrington, *The Poetic Edda* (Oxford 1996, rev. 2014); Anthony Faulkes, *Edda* (Snorri, Everyman 1987, and Viking Society 2005 edition); *Skaldic Poetry of the Scandinavian Middle Ages*, gen. ed. Margaret Clunies Ross (Brepols, 2007– , definitive skaldic corpus).

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
The Norse specialist consensus is that Thor/Jörmungandr is a chaoskampf myth, but with a specific structural note: the *cosmogonic* victory is displaced to *eschatology* — Thor defeats the serpent at the end, not at the beginning, of cosmic time.
- **Jan de Vries, *Altgermanische Religionsgeschichte* (2nd ed., 1956–57)**: treats it as the Germanic reflex of the Indo-European storm-god/serpent myth.
- **Georges Dumézil** (*Les Dieux des Germains*, 1959) reads Thor within the Indo-European "second function" warrior-god matrix.
- **Rudolf Simek, *Dictionary of Northern Mythology* (Brewer 1993; Ger. 1984)** s.v. "Midgard Serpent," "Thor": explicitly chaoskampf.
- **John Lindow, *Norse Mythology* (Oxford 2001; ABC-CLIO 2001)**: frames Jörmungandr as the paradigmatic "bound monster" and reads the fishing episode as a chaoskampf whose resolution is delayed to Ragnarök.
- **Margaret Clunies Ross, *Prolonged Echoes*, 2 vols. (Odense 1994, 1998)**, and "Two of Þórr's Great Fights according to *Hymiskviða*" (1989): nuances the reading — the fishing is about cosmic *balance* as much as cosmic *combat*.
- **Preben Meulengracht Sørensen, "Thor's Fishing Expedition" (*Words and Objects*, 1986)**: classic reading of the fishing episode as a controlled reenactment of cosmogonic combat, emphasizing that the serpent's *release* (not only its defeat) is part of cosmic order. Meulengracht Sørensen thus offers the most influential qualification: the Norse myth is *not* a straightforward kill-the-dragon-create-the-world pattern.
- **Gro Steinsland** and **Kees Samplonius** also stress the boundary-maintenance function.
- **Anders Hultgård** and **Anatoly Liberman** compare to Indo-European parallels but note the skaldic attestations may depict an original *successful* kill (decapitation), which would make the Ragnarök mutual death a later development.
Dissent/qualification: the unambiguous cosmogonic killing is attested (a) in likely-earliest skaldic iconography (decapitation in *Húsdrápa*) but (b) only in *eschatology* in the Edda prose tradition. This bifurcation is the substantive scholarly wrinkle.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
- Gunkel (1895) cited Thor/Jörmungandr as the Germanic parallel.
- Watkins (*How to Kill a Dragon*, 1995) reads the skaldic kennings as direct reflexes of the Indo-European *gʷhen- ogʷhim* formula (Thor *bana ormi* "bane to the serpent"); he treats Thor as a Germanic Indra.
- M. L. West, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth* (Oxford 2007), chapter 6 ("Storm and Stream"), explicitly places Thor among the storm-god / water-dragon complex alongside Indra, Tarḫunna, and the Balto-Slavic storm god.
- Forsyth, Wakeman, Day, Lincoln, and Ogden all include it.

### Verdict
- **Tier: CORE**
- **Confidence: HIGH** (with a MEDIUM asterisk on the "eschatology-only" caveat)
- The draft's instinct is correct: Thor/Jörmungandr is a canonical chaoskampf because (a) the skaldic corpus depicts a completed cosmogonic kill (decapitation), (b) both the fishing episode and Ragnarök combat are explicitly combat with a primordial boundary-serpent, and (c) Watkins shows the formulaic Indo-European pattern is preserved in the very diction of the skalds. The eschatological displacement is a real feature but does not remove it from the category — it defines the Germanic subtype.

## 4. Susanoo vs. Yamata no Orochi (Japanese; Kojiki, Nihon Shoki)

### What the story tells
Expelled from heaven for defiling his sister Amaterasu's palace, the storm-god Susanoo descends to Izumo (western Japan), where he encounters an elderly couple and their daughter Kushinada-hime. Their seven other daughters have been eaten in successive years by Yamata no Orochi, an eight-headed, eight-tailed serpent whose body spans eight valleys and eight hills and whose belly is perpetually bleeding. Susanoo transforms Kushinada into a comb which he places in his hair, directs the couple to brew eight vats of strong sake and to build eight gates with platforms, each holding a vat. The serpent's eight heads each drink, become drunk, and sleep; Susanoo dismembers the serpent. In one tail he finds the sword **Kusanagi-no-tsurugi** ("Grass-cutter"), which he sends up to Amaterasu; it becomes one of the three Imperial Regalia. He marries Kushinada and builds the first palace at Suga. *Kojiki* 1.19–1.20 (Philippi, 1968, pp. 88–92); *Nihon Shoki* I, main text and six variant "one writing says" accounts (Aston 1896, vol. I pp. 52–59).

Cosmological/theological work: The myth is heavily polyvalent — Izumo origin myth; sword-etiology legitimating imperial regalia; hieros-gamos establishing Susanoo's dynasty; possibly an iron-age metallurgical allegory (the bleeding belly = iron-stained rivers; the sword in the tail = smelting); a rice/flood allegory (the serpent as the Hi River; the victims as rice paddies). It is *not* a cosmogony — the world already exists; Susanoo is not the creator of order but an agonistic latecomer-hero whose reintegration into the pantheon follows the killing.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Kojiki** ("Record of Ancient Matters," 712 CE), Old Japanese in man'yōgana script; a single manuscript tradition (Shinpukuji-bon, 1371–72, oldest complete).
- **Nihon Shoki** / **Nihongi** (720 CE), in classical Chinese; much better preserved; with multiple variant tellings juxtaposed ("issho" — "one writing says").
- Also: *Izumo no kuni Fudoki* (733 CE) — regional gazetteer, preserves alternate Izumo-local traditions of Susanoo without the Orochi episode in the form the Kojiki gives it.
- **Key editions / translations**: Donald L. Philippi, *Kojiki* (University of Tokyo Press 1968; still the scholarly standard); Basil Hall Chamberlain, *Ko-ji-ki* (1882, dated but still cited); Gustav Heldt, *The Kojiki: An Account of Ancient Matters* (Columbia 2014); W. G. Aston, *Nihongi* (1896, still the only full English translation). Japanese editions: *Shinpen Nihon koten bungaku zenshū* (Shōgakukan).

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
This is where careful distinctions matter. **Japanese-specialist scholarship does not primarily frame Susanoo/Orochi as chaoskampf.** The dominant Japanese-language framings are:
- **Sword/regalia etiology** — Matsumae Takeshi, Ōbayashi Taryō.
- **Metallurgical allegory** (iron smelting along the Hi river) — Nelly Naumann, *Die Einheimische Religion Japans* (2 vols., Brill 1988–94); Naumann, "Old Japanese Sword Names" (1959) and *Die Mythen des alten Japan* (1996).
- **Flood-control / rice-agriculture allegory** — Matsumura Takeo, Tsuda Sōkichi.
- **Imperial-legitimation / court-mythology construction** — Tsuda Sōkichi (early 20th c.); more recently, **Isomae Jun'ichi, *Japanese Mythology: Hermeneutics on Scripture* (Equinox 2010)** and **Michael Como, *Weaving and Binding: Immigrant Gods and Female Immortals in Ancient Japan* (Hawaii 2009)**, both of whom stress the 8th-century political construction of the Kojiki/Nihon Shoki narratives and are skeptical of projecting universalist Indo-European categories onto them. Herman Ooms, *Imperial Politics and Symbolics in Ancient Japan* (Hawaii 2009), is similarly cautious.

The **Indo-European / chaoskampf reading** is primarily a Western comparativist position, with major advocates being:
- **C. Scott Littleton**, "The 'Kingship in Heaven' Theme" (1969); *The New Comparative Mythology* (3rd ed., Berkeley 1982); "Susa-nö-wo versus Ya-mata nö woröti: An Indo-European Theme in Japanese Mythology," *History of Religions* 20 (1981): 269–80 — the key article that argues for genetic Indo-European transmission (via Scythian/Saka intermediaries) of the dragon-slaying pattern into Japanese myth. Controversial.
- **Atsuhiko Yoshida**, "Japanese Mythology and the Indo-European Trifunctional System" (*Diogenes*, 1977) — a rare Japanese scholar who endorses Dumézilian/Indo-European comparison; also worked with Obayashi.
- **Ōbayashi Taryō**, though trained in Vienna (Wilhelm Schmidt's *Kulturkreis* school) and working comparatively, actually argued for a *Southeast Asian / Austronesian* source for much of the dragon imagery, not an Indo-European one. His position partially undercuts Littleton's.
- **Nelly Naumann** accepts comparative parallels but insists the core of the Orochi episode is metallurgical-etiological and Izumo-local, not a chaoskampf.
- **Michael Witzel**, *Origins of the World's Mythologies* (2012), fits it into his "Laurasian" macro-scheme (so: yes, but at an extremely high level of abstraction that collapses most distinctions).

**Dissent is substantial.** Isomae, Como, Ooms, and implicitly much recent Japanese-studies scholarship regard the Indo-European chaoskampf framing as a post hoc fit imposed by Western comparativists. Even sympathetic readers such as Naumann restrict the comparative claim to surface similarity.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
- **Gunkel (1895)** does not include Japan.
- **Fontenrose, *Python* (1959)** cites Orochi as a comparative parallel to Python/Typhon.
- **Watkins (1995)** does not treat Japanese material (it falls outside Indo-European).
- **West (2007)** likewise does not include Susanoo.
- **Forsyth (1987), Wakeman (1973), Day (1985), Lincoln (1981)** — do not use it as a primary example; at most a footnote analogy.
- **Ogden (*Drakōn*, 2013)** briefly mentions Japan only as a non-Mediterranean parallel.
- **Littleton (1981)**, **Yoshida (1977)**, **Witzel (2012)** are the main positive advocates.
Watkins's "HERO SLAY SERPENT" formula is, by his own restriction, Indo-European *linguistic* — it captures *hann han āhīm* phraseology. Japanese is not Indo-European, so the formula does not apply at the linguistic level. At the *narrative/structural* level, the pattern obviously fits. The methodological question (structural analogy vs. genetic descent) is unresolved.

### Verdict
- **Tier: RELATED** (not Core; not Provisional)
- **Confidence: MEDIUM**
- The draft's note that Susanoo is "strong early Japanese" is defensible as a narrative observation but should not be upgraded to chaoskampf-core. Japanese-specialist scholarship overwhelmingly reads the myth as sword-etiology / metallurgical / Izumo-local, not as cosmogonic combat, and the Indo-European chaoskampf reading (Littleton, Yoshida) is a minority comparativist position widely regarded by Japanologists as overreach. The story should appear in a chaoskampf book as a *structurally analogous* dragon-slaying narrative that illustrates the motif's reach — with explicit acknowledgment that the chaoskampf label is applied from outside the tradition.
