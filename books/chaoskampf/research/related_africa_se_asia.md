# Related Chaoskampf Stories: Africa & Southeast Asia

Research note prepared 2026-04-23 for the chaoskampf book. For each story: textual anchors, scholarly consensus, appropriation warnings, and a verdict. Weighting of sources prioritises area specialists (Africanist / Philippine-specialist) over comparativist generalists.

---

## 1. Bakunawa (Philippines; Visayan / Bikol oral tradition)

### What the story tells
In the best-known Visayan recension ("Bakunawa and the Seven Moons"), the supreme deity Bathala creates seven moons to light the night sky. The Bakunawa, a vast serpent/dragon dwelling in the sea (or alternately the sky or underworld), rises and swallows six of the moons one by one, driven by envy of their beauty. To save the seventh and final moon (Bulan), humans beat gongs, bamboo clappers, and pots to drive the beast away; this is the aetiological explanation for the noise-making practice during lunar eclipses. Bikol variants describe Bakunawa alternately as a beautiful diwata-turned-serpent, a naga, or Irago. Regional variants elsewhere in the archipelago replace the serpent with other animals: the Tagalog/Kapampangan **Laho / Láwû** (bird-dragon swallowing the sun), the Hiligaynon **Bawa/Olimaw**, the Bagobo **Minokawa** (giant bird), and the Maranao **Arimaonga** (giant crab).

Textual anchor: the canonical modern retelling is Damiana L. Eugenio, ed., *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths* (University of the Philippines Press; the PFL series, vol. II). Eugenio's version is itself based on Fernando Buyser's 1913 Cebuano-Bisaya compilation *Mga Sugilanong Karaan* (Sugbo/Cebu, 1913), which contains the earliest printed version.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Fernando A. Buyser**, *Mga Sugilanong Karaan* (Cebu/Sugbo, 1913) — original Cebuano (Bisaya) text of "Bakunawa ug ang Pito ka Bulan"; Buyser was a Cebuano priest and poet who compiled Visayan folklore from oral informants in the early twentieth century. Reprinted in the 1926 *Mga Sugilanong Pilipinhon (Folk Lore Filipino)*. Language of performance: Cebuano; language of record: Cebuano (not translated at point of record, unlike much colonial material).
- **Mansueto Porras**, *Signosan* (1919) — interprets the Bakunawa cycle as a geomantic calendar.
- **Damiana L. Eugenio**, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths* (UP Press, 1993/2001), volume II of her seven-volume *Philippine Folk Literature* series; the standard scholarly anthology, Eugenio being "Mother of Philippine Folklore."
- **Francisco R. Demetrio, S.J.**, *Dictionary of Philippine Folk Beliefs and Customs* (Xavier University, 1970), also "Towards a Classification of Bisayan Folk Beliefs" (*Asian Folklore Studies*, 1969) — early academic notice that Bakunawa is "a big snake" that causes eclipses.
- **Maximo D. Ramos**, *The Creatures of Philippine Lower Mythology* (UP Press, 1971; PhD diss. UP 1965) — places Bakunawa within his "Dragons" taxonomic category, alongside the Tagalog laho, and notes the eclipse-swallowing motif is pan-Philippine.

Caveats: Buyser was a Catholic priest writing in an already-Christianised Visayas; his 1913 text mentions "Bathala" but in a framework mediated by four centuries of Spanish Catholic overlay. The motif of Bakunawa being driven by envy and coiling around the earth has been compared (in Buyser's own gloss and later commentators) with Revelation 12; it is not certain how much of the moral-theological scaffolding is pre-Hispanic. Oral-performance context for the pre-1913 tradition is essentially lost; we have only Buyser's printed distillation.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
The Bakunawa tale is **not** framed as a *chaoskampf* combat myth by Philippine-specialist folklorists. Eugenio, Demetrio, Ramos, and Menez treat it primarily as:
1. An **aetiological eclipse myth** explaining noise-making ritual;
2. A **geomantic / calendrical tradition** (Porras; Demetrio) — Bakunawa's movements as time-keeping;
3. A **syncretic Indic layer** (Ramos; Wikipedia citations to Scott, Jocano) — syncretised with the Vedic Rāhu/Ketu eclipse-demon via the Indianisation of insular SE Asia.

Crucially, in most Visayan variants there is **no hero who slays Bakunawa**. The monster is merely driven off, temporarily, by collective human noise-making; it returns at every eclipse. This is the opposite of a chaoskampf victory narrative — it is a recurring, ritualised repulsion, closer to the Vedic Rāhu pattern (where Rāhu's severed head perpetually chases and briefly swallows the sun/moon) than to Marduk–Tiāmat or Zeus–Typhon. Some late/literary versions introduce the hero-figure **Bulan** (the moon-boy) or a human hero who hides the last moon, but these are minor variants and often post-Buyser literary embellishments.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- William Henry Scott (*Barangay*, 1994) and F. Landa Jocano (*Outline of Philippine Mythology*, 1969) both caution against reading pre-Hispanic Philippine cosmology through imported templates.
- The "dragon" label for Bakunawa is itself a colonial/English translation artefact; Buyser's Cebuano uses *bakunawa* (possibly from *baku* "bent/curved" + *nawa* "snake/naga"), and the creature is variously a serpent, eel-dragon, or mermaid-naga depending on locality. Ramos's taxonomy uses "dragon" as an etic English category only.
- The syncretic-Indic reading (Bakunawa ← Rāhu/nāga) means any chaoskampf framing risks double importation — first via Hindu-Buddhist overlay, then via Eurasian comparative mythology.

### Verdict
- **RELATED** (eclipse-serpent / cosmic-disorder adjacent), not CORE.
- **Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH**.
- Rationale: Bakunawa is unambiguously a monstrous serpent threatening cosmic order (the moons), but the narrative lacks the decisive hero-vs-monster combat-and-kill that defines chaoskampf proper. It belongs in the book as a comparative foil — an eclipse-demon tradition where the "combat" is ritualised human noise rather than divine slaying — useful for showing the range of cosmic-disorder responses beyond the Eurasian template.

## 2. Bida (West Africa; Soninke Wagadu / Ghana Empire origin legend)

### What the story tells
Bida (Soninke *bida* = "python" / "boa") is a great serpent — variously one- or seven-headed — living in a well or grove at the founding-site of the city of Koumbi Saleh (the capital of ancient Ghana/Wagadu). The ancestor Dinga migrates west, and his son Dyabé Cissé negotiates a pact with the resident serpent Bida: in exchange for the annual (in some versions septennial) sacrifice of the most beautiful virgin of the kingdom, Bida guarantees gold (which falls as "rain of gold" three times a year) and seasonal rains. The pact endures for generations until the turn of Sia / Siya Yatabaré, the most beautiful girl of Wagadu, whose fiancé Mamadi Sefe Dekote ("Mamadi the Taciturn") hides in ambush. When Bida raises his head three times before swallowing the bride, Mamadi beheads him on the third rise. The severed, airborne head curses the kingdom: "For seven years, seven months, and seven days, Wagadu shall receive no rain of gold and no rain of water." The drought follows, Wagadu disperses, and this is the aetiological explanation for the Soninke diaspora and (in some tellings) for the desertification of the Sahel.

Canonical retellings: Leo Frobenius, *Atlantis: Volksmärchen und Volksdichtungen Afrikas* vol. VI: *Spielmannsgeschichten der Sahel* (1921), containing "Die Tötung des Bida" / "The Killing of the Bida Dragon" as part of what Frobenius called the Soninke "Dausi" epic cycle. Also Charles Monteil, "La légende du Ouagadou et l'origine des Soninké" (*Mélanges ethnologiques*, IFAN Dakar, 1953 — the standard French colonial-era recording). Germaine Dieterlen and Diarra Sylla, *L'empire de Ghana: Le Wagadou et les traditions de Yéréré* (Paris: Karthala / ACCT, 1992).

### Primary textual attestation
- **Leo Frobenius**, *Atlantis* vol. VI (1921), *African Genesis* (1937) — earliest European printed versions; collected c. 1908–1909 in Borgu (modern Benin), *not* in the Soninke heartland. Stephen Belcher (below) and Ralph Austen have flagged Frobenius's texts as heavily literary and ideologically coloured (Frobenius's "Atlantis" thesis, diffusionist fantasy); corroboration from independent sources required.
- **Charles Monteil**, "La légende du Ouagadou" (*Mélanges ethnologiques*, IFAN Mém. 23, Dakar, 1953); Monteil was the senior French Africanist administrator and his version remains a reference.
- **Germaine Dieterlen with Diarra Sylla**, *L'empire de Ghana: Le Wagadou et les traditions de Yéréré* (Karthala, 1992) — a late-career Dieterlen collaboration with a Soninke informant (Diarra Sylla); reviewed in *Outre-Mers* 81 (1994).
- **Mamadou Diawara**, *La Graine de la parole* (Franz Steiner Verlag, 1990) and subsequent articles — methodological critique of Soninke oral tradition, including the politics of who controls the Wagadu narrative (griot castes, noble lineages).
- **David C. Conrad** (ed.), *A State of Intrigue: The Epic of Bamana Segu* and "A Town Called Dakajalan" (*JAH* 1994); with Humphrey J. Fisher, "The Conquest that Never Was: Ghana and the Almoravids" (*History in Africa* 9 and 10, 1982–83).
- **Ralph A. Austen** (ed.), *In Search of Sunjata: The Mande Oral Epic as History, Literature, and Performance* (Indiana UP, 1999) — methodological touchstone.
- **Stephen Belcher**, *Epic Traditions of Africa* (Indiana UP, 1999) and *African Myths of Origin* (Penguin, 2005) — English-language scholarly overview.

Caveats: Frobenius's "Dausi" as an integrated epic cycle is now widely considered a **scholarly fiction** — a literary stitching-together of disparate narrative fragments. Wikipedia (citing Belcher) notes "Gassire's Lute" in particular has never been independently corroborated in Soninke oral tradition. The Bida episode itself, however, *is* attested independently in multiple recordings (Monteil, Dieterlen-Sylla, Meillassoux, and others), so its existence as a Soninke legend is secure; what is contested is Frobenius's framing of it as part of a grand Dausi epic.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
Africanist scholarship does **not** frame Bida as a chaoskampf story. Monteil, Dieterlen, and Conrad treat it primarily as:
1. A **foundation legend / charter myth** for the Ghana/Wagadu polity, explaining the Soninke diaspora and the political-religious origins of the Cissé lineage;
2. A **pact-with-the-genius-loci** tradition — Bida is a *jinnu* or territorial python-spirit (cf. the wider West African python-cult belt from the Fon/Dahomean Dangbé at Ouidah to the Bambara/Bozo water-spirit complexes), whose covenant with human rulers guarantees fertility;
3. A **climatological memory** — Conrad, Fisher, and others read the seven-year drought curse as a crystallised memory of the climatic deterioration (dry Sahel, southward shift of agricultural frontier c. 1000–1200 CE) that contributed to Wagadu's actual historical decline.

The **"reverse chaoskampf"** reading — that slaying the serpent brings *disorder* rather than order, inverting Marduk/Apep/Perseus — is a **modern comparativist framing**, not established in the Africanist primary literature. It appears in online mythology encyclopaedias and some comparativist popularisations (e.g. the *Circle of the Dragon* website; various Wikipedia "Chaoskampf" expansions), but I could not locate a peer-reviewed Africanist publication by Monteil, Meillassoux, Dieterlen, Diawara, Conrad, Austen, or Belcher that explicitly labels the Bida story "chaoskampf" (reverse or otherwise). Meillassoux's name is sometimes attached to this reading in tertiary sources but he is better known for his Marxist-anthropological work on Soninke slavery and kinship (*Anthropologie de l'esclavage*, 1986) than for mytho-comparative essays on Bida.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- **Frobenius skepticism**: any chaoskampf framing built on the Frobenius text must be corrected against later, better-attested Soninke recordings (Monteil, Dieterlen-Sylla, Meillassoux).
- **West African python-cult context**: the Bida covenant belongs to a regional pattern of serpent-spirit alliances (Dangbé at Ouidah; Ninkinanka in Senegambia; the Bozo water-pythons of the Niger Bend). Reading it through Marduk-Tiāmat flattens this distinctive West African theology in which the snake is not disorder but *guarantor* of order.
- **Moral inversion**: in most Soninke tellings the hero Mamadi is **not** valorised — he is a disruptor whose love-rescue destroys the commonwealth. The story's moral gravity is closer to Greek tragedy (hubris bringing ruin) than to chaoskampf triumphalism.
- The post-colonial Soninke griot tradition is heavily mediated by noble-patron politics (Diawara); "the" Bida legend varies substantially across recensions (Kaarta, Guidimakha, Jaara, Gajaaga).

### Verdict
- **RELATED / PROVISIONAL** — belongs in a chaoskampf book precisely as a **counter-example** or foil, not as a core instance.
- **Confidence: HIGH** (that Bida is *not* a standard chaoskampf); **MEDIUM** (for the "reverse chaoskampf" reading as specifically Africanist — that framing is largely comparativist).
- Rationale: The Bida legend features a serpent-slaying hero, but the killing of the serpent brings *drought, curse, and dispersal* — the narrative charge is opposite that of Marduk, Apep, or Perseus. Africanist specialists read it as a charter myth and climatological memory, not as combat-with-chaos. It is valuable in the book as a demonstration that serpent-slaying is not universally an ordering act; in West African traditions the serpent can be the *covenant partner* of civilisation.

## 3. Mwindo vs. Kirimu (Nyanga / DR Congo; Epic of Mwindo)

### What the story tells
Mwindo, the miraculously-born, diminutive but preternaturally powerful son of chief Shemwindo of the Banyanga (Nyanga), lives through an arc of rejection, supernatural quest, descent to the underworld of Muisa, and final consolidation of chiefly authority. Midway through his career, after returning from exile, Mwindo goes hunting against his father's explicit warning. He encounters **Kirimu**, a great beast of the forest described in Rureke's recitation (as edited by Biebuyck and Mateene) as having a black hide, teeth like a dog, the tail of an eagle, and seven horned heads — the blood-brother of the sky-god and lightning-master **Nkuba** (Mukiti's kin). Mwindo slays Kirimu and recovers three hunters the dragon had swallowed. Nkuba, furious at the death of his favoured creature, snatches Mwindo into the sky and subjects him to a year of alternating penances — scorching sun-heat and icy rain-cold — until Mwindo swears henceforth never to kill another living thing unjustly. Only after this chastening is he returned to earth and re-installed as chief. The Kirimu episode thus functions structurally as the hero's **moment of hubris**: his lethal prowess must be humbled before he can rule.

Textual anchor: Daniel Biebuyck and Kahombo C. Mateene, *The Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (Congo Republic)* (University of California Press, 1969), the bilingual Nyanga-English edition based on the twelve-day 1956 performance of bard Shé-kárísi (literally "master-of-the-karisi-cult") Candi Rureke at Bese village, Walikale territory, North Kivu.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Daniel P. Biebuyck & Kahombo C. Mateene**, *The Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (Congo Republic)* (UC Press, 1969) — the canonical scholarly edition. Biebuyck began Nyanga fieldwork in April 1954; the core recording was made in 1956 from Shé-kárísi Candi Rureke. The text is bilingual with tonal indications, and Biebuyck's introduction situates the epic in Nyanga political structure, *karisi* cult practice, and initiatory ideology.
- **Daniel P. Biebuyck**, *Hero and Chief: Epic Literature from the Banyanga (Zaire Republic)* (UC Press, 1978) — additional Nyanga epic variants (Mwindo Mboru, Mwindo Meshe, Kahindo) showing the 1956 Rureke performance is one version among several.
- **Kahombo C. Mateene**, further Nyanga linguistic and textual work published in Kinshasa.
- Performance medium: Nyanga (Bantu, zone JD.501); simultaneous translation by Mateene, himself a Mu-Nyanga speaker.

Caveats: The epic was collected under Belgian colonial administration (pre-1960), which shaped access and patronage. Biebuyck repeatedly emphasises that Rureke's twelve-day performance was **not a normal performance context** — no single audience would ever hear the whole cycle in conventional village settings; pieces (*karisi*) are typically sung in initiation or ritual contexts. The continuous narrative is therefore partly a scholarly elicitation artefact. This is acknowledged explicitly in Biebuyck's preface and discussed by Okpewho (below).

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
Africanist epic scholarship **does not** frame the Mwindo–Kirimu combat as chaoskampf. The dominant readings are:
1. **Hero-initiation / monomyth** — the Kirimu episode is the hubris-and-chastening stage of a Campbellian/Lord-Raglanesque heroic arc; this is the reading implicit in Biebuyck's introduction and developed explicitly by **Isidore Okpewho**, *The Epic in Africa: Toward a Poetics of the Oral Performance* (Columbia UP, 1979) and *Myth in Africa* (Cambridge UP, 1983). Okpewho argues the dragon-fight shows the hero's *excess* rather than cosmic order-making.
2. **Initiation-cult ideology** — Biebuyck ties Mwindo's career to Nyanga *mumbira* and *karisi* initiation practices; Kirimu is part of a test sequence, not a cosmogonic monster.
3. **African epic / chiefly charter** — **Stephen Belcher**, *Epic Traditions of Africa* (Indiana UP, 1999), chapter on Central African epic, treats Mwindo as a chiefly-validation narrative paralleling Sunjata and the Lianja (Mongo) cycle.
4. **Storytelling poetics** — **Harold Scheub**, *A Dictionary of African Mythology* (Oxford UP, 2000) and *The African Storyteller* (Kendall Hunt) fold Kirimu into a broader pattern of African ogre-and-hero narratives, not into a chaoskampf schema.

The Kirimu fight is sometimes **compared** in passing to Odysseus–Polyphemus (both involve the hero defeating the favourite of a powerful god and being punished for it), most notably in online synopses and secondary works; this is a Greek-hero parallel, not a cosmogonic-combat parallel. No Africanist specialist — Biebuyck, Mateene, Okpewho, Scheub, Belcher — classifies Mwindo-vs-Kirimu as chaoskampf.

### Appropriation / framing warnings
- **Structural position matters**: Kirimu appears *mid-epic*, long after the cosmos is already in place. This is not a cosmogonic opponent (no Tiāmat, no Apep-at-the-horizon). The battle does not establish world order; it tests a chief-in-the-making.
- **Moral valence inverted from chaoskampf**: killing Kirimu is explicitly *wrong* in the narrative. Mwindo is punished by the sky-god and returns chastened. In chaoskampf, the hero is rewarded and enthroned for the killing; in Mwindo, the hero is humbled.
- **Colonial-era elicitation**: the 1956 Rureke performance is an atypical continuous recitation made at Biebuyck's request. Any comparative reading should foreground Biebuyck's own caveats about performance context.
- **Comparative-mythology appropriation risk**: because Kirimu is "a seven-headed dragon," web-tier sources have attached the chaoskampf label retrofitted from the Wagadu-Bida pattern. This should be resisted — the Nyanga epic is a hero-biography, not a cosmogonic combat.

### Verdict
- **EXCLUDE** from the chaoskampf CORE; **RELATED** only in the thin sense that a seven-headed serpent is killed by a hero.
- **Confidence: HIGH**.
- Rationale: Africanist scholarship unanimously reads the Kirimu episode as hero-initiation and hubris-chastening, not as cosmogonic combat. Importing chaoskampf here misreads the moral and structural meaning of Mwindo's dragon-killing, which the epic itself presents as a *fault* requiring atonement. If included in the book at all, it should be as a deliberate counter-example showing where the chaoskampf template fails to travel.

## Cross-cutting observations

- All three stories feature a hero-vs-serpent encounter, yet none fits a strict chaoskampf template as defined on the Marduk-Tiāmat / Baal-Yam / Apep axis.
- **Bakunawa** is an eclipse-demon without a combat resolution; **Bida** is a serpent-slaying that brings disorder; **Mwindo–Kirimu** is a hero-initiation hubris episode. Each productively shows the **limits** of the chaoskampf template outside its Eurasian core.
- For publication: frame these as "related" or "comparative foils" rather than core exemplars. Lean on area specialists (Eugenio, Ramos, Menez, Demetrio for the Philippines; Monteil, Dieterlen, Diawara, Conrad, Belcher for the Soninke; Biebuyck, Mateene, Okpewho, Scheub for Nyanga) and avoid chaoskampf-ising serpent-hero material that the source traditions and their scholars read differently.
