# Core Chaoskampf Cases — Ancient Near East & Mediterranean
Research note for the Chaoskampf book project. Focus: five candidate "core" stories (Marduk/Tiamat, Baal/Yamm-Lotan, Zeus/Typhon, Storm-god/Illuyanka, Apollo/Python). Scope: textual attestation, scholarly consensus on Chaoskampf status, and recommended tier.

General caveat on terminology. Hermann Gunkel's coinage *Chaoskampf* (*Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, 1895) has been under sustained revision for more than a century. Key landmarks in the later debate: Mary K. Wakeman, *God's Battle with the Monster* (Brill, 1973); John Day, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea* (Cambridge UP, 1985); Neil Forsyth, *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth* (Princeton UP, 1987); Calvert Watkins, *How to Kill a Dragon* (OUP, 1995); the Eisenbrauns volumes *Creation and Chaos* (ed. Scurlock & Beal, 2013, reassessing Gunkel) and *Creation and Destruction* (ed. Ortlund, 2010); and most pointedly Debra Scoggins Ballentine, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* (OUP, 2015), who argues that the combat enemies are better read as "agents of an alternative divine power structure" than as "chaos," and urges scholars to drop "*Chaoskampf*" as a framing term. The catalog should flag this live debate rather than assume the 19th-century inheritance uncritically.

## 1. Marduk vs. Tiamat (Enūma Eliš, Babylonian)

### What the story tells
The *Enūma Eliš* ("When on High"), preserved on seven tablets, narrates the emergence of the gods from the primal mingling of Apsû (sweet water) and Tiamat (salt sea). Ea kills Apsû; Tiamat, provoked by the younger gods' clamour, produces an army of eleven monsters (mušḫuššu, bašmu, ugallu, etc.) under her consort Qingu. After the older gods fail, the young storm-god Marduk is elevated to supreme kingship in exchange for fighting her. On Tablet IV, Marduk traps Tiamat in a net, forces the evil wind into her mouth, splits her body like a shellfish, and fashions the cosmos from her corpse: sky from one half, earth from the other, Tigris and Euphrates from her eyes, mountains from her udder (Tablet V). On Tablet VI he creates humankind from the blood of the executed Qingu and founds Babylon/Esagila; Tablet VII proclaims his fifty names. The work does explicit cosmogonic, royal-legitimation, and cultic work for Babylon and for Marduk's supremacy over older gods (Enlil, Anu, Ea).

### Primary textual attestation
- Source: Akkadian, Standard Babylonian dialect, seven clay tablets. Extant copies are Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian (mostly Nineveh, Assur, Kish, Sippar, Babylon, Uruk), well preserved with some lacunae on Tablets V and VII.
- Date of composition: most commonly placed in the reign of Nebuchadnezzar I (late 12th c. BCE) following Lambert; Kassite dating (Sommerfeld) has been proposed and countered.
- Editions / translations to rely on: W. G. Lambert, *Babylonian Creation Myths* (Eisenbrauns, 2013) — the definitive critical edition; Benjamin Foster, *Before the Muses* (3rd ed., 2005) — standard English translation; Stephanie Dalley, *Myths from Mesopotamia* (OUP, rev. 2000); Thorkild Jacobsen, *The Treasures of Darkness* (Yale, 1976) for interpretive reading; Gösta Gabriel, *enūma eliš* (Mohr Siebeck, 2014) — recent literary study.

### Scholarly consensus on Chaoskampf status
Unambiguously the paradigm case. Gunkel built his original *Chaoskampf* thesis around it; subsequent generations have confirmed the identification while refining its semantics. Jacobsen (*Treasures of Darkness*, 1976; "The Battle between Marduk and Tiamat," *JAOS* 88, 1968) read it as a storm-vs-sea cosmogonic combat; Lambert (*Babylonian Creation Myths*, 2013) treats it as the theological legitimation of Marduk's rise and of Babylon's supplanting Nippur; Wakeman (1973) and Day (1985) use it as the reference point for reading the Hebrew *tĕhôm* / Leviathan / Rahab passages; Forsyth (1987) places it at the head of his genealogy of the combat myth. Dissent is narrow rather than wide: Ballentine (2015) and in a slightly different register Tsumura (*Creation and Destruction*, 2005) argue that "chaos" is the wrong category — Tiamat is a rival divine sovereign, not formless disorder — but both still treat the narrative as the central conflict-myth exemplar. No specialist disputes that this is a combat-and-kingship cosmogony.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Universally treated as one of the two or three anchoring cases. Gunkel (1895), Wakeman (1973), Forsyth (1987), Day (1985), and Ballentine (2015) all take it as the reference exemplar for the West Asian branch. West (*The East Face of Helicon*, OUP, 1997) uses it as the primary Mesopotamian comparandum for Hesiod's Typhonomachy and succession narrative. Watkins (1995) excludes it from his strict Indo-European *áhann áhim* formula corpus, precisely because it is non-IE, but treats it as the typological analogue against which the IE formula stands out. Lincoln (*Theorizing Myth*, 1999; *Gods and Demons*, 2012) engages it as royal ideology.

### Verdict
- **Tier: Core.**
- **Confidence: HIGH.**
- Rationale: The most securely attested, most thoroughly edited, and most universally accepted specimen of divine combat producing cosmic and political order. Every comparativist from Gunkel onward treats it as foundational. The only scholarly caveat is terminological (Ballentine's "agent of alternative order" vs. "agent of chaos"), which the book should note but which does not dislodge the case from Core status.

## 2. Baal vs. Yamm and Baal vs. Lotan (Ugaritic Baal Cycle)

### What the story tells
Six cuneiform alphabetic tablets from Ras Shamra (ancient Ugarit), conventionally KTU 1.1–1.6, narrate the storm-god Baʿlu/Haddu's rise to kingship over the gods. The first arc (KTU 1.1–1.2, "Baal and Yamm") opens with El granting kingship to Yamm ("Sea," also Nahar "River"), who demands Baal's submission; Baal, armed with two clubs (Ayamur "Driver" and Yagrush "Chaser") fashioned by the craftsman god Kothar-wa-Khasis, strikes Yamm between the eyes and slays him. Anat and Athirat then secure permission from El to build Baal a palace on Mount Ṣapānu (KTU 1.3–1.4). The second arc (KTU 1.5–1.6) tells of Baal's death at the hands of Mot ("Death"), descent and revival, and return. Within this cycle, the separate Lôtan / Litan passage (KTU 1.5 i 1–3) retrospectively celebrates Baal's (or Anat's) defeat of the seven-headed writhing serpent Lôtan (Hebrew Leviathan). The cycle establishes divine kingship, Baal's enthronement on Ṣapānu, and the seasonal / meteorological order; it is *not* a cosmogony in the *Enūma Eliš* sense.

### Primary textual attestation
- Source: Ugaritic (Northwest Semitic), alphabetic cuneiform, Late Bronze Age (c. 1350–1200 BCE). The six main tablets are damaged but the plot is reconstructable in outline; the Lôtan passage is short but unambiguous.
- Editions / translations to rely on: Mark S. Smith, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume I: KTU 1.1–1.2* (Brill, 1994) — Baal-Yamm arc; Mark S. Smith & Wayne T. Pitard, *The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, Volume II: KTU 1.3–1.4* (Brill, 2009) — palace-building; Dennis Pardee's translations in Hallo's *Context of Scripture* (Brill, 1997); N. Wyatt, *Religious Texts from Ugarit* (Sheffield Academic, 2nd ed. 2002); older but still useful: J. C. L. Gibson, *Canaanite Myths and Legends* (T&T Clark, 1978).

### Scholarly consensus on Chaoskampf status
Specialists treat Baal-Yamm and Baal-Lôtan as a clear case of the combat-myth pattern, but with an important qualification: the text is a *kingship* narrative, not a creation narrative. Smith (*The Origins of Biblical Monotheism*, OUP, 2001; *The Early History of God*, 2nd ed. 2002) and Smith/Pitard (2009) emphasize that Baal establishes order and rule rather than creating the cosmos — the gods, sea, and earth already exist. Day (1985) still reads the narrative as the Canaanite template underlying the Hebrew Bible's dragon-and-sea imagery (Pss 74, 89, 93, 104; Isa 27:1; Job 26). Forsyth (1987) and Wakeman (1973) both include it in the core combat-myth genealogy. Ballentine (2015) uses it as a prime example of her revisionist thesis: Yamm is "an agent of an alternative divine power structure" legitimated by El, not a chaos monster, and his defeat is a *political* restructuring of divine hierarchy. This reading is increasingly influential. The Lôtan/Litan passage is separately relevant because its phraseology ("smote Litan the wriggling serpent, finished off the writhing serpent, Encircler-with-seven-heads") is the closest Semitic parallel to Isa 27:1 and Ps 74:14 and the direct background of Leviathan.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Core comparandum in every major comparative treatment: Gunkel's thesis needed the Ugaritic discoveries (Ras Shamra, from 1929) to stand on solid ground; Day (1985), Wakeman (1973), Forsyth (1987), and Ballentine (2015) all use it as the Northwest Semitic anchor. West (*East Face of Helicon*, 1997) links it to Zeus's Typhon-combat via Mount Casius/Ṣapānu. Smith's *Poetic Heroes* (Eerdmans, 2014) treats it in comparative Mediterranean context.

### Verdict
- **Tier: Core.**
- **Confidence: HIGH.**
- Rationale: The textual attestation is excellent, the combat structure is unambiguous, and the tradition is central to comparative work. Flag explicitly that this is a *kingship / order-establishing* combat rather than a cosmogony — and that Ballentine (2015) pushes back on the word "chaos" itself. Both points strengthen rather than weaken the case by forcing the book to articulate what kind of "order-making" Chaoskampf actually does.

## 3. Zeus vs. Typhon (Greek; Hesiod, Apollodorus, Nonnus)

### What the story tells
In Hesiod's *Theogony* 820–880 (c. 700 BCE), after Zeus has defeated the Titans and established Olympian order, Gaia lies with Tartaros and bears Typhoeus — a monstrous last-born challenger with a hundred snake heads, fiery eyes, and a polyphonic voice. Typhoeus would have ruled gods and mortals had Zeus not struck first; Zeus blasts him with thunderbolts, sets him on fire, and hurls him into Tartaros, whence he is the source of storm winds. Apollodorus, *Bibliotheca* 1.6.3 (1st–2nd c. CE, drawing on older mythography) adds: Zeus pursues Typhon to Mount Kasion (Mount Casius, North Syria, i.e., Jebel al-Aqra / Ugaritic Ṣapānu); Typhon wrests Zeus's adamantine sickle, severs his sinews, and hides them in the Corycian cave in Cilicia, guarded by the she-dragon Delphyne; Hermes and Aigipan recover them; Zeus pursues Typhon through Thrace and finally buries him under Mount Etna. Nonnus, *Dionysiaca* 1–2 (5th c. CE) elaborates this into a long Typhonomachy in which Typhon steals Zeus's thunderbolts during his dalliance with Pluto, and is tricked by Cadmus's pipe-playing into relinquishing them. The myth secures Olympian cosmic kingship after the Titanomachy and, in later versions, assimilates to cosmic-order and weather-formation etiologies.

### Primary textual attestation
- Source: Greek, multiple periods. Hesiod *Theogony* 820–880 (c. 700 BCE); Homeric *Iliad* 2.780–783 (passing reference to Typhoeus in Arima); Pindar *Pythian* 1.15–28 and *Olympian* 4 (5th c. BCE); Aeschylus *Prometheus Bound* 351–372; Apollodorus *Bibliotheca* 1.6.3 (compiled 1st–2nd c. CE); Nonnus *Dionysiaca* 1.154–2.712 (5th c. CE); Oppian, Strabo 16.2.7 (Corycian cave), Antoninus Liberalis.
- Editions / translations to rely on: M. L. West, *Hesiod: Theogony* (OUP, 1966), critical edition with commentary; Glenn Most, *Hesiod* (Loeb, 2006); J. G. Frazer, *Apollodorus* (Loeb, 1921) still standard; W. H. D. Rouse, *Nonnos Dionysiaca* (Loeb, 1940); modern treatment in Daniel Ogden, *Drakōn: Dragon Myth and Serpent Cult in the Greek and Roman Worlds* (OUP, 2013), and Ogden, *Dragons, Serpents, and Slayers in the Classical and Early Christian Worlds* (OUP, 2013).

### Scholarly consensus on Chaoskampf status
Specialists treat the Typhonomachy as the Greek flagship of the combat myth. West (*East Face of Helicon*, OUP, 1997; *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, OUP, 2007) argues extensively that it is a Hellenic transformation of a Near Eastern inheritance: the Hurro-Hittite Kumarbi Cycle (Song of Ullikummi, Song of Hedammu) and the Baal-Yamm narrative, geographically mediated by Mount Kasion/Ṣapānu. W. Burkert (*The Orientalizing Revolution*, Harvard, 1992) makes the same argument in condensed form. Watkins (1995) treats Typhon as a Greek exemplar of the Indo-European *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula, noting Hesiod's diction. Fontenrose (*Python*, UC Press, 1959) treated Typhon and Python as variants of a single underlying Combat Myth, with Typhon the more cosmic and Python the more local form. Ogden (*Drakōn*, 2013) classifies Typhon as a "composite" anguiform, distinct from "pure" drakontes like Python, but both as combat-myth adversaries. Forsyth (1987) and Lincoln (*Theorizing Myth*, 1999) read it as succession-myth and sovereignty narrative. Dissent is minimal: some (e.g., J. Strauss Clay, *Hesiod's Cosmos*, Cambridge UP, 2003) emphasize internal Hesiodic narrative logic over diffusion, but do not deny the combat-myth structure.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Central in every comparative treatment. Gunkel included it; West (1997, 2007) makes it the hinge of Greek-Near-Eastern comparison; Burkert (1992) treats it as orientalizing; Watkins (1995) gives it detailed IE-formulaic analysis; Fontenrose (1959) built an entire comparative framework around it; Ogden (2013) provides the current survey of Greco-Roman material; Forsyth (1987) places it in the genealogy leading to apocalyptic combat. Lincoln's *Gods and Demons* (2012) treats Typhon in political-ideological terms.

### Verdict
- **Tier: Core.**
- **Confidence: HIGH.**
- Rationale: Extensive ancient attestation across seven-plus authors spanning 1200 years, unambiguous combat-and-sovereignty structure, documented Near Eastern genealogy (Kumarbi, Baal, Tiamat), and universal inclusion in comparative scholarship. Canonical Greek exemplar.

## 4. Tarḫunna / Teshub vs. Illuyanka (Hittite)

### What the story tells
The Illuyanka myth, CTH 321, is preserved on a group of Hittite tablets including KBo 3.7, KUB 17.5, KUB 17.6, and related fragments. The text itself frames the tale as the *mukessar* ("invocation") recited at the spring *purulli* festival. Two consecutive, divergent versions are transmitted on the same tablet group, attributed to a priest named Kella of the storm-god of Nerik.

Version 1: The storm-god is defeated by the serpent Illuyanka at a first encounter in the city Kiskilussa. The goddess Inara prepares a banquet, enlists the mortal Hupasiya (who demands she sleep with him in payment), and lures Illuyanka and his offspring out of their hole; they gorge themselves, cannot return, and Hupasiya binds the dragon with rope so that the storm-god can kill him. Inara then settles Hupasiya in a house with a taboo against looking out the window, which he eventually violates.

Version 2: The serpent defeats the storm-god and takes his heart and eyes. The storm-god marries the daughter of a poor man and fathers a son; the son marries Illuyanka's daughter and, on his father's instruction, asks the serpent's household for the heart and eyes as a bride-price. They are returned, the storm-god recovers his strength, kills Illuyanka, and also kills his own son, who loyally asked to be killed along with the serpent-household to whom he now belonged.

The framing explicitly identifies the narrative as an etiology for the *purulli* festival, a spring / New Year-type celebration tied to fertility and royal renewal.

### Primary textual attestation
- Source: Hittite cuneiform, Late Bronze Age (tablets Middle/New Hittite, c. 16th–13th c. BCE). Partial but extensively reconstructable.
- Editions / translations to rely on: Gary Beckman, "The Anatolian Myth of Illuyanka," *Journal of the Ancient Near Eastern Society* 14 (1982): 11–25 — the standard modern philological edition and translation; Harry A. Hoffner Jr., *Hittite Myths* (SBL Writings from the Ancient World, 2nd ed. 1998) — standard English collection with both versions; V. Haas, *Geschichte der hethitischen Religion* (Brill, 1994) and *Die hethitische Literatur* (de Gruyter, 2006); Ph. H. J. Houwink ten Cate's older but still cited philological work. Amir Gilan's more recent studies reassess ritual and performance context.

### Scholarly consensus on Chaoskampf status
Specialists treat Illuyanka as a genuine storm-god-vs-serpent combat, but with two important qualifications. First, it is not cosmogonic: there is no creation from the serpent's body, no ordering of the cosmos, and the narrative is explicitly anchored in the recurring *purulli* ritual cycle rather than in origins. Second, in both versions the storm-god cannot defeat the serpent alone — Version 1 requires Inara, Hupasiya, and trickery; Version 2 requires a half-mortal son and an exchange scheme. Beckman (1982), Hoffner (1998), Houwink ten Cate, and Haas agree on the combat-myth identification while emphasizing the *ritual-cyclical* character; newer readings (Gilan) read the text against performance and even carnivalesque inversion ("Hittite Saturnalia"). The myth was first brought into the Chaoskampf conversation by W. Porzig (1930), who drew the Typhon parallel; West (1997) and Burkert (1992) consolidated the Hittite-to-Greek transmission argument. No specialist denies that this is a dragon-combat of the storm-god; what is debated is its *function* (ritual-seasonal vs. cosmogonic) and the degree to which the trickster-helper element marks a sub-type.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Central in Indo-European and Mediterranean comparative work. Watkins (*How to Kill a Dragon*, 1995) gives a detailed analysis of the Hittite text as an exemplar of the IE *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula (note *MUŠilluyankan=kuenta*, with *kuen-* cognate to Vedic *han-* in *áhann áhim*); West (1997, 2007) treats the Kumarbi Cycle (Ullikummi, Hedammu) and Illuyanka together as the Anatolian inheritance feeding into Hesiod's Typhonomachy. Forsyth (1987) includes it in his opening genealogy. Fontenrose (1959) treats it as a combat-myth variant.

### Verdict
- **Tier: Core.**
- **Confidence: HIGH.**
- Rationale: Strong ancient textual attestation, unambiguous storm-god-vs-serpent structure, and central status in the Indo-European and Near-Eastern-to-Greek comparative chain (Watkins, West, Burkert). The book should flag the non-cosmogonic, ritual-cyclical framing and the trickster/helper sub-type, but these are *features* of this variant, not disqualifiers. The two divergent versions on the same tablet group are themselves a valuable data point on how ancient audiences were comfortable with multiple tellings of the same Chaoskampf narrative.

## 5. Apollo vs. Python (Greek; Homeric Hymn, Pausanias, Ovid)

### What the story tells
The story has several extant forms and the variants differ meaningfully. In the *Homeric Hymn to Apollo* (conventionally *Hymn* 3, usually dated late 7th / early 6th c. BCE; some sections 522 BCE), the young Apollo, seeking a site for his oracle, kills a monstrous *drakaina* (she-dragon) at Krisa / Pytho who had been ravaging the countryside and who was the foster-mother of the monster Typhaon (Hera's parthenogenetic son); Apollo shoots her with his bow, she rots on the spot, and the place is therefore named Pytho (from *pythein*, "to rot"), with Apollo taking the title Pythios. The dragon in the Hymn is unnamed, female, and linked to Typhaon. In later sources (Ephorus, Apollodorus 1.4.1, Hyginus, Ovid *Metamorphoses* 1.438–451) the serpent is male and named Python; Ovid adds that Python was spawned by the mud left after Deucalion's flood. Pausanias (*Description of Greece* 10.6.5–6, 2nd c. CE) reports competing Delphic traditions in which the oracle site had earlier belonged to Gaia, Themis, and/or Poseidon before Apollo's arrival, and the Pythian Games are aetiologised as funeral games for Python. A separate version (Plutarch *De defectu oraculorum*, Aelian) has Apollo exiled to Thessaly for shedding blood and purified at the Vale of Tempe — this is the form preserved in the *Septerion* festival at Delphi. The narrative's cosmological work is limited: it establishes the Delphic oracle and Apollo's claim to it, legitimates the Pythian Games, and explains toponyms and cult titles. It is closer to a *cult-foundation* combat than to a cosmogonic one.

### Primary textual attestation
- Source: Greek, multiple periods. *Homeric Hymn to Apollo* (Pythian section, late 7th / early 6th c. BCE); Pindar frr.; Ephorus (4th c. BCE, via Strabo 9.3.12); Apollodorus *Bibliotheca* 1.4.1; Ovid *Metamorphoses* 1.438–451; Hyginus *Fabulae* 140; Pausanias 10.6.5–6; Plutarch *De defectu oraculorum*, *De E apud Delphos*; Aelian. Iconographic evidence from archaic vase-painting onward.
- Editions / translations to rely on: Joseph Fontenrose, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins* (UC Press, 1959) — still the monograph on the myth; Andrew Faulkner (ed.), *The Homeric Hymns: Interpretative Essays* (OUP, 2011); Jenny Strauss Clay, *The Politics of Olympus* (Princeton, 1989) on the Hymn; Daniel Ogden, *Drakōn* (OUP, 2013) and *Dragons, Serpents, and Slayers* (OUP, 2013) for the current treatment across sources; Pausanias, ed. Frazer or Habicht; Frazer's or Smith's *Homeric Hymns* (Loeb).

### Scholarly consensus on Chaoskampf status
This is the genuinely contested case among the five. Specialists agree the story is a divine-combat-with-serpent narrative and that it shares the formulaic patterning of the IE dragon-slaying corpus (Watkins). But several serious qualifications weaken its Chaoskampf status in the strict sense:

1. **Non-cosmogonic.** Fontenrose (1959) himself stressed that Apollo-Python is a *cult-foundation* and oracle-transfer myth, not a cosmogony. It explains how Delphi came to be Apollo's, not how the cosmos came to be ordered.
2. **Succession motif, not primordial combat.** The serpent (with Gaia / Themis / Poseidon) represents a *prior* divine regime at Delphi. This is closer to the Olympian-supplants-Chthonic succession pattern than to primordial chaos-combat. Pausanias explicitly presents it this way.
3. **Cult-ritual function.** The *Septerion* octennial festival re-enacted Apollo's flight and purification rather than a combat-victory; W. Burkert (*Homo Necans*, 1983; *Greek Religion*, 1985) reads this as a purification / New Fire ritual whose aetiology is the Python killing.
4. **Variant diversity.** The dragon's sex, parentage, name, and role change across sources (drakaina foster-mother of Typhaon in the Hymn; male Python born of Deucalion's mud in Ovid; Typhon's associate in late sources).

Fontenrose nevertheless includes Apollo-Python in his comparative Combat Myth analysis alongside Zeus-Typhon, Marduk-Tiamat, and Baal-Yamm, and demonstrates the structural parallels in detail — he is the scholar most responsible for keeping it in the comparative corpus. Ogden (2013) treats Python as the paradigmatic "pure" drakon of Greek myth. Watkins (1995) discusses it as an IE formula reflex. Day (1985) and Forsyth (1987) mention it in passing. The current Chaoskampf catalog's own flag ("lower-confidence core") is well-founded: this is the case where specialists most often qualify the identification.

### Comparative-mythology treatment
Fontenrose (1959) is the indispensable treatment and remains the reason Apollo-Python stays in the comparative conversation. Ogden (2013) follows him but organises Python under "pure drakontes" rather than cosmic combat. Watkins (1995) cites it briefly as an IE formula reflex. West (1997) says less about it than about Typhon. Forsyth (1987), Wakeman (1973), and Day (1985) do not give it major weight.

### Verdict
- **Tier: Core (lower-confidence) — or Related, defensibly.**
- **Confidence: MEDIUM.**
- Rationale: The current draft's "lower-confidence core" label is accurate. The combat-with-serpent structure is present and ancient, and Fontenrose's comparative reading keeps Apollo-Python on the Chaoskampf map. But the story is cult-foundational and oracle-transfer rather than cosmogonic, and the serpent functions more as *prior chthonic regime* than as chaos-adversary. If the book's Core tier is meant to emphasize cosmic order-making, Apollo-Python sits awkwardly there; it fits better as a succession/cult-foundation variant. A defensible editorial choice is to demote it to Related and keep Fontenrose front and centre; an alternative is to retain it in Core but make the qualification explicit in-chapter.

## Summary catalog flags
- Marduk/Tiamat — Core, HIGH. Canonical.
- Baal/Yamm–Lotan — Core, HIGH. Flag: kingship, not cosmogony; Ballentine's terminological critique.
- Zeus/Typhon — Core, HIGH. Flag: documented Near Eastern inheritance (Kumarbi, Ṣapānu).
- Storm-god/Illuyanka — Core, HIGH. Flag: ritual-cyclical (purulli festival), non-cosmogonic, trickster-helper sub-type, two divergent versions on the same tablet.
- Apollo/Python — Core (lower-confidence) or Related, MEDIUM. The case where the Core designation is most contestable; Fontenrose is the anchor scholar but he himself frames it as cult-foundational. Consider demoting to Related.

## Uncertain / unverified items to check before citing
- Exact date of composition for the Homeric Hymn to Apollo's Pythian section (various scholars place it anywhere from late 7th c. to 522 BCE).
- Whether Houwink ten Cate produced a consolidated edition of Illuyanka or only shorter philological studies — verify before citing a specific title.
- Whether the "Ballentine 2015" argument against "chaos" language has been adopted by later Ugaritic specialists (Smith, Pardee) or only within biblical-studies circles — worth checking post-2020 Ugaritic literature for reception.
