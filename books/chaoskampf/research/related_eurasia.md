# Chaoskampf — Related Entries: Eurasia

Research notes on candidate combat-with-chaos stories from Slavic, Armenian, Finnish, early-Christian (Revelation), and Zoroastrian-eschatological traditions. Each entry is assessed for inclusion as CORE / RELATED / PROVISIONAL / EXCLUDE.

---

## 1. Perun vs. Veles (Slavic "Basic Myth")

### What the story tells
In the reconstruction proposed by Ivanov & Toporov (1974), the serpentine/chthonic god Veles — associated with water, cattle, the underworld, and the roots of the World Tree — provokes the sky/thunder god Perun by stealing his cattle, his wife, or his children, and hiding in a tree, a stone, or under the earth. Perun pursues Veles across the sky, shattering Veles' hiding places with lightning. When Perun strikes, Veles is defeated and what was stolen is released: rain falls, cattle are restored, cosmic order returns. The narrative is read as a seasonal cycle — drought = Veles ascendant; rain = Perun's victory.

Important: **the narrative above is a reconstruction, not a textually attested story.** No medieval Slavic source tells this tale as a connected narrative. The reconstruction is assembled from (a) Baltic Perkūnas–Velnias parallels, (b) Slavic folklore fragments (notably South Slavic *Perperuna*/*Dodola* rain-dance material gathered by Katičić), (c) toponymic distribution of Perun- and Veles-linked placenames, and (d) comparative Indo-European (Vedic Indra vs. Vṛtra) parallelism.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Procopius, *De Bello Gothico* (6th c.):** South Slavs worshipped "the maker of lightning" as the sole lord — not named Perun, but identified by modern scholarship as him.
- **Primary Chronicle (*Povest' vremennyh let*, 12th c.):** treaties of Oleg (907) and Igor (945) swear by Perun and Volos/Veles; Vladimir's pantheon of 980 lists Perun with a silver head and gold moustache. *Perun and Veles appear side by side as co-guarantors of oaths — not as enemies.*
- **South Slavic folk songs** (Katičić, Belaj): fragments read as mythic residue — e.g. hunts of a serpent in an oak, rain-processions for Perperuna/Dodola.
- **No medieval prose or poetic text narrates a Perun–Veles combat.** The myth is a philological-folkloristic reconstruction.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Vyacheslav Ivanov & Vladimir Toporov (1965, 1974):** proposed the "basic myth" (*osnovnoj mif*) — a thunder-god-vs-serpent structure underlying Slavic religion, parallel to Indra–Vṛtra, Þórr–Jǫrmungandr, Zeus–Typhon.
- **Roman Jakobson** (earlier, independently): read Perun–Veles as a Mitra–Varuṇa–type functional dyad (sovereignty vs. magic/oaths), **not** as combatants. Jakobson identified Veles with Varuṇa, god of oaths — consistent with Veles' appearance alongside Perun in oath-ratification rather than opposed to him.
- **Aleksander Gieysztor (1982, *Mitologia Słowian*):** published the first sustained critique, arguing the combat structure is not supported by the Slavic evidence itself.
- **Leszek Moszyński:** sceptical of the whole "basic myth" program on philological grounds — challenged the etymological moves that underpin the reconstruction.
- **Anatoly Liberman:** has criticized the over-reliance on Indo-European comparativism at the expense of attested Slavic material.
- **Patrice Lajoye (*Perun, dieu slave de l'orage*, 2015; *New Researches on the Religion and Mythology of the Pagan Slavs*, 2019/2023):** distances himself from the Ivanov–Toporov reconstruction; treats Perun principally as a storm-god attested by toponymy and oath-formulae, not as the hero of a reconstructible combat myth. The serpent-slaying motif in Russian folklore/bylinas (Dobrynya and the Zmey) is analysed as a *later* Christianized hagiographic pattern rather than direct continuation of a pagan combat myth.
- **Mate Kapović** (Croatian Indo-Europeanist, Slavist): has expressed strong scepticism about Ivanov–Toporov's methods; argues that the reconstructed "formula" *Perun smites Veles* has **no adduced textual evidence** — it is a conjecture dressed as a recovery.
- **Toporov himself later revised his position**, acknowledging that Veles is closer to Varuṇa than to Vṛtra, which effectively dismantles the original chaoskampf framing.

**Current Slavistic consensus:** the Ivanov–Toporov combat reconstruction is no longer mainstream. Most current scholars either (a) treat Perun and Veles as complementary functions in a Dumézilian/Jakobsonian dyad, or (b) reject the reconstruction outright as speculative. The serpent-slayer motif does exist in Slavic folk epic (e.g. Dobrynya Nikitich), but is best understood as a post-Christian hagiographic layer (St George, Theodore Tiron) interacting with older Indo-European residue, not as direct evidence of a pagan Perun–Veles combat.

### Verdict
- **PROVISIONAL** — leaning **EXCLUDE from CORE**, **acceptable as RELATED** if framed carefully.
- **Confidence: MEDIUM**
- Rationale: The story is genuinely Indo-European-patterned and genuinely interesting for a chaoskampf book, but it is a **reconstruction**, not an attested myth. Including it as CORE would misrepresent the evidentiary situation. If included, it must be flagged as reconstructed and the Ivanov–Toporov hypothesis must be presented alongside the Jakobson/Gieysztor/Lajoye/Kapović objections. The honest placement is RELATED with an explicit methodological caveat.

## 2. Vahagn vs. the Vishaps (Armenian)

### What the story tells
Vahagn — epithet *Višapak'aġ* ("Dragon-Reaper" / "Reaper of Vishaps") — is the warrior-storm-fire god of pre-Christian Armenia who hunts and slaughters the *vishaps*, the dragon-serpents of Armenian cosmology. The surviving fragment (recorded by Movses Khorenatsi) is the famous birth-hymn: heaven and earth were in labour, and the "purple sea" was in labour; from the sea came forth a red reed, smoke rose from the reed, flame rose from the reed, and out of the flame ran forth a youth — fiery-haired, fire-bearded, with eyes like two suns. Khorenatsi then tells us the rest of the song recounted how Vahagn fought and defeated *vishaps* — but **the dragon-fighting verses are lost**. What survives textually is the cosmogonic birth; what survives in living tradition are (a) the epithet *Vishapakagh*, (b) folktales of Vahagn stealing straw from the storm-giant Baršamin (the Milky Way = "Straw Thief's Road"), (c) the Armenian folk-hero Tork' Angełea and related dragon-slayers, and (d) the material culture of *vishapakar* ("dragon-stones").

### Primary textual attestation
- **Movses Khorenatsi, *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* ("History of the Armenians"), Book I, ch. 31 (I.xxxi):** the Vahagn birth-hymn, presented by Khorenatsi as an ancient Armenian cultic song sung by bards. Khorenatsi's dating is contested — traditionally 5th c., but revisionist scholarship (Robert Thomson, and earlier Cyril Toumanoff) places him as late as 8th–9th c. Either way the hymn preserves pre-Christian material.
- **Agathangelos, *History of the Armenians* (5th c.):** records Vahagn's cult centre at Aštišat with images and sacrifices; Gregory the Illuminator's conversion narrative involves dismantling Vahagn's shrines. This attests cult, not narrative.
- ***Vishapakar* stelae:** 150+ monoliths (Mt. Aragats, Gegham range, Lake Sevan, Lake Van region, Trialeti), carved as fish or bovine-hide forms, sited at high-altitude springs and prehistoric irrigation channels. New radiocarbon dating (Tirinkatar, 2024–2025) places installation c. 4200–4000 BCE — older than the historically attested Vahagn cult, pointing to a deep water-serpent-cult substrate that the later Vahagn myth appropriated.
- **Folk tradition** (collected 19th–20th c.): the Milky Way as *Yardgoghi ch'anaparh* ("Straw-Thief's Road"), where Vahagn steals straw from the dragon/giant — cognate with the Vedic Indra–Vṛtra "cattle raid" structure.
- **Textual security:** the *surviving* hymn fragment is cosmogonic-theogonic, not combat narrative. The combat is attested **by title and by Khorenatsi's paraphrase**, not by any preserved epic text.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **James R. Russell (*Zoroastrianism in Armenia*, 1987, Harvard Iranian Series):** situates Vahagn as the Armenian reflex of Iranian Verethragna (Vərəθraγna, "smiter of obstruction"), but emphasizes that the Armenian Vahagn has absorbed distinctively Armenian features — notably the dragon-slaying role, which the Avestan Verethragna does not clearly have. Russell treats the Aštišat Vahagn cult as a syncretism: Iranian name + divine epithets, pre-Iranian (Urartian/Indigenous) storm-and-dragon substrate. His *Poets, Heroes, and their Dragons* (Brill, 2020, 2 vols) is the most recent extended treatment.
- **Georges Dumézil:** noted that Vahagn looks closer to Vedic Vṛtrahan-Indra (a serpent-slayer) than to Avestan Verethragna (who is not primarily a dragon-slayer) — suggesting Vahagn preserves an older Indo-Iranian warrior-dragon-slayer type that Zoroastrianism had partly demoted in Iran proper.
- **Armen Petrosyan** (*The Indo-European and Ancient Near Eastern Sources of the Armenian Epic*, 2002; "Indo-European \**wel-* in Armenian Mythology," *JIES* 44, 2016): argues Vahagn is a native Armenian storm/dragon-slayer reformulated with an Iranian name, not a straightforward Iranian import. Places Vahagn within the Indo-European \**wel-* complex (the serpent's name/root that underlies Vala, Vṛtra, Vel-nias, Vélns, and possibly *Veles*) — i.e. Vahagn is the Armenian cognate of the IE dragon-slaying thunder-god combat myth. For Petrosyan, this *is* a chaoskampf, and it is genuinely Armenian, not merely borrowed.
- **Martin West (*Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, 2007):** cites the Vahagn hymn as one of the clearest IE thunder-dragon-slayer survivals, alongside Indra–Vṛtra, Þórr–Jǫrmungandr, and Zeus–Typhon.

**Current consensus:** Vahagn is widely accepted as a genuine Armenian dragon-slayer, and his combat with the vishaps is accepted as a real mythologem — with the important qualification that the narrative text itself is *lost*. What we have is the epithet, cult, comparative reconstruction, and folkloric residue. There is no dispute analogous to the Perun/Veles debate — the Vahagn dragon-combat is not a modern reconstruction but an ancient tradition reported by Khorenatsi.

### Verdict
- **RELATED** (strong candidate; could be argued as CORE but the *narrative* is fragmentary)
- **Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH**
- Rationale: The existence of a Vahagn-vs-vishap combat tradition is securely attested (epithet, Khorenatsi's paraphrase, cult, folktale). What we *lack* is the actual text of the combat. This makes Vahagn weaker than Indra–Vṛtra or Marduk–Tiamat for textual discussion, but stronger than Perun–Veles for attestation. Best treated as RELATED, with the vishapakar archaeology as a striking material anchor showing the depth of the Armenian serpent-cult the myth presupposes.

## 3. Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso (Finnish, *Kalevala*)

### What the story tells
Iku-Turso ("Eternal Turso"; also Meritursas "Sea-Turso," Tursas) is a malevolent sea-monster of Finnic tradition. In the Kalevala, he appears chiefly in two episodes:

- **Runo II (Canto 2):** Iku-Turso rises from the sea and sets fire to a stack of hay; from the ashes sprouts the cosmic giant oak that blocks sun and moon until felled — a cosmogonic sequence. No direct combat with Väinämöinen here; Iku-Turso is a disruptive primordial water-creature.
- **Runo XLII (Canto 42):** During the Sampo-theft expedition against Pohjola, Louhi (Mistress of the North) summons Iku-Turso to stop the returning heroes. Väinämöinen seizes the sea-monster by the ears and uses magical incantation (not a weapon) to compel him to swear an oath never again to rise against mankind. Iku-Turso sinks and is never seen again.

The more famous combat of Runo XLIII is not against Iku-Turso but against Louhi herself transformed into a giant eagle, who snatches at the Sampo until it is shattered into the sea. So "Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso" is a **sub-episode of the Sampo cycle**, and the combat is resolved by **song/oath** rather than by physical slaying.

Other Väinämöinen "combats" worth flagging: the singing-duel with Joukahainen (Runo III) — a duel of magic song, the archetype of Finnic *tietäjä* (seer-shaman) combat; and the monstrous pike he kills en route, from whose jawbone the first kantele (zither) is made.

### Primary textual attestation
- **Elias Lönnrot, *Kalevala*:** Old Kalevala 1835 (12,078 verses); New Kalevala 1849 (22,795 verses, 50 runos). Lönnrot compiled the epic from field-collected Karelian, Ingrian, and Finnish oral runes, but *selected, ordered, harmonized, and occasionally composed* connective passages. Estimates put Lönnrot's own composition at only ~3% of the text, but his editorial arrangement is substantial: the narrative arc (including the Sampo quest sequence and its monster-encounters) is Lönnrot's synthesis, not a pre-existing unified epic.
- **SKVR (*Suomen Kansan Vanhat Runot*):** the 34-volume corpus (publ. 1908–1997) of the actual collected runes. Iku-Turso / Tursas appears in multiple regional variants but **not as a narrative combat opponent of Väinämöinen.** The figure is more commonly an invoked sea-monster in incantations (*loitsut*) and etiological rune fragments about cosmic fire/oak.
- **Textual security:** the Kalevala text is stable and a literary monument; the **underlying folk tradition** for the specific Väinämöinen–Iku-Turso encounter is thin. Lönnrot wove it together from scattered lyric and incantation fragments.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Anna-Leena Siikala (*Mythic Images and Shamanism*, FF Communications 280, 2002; *Itämerensuomalaisten mytologia*, 2012 / Eng. *Mythologies of the Finnic Peoples*, 2019):** treats Väinämöinen primarily as a *tietäjä*-shaman archetype whose power is **song**, not weaponry. His confrontations are *verbal-magical*, not martial. Siikala explicitly resists reading Finnic hero-combat episodes as Indo-European-style dragon-slaying; the underlying logic is shamanic (binding, sinking, compelling oaths), not cosmogonic combat. Iku-Turso is best read as a disruptive water-spirit the shaman-hero subdues, not a Chaos-dragon to be slain.
- **Lauri Honko:** emphasized the *genre hybridity* of the Kalevala — Lönnrot fused incantations (*loitsut*), lyric laments, and narrative epic into a constructed whole. Reading a unified "Chaoskampf" out of the Kalevala risks mistaking Lönnrot's editorial scaffolding for an ancient myth.
- **Matti Kuusi (*Finnish Folk Poetry: Epic*, 1977):** produced the critical anthology separating *actual* rune material from Lönnrot's shaping. Kuusi stressed that categories like "the people of Kalevala" were Lönnrot's invention, and that the epic heroes had multiple, often contradictory regional identities. For the Sampo cycle, Kuusi traces core elements to genuine Karelian song-tradition, but the specific Iku-Turso subduing is a weaker textual attestation.
- **Martin West (*Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, 2007):** includes some Finnic material as marginal to IE chaoskampf, but Finnic languages are *non-IE* (Uralic); parallels therefore signal either areal contact (with Baltic/Germanic) or convergent mythopoeic patterning, not genetic IE inheritance.
- **Juha Pentikäinen:** treats Väinämöinen's encounters as shamanic seances rather than heroic combats. Väinämöinen "wins" by superior knowledge (singing the opponent into the earth, into the sea, into binding oaths) — a pattern distinct from the IE dragon-slayer where the opponent is physically destroyed.

**Current Finnish folkloristic consensus:** the Väinämöinen–Iku-Turso encounter is **not** treated as a chaoskampf. It is treated as a shamanic binding-incantation episode, heavily shaped by Lönnrot's narrative scaffolding, drawing on Finnic incantation tradition where sea-monsters are *bound and sent back*, not slain. The Sampo-cycle *as a whole* has some mythic-cosmic resonance (cosmic mill, pursuit, shattering) but its central adversary is Louhi-as-eagle in Runo 43, not Iku-Turso, and the cycle resists neat chaoskampf analysis.

### Verdict
- **EXCLUDE** (or **PROVISIONAL** at most — only if a book explicitly wants to discuss Lönnrot's 19th-century literary-chaoskampf *reception*)
- **Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH**
- Rationale: The Iku-Turso encounter does not function as a chaoskampf in the underlying folk tradition — it is a shamanic binding within a compiled literary epic. Treating it as cognate to Indra–Vṛtra or Marduk–Tiamat would misrepresent Finnish folkloristics and would elevate Lönnrot's 1849 editorial choices to the status of ancient myth. If the book wants a Finnic presence, the more honest move is a short note acknowledging Finnish mythology's *different grammar of heroic conflict* (shamanic song-combat, not dragon-slaying) rather than forcing Väinämöinen–Iku-Turso into a chaoskampf slot.

## 4. The Red Dragon of Revelation 12 (New Testament Apocalypse of John)

### What the story tells
Revelation 12 opens with a "great sign" in heaven: a woman clothed with the sun, the moon under her feet, crowned with twelve stars, crying in labour (12:1-2). A second sign appears — a great fiery-red dragon with seven heads, ten horns, and seven diadems, whose tail sweeps a third of the stars from heaven (12:3-4). The dragon stands before the woman to devour her child at birth; the child — "who is to rule all the nations with an iron rod" — is caught up to God (12:5). The woman flees to the wilderness, where she is nourished 1,260 days (12:6). Then war breaks out in heaven: Michael and his angels fight the dragon and his angels; the dragon is defeated and cast down to earth — "that ancient serpent, who is called the Devil and Satan, the deceiver of the whole world" (12:7-9). A victory hymn celebrates the casting down (12:10-12). The dragon then pursues the woman, who is given eagle's wings; he vomits a river after her, but the earth swallows the flood; frustrated, the dragon turns to make war on "the rest of her offspring" — those who keep God's commandments and hold the testimony of Jesus (12:13-17).

The combat proper between Michael and the dragon (12:7-9) is the reflex most commentators pick out as *the* chaoskampf moment. The satanic identification in 12:9 ("that ancient serpent") is a deliberate allusive tissue binding together Genesis 3, Isaiah 27:1 (Leviathan the twisting serpent), and Daniel 7 (the beast-from-the-sea complex).

### Primary textual attestation
- **Revelation (Apocalypse of John), ch. 12:** securely part of the New Testament canon; Greek text preserved in 𝔓⁴⁷, Codex Sinaiticus (א), Alexandrinus (A), Ephraemi Rescriptus (C), and a full apparatus. Text-critical situation for ch. 12 is stable.
- **Dating:** scholarly consensus places composition c. 95 CE (Domitianic persecution, per Irenaeus, *Adv. Haer.* 5.30.3) — though a minority (e.g. Kenneth Gentry) argues for pre-70 CE (Neronian) dating.
- **Language:** Koine Greek, heavily Semitizing, saturated with Hebrew-Bible intertextuality (Genesis 3, Daniel 7, Isaiah 27, Psalm 74, Ezekiel 32).

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Adela Yarbro Collins, *The Combat Myth in the Book of Revelation* (HDR 9, 1976; reissued 2001):** the foundational modern treatment. Collins argues Rev 12 is a **hybrid of Near Eastern and Greco-Roman combat-myth patterns**, not a single source. The Leto–Python–Apollo myth (Hyginus, *Fab.* 140; Lucan, Plutarch) supplies the woman-in-labour-pursued-by-dragon pattern; the Egyptian Isis–Set–Horus myth supplies the persecuted-mother-divine-child pattern; and the Jewish chaoskampf inheritance (Leviathan, Rahab, Ps 74, Isa 27) supplies the cosmic ancient-serpent identification. Collins treats Rev 12 unambiguously as a **New Testament reinscription of the combat myth**.
- **David E. Aune, *Revelation* (Word Biblical Commentary, 3 vols., 1997–1998; vol. 2 covers ch. 6-16):** the standard scholarly commentary. Aune catalogues parallels exhaustively (Leto/Python; Isis/Horus/Seth-Typhon; Babylonian Marduk/Tiamat; Ugaritic Baal/Yamm/Lotan; Hebrew Leviathan/Rahab). Aune treats 12:7-9 as deploying the "legendary narrative pattern of combat between hero and adversary" and explicitly links the "ancient serpent" to the Hebrew Bible Leviathan/chaos-dragon tradition. Aune's position: Rev 12 is a self-consciously composed combat myth using a syncretic repertoire.
- **Gregory K. Beale, *The Book of Revelation* (NIGTC, 1999):** emphasizes the Daniel 7 / Old Testament background above pagan parallels; reads the dragon primarily as the Old Testament chaos-opponent eschatologized. Compatible with, but less comparativist than, Collins/Aune.
- **Bruce Malina, *On the Genre and Message of Revelation: Star Visions and Sky Journeys* (1995) and *Social-Science Commentary on the Book of Revelation* (with Pilch, 2000):** reads Rev 12 as astral/astrological — the woman = Virgo, the dragon = Hydra/Draco, the episode reflects observational astronomy of c. 96 CE rather than a pure literary appropriation of combat-myth. Malina's reading is minority and has been critiqued, but it is a significant alternative frame.
- **John Day, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea: Echoes of a Canaanite Myth in the Old Testament* (UCOP 35, CUP 1985; reissued Wipf & Stock 2021):** the landmark study of the Hebrew Bible Leviathan/Rahab tradition. Day demonstrates the Canaanite (Ugaritic Baal–Yamm/Lotan) substrate behind biblical passages (Isa 27:1, Job 26:12-13, Ps 74:13-14, Ps 89:10-11) and explicitly traces the **"eschatologization of the divine conflict with the dragon"** into Daniel and then into Revelation 12. For Day, Rev 12 is the direct descendant of the Hebrew Bible chaoskampf, which is itself the direct descendant of the Canaanite combat myth.
- **Richard Bauckham, *The Climax of Prophecy* (1993):** similar tradition-history framework; the ancient-serpent identification in 12:9 deliberately telescopes Gen 3, Isa 27, and Canaanite mythology.

**Consensus:** Rev 12 is *the* most unambiguously accepted NT chaoskampf reflex. Virtually every major modern commentary treats it as a deliberate redeployment of the ancient Near Eastern / Greco-Roman combat myth repertoire, Christianized and eschatologized. **Rev 12 is the NT endpoint of the same textual lineage that runs through Ugaritic Baal–Yamm, Hebrew Bible Leviathan/Rahab, and intertestamental Daniel 7.**

### Coordination note — YHWH vs. Leviathan/Rahab
If the book adds **YHWH vs. Leviathan/Rahab** as a separate CORE entry (recommended), Rev 12 should be cross-referenced as its **eschatological New Testament reflex**. The continuity is explicit in the text (12:9 "ancient serpent") and is the scholarly mainstream (Day, Collins, Aune). Treat them as bookends of the same canonical trajectory.

### Verdict
- **CORE**
- **Confidence: HIGH**
- Rationale: Rev 12 is textually secure, scholarly consensus reads it as a combat myth, and it supplies the most canonically explicit Christianization of the ancient Near Eastern dragon-slaying pattern. Excluding it would leave a chaoskampf survey missing its most influential post-biblical reflex. Should be paired with a Hebrew Bible Leviathan/Rahab chapter as the closing NT bracket of that lineage.

## 5. Frashokereti — the Zoroastrian Eschatological Renovation and the Final Defeat of Aži Dahāka / Angra Mainyu

### What the story tells
Zoroastrian cosmic time is tripartite: *Bundahišn* ("creation"), *Gumēzišn* ("mixture" — the present age of struggle), and *Wizārišn* ("separation" / end-time). *Frašōkərəti* (Middle Persian *Frašegird*, "making wonderful / renewal") is the final event of the third age: the purification and restoration of the cosmos to its primordial perfection.

The eschatological sequence (fully elaborated in the *Greater Bundahišn*, chs. 30 and 34; partly alluded to in the Avesta — *Yašt* 19 *Zamyād*, *Yasna* 30, 45, 48 — and dramatized in the apocalyptic *Zand ī Wahman Yasn*):

- **Final age of crisis:** social and cosmic collapse; the sun and moon darken; winter covers the world.
- **Aži Dahāka breaks loose:** the three-mouthed, three-headed dragon, bound by Thraētaona (Av. Θraētaona; MP Frēdōn/Feridun) to Mt. Damāvand at the end of his primordial defeat (*Yašt* 5.33-35; *Yašt* 19.92; *Yasna* 9.7-8; *Šāhnāma* rendering), tears free from his chains (*Bundahišn* 29.9; *Zand ī Wahman Yasn* 9). He devours a third of humanity and livestock.
- **Kərəsāspa (MP Kirsāsp / Garshāsp) revives:** the epic dragon-slayer hero — already slayer of the horned dragon Sruvara, of Gandarəβa, and of other monsters — is roused from suspended sleep and, wielding his mace, **slays Aži Dahāka** in the final battle (*Bundahišn* 29.9; *Dēnkard* 7.10; *Zand ī Wahman Yasn* 9.14-23).
- **Saošyant (Av. *Saoš́yant*, "the one who will bring benefit"; the final Astvat-ərəta, "who embodies righteousness") rises** — born miraculously from a virgin (Ēredat-fəδrī) bathing in Lake Kąsaoiia, impregnated by Zarathustra's preserved seed. He leads the resurrection of the dead (*tan ī pasēn*, the "future body") over 57 years (*Bundahišn* 34).
- **Ordeal of molten metal:** Airyaman and Ātar melt the mountains into a river of molten metal; the righteous pass through as warm milk, the wicked are purged (*Bundahišn* 34.18-19).
- **Final defeat of Angra Mainyu:** the molten river flows into Hell, destroying Angra Mainyu and the last remnant of evil. The world is restored to perfect, static goodness (*Bundahišn* 34.27-32).

### Primary textual attestation
- **Avesta (earliest stratum):** the Gāθic (Old Avestan) material hints at eschatological renewal (*Yasna* 30.9 *frašōtəməm*, 34.15, 46.19); the Young Avestan *Yašt* 19 (*Zamyād Yašt*) is the most detailed Avestan treatment of the future renewal and the coming of Astvat-ərəta. Aži Dahāka's primordial binding is in *Yašt* 5.29-35, *Yašt* 19.92, *Yasna* 9.7-8.
- **Pahlavi / Middle Persian sources (Sasanian to early Islamic, 6th–10th c.):**
  - **Bundahišn** (Greater Indian Bundahišn, chs. 30 + 34): the canonical eschatological narrative.
  - **Dēnkard**, book 7 ch. 10, book 9: eschatological and hero-cycle material.
  - **Zand ī Wahman Yasn** ("Commentary on the Bahman Yašt"): late Sasanian / early Islamic apocalypse, ed. Carlo Cereti 1995.
  - **Dādestān ī Dēnīg**, **Pahlavi Rivayat**: supplementary eschatological material.
- **Textual security:** the composite is late Sasanian / early Islamic redaction of older Avestan substrate. Mary Boyce and Prods Skjærvø concur that the core eschatological scenario is genuinely pre-Islamic, though final literary form post-dates the conquest. The *Avestan* evidence for the full scenario is sparse; the bulk of the narrative is Pahlavi.

### Scholarly consensus on chaoskampf status
- **Mary Boyce (*A History of Zoroastrianism*, 3 vols., 1975–1991; *Zoroastrians: Their Religious Beliefs and Practices*, 1979):** treats Frashokereti as the cosmic-eschatological *culmination* of the Gumēzišn struggle — a **renovation** framed around a **final combat**, but dominantly a restoration. Boyce emphasizes the likely Zoroastrian priority for apocalyptic and messianic motifs (virgin-born saviour, resurrection, ordeal, final judgment) and their influence on Second Temple Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.
- **Prods Oktor Skjærvø (*The Spirit of Zoroastrianism*, 2011; editions of Zoroastrian texts):** stresses the Gumēzišn / Wizārišn structure; treats Frashokereti as fundamentally a **cosmic restoration**, within which the Aži Dahāka / Kərəsāspa combat is a *sub-episode* — not the cosmogonic centrepiece but a narrative-climactic element inherited from the older hero-cycle.
- **Almut Hintze (*Zamyād Yašt*, 1994 critical edition; Yasna Haptaŋhāiti, 2007):** has done the most precise philological work on *Yašt* 19, the central Avestan source. Hintze reads the Zamyād Yašt's portrayal of future-renewal as connecting the *xᵛarənah* (divine fortune) to the coming Saošyant — the combat with Aži Dahāka is integral but embedded in a larger glory-restoration frame.
- **Touraj Daryaee ("Apocalypse Now: Zoroastrian Reflections on the Early Islamic Centuries," 1998; *Sasanian Persia*, 2009):** reads the late apocalyptic texts (especially *Zand ī Wahman Yasn*) as shaped by post-Sasanian historical trauma (Arab conquest, loss of empire) — arguing that the *literary form* of the Zoroastrian apocalypse is late, even where the underlying mythologem is older. Flags the methodological problem of separating pre-Islamic myth from early-Islamic-era framing.
- **Anders Hultgård** (important for Zoroastrian apocalyptic, e.g. "Persian Apocalypticism," *The Encyclopedia of Apocalypticism* vol. 1, 1998): treats Zoroastrianism as a, arguably *the*, formative source of Jewish and Christian apocalyptic eschatology.

**Current consensus:** Frashokereti is best characterized as a **cosmic-renovation framework** that **contains** a final combat episode (Kərəsāspa vs. Aži Dahāka, Saošyant vs. the forces of Angra Mainyu). The *center of gravity* is restoration, resurrection, purification — not dragon-slaying. The dragon-slaying is the *eschatological closing-bracket* of the Thraētaona / Aži Dahāka combat that began in the primordial age: Thraētaona binds Aži Dahāka at the mythic beginning; Kərəsāspa slays him at the mythic end. The serpent's story is thus a *frame* around cosmic history.

### Coordination note — Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka
If the book adds **Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka** as a separate CORE entry (strongly recommended — it is one of the best-attested IE dragon-slaying myths, with Avestan anchors in *Yašt* 5.33-35, *Yašt* 19.92, *Yasna* 9.7-8), then Frashokereti should be treated as the **eschatological reflex** of that same serpent-myth. The two form a mythic inclusio: Aži Dahāka bound (primordial) → Aži Dahāka released (eschatological crisis) → Aži Dahāka slain (Kərəsāspa, at Frashokereti). This is one of the most structurally satisfying Chaos-dragon narrative arcs in any tradition — arguably the fullest version of "chaos bound at the beginning, released at the end, destroyed in the renewal."

### Verdict
- **RELATED** (with the caveat that the *Thraētaona–Aži Dahāka* myth proper is CORE)
- **Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH**

As a standalone entry it is a RELATED survey of how the combat myth is eschatologized in Zoroastrian thought. As a *closing bracket* to the Thraētaona–Aži Dahāka CORE entry, it is essential. The honest placement: cover Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka as CORE; cover Frashokereti as the eschatological pendant in either a short RELATED section or as the final part of the Thraētaona entry.
