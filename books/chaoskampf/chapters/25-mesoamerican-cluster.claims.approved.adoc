=== Source overview

The chapter treats two clearly-distinct Mesoamerican cosmogonic sub-cases that share the post-conquest textual situation of the Coatepec narrative in the previous chapter and that contribute to the present book's comparative argument in different ways: the Cipactli dismemberment-cosmogony, in which Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl pull apart the body of the primordial earth-monster to form sky and earth, and the Quetzalcoatl-vs-Tezcatlipoca alternation across the five cosmic ages (the five Suns) of Mesoamerican cosmology.footnote:[Per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml; López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum: Pathways of Mesoamerican Mythology* (Albuquerque: University of New Mexico Press; ISBN 9780826313942), chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony and the Suns cycle as the principal Mesoamerican cosmogonic narratives outside the Coatepec material; Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice: The Aztec Empire and the Role of Violence in Civilization* (Boston: Beacon Press; ISBN 9780807046425), chapters on the five Suns as the Mexica framework for cosmic time-reckoning.]

The two sub-cases share the post-conquest character of the surviving textual record: like the Coatepec narrative treated in chapter 24, neither the Cipactli dismemberment-cosmogony nor the Quetzalcoatl-vs-Tezcatlipoca alternation across the five Suns has a pre-conquest written-prose witness in the strict sense, since Mesoamerican writing-systems before 1521 CE were primarily pictographic-logographic and the Mexica did not record their narrative literature in continuous alphabetic prose; the surviving textual witnesses to both narratives are post-conquest, written down by Nahua informants and Spanish friars in the generation after the fall of Tenochtitlan in 1521 CE.footnote:[López Austin 1993, chapter on the Mesoamerican post-conquest textual record, on the absence of pre-conquest continuous-prose witnesses for Mesoamerican narrative literature and on the post-conquest character of the surviving alphabetic-text witnesses to the Cipactli cosmogony and the Suns cycle; Carrasco 1999, chapter on the five Suns, on the same post-conquest situation; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml special_instructions on the post-conquest source-witness situation paralleling Ch24.]

The post-conquest written witnesses to both sub-cases are filtered through Spanish-language redaction and Spanish-friar conceptual frames in ways that the chapter must register methodologically: the Franciscan and Dominican friars whose pastoral aim included the documentation of pre-conquest Mexica religion in order that Christian missionaries would be able to recognise and root out its survivals brought to that documentation pre-existing notions (creation from divine division, sequential cosmic ages bounded by catastrophe, dual antagonistic deities) drawn from biblical-and-medieval Christian sources, with the consequent risk of category-import in the post-conquest narratives.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, chapter on the five Suns, on the methodological situation of the post-conquest textual record and on the Spanish-friar conceptual frames through which Sahagún, Olmos, and Durán mediated their access to the pre-conquest Mexica narrative material; López Austin 1993 on the same methodological situation and on the consequent risk of category-import from biblical-and-medieval Christian creation-and-cosmic-ages notions.]

=== Cipactli — the dismemberment-cosmogony

Cipactli is, in the Mesoamerican mythological tradition preserved in the post-conquest textual record, the primordial earth-monster — a vast crocodilian or saurian figure whose body floats in the primeval waters before the cosmos has been formed and from whose body the present sky and earth are made — and the figure's name is conventionally glossed in modern Mesoamericanist scholarship as the first day-sign of the Mexica twenty-day count, the *cipactli* day-sign that depicts a stylised crocodile-head and that anchors the calendrical sequence in the primordial earth-monster's identity.footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony, on Cipactli as the primordial crocodilian / saurian earth-monster of the Mesoamerican mythological tradition, on the figure's body floating in the primeval waters before the cosmos has been formed, on the Mexica twenty-day-count *cipactli* day-sign that depicts a stylised crocodile-head, and on the calendrical anchoring of the day-count sequence in the primordial earth-monster's identity; glossary 'Cipactli' first_mention_gloss.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=The Myths of the Opossum, ch. on Cipactli and the Suns cycle

The Cipactli cosmogony, as preserved most fully in the *Histoyre du Mechique*, opens before the present cosmos exists: the primordial waters cover everything, the primordial earth-monster Cipactli swims in those waters, and the gods Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl — present together at the pre-cosmic stage — undertake the work of cosmogonic ordering by acting jointly upon Cipactli's body to make sky and earth from her substance.footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony, on the *Histoyre du Mechique* narrative-opening with the primordial waters, on Cipactli's swimming in those waters, on the joint pre-cosmic presence of Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl at the pre-cosmic stage, and on the cosmogonic-ordering work the two gods undertake on Cipactli's body to make sky and earth from her substance; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml family_members entry on the *Histoyre du Mechique* opening.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=The Myths of the Opossum, ch. on Cipactli and the Suns cycle

Tezcatlipoca's first action in the *Histoyre du Mechique* narrative is to use his foot as bait: he places his foot in the primordial waters, Cipactli rises and bites the foot, and in the violence of the bite Tezcatlipoca loses the foot — the lost foot being the iconographic origin of one of Tezcatlipoca's persistent representational features in the surviving Mexica pictorial record, where the god is conventionally depicted with one foot replaced by an obsidian mirror or a serpent.footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony, on Tezcatlipoca's first action of using his foot as bait in the *Histoyre du Mechique* narrative, on Cipactli rising from the primordial waters to bite the foot, on Tezcatlipoca's losing the foot in the violence of the bite, and on the lost foot as the iconographic origin of one of Tezcatlipoca's persistent representational features in the surviving Mexica pictorial record (the foot conventionally replaced by an obsidian mirror or a serpent); glossary 'Tezcatlipoca' first_mention_gloss.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=The Myths of the Opossum, ch. on Cipactli and the Suns cycle

Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl together then seize Cipactli's body and pull her apart — the act being a forceful tearing rather than a slaying in any narrowly-combative sense — and from the upper half of her body they form the sky, fixing it above the primordial waters; from the lower half they form the earth, settling it on the surface of the primordial waters; and from her constituent parts (her hair, her skin, her eyes, her mouth, the small organs of her body) they form the trees, the grasses, the springs, the caves, the rivers, and the other landscape-features of the present world.footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony, on Tezcatlipoca and Quetzalcoatl together seizing Cipactli's body and tearing her apart, on the forceful-tearing rather than narrowly-combative character of the act, on the sky formed from the upper half of her body, on the earth formed from the lower half settled on the surface of the primordial waters, and on the trees / grasses / springs / caves / rivers / other landscape-features formed from her constituent parts (hair, skin, eyes, mouth, small organs of the body) per the *Histoyre du Mechique* narrative; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml family_members entry on the dismemberment narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=The Myths of the Opossum, ch. on Cipactli and the Suns cycle

The Cipactli narrative is in scope.md's terms a dismemberment-cosmogony rather than a combat-cosmogony: the present cosmos is established by the division of a pre-existing primordial body into ordered parts (sky / earth / landscape-features) rather than by a combat in which one party defeats and slays the other and the slaying establishes the cosmos as its consequence; the act of tearing-apart is functionally cosmogonic but structurally non-combative, since Cipactli does not contest the division and the gods do not fight her in the sense the Coatepec, Marduk-Tiamat, or Indra-Vṛtra narratives understand fighting.footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony, on the structural distinction between dismemberment-cosmogony (present cosmos established by division of a pre-existing primordial body into ordered parts) and combat-cosmogony (present cosmos established by a combat in which one party defeats the other), on the Cipactli narrative as the former rather than the latter type, and on the functionally-cosmogonic but structurally-non-combative character of the tearing-apart; scope.md §"Excluded adjacent motifs" 1 on dismemberment cosmogonies without combat as out-of-scope; per brief 25-mesoamerican-cluster.yaml special_instructions on the scope.md boundary case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lopez-austin-myths ; loc=The Myths of the Opossum, ch. on Cipactli and the Suns cycle

Cipactli's defeat — if "defeat" is the right word for an act in which the primordial earth-monster does not actively contest the division of her body — registers in the post-Cipactli cosmos as the surface of the earth itself: the earth as the body of the dismembered primordial monster is iconographically present in the surviving Mexica pictorial record (the earth-goddess Tlaltecuhtli, whose face is widely depicted on the underside of monumental Mexica sculpture, is in some readings the surviving consciousness of Cipactli embedded in the earth's surface), and the earth's perceived hunger for blood and human hearts is read in the post-conquest Mexica theological tradition as the dismembered earth-monster's continuing demand to be fed.footnote:[López Austin 1993, *The Myths of the Opossum*, chapter on the Cipactli cosmogony, on Cipactli's defeat registering in the post-Cipactli cosmos as the surface of the earth itself, on the earth-goddess Tlaltecuhtli (whose face is widely depicted on the underside of monumental Mexica sculpture) as in some readings the surviving consciousness of Cipactli embedded in the earth's surface, and on the earth's perceived hunger for blood and human hearts as the dismembered earth-monster's continuing demand to be fed in the post-conquest Mexica theological tradition.]

=== Quetzalcoatl vs. Tezcatlipoca — the alternation across the five Suns

Quetzalcoatl ("Feathered Serpent") and Tezcatlipoca ("Smoking Mirror") are in the Mesoamerican mythological tradition the two principal antagonist-and-collaborator deities whose alternation across cosmic ages structures the Mexica cosmological reckoning of time: the two gods alternate as rulers of successive cosmic ages — the five Suns — and each age ends in catastrophe before the next age (and the next ruling god) begins.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, chapter on the five Suns, on Quetzalcoatl ("Feathered Serpent") and Tezcatlipoca ("Smoking Mirror") as the two principal antagonist-and-collaborator deities of the Mesoamerican mythological tradition, on their alternation as rulers of successive cosmic ages, on the five Suns as the framework within which the alternation is reckoned, and on the catastrophic ending of each age before the next age and the next ruling god; López Austin 1993 on the same alternation; glossary 'Quetzalcoatl', 'Tezcatlipoca', and 'the five Suns' first_mention_gloss entries.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on the five Suns

The five Suns sequence as preserved in the *Leyenda de los Soles* (1558) and in the corroborating sixteenth-century witnesses (*Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas*, *Florentine Codex*, *Anales de Cuauhtitlán*) names each cosmic age by its presiding element-and-fate and reckons the present age as the Fifth: the First Sun (Nahui Ocelotl, "Four Jaguar"), under Tezcatlipoca, ends with the cosmic age's inhabitants devoured by jaguars; the Second Sun (Nahui Ehecatl, "Four Wind"), under Quetzalcoatl, ends with the inhabitants destroyed by hurricanes and transformed into monkeys; the Third Sun (Nahui Quiahuitl, "Four Rain"), under Tlaloc, ends with the inhabitants destroyed by a rain of fire and transformed into birds; the Fourth Sun (Nahui Atl, "Four Water"), under Chalchiuhtlicue, ends with the inhabitants destroyed by a flood and transformed into fish; and the Fifth Sun (Nahui Ollin, "Four Movement"), under Tonatiuh-Huitzilopochtli, is the present age, predicted to end in earthquakes.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, chapter on the five Suns, on the *Leyenda de los Soles* (1558) and the corroborating sixteenth-century witnesses (*Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas*, *Florentine Codex*, *Anales de Cuauhtitlán*) on the five Suns sequence and its naming-and-fate scheme — Nahui Ocelotl ("Four Jaguar") under Tezcatlipoca, Nahui Ehecatl ("Four Wind") under Quetzalcoatl, Nahui Quiahuitl ("Four Rain") under Tlaloc, Nahui Atl ("Four Water") under Chalchiuhtlicue, Nahui Ollin ("Four Movement") under Tonatiuh-Huitzilopochtli — and on the catastrophic ending of each age; López Austin 1993 on the same sequence.]

The cosmological function of the five Suns sequence in the Mexica theological tradition is the location of the present age (the Fifth Sun, Nahui Ollin, under Tonatiuh-Huitzilopochtli) within a wider sequence of ages that have already ended in catastrophe and that imply the present age's eventual end in earthquakes, with the implication that the present cosmos is contingent rather than eternal and that its continuation depends on the proper ritual maintenance of the relationship between the gods and the human community.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, chapter on the five Suns, on the cosmological function of the sequence in the Mexica theological tradition as the location of the present age within a wider sequence of catastrophe-bounded ages, on the contingent character of the present cosmos in this framework, and on the dependence of the cosmos's continuation on the proper ritual maintenance of the relationship between the gods and the human community; López Austin 1993 on the same cosmological function.]

The Quetzalcoatl-vs-Tezcatlipoca alternation across the first two Suns is the load-bearing structural feature of the Mexica reading of the sequence: the two gods are not simply consecutive rulers but are paired antagonists whose mutual toppling constitutes the sequence's narrative engine, with each god in turn being struck down by the other (Tezcatlipoca thrown into the sea by Quetzalcoatl in some strata of the post-conquest record; Quetzalcoatl driven from his rule by Tezcatlipoca in others) before the next age opens.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice*, chapter on the five Suns, on the Quetzalcoatl-vs-Tezcatlipoca alternation as the load-bearing structural feature of the Mexica reading of the sequence, on the pairing of the two gods as antagonists rather than as simply consecutive rulers, on Tezcatlipoca thrown into the sea by Quetzalcoatl in some strata of the post-conquest record, and on Quetzalcoatl driven from his rule by Tezcatlipoca in others; López Austin 1993 on the same antagonistic pairing.]
