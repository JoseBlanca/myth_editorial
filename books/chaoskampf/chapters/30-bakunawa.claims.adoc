=== Source overview

This chapter treats two inclusion-marginal cases drawn from outside the Eurasian combat-myth corpus that the present book treats in its core-tier chapters: (i) the Philippine (Visayan / Bikol) oral tradition in which the serpent-dragon Bakunawa devours six of the seven original moons and is driven off by communal noise-making during eclipses, and (ii) the Altaic / Turkic-Mongol religious-cosmological pairing of the underworld-god Erlik against the sky-god Ülgen, in which the opposition is dualistic rather than resolved through single combat — with the chapter's two-part structure following from the brief's special_instructions on the chapter's organising as a Bakunawa half and an Erlik / Ülgen half, consolidated in a closing typology-and-forward-links section.footnote:[Per brief 30-bakunawa.yaml summary on the Bakunawa material (the serpent-dragon devours six of the seven original moons; communal noise-making during eclipses drives the serpent off; there is no hero who slays it — the combat is communal and recurring) and on the Erlik / Ülgen material (in Altaic and broader Turkic-Mongol mythology, the sky-god Ülgen opposes the underworld-god Erlik; the opposition is dualistic and cosmogonic rather than straightforwardly combat-resolved); Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths* (Quezon City: University of the Philippines Press, 2001; ISBN 9789715421140), sections on Bakunawa and the moons; Eliade 1964, *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy*, trans. === Bakunawa: the Philippine context and the eclipse-and-moon narrative

The Bakunawa narrative's geographical and cultural setting is the central and southern Philippines — principally the Visayan island group (the central archipelago between Luzon and Mindanao, including Cebu, Panay, Negros, Bohol, Samar, Leyte, and adjacent islands) and the Bikol region of southeastern Luzon, with attestations in adjacent regional traditions including parts of Mindanao and Tagalog-speaking Luzon — across which the narrative is recorded under regionally-variant names and within regionally-variant performance contexts but with a shared narrative core treating the serpent-dragon's relation to the moon and to communal ritual response during eclipses.footnote:[Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths*, sections on Bakunawa and the moons, on the Visayan and Bikol heartlands of the Bakunawa narrative, on the wider attestation across adjacent Philippine regional traditions including parts of Mindanao and Tagalog-speaking Luzon, on the regionally-variant names and performance contexts, and on the shared narrative core treating the serpent-dragon's relation to the moon and to communal ritual response during eclipses; per glossary 'Bakunawa' first_mention_gloss on the Philippine (Visayan / Bikol) serpent-dragon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=eugenio-philippine-myths ; loc=Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths, sections on Bakunawa and the moons

Bakunawa is in the cycle the serpent-dragon of the deep sea, conceived in the Visayan and Bikol oral-tradition record as a vast serpentine creature dwelling in the ocean depths (or, in some recordings, in the underworld) whose periodic emergence threatens the celestial order rather than the cosmographic order of the world's surface, with the figure's principal narrative function being the eclipse-causing devourer of the moon rather than the cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing adversary of a hero-deity.footnote:[Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths*, sections on Bakunawa and the moons, on Bakunawa as the serpent-dragon of the deep sea conceived as a vast serpentine creature dwelling in the ocean depths or in the underworld, on the figure's periodic emergence threatening the celestial order rather than the cosmographic order of the world's surface, and on the figure's principal narrative function as the eclipse-causing devourer of the moon rather than as the cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing adversary of a hero-deity; per glossary 'Bakunawa' first_mention_gloss on the figure's eclipse-and-noise-making narrative role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=eugenio-philippine-myths ; loc=Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths, sections on Bakunawa and the moons

The cycle's load-bearing mythic premise in the most widely attested form is that there were originally seven moons in the sky, of which six were devoured in succession by Bakunawa across the early time of the world, with only the last (single) moon — the one familiar to present human experience — remaining in the sky after the devourings, with the recurrent eclipse-events of the present epoch being understood as Bakunawa's continuing attempts to devour the seventh and last moon.footnote:[Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths*, sections on Bakunawa and the moons, on the cycle's load-bearing mythic premise of the seven original moons of which six were devoured in succession by Bakunawa across the early time of the world, on the seventh and last moon as the one familiar to present human experience, and on the recurrent eclipse-events of the present epoch as Bakunawa's continuing attempts to devour the seventh and last moon; per glossary 'Bakunawa' first_mention_gloss on the seven-moons devouring pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=eugenio-philippine-myths ; loc=Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths, sections on Bakunawa and the moons

The cycle's principal ritual correlate is the communal noise-making practice during eclipses: in the recorded versions, when the moon is observed to be in eclipse — interpreted within the tradition as Bakunawa's devouring action upon the last moon in progress — the human community responds collectively by beating drums, sounding gongs, shouting, banging metal pots and other resonant objects, and otherwise making sustained loud noise to startle Bakunawa into releasing the moon and retreating from the human-celestial sphere, with the noise-making being a communal-ritual practice rather than a hero-led act of slaying.footnote:[Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths*, sections on Bakunawa and the moons, on the communal noise-making practice during eclipses as the cycle's principal ritual correlate, on the eclipse interpreted within the tradition as Bakunawa's devouring action upon the last moon in progress, on the practices of beating drums and sounding gongs and shouting and banging metal pots and other resonant objects, on the noise-making's purpose of startling Bakunawa into releasing the moon and retreating from the human-celestial sphere, and on the practice's communal-ritual character rather than hero-led-slaying character; per brief 30-bakunawa.yaml summary on the communal noise-making (drums, gongs, shouting) as the eclipse-response practice.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=eugenio-philippine-myths ; loc=Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths, sections on Bakunawa and the moons

The cycle's outcome in any given eclipse-event is therefore Bakunawa's retreat rather than Bakunawa's death: the communal noise drives the serpent off, the moon is released and reappears in the sky, the human community resumes its ordinary rhythm, and the encounter ends in a temporary equilibrium that is itself open to renewed disruption at the next eclipse — making the cycle structurally a recurring communal-maintenance practice rather than a one-time decisive combat with cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing consequences.footnote:[Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths*, sections on Bakunawa and the moons, on the cycle's outcome as Bakunawa's retreat rather than Bakunawa's death, on the communal noise driving the serpent off and the moon being released and reappearing, on the human community's resumption of its ordinary rhythm, on the temporary-equilibrium character of the resolution open to renewed disruption at the next eclipse, and on the cycle's structural character as a recurring communal-maintenance practice rather than a one-time decisive combat; per brief 30-bakunawa.yaml summary on the absence of any hero who slays the serpent and on the communal-and-recurring character of the combat.]

=== Bakunawa: communal-ritual register and inclusion-marginal status

The Bakunawa cycle's primary cultural-historical function within Visayan and Bikol oral tradition is eclipse-aetiology rather than cosmogony or sovereignty-establishment: the cycle explains why eclipses occur — Bakunawa's devouring of the moon — and why the human community responds with communal noise-making during them — to drive Bakunawa off — without addressing the question of how the cosmos came into being or how political authority is constituted, with the cycle's narrative-and-ritual work being directed at a specific recurring observable phenomenon rather than at first-order cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing questions.footnote:[Eugenio 2001, *Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths*, sections on Bakunawa and the moons, on the cycle's primary cultural-historical function as eclipse-aetiology rather than cosmogony or sovereignty-establishment, on the cycle's explanatory work being directed at the recurring observable phenomenon of eclipses and the human-community ritual response to them rather than at first-order cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing questions, and on the cycle's narrative-and-ritual function as a specific aetiological-and-communal-ritual practice within Visayan and Bikol communal life; per brief 30-bakunawa.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the eclipse-aetiology and communal-ritual function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=eugenio-philippine-myths ; loc=Philippine Folk Literature: The Myths, sections on Bakunawa and the moons

=== Erlik and Ülgen: the Altaic / Turkic-Mongol cosmographic frame
