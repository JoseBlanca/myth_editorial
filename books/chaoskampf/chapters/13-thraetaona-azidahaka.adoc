[[ch-13-thraetaona-azidahaka]]
== Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka (and the Final Renovation)

This chapter treats the Iranian combat-myth in which the Avestan hero Thraētaona — a hero-slayer whose name surfaces in Middle Persian as Frēdōn and in New Persian as Fereydun in the Shāhnāmeh — slays the three-headed dragon Aži Dahāka, together with its eschatological pendant in which the rebound dragon escapes at the end of time and is killed a second and final time by the revived hero Kərəsāspa.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48 (Avestan and Pahlavi material on Thraētaona); Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan attestations of Aži Dahāka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The chapter's primary scholarly anchor is Calvert Watkins's *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics*, published by Oxford University Press in 1995, whose Part V chapters 44 to 48 treat the Avestan and Pahlavi material on Thraētaona within the wider Indo-European *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula.footnote:[Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics*, Part V chs. 44–48 on the Avestan and Pahlavi Thraētaona material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

Watkins's monograph is printed only; in the present chapter it is cited at section level — Part V — and at chapter level within Part V (chs. 44–48), in line with the established discipline for printed comparative anchors in this book.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48 (printed only; cited at section and chapter level per brief).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The chapter's foundational philological anchor is Émile Benveniste and Louis Renou's *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna: Étude de mythologie indo-iranienne*, published by the Imprimerie Nationale in Paris in 1934 as Cahiers de la Société Asiatique 3.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, *Vṛtra et Vṛθragna: Étude de mythologie indo-iranienne*, Cahiers de la Société Asiatique 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Benveniste and Renou 1934 is the standard early-twentieth-century Indo-Iranian comparative monograph that integrates the Avestan hymn-allusions to Aži Dahāka with the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra material and with the Pahlavi narrative expansions; it remains the foundational reference work for the Iranian side of the Indo-Iranian combat-myth.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the integrated Indo-Iranian comparative reading; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on Benveniste and Renou's foundational status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Benveniste and Renou 1934 is digitised at gallica.bnf.fr; the present chapter cites it at section level rather than at sub-page granularity, in keeping with the book's discipline for older philological monographs.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934 (Gallica BnF stable URL https://gallica.bnf.fr/), cited at section level per brief.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Avestan corpus on which the chapter relies — principally the *Yašt*s (the hymns to individual divinities) and the *Vendīdād* (the priestly law-code) — is the earlier and allusive layer of the tradition. The Pahlavi corpus on which the chapter relies — principally the *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 — is the post-Achaemenid layer that supplies the fuller narrative.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan hymn-allusions in the *Yašt*s and *Vendīdād*; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 as the narrative-expansion layer.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

This Avestan-Pahlavi temporal layering is structurally load-bearing for the chapter. The chapter must distinguish the earlier Avestan hymn-allusions from the later Pahlavi narrative compilation rather than merging them into a single homogeneous account, and every section that draws on the Pahlavi sources flags their post-Achaemenid date explicitly.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Avestan-Pahlavi temporal layering as a load-bearing distinction; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the same distinction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The Avestan textual tradition itself spans a long compositional period: the *Gāθās* attributed to Zarathuštra are the earliest stratum and lie outside the chapter's focus, while the Younger Avestan *Yašt*s on which the chapter principally draws are conventionally dated to the second half of the first millennium BCE, with the corpus reaching written transmission only late in antiquity.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Younger Avestan *Yašt*s as the principal locus of the Aži Dahāka allusions; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the dating of the *Yašt* corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Pahlavi (Middle Persian) Zoroastrian corpus on which the chapter draws was redacted under Sasanian patronage and into the early Islamic centuries: the *Bundahišn* in the form transmitted to us is conventionally dated to the late Sasanian and early Islamic period (c. 8th–9th c. CE), and *Dēnkard* book 7 is of comparable date.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 dating; per chaoskampf scope.md on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* as the latest in-scope primary source.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The principal Avestan loci treated in the chapter are *Yašt* 5 (the *Ābān Yašt*, the hymn to the goddess Arədvī Sūrā Anāhitā) at stanzas 33–35, *Yašt* 14 (the *Bahrām Yašt*, the hymn to the victory-god Vərəθraγna), and *Yašt* 19 (the *Zamyād Yašt*, the hymn to the *xᵛarənah* or kingly glory).footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on *Yašt* 5.33–35, *Yašt* 14, and *Yašt* 19 as the principal Avestan loci for the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35, Yt. 14, Yt. 19

The principal Pahlavi loci are the *Bundahišn* (the cosmogonic compendium) and *Dēnkard* book 7 (the eschatological-and-prophetic exposition), both of which contain extended narrative material on Thraētaona's combat with Aži Dahāka and on the eventual final killing of the dragon at _Frashokereti_ — the Avestan term for the final renovation, the Zoroastrian eschatological end-time treated at length in Movement III below.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 as the principal Pahlavi narrative loci; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the italicisation of _Frashokereti_ as a ritual-term at first body mention.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The chapter's structural lacuna — the gap that the chapter's source-strategy must address explicitly — is that the Avestan hymn-allusions are allusive rather than narrative. The Avestan corpus presupposes the combat-narrative but does not narrate it at length, and the fuller narrative survives only in the post-Achaemenid Pahlavi compilations.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Avestan-allusive vs Pahlavi-narrative structural lacuna; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the allusive character of the Avestan hymn-references.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The chapter's secondary survey-anchor for the long-trajectory combat-myth perspective is Neil Forsyth's *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth*, published by Princeton University Press in 1987, which treats the Iranian Aži Dahāka material in the context of the Achaemenid-Hellenistic-Christian transmission of combat-myth motifs.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth*, on the Iranian combat-myth in the long-trajectory comparative perspective.]

The chapter's secondary anchor for the Indo-European comparative dimension — the Vedic Trita Āptya / Iranian Thraētaona cognancy and the wider IE *HERO SLAY SERPENT* pattern — is M.L. West's *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, published by Oxford University Press in 2007, whose chapter 5 treats the Iranian reflexes alongside the Vedic and other Indo-European material.footnote:[West 2007, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, ch. 5, on the Iranian reflexes within the Indo-European comparative frame.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 5

The chapter's anchor for the eschatological dimension of the combat — the Frashokereti material that closes Iranian cosmic time — is Hermann Gunkel's *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit*, whose Teil II treats the *Endzeit* eschatological combat material within the *Urzeit*-*Endzeit* parallelism that Gunkel's monograph established as a category.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the Frashokereti material as Iranian *Endzeit* combat-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II

What follows takes the chapter in five movements, after a short orientation in Zoroastrian cosmology: Movement I treats the Avestan hymn-allusions as the earliest layer; Movement II treats the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* narrative as the post-Achaemenid expansion that supplies the binding under Damāvand; Movement III treats _Frashokereti_, the eschatological pendant in which the dragon escapes and is killed by Kərəsāspa; a fourth section then sets out the Indo-Iranian philological cognancy with the Vedic Trita-Āptya and Indra-Vṛtra material; and a brief reception note flags the Shāhnāmeh recasting of Thraētaona as Fereydun and Aži Dahāka as the tyrant Zahhāk, which lies outside the chapter's primary scope.

=== Setting and Zoroastrian cosmology

The Zoroastrian religious framework within which the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat is set is dualistic: the cosmos is the arena of a structural opposition between the supreme creator-god Ahura Mazdā — Avestan for "Wise Lord" — and the evil spirit Angra Mainyu, with each cosmic agent and event aligned with one or the other side of the opposition.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Zoroastrian dualistic framework; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the dualistic frame within which the Thraētaona combat is set.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

In the Pahlavi tradition the same Wise Lord appears under the Middle Persian name Ohrmazd, and the chapter uses the Avestan form Ahura Mazdā when treating Avestan material and the Pahlavi-context name where the post-Achaemenid sources are under discussion.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Ahura Mazdā as the supreme creator-god; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the Avestan-Pahlavi name discipline.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Angra Mainyu — Avestan for "the evil spirit" — is the cosmic adversary of Ahura Mazdā; in the Pahlavi tradition the same figure appears under the Middle Persian name Ahriman, and the chapter again uses the Avestan form when treating Avestan material and the Pahlavi-context name where the post-Achaemenid sources are under discussion.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Angra Mainyu as the cosmic adversary; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the Avestan-Pahlavi name discipline.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The dragon Aži Dahāka belongs, in both the Avestan and the Pahlavi traditions, to the side of Angra Mainyu / Ahriman. He is a creature of the evil spirit, and the combat against him is structurally aligned with the wider cosmic struggle of Ahura Mazdā's order against Angra Mainyu's destructive opposition.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Aži Dahāka as a creature of Angra Mainyu / Ahriman; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the dualistic alignment of the dragon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Within this Zoroastrian cosmology, heroic-figure combats against creatures of Angra Mainyu — the Thraētaona combat with Aži Dahāka being the chapter's principal instance — function as participations in the cosmic struggle. The hero acts on the side of Ahura Mazdā's order in dispatching agents of the evil spirit's destructive opposition.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on heroic combat as participation in the Mazdayasnian cosmic struggle; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the same alignment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 45

The Avestan name Thraētaona — written in the Avestan script and conventionally transliterated with the macron forms ē, ā per the chapter's glossary discipline — designates a hero of the Āθβiia line. The patronymic Āθβiia is itself part of the Indo-Iranian inheritance that aligns Thraētaona with the Vedic Trita Āptya treated comparatively in the Indo-Iranian-cognancy section below.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Thraētaona's Āθβiia patronymic and the Indo-Iranian cognancy; West 2007, ch. 5, on the same patronymic.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Thraētaona's onomastic afterlife in the Iranian tradition runs Avestan Thraētaona → Middle Persian Frēdōn → New Persian Fereydun, and the chapter uses the Avestan form Thraētaona throughout the Avestan and Pahlavi-narrative discussion, reserving Frēdōn for explicit Middle Persian contexts and reserving Fereydun for the briefly flagged Shāhnāmeh-reception note.footnote:[Per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the Thraētaona / Frēdōn / Fereydun onomastic discipline; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan-to-New-Persian onomastic chain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Aži Dahāka — Avestan for "Dahāka the dragon," with *aži* the inherited Indo-Iranian word for "serpent" — is the three-headed, six-eyed, three-jawed dragon-tyrant of the Avestan and Pahlavi tradition. The chapter uses the diacritical form Aži Dahāka throughout in line with the locked glossary discipline.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan name Aži Dahāka; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the diacritical form as locked.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Avestan word *aži* — the generic term for "serpent" — is philologically cognate with Vedic *áhi*, the word that the Rigvedic combat-formula *áhann áhim* ("he killed the serpent") uses for Vṛtra. The cognancy of *aži* and *áhi* is one of the philological pillars of the Indo-Iranian comparative reading taken up in the cognancy section below.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on *aži* / *áhi* cognancy; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The mountain Damāvand — a peak in the Alborz range of northern Iran — is the cosmogeographic feature under which Aži Dahāka is bound in the Pahlavi tradition. In the Avestan corpus the binding-mountain is not yet named in the surviving allusions, and Damāvand is, in the surviving texts, a Pahlavi-narrative localisation rather than an Avestan one.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on Damāvand as the Pahlavi-narrative binding-locus; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the absence of the named binding-mountain in the surviving Avestan allusions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

=== Movement I — Thraētaona's combat in the Avestan tradition

The principal Avestan locus for Thraētaona's combat with Aži Dahāka is *Yašt* 5 stanzas 33 to 35 — three stanzas embedded within the *Ābān Yašt*, the hymn to the goddess Arədvī Sūrā Anāhitā, the Avestan goddess of waters.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on *Yašt* 5.33–35 as the principal Avestan locus for Thraētaona's combat; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the same passage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35

In *Yašt* 5.33–35 Thraētaona prays to Anāhitā for victory over Aži Dahāka, offering the goddess prescribed sacrificial gifts and asking her to grant him strength sufficient to defeat the three-headed dragon. The passage is the foundational Avestan hymn-attestation of the combat.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Thraētaona's prayer to Anāhitā in *Yašt* 5.33–35; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the prayer-to-Anāhitā frame.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35

The *Yašt* 5.33–35 attestation is hymnic and allusive rather than narrative. The stanzas presuppose the audience's familiarity with the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat-narrative and embed a brief reference to it within the hymn's wider catalogue of heroes who pray to Anāhitā for victory.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the hymnic-allusive character of the *Yašt* 5.33–35 attestation; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the presupposed-narrative frame.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35

In the *Yašt* 5.33–35 hymn-passage Aži Dahāka is described in formulaic terms as the three-headed, six-eyed, three-jawed dragon. The morphological description that becomes the standard portrait of the dragon throughout the Avestan-and-Pahlavi tradition is therefore already in place in the earliest extant Avestan attestation.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the formulaic three-headed / six-eyed / three-jawed description in *Yašt* 5.33–35; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the morphological formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35

The three-headed character of Aži Dahāka is one of the Avestan tradition's most stable features: it is attested already in the *Yašt* 5 hymn-passage, recurs across the further Avestan attestations, and is preserved across the entire Pahlavi narrative tradition without variation.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the stability of the three-headed feature across Avestan and Pahlavi attestations; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the same stability.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

A further Avestan locus relevant to the combat — though one in which Thraētaona is not himself a principal exemplary figure — is *Yašt* 14, the *Bahrām Yašt*, the hymn to Vərəθraγna, the Avestan god whose name is philologically cognate with Vedic Vṛtrahán ("smiter of Vṛtra") and who is the Avestan personification of victorious-warrior force.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on *Yašt* 14 (the *Bahrām Yašt*) and the Vərəθraγna theology; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 14

*Yašt* 14 supplies the theological framework of Vərəθraγna's victorious force within which Thraētaona's combat is to be understood, even though the specific Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka narrative is more directly attested in *Yašt* 5 and *Yašt* 19. The hymn catalogues Vərəθraγna's incarnation-forms and praises victorious-warrior force more broadly, rather than featuring Thraētaona's combat as one of its principal exemplary cases.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vərəθraγna theological frame in *Yašt* 14; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the same frame.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 14

The Vərəθraγna ~ Vṛtrahán cognancy that is the central philological argument of Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph aligns the Avestan victory-god directly with the Vedic Indra's standard epithet Vṛtrahán, and the chapter's *Yašt* 14 material is the Avestan side of that comparative philological argument.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vərəθraγna ~ Vṛtrahán philological cognancy as the monograph's central argument; West 2007, ch. 5, on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 14

The third principal Avestan locus for the combat is *Yašt* 19, the *Zamyād Yašt* — the hymn to the *xᵛarənah*, the Avestan term conventionally translated "kingly glory" or "(royal) splendour," which designates the divinely-bestowed charisma legitimating Iranian sovereignty.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on *Yašt* 19 (the *Zamyād Yašt*) and the *xᵛarənah* theology; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44, 47, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 19

*Yašt* 19 places Thraētaona's combat with Aži Dahāka within the *xᵛarənah*-theology. Its narrative-allusions present the dragon's defeat as a moment in the long history of the kingly glory's transmission from one divinely-favoured ruler-or-hero to another, and Thraētaona's victory is one of the *xᵛarənah*'s exemplary heroic episodes.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the *xᵛarənah*-theology in *Yašt* 19 and Thraētaona's place within it; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44, 47, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 19

The royal-legitimation function of the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat — the function-type that the brief identifies as one of the chapter's two principal foci — is grounded in *Yašt* 19's *xᵛarənah*-theology. The slaying of the dragon-tyrant is presented as a moment in the legitimating transmission of the kingly glory, and the combat thereby becomes a model of legitimate sovereignty's victory over usurping tyranny.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the royal-legitimation function grounded in the *Yašt* 19 *xᵛarənah*-theology; Forsyth 1987, on the same royal-legitimation function in the Iranian combat-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 19

[VARIANT: primary=Avestan hymn-allusions (Yt. 5.33–35, Yt. 14, Yt. 19) — earlier and allusive | alt=Pahlavi narrative compilation (Bundahišn, Dēnkard book 7) — later and narrative | chosen: present-both, marked-temporally per brief | reason: the brief stipulates that the Avestan-Pahlavi temporal layering is structurally load-bearing and must be preserved at every load-bearing point; the chapter therefore presents both layers in distinct sub-sections (Movement I = Avestan, Movement II = Pahlavi) and flags the post-Achaemenid date of the Pahlavi material wherever it is drawn upon.]

The Avestan hymn-allusions to the Thraētaona combat are scattered across the *Yašt* corpus and across the *Vendīdād* (the priestly law-code). Beyond the principal *Yašt* 5 / *Yašt* 14 / *Yašt* 19 loci, there are further allusions in *Yašt* 9 (the *Gōš Yašt*), *Yašt* 13 (the *Frawardīn Yašt*), and *Yašt* 17 (the *Aši Yašt*).footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the wider distribution of Thraētaona allusions across the *Yašt* corpus and the *Vendīdād*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 9, 13, 17; Vendīdād passim

The *Vendīdād* allusions, occurring in a priestly-law-code context rather than a hymnic context, treat the Thraētaona combat as part of the legendary-prehistorical horizon presupposed by the law-code's purity rules and ritual prescriptions. Aži Dahāka is named there as the type-specimen of the destructive demonic creature.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the *Vendīdād* allusions and the legendary-prehistorical frame.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Vendīdād passim

The *Vendīdād*'s inclusion of Aži Dahāka in its catalogue of demonic creatures aligns the dragon with the wider Avestan demonological taxonomy: Aži Dahāka belongs to the class of *daēvic* (demonic) creatures associated with Angra Mainyu, and the priestly-purity vocabulary that the *Vendīdād* applies to demonic things applies to him directly.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Aži Dahāka in the Avestan demonological taxonomy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Vendīdād passim

In the Avestan hymn-allusions Aži Dahāka's three heads carry a triple-formulaic description: three heads, six eyes (two per head), three jaws (one per head), and the formula is preserved with metrical regularity across all the hymnic attestations.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the metrical-formulaic stability of the three-headed / six-eyed / three-jawed description; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35

A further triple-formulaic feature of Aži Dahāka in the Avestan attestations is the description of him as containing, or having within him, "a thousand of every kind" of noxious creature — vermin, scorpions, frogs, lizards — a hyperbolic catalogue that emphasises the dragon's character as a concentrated locus of demonic-creaturely opposition to Ahura Mazdā's good creation.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the "thousand-of-every-kind" formula; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the demonic-creaturely catalogue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The combat-narrative that the Avestan hymn-allusions presuppose — without narrating it at length — has Thraētaona dispatching the dragon by smiting, and the principal verb used in the formulaic poetic vocabulary is the Avestan *jan-* "to smite," which is philologically cognate with the Vedic *han-* of the Indra-Vṛtra formula *áhann áhim*.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan *jan-* / Vedic *han-* cognancy; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The cognancy of Avestan *jan-* (Thraētaona's smiting of Aži Dahāka) and Vedic *han-* (Indra's smiting of Vṛtra) is, alongside the *aži* / *áhi* cognancy, a principal philological pillar of Benveniste and Renou's argument and of Watkins's wider Indo-European *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula treated in the cognancy section below.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the *jan-* / *han-* cognancy as a principal philological pillar; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the same cognancy as a pillar of the IE formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

In the Avestan hymn-allusions the consequence of Thraētaona's victory is presented in legendary-historical rather than cosmogonic terms. The cosmos has already been established by Ahura Mazdā's creative act, and Thraētaona's combat is a moment within that already-established cosmos rather than a moment of its founding.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the legendary-historical (rather than cosmogonic) framing of the Thraētaona combat in the Avestan attestations; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Avestan combat is therefore not a cosmogonic combat in the Marduk-Tiamat or Ninurta-Asag sense. The combat does sovereignty-and-cosmological work within an already-ordered cosmos rather than founding the cosmos through the combat itself, and the chapter's classification of the combat under the sovereignty / royal-legitimation function-type follows from this textual situation.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the sovereignty-and-cosmological (rather than cosmogonic) function of the Avestan Thraētaona combat; per chaoskampf scope.md on the sovereignty function-type.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Avestan attestations do not attest to Aži Dahāka's binding under Damāvand or to his eventual escape and final killing at the end of time. Those narrative elements appear in the surviving record only in the Pahlavi compilations, and the chapter therefore confines them to Movement II (Pahlavi narrative) and Movement III (Frashokereti) below.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the absence of the binding-and-final-killing narrative from the surviving Avestan attestations; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the same absence and on the Pahlavi narrative-expansion locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Across the three principal *Yašt* loci, the further *Yašt* allusions, and the *Vendīdād* references, the Avestan attestations together present a Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat that is morphologically stable (three-headed dragon, hero-slayer of the Āθβiia line), allusively sketched (dragon-slaying presupposed but not narrated), and theologically aligned with both Ahura Mazdā's order against Angra Mainyu's opposition and with the *xᵛarənah*-theology of legitimate Iranian kingship.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the morphological stability and theological alignment of the Avestan Thraētaona attestations; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

=== Movement II — The Pahlavi narrative

The Pahlavi *Bundahišn* — the "Book of the Primal Creation," redacted in late Sasanian / early Islamic Middle Persian — contains the fullest surviving narrative of Thraētaona's combat with Aži Dahāka, and the narrative there is to be read as a post-Achaemenid Middle Persian narrative-compilation rather than as an Avestan-period composition.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44, 46, on the *Bundahišn* as the principal Pahlavi narrative-compilation for the Thraētaona combat; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the *Bundahišn* dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44, 46

The post-Achaemenid date of the *Bundahišn* — late Sasanian / early Islamic, c. 8th–9th century CE — is structurally important for the chapter's argument. The *Bundahišn* contains many of the most striking narrative features of the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka tradition (the binding under Damāvand, the eventual escape, the revived hero), and the chapter must flag that these features are attested only in the late compilation.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the post-Achaemenid date of the *Bundahišn* as structurally important; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the late attestation of the binding-and-final-killing material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The qualification "post-Achaemenid" is not equivalent to "late and unreliable." Pahlavi material often preserves substantively earlier Avestan tradition, and the *Bundahišn*'s narrative may well preserve narrative-content older than its written redaction. What the chapter flags is that the *Bundahišn* is the surviving textual locus, not that the narrative-content originated in the Sasanian period.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the *Bundahišn*'s preservation of substantively older narrative-content; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The *Bundahišn*'s narrative of the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat opens with the dragon's prehistory. In the Pahlavi telling, Aži Dahāka is created or fashioned by Angra Mainyu / Ahriman as a destructive instrument against the good creation of Ahura Mazdā / Ohrmazd, and the dragon's monstrous morphology is a direct consequence of his Ahrimanic origin.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the *Bundahišn*'s account of Aži Dahāka's Ahrimanic origin; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

In the *Bundahišn* Aži Dahāka is described in the same triple-formulaic terms as in the Avestan hymn-allusions — three heads, six eyes, three jaws — and the Pahlavi narrative thereby preserves the Avestan morphological formula intact across the post-Achaemenid redaction.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 45–46, on the morphological continuity of the three-headed / six-eyed / three-jawed description from the Avestan to the Pahlavi tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 45-46

The Pahlavi *Bundahišn* gives the dragon a tyrannical-political role that the Avestan attestations only allusively presuppose. Aži Dahāka rules the world for a thousand years as a usurping tyrant, displacing legitimate rule, before Thraētaona arises to overthrow him and to restore legitimate sovereignty.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the dragon's thousand-year tyranny and the Pahlavi-narrative political frame; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The thousand-year tyranny of Aži Dahāka is the Pahlavi narrative's principal articulation of the royal-legitimation function-type. The combat against the dragon is presented as the overthrow of usurping tyranny and the restoration of legitimate rule, and the *Bundahišn* thereby gives the *xᵛarənah*-theology of *Yašt* 19 a concrete narrative shape.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the Pahlavi narrative's articulation of the royal-legitimation function-type; Forsyth 1987, on the same articulation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

In the Pahlavi narrative Thraētaona is the descendant of the Āθβiia line who arises to confront Aži Dahāka after the dragon's long tyranny, and his confrontation is presented as the divinely-sanctioned overthrow of the tyrant rather than as a private heroic feat.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on Thraētaona's divinely-sanctioned overthrow of Aži Dahāka in the Pahlavi narrative; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The combat itself in the *Bundahišn* is a single combat: Thraētaona alone confronts Aži Dahāka, and the narrative emphasises Thraētaona's mace as the instrument of the killing, with the mace-attack as the central narrative beat.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the single-combat structure and the mace-attack of the Pahlavi narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The *Bundahišn*'s narrative does not, however, conclude with Aži Dahāka's death. The dragon is not killed by Thraētaona but bound — held in living captivity — and the binding-rather-than-killing is a structurally load-bearing narrative choice that opens the way to the eschatological-pendant material of Movement III below.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the binding-rather-than-killing structure as load-bearing for the eschatological pendant; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The Pahlavi *Bundahišn* situates the binding at Mount Damāvand — the principal peak of the Alborz range in northern Iran — and Thraētaona is presented as the one who chains the dragon there and seals him beneath the mountain for the duration of the present cosmic age.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the Damāvand binding-locus in the Pahlavi narrative; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same localisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The Damāvand binding-locus gives Aži Dahāka a cosmogeographic role that the Avestan attestations do not attest. The dragon is not just a tyrant overthrown — he is a permanent under-mountain captive whose continuing presence in the sacred landscape structures the cosmography of the present age.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the cosmogeographic role of Aži Dahāka under Damāvand; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The cosmological function-type that the brief identifies as one of the chapter's two principal foci — alongside the royal-legitimation function — is grounded in this Pahlavi-narrative cosmogeographic role of the bound dragon under Damāvand. The dragon's containment is not just a one-off victory but a permanent feature of the structured cosmos.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the cosmological function-type as grounded in the Damāvand binding; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The binding-not-killing structure of the Pahlavi narrative is the structural feature that distinguishes the Iranian Aži Dahāka tradition from the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra tradition treated in the next chapter of the present book. In the Vedic tradition Vṛtra is killed outright, whereas in the Iranian tradition Aži Dahāka is bound and survives in captivity for the duration of the present age.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the binding-not-killing structure as distinguishing the Iranian from the Vedic tradition; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same distinguishing feature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

[INFERENCE: the binding-not-killing structure of the Iranian tradition opens the way to the Frashokereti eschatological pendant — a dragon that is bound rather than killed can escape and require a second-and-final killing — whereas the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra tradition's outright killing of the serpent forecloses any equivalent eschatological pendant; this structural observation is implicit in Watkins's Part V treatment but is not stated as a contrastive thesis there | source: Watkins 1995 Part V chs. 46–48 on the binding-not-killing structure; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the binding | scope: structural inference about the Iranian tradition's eschatological openness as a consequence of the binding-not-killing choice.]

The *Bundahišn*'s further treatment of the bound Aži Dahāka under Damāvand includes a continuing-threat dimension: the dragon, though bound, remains alive under the mountain, and his continuing presence is a continuing source of cosmic-religious anxiety that the eschatological pendant of Movement III then resolves.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the continuing-threat dimension of the bound Aži Dahāka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The *Dēnkard* book 7 — the Pahlavi prophetic-and-eschatological exposition — supplements the *Bundahišn* narrative with further detail on the long horizon that connects Thraētaona's binding of the dragon to the eventual eschatological release-and-killing, and the chapter therefore treats the *Dēnkard* book 7 material principally in Movement III below.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on *Dēnkard* book 7 as the Pahlavi prophetic-eschatological supplement to the *Bundahišn* narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The Pahlavi narrative tradition is not internally homogeneous: there are minor variations in detail across the *Bundahišn* manuscripts and across the related Pahlavi compilations (the *Dēnkard*, the *Pahlavi Rivayats*, and the *Mēnōg ī Xrad*), and Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph is the standard early-twentieth-century philological treatment of these textual variants.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the textual variation across the Pahlavi compilations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Pahlavi-narrative expansion of the Avestan hymn-allusions is best understood as a productive elaboration of presupposed-but-unwritten earlier tradition rather than as Sasanian invention. The morphological details (three heads, six eyes, three jaws) and the heroic-genealogical frame (Thraētaona of the Āθβiia line) are continuous with the Avestan attestations, and what the Pahlavi tradition adds is the narrative connective tissue that the Avestan hymns presupposed.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Pahlavi narrative as productive elaboration of presupposed earlier tradition; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The political-historical context in which the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* was redacted — late Sasanian Iran and the early Islamic centuries — shaped the narrative's emphases. The royal-legitimation function-type that the *xᵛarənah*-theology of *Yašt* 19 already underwrites receives in the Pahlavi narrative a more concrete tyrant-overthrow articulation that maps onto Sasanian-period legitimation discourse.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, on the late-Sasanian and early-Islamic context of the *Bundahišn* redaction; Lincoln 2012, on Iranian combat-myth as political-ideological discourse.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=passim

The legitimate-versus-tyrannical-rule frame of the Pahlavi narrative presents Aži Dahāka as a model of tyranny — a hostile alternative model of rule rather than a Gunkelian-style personification of chaos — and this articulation is one of the points at which Ballentine's terminological critique (that "chaos" is the wrong word for combat-myth adversaries) finds explicit textual purchase in the Iranian material.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, on Aži Dahāka as model of tyrannical rule; per chaoskampf scope.md on the Ballentine terminological critique.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=passim

The *Bundahišn*'s account also locates the Thraētaona combat within a long Iranian heroic-genealogical sequence — the Pīšdādian dynasty in the Pahlavi historiographical scheme, of which Thraētaona is a member — and the combat thereby becomes a moment within an extended legendary-historical sequence rather than an isolated mythic episode.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the Pīšdādian-dynastic frame of the Pahlavi narrative; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The Pahlavi narrative's location of Thraētaona within the Pīšdādian dynasty is the structural articulation that the New Persian Shāhnāmeh reception then takes up and recasts in Islamic-era literary form, and the chapter notes this connection only to flag it for the brief reception note below — the Shāhnāmeh material itself is out of primary scope per brief.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Shāhnāmeh reception as out of primary scope; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the Pahlavi-Shāhnāmeh connection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

In the *Bundahišn* the combat ends — within the present cosmic age — with the dragon's binding rather than his death, with the mountain Damāvand sealing him, and with Thraētaona departing the scene as the legitimate overthrower of the tyrant. The narrative arc of the combat-against-tyranny is closed within Movement II, but the dragon himself is not.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the binding-not-killing closure of Movement II in the Pahlavi narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

Movement II therefore closes with the dragon-tyrant overthrown, the legitimate Pīšdādian rule restored, and Aži Dahāka chained beneath Damāvand. The combat has done its sovereignty / royal-legitimation work and its cosmological / cosmogeographic work, but the dragon remains alive and the tradition retains an unresolved narrative thread that Movement III then takes up as the eschatological pendant.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the closure of Movement II with the dragon bound under Damāvand and on the unresolved thread that opens Movement III; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

=== Movement III — Frashokereti (the eschatological pendant)

_Frashokereti_ — the Avestan term conventionally translated "the final renovation," for which Middle Persian sources use the cognate _frašegird_ — designates the Zoroastrian end-time at which the long cosmic struggle of Ahura Mazdā's order against Angra Mainyu's opposition is resolved through a final reordering of the cosmos, the resurrection of the dead, and the elimination of evil.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on Frashokereti as the Iranian eschatological end-time; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on Frashokereti within the *Endzeit* category.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The Avestan locus for the Frashokereti material relevant to the Aži Dahāka tradition is principally *Yašt* 19 — the *Zamyād Yašt*, the same hymn that supplies the *xᵛarənah*-theology of Movement I — whose later stanzas treat the eschatological dimension of the *xᵛarənah* and the heroic-figures who will be revived for the final renovation.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on *Yašt* 19's eschatological material; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 47–48, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 19

The Avestan attestation of the Frashokereti-and-Aži-Dahāka material is, like the combat-attestations of Movement I, hymnic and allusive rather than narrative. The *Yašt* 19 stanzas presuppose an eschatological narrative without narrating it at length, and the fuller narrative survives only in the Pahlavi compilations.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the hymnic-allusive character of the Avestan Frashokereti material; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Pahlavi narrative-expansion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 19

The Pahlavi sources — principally the *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 — supply the fuller narrative of Frashokereti, including the specific narrative of Aži Dahāka's escape from his Damāvand binding and his second and final killing by the revived Kərəsāspa. This material is post-Achaemenid, and the chapter flags its late attestation explicitly.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 as the principal sources for the Frashokereti-and-Aži-Dahāka narrative; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the post-Achaemenid date of the Pahlavi narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The eschatological narrative opens with Aži Dahāka's escape from his Damāvand binding. The chains that Thraētaona placed on him at the close of Movement II will, at the end of cosmic time, weaken and break, and the dragon will emerge from beneath the mountain and resume his destructive activity in the world.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Pahlavi narrative of Aži Dahāka's escape from Damāvand at the end of cosmic time; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The dragon's escape is a moment of intense cosmic-religious crisis. In the Pahlavi narrative Aži Dahāka, freed from his binding, devours a third of humanity and a third of the animals — a destructive eruption that requires a heroic response of the same magnitude as the original Avestan combat.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the destructive eruption of the freed Aži Dahāka in the Pahlavi narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The heroic response is supplied not by Thraētaona — who in the Pahlavi narrative is no longer present at the end of cosmic time — but by Kərəsāspa, the second principal Avestan-Pahlavi heroic-figure, who has been preserved in a long stasis throughout the present age and is revived at Frashokereti specifically for this final combat.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on Kərəsāspa as the Frashokereti dragon-slayer; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Kərəsāspa's heroic-genealogical role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

Kərəsāspa — Avestan with the schwa-vowels ə (preserved in the chapter's glossary discipline at first scholarly mention and rendered as "Keresaspa" in subsequent body prose) — is the Avestan hero whose long-stasis-and-revival narrative supplies the Frashokereti combat with its second-killer figure.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Kərəsāspa's name and heroic role; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the schwa-vowel diacritical discipline.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

In the Avestan and Pahlavi traditions Keresaspa is a hero of the legendary-prehistorical horizon — like Thraētaona, a member of the Pīšdādian-frame heroic genealogy in the Pahlavi reckoning — and he is the second Iranian hero (alongside Thraētaona) whose combat-record places him among the dragon-slayers.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Kərəsāspa as a second Iranian dragon-slayer alongside Thraētaona; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 47, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Keresaspa's long-stasis state — neither dead nor active, but held in a divinely-protected sleep through the duration of the present cosmic age — is the narrative device that connects his Avestan-period heroic-figure to the Frashokereti eschatological combat, and it is treated as a deliberate provision for the final combat rather than as accidental.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 47–48, on Kərəsāspa's long-stasis as deliberate provision for the Frashokereti combat; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 47-48

The revival of Keresaspa at Frashokereti — the moment at which his long stasis ends and he rises to confront the freed Aži Dahāka — is the principal Iranian instance of a revived-hero motif and is the Frashokereti narrative's structural pivot from the present cosmic age into the final renovation.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on Kərəsāspa's revival as the structural pivot of the Frashokereti narrative; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The second and final combat — Keresaspa against the freed Aži Dahāka at the end of cosmic time — is presented in the Pahlavi narrative as the definitive resolution of the long combat-thread that Thraētaona's Avestan-period victory left unresolved. The dragon is now killed outright, the binding-not-killing closure of Movement II is converted into a killing-and-final-resolution closure of Movement III.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on Kərəsāspa's killing of Aži Dahāka as the definitive closure of the combat-thread; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The killing of Aži Dahāka by Keresaspa is the Iranian tradition's eschatological combat — the combat that closes cosmic time — and it is the principal Iranian instance of the eschatological function-type that the chaoskampf scope.md identifies as the fourth of the four order-making function-types treated in the book.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the eschatological function-type of the Kərəsāspa-Aži Dahāka combat; per chaoskampf scope.md on the eschatological function-type.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

After the killing, the Pahlavi narrative continues with the wider Frashokereti scenario: the resurrection of the dead, the molten-metal ordeal that purifies the resurrected, the reunification of soul and body, and the final reordering of the cosmos under Ahura Mazdā's sovereignty without remaining opposition from Angra Mainyu.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the wider Frashokereti scenario in the Pahlavi narrative; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the eschatological-renovation framework.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The Frashokereti narrative thereby gives the chapter's Aži Dahāka material a complete arc. The Avestan-Pahlavi tradition opens with the dragon at large in the world, has Thraētaona overthrow and bind him under Damāvand at the close of Movement II, retains him as a continuing under-mountain captive throughout the present age, has him escape and erupt destructively at the end of time, and closes with Keresaspa's revived-hero killing of him in Movement III.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46–48, on the complete narrative arc of the Aži Dahāka material from Movement II through Movement III; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same arc.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46-48

The two killings — Thraētaona's Avestan-period overthrow-and-binding (Movement II) and Keresaspa's Frashokereti final killing (Movement III) — are not duplicates of one another but stand in a structural pendant-relation. The first combat establishes the present age, the second combat closes it, and the dragon's binding-and-eventual-escape is the narrative thread that links the two.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the structural pendant-relation of the two combats; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The pendant-structure of the two combats means that the Iranian tradition is in an important sense uniquely double — articulated with both an *Urzeit* combat (Thraētaona at the founding of the present age) and an *Endzeit* combat (Keresaspa at its close) — and Gunkel's 1895 *Urzeit*-und-*Endzeit* parallelism, which the title of his monograph names, finds in the Iranian tradition one of its clearest exemplifications.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the *Urzeit*-und-*Endzeit* parallelism in the Iranian tradition; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the doubled-combat structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil II

The Iranian doubled-combat structure is comparatively significant for the chapter's wider book-context. It is the structural feature that aligns the Iranian material with the Norse Ragnarök tradition (where Thor's earlier encounters with Jörmungandr are recapitulated by the final Ragnarök combat) and with the Christian Apocalypse of John's chapter 12 / chapter 20 binding-and-final-loosing of the dragon-Satan figure.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the doubled-combat structural alignment with Ragnarök; Forsyth 1987, on the alignment with Revelation; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same comparative alignment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

[INFERENCE: the Iranian doubled-combat structure (Movement II Thraētaona-binding + Movement III Kərəsāspa-final-killing) is a particularly close structural-and-functional cognate of the Revelation 20 binding-and-final-loosing-and-final-defeat structure of the dragon-Satan, suggesting that the long-discussed possibility of Iranian-to-Jewish-and-Christian influence during the Achaemenid and Hellenistic periods has at least one structural pivot in the Aži Dahāka material; this is treated as a long-standing scholarly question rather than as an established conclusion | source: Forsyth 1987 on the Iranian-to-Jewish-and-Christian influence question; Watkins 1995 Part V ch. 48 on the doubled-combat structure | scope: structural inference about the comparative-influence question, not a claim of established direct influence.]

The temporal-layering caveat applies particularly strongly to the Frashokereti material. The binding-under-Damāvand, the eventual escape, and the revived-hero second-killing are most fully attested in the post-Achaemenid Pahlavi compilations, and any comparative argument about Iranian-to-Jewish-and-Christian influence must reckon with the fact that the textual fixation of these features is later than the Achaemenid-and-Hellenistic period in which the influence-question is conventionally located.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the temporal-layering caveat as load-bearing for any influence-argument; Forsyth 1987, on the same caveat; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the dating of the Pahlavi-attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=passim

The temporal-layering caveat does not, however, foreclose the possibility that the narrative-content of the Frashokereti tradition is substantively earlier than its Pahlavi textual fixation. As Watkins notes, Pahlavi material often preserves substantively earlier Avestan-and-pre-Avestan tradition, and the structural pendant-relation between Movement II and Movement III is plausibly older than its surviving Pahlavi attestation.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on Pahlavi preservation of substantively earlier tradition; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same preservation-question.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The eschatological function-type of the Frashokereti combat is one of the chapter's principal forward-links to Part II of the present book. The comparative chapter on eschatological displacement (Part II C4) treats the Iranian Frashokereti material alongside Norse Ragnarök and the Christian Apocalypse of John as the three principal Indo-European-and-related eschatological-combat traditions.footnote:[Per chaoskampf scope.md on Part II C4 as the eschatological-displacement comparative chapter; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Frashokereti-Ragnarök-Revelation comparative grouping; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the same grouping.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The Frashokereti combat is also one of the book's principal instances of a binding-followed-by-eventual-final-killing structure — distinct from outright-killing combats like Indra-Vṛtra and from cyclical-maintenance combats like Ra-Apep — and it is therefore structurally load-bearing for the book's typology of order-making function-types within the chaoskampf category.footnote:[Per chaoskampf scope.md on the four order-making function-types; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the binding-and-final-killing structure as a distinct function-type.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The cosmic-renovation that follows the Frashokereti combat — the resurrection of the dead, the molten-metal purification, the elimination of evil — gives the Iranian tradition's eschatological closure a more positively-articulated form than the Norse Ragnarök's near-extinction-and-renewal. The Iranian eschatology is renovation rather than destruction-and-rebirth, and the combat against Aži Dahāka is the precondition for the renovation rather than its destructive opening.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the renovation character of the Iranian eschatology; Gunkel 1895, Teil II, on the comparative typology of eschatological closures.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The Iranian doubled-combat structure (Movement II + Movement III) gives Aži Dahāka a long narrative life. The dragon is bound rather than killed at the close of the Avestan-period combat, persists in living captivity throughout the present age, escapes at the end of time, and is finally killed by the revived Keresaspa — a dramatic arc that few other combat-myth dragons in the book sustain.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46–48, on the long narrative life of Aži Dahāka across the doubled-combat structure; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46-48

The chapter's treatment of Movement III therefore closes with the dragon definitively killed, the cosmos renovated, and the long combat-thread of the Iranian tradition resolved — a closure that the Avestan-only attestations would not, on their own evidence, supply but that the Pahlavi narrative-compilation reconstructs from the Avestan allusions and from whatever earlier narrative-tradition the *Bundahišn* and *Dēnkard* book 7 preserved.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the closure of the Iranian combat-thread at Frashokereti; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Pahlavi reconstruction from the Avestan allusions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

=== Indo-Iranian cognancy

The Avestan name Thraētaona and the Vedic name Trita Āptya are philologically cognate. Both derive from the Indo-Iranian numeral root *tri-* "three" with a heroic-name-forming suffix, and both belong to a heroic-figure who slays a three-headed serpent — the philological cognancy is one of the clearest in the Indo-Iranian comparative repertory.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Thraētaona / Trita Āptya philological cognancy; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same cognancy; West 2007, ch. 5, on the Indo-Iranian dragon-slayer cognancy; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Vedic Trita Āptya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Avestan patronymic Āθβiia (Thraētaona's father-line) is itself philologically cognate with the Vedic Āptya (the patronymic-or-epithet of Trita): the cognancy operates not just at the level of the hero-name but also at the level of the heroic-genealogical structure within which the hero is placed.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Āθβiia / Āptya cognancy; West 2007, ch. 5, on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Vedic Trita Āptya is the older Indo-Iranian dragon-slayer figure that the Indra-Vṛtra material superseded. In the Rigveda, Trita is preserved as a marginal figure who shares some of Indra's combat-attributes (RV 10.99.6 in particular has Trita slaying a three-headed adversary), and the textual situation suggests that an earlier Trita-tradition has been displaced by the dominant Indra-tradition.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Trita Āptya at RV 10.99.6 as a marginalised earlier dragon-slayer; West 2007, ch. 5, on the Trita-Indra succession; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same succession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.99.6

Rigveda 10.99.6 — the principal Vedic locus for Trita Āptya as dragon-slayer — has Trita slaying a three-headed adversary, and the three-headed-ness of the serpent in this Vedic passage is the principal structural parallel to Aži Dahāka's three-headed-ness in the Avestan tradition.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 10.99.6 and Trita's slaying of the three-headed adversary; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 45, on the structural three-headed parallel.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.99.6

The structural parallel between Trita's three-headed adversary (Rigveda 10.99.6) and Thraētaona's three-headed Aži Dahāka (Yašt 5.33–35 and the wider Avestan corpus) is the principal Indo-Iranian narrative parallel that, alongside the philological cognancies, supports the reconstruction of an inherited Indo-Iranian dragon-slaying tradition.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the Indo-Iranian narrative-and-philological parallel; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Vedic side of the parallel.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-45

[INFERENCE: the Avestan Thraētaona / Vedic Trita cognancy together with the structural parallel between three-headed Aži Dahāka and Trita's three-headed adversary at RV 10.99.6 supports the reconstruction of an inherited Indo-Iranian three-headed-serpent-slaying narrative that predates both the Avestan and Vedic textual traditions and that is independently preserved in each — an inference that Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph and Watkins's 1995 monograph both develop, and that aligns the Iranian Aži Dahāka material with the Vedic Trita Āptya material as joint witnesses to a pre-textual Indo-Iranian inheritance | source: Benveniste and Renou 1934 passim; Watkins 1995 Part V chs. 44–45; West 2007 ch. 5 | scope: synthesis-claim about the Indo-Iranian inheritance behind the two textual traditions.]

The Avestan word *aži* and the Vedic word *áhi* — both translatable as "serpent" — are likewise philologically cognate, and both occur as the generic-noun of the dragon-slaying combat formula in their respective traditions: Avestan *jan- aži-* "smite the serpent" parallels Vedic *han- ahi-* "smite the serpent."footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the *aži* / *áhi* cognancy; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the formulaic cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Vedic combat-formula *áhann áhim* ("he killed the serpent") in Rigveda 1.32 — the Indra-Vṛtra hymn — is, on Watkins's analysis, the principal Indo-European philological evidence for an inherited *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula, and the Avestan *jan-aži-* of the Thraētaona tradition is the Iranian formulaic reflex of the same inherited formula.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the *áhann áhim* / *jan-aži-* formulaic cognancy as evidence for the IE *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Vedic side at RV 1.32.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-45

The Avestan victory-god Vərəθraγna and the Vedic Indra epithet Vṛtrahán — both translatable as "smiter of Vṛtra" / "victory-bringer," and both philologically derived from the same root *vṛ-* "to obstruct" / "to enclose" — are a third pillar of the Indo-Iranian comparative philology that connects the Avestan and Vedic combat-traditions.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vərəθraγna / Vṛtrahán cognancy as the central argument of the monograph; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 44, on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The three philological pillars of the Indo-Iranian comparative reading — Thraētaona / Trita, *aži* / *áhi*, Vərəθraγna / Vṛtrahán — are mutually reinforcing. Any one of them would on its own argue for an Indo-Iranian inherited combat-formula, and their joint occurrence within a single comparative argument is what makes Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph the foundational work in the field.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the joint-argumentative force of the three philological pillars; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Watkins's 1995 *How to Kill a Dragon* takes Benveniste and Renou's Indo-Iranian comparative reading and embeds it within a wider Indo-European *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formulaic argument that adds Greek, Hittite, Norse, and Old English reflexes to the Indo-Iranian core. In Watkins's argument, the Iranian Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka material is the principal Indo-Iranian witness to the IE formula.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the Iranian Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka material as the principal Indo-Iranian witness to the IE *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

The Indo-Iranian cognancy means that the Iranian Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka chapter and the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra chapter that follows it (chapter 14) treat closely-cognate material from two divergent textual traditions, and the joint presence of the two chapters in the present book is a deliberate methodological choice that displays the Indo-Iranian comparative argument in full.footnote:[Per chaoskampf scope.md on the Iran-and-India clusters; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the joint Indo-Iranian display; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The structural differences between the Iranian and Vedic combat-narratives — the Iranian binding-not-killing structure versus the Vedic outright-killing, the Iranian doubled-combat across an *Urzeit*-and-*Endzeit* axis versus the Vedic single-combat — are themselves philologically and comparatively significant. The two traditions divergently elaborated a shared Indo-Iranian inheritance, and the divergences are reading-keys to the distinct theological-and-cosmological pressures of the Avestan and Vedic priestly traditions.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 46, 48, on the structural divergences between the Iranian and Vedic elaborations; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same divergences.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 46, 48

The Iranian tradition's preservation of an explicit eschatological combat (Frashokereti) is, on the Indo-Iranian comparative reading, a feature that the Vedic tradition does not retain in any closely-comparable form, and the Iranian Frashokereti material is therefore one of the points at which the Indo-Iranian inheritance has been productively elaborated in distinct directions in the two traditions.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 48, on the Iranian Frashokereti as a distinctive elaboration not retained in the Vedic tradition; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 48

The Iranian tradition's location of the dragon under Mount Damāvand — a real cosmogeographic feature of the Iranian landscape — likewise has no close Vedic parallel. The Vedic Indra-Vṛtra combat is geographically placed only loosely (in the mountainous regions where rivers are dammed), and the Iranian under-Damāvand binding is a distinctive Iranian articulation of the dragon's continuing cosmographic presence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the Iranian Damāvand-localisation as without close Vedic parallel; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

[INFERENCE: the Iranian-Vedic divergence of structure (binding-and-final-killing in Iran versus outright-killing in India) is plausibly correlated with the divergent theological frames of the two traditions — Zoroastrian dualism with its sharp Ahura-Mazdā / Angra-Mainyu opposition and its eschatological resolution requires a dragon that survives to be finally killed at the end of time, whereas the Vedic tradition's less-dualistic theological frame does not require an eschatological pendant — this is a structural-theological correlation that Benveniste and Renou and Watkins each individually note but neither formalises as a comparative thesis | source: Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the Iranian dualistic frame; Watkins 1995 Part V chs. 46, 48 on the structural divergences; West 2007 ch. 5 on the comparative typology | scope: structural-theological inference about the Iranian-Vedic divergence as theologically motivated rather than accidental.]

The Indo-Iranian cognancy material is the chapter's principal forward-link to chapter 14 of the present book (Indra vs. Vṛtra) and to the Part II C5 comparative chapter on the Indo-European *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula, where the Iranian and Vedic material together with the wider IE reflexes treated by Watkins receive a unified comparative treatment.footnote:[Per chaoskampf scope.md on Part II C5 as the comparative chapter on the IE *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the comparative grouping; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Ch14 forward-link.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-45

The Indo-Iranian comparative reading also has a typological-comparative dimension that connects the Iranian Aži Dahāka material to the Hurro-Hittite Illuyanka tradition treated in chapter 6 of the present book. Watkins's *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula treats Illuyanka as another reflex of the inherited IE formula, and Aži Dahāka and Illuyanka therefore stand alongside one another within Watkins's wider Indo-European-and-adjacent comparative repertory.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V on the Iranian and Hurro-Hittite reflexes within the IE *HERO SLAY SERPENT* formula; per chaoskampf scope.md on Ch6 as the Illuyanka chapter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48

=== Reception (briefly)

The most influential post-Avestan-and-Pahlavi reception of the Aži Dahāka tradition is the New Persian Shāhnāmeh of Firdowsi, composed c. 1000 CE, which recasts Thraētaona as Fereydun and recasts Aži Dahāka as the tyrant Zahhāk in an Islamic-era epic-literary form. The chapter flags this reception's existence but does not narrate its content, in line with the brief's stipulation that the Shāhnāmeh material is out of primary scope.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Shāhnāmeh as Islamic-era reception out of primary scope; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the Shāhnāmeh as the principal post-Pahlavi reception; per chaoskampf scope.md on the Period exclusion of post-medieval primary material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The Shāhnāmeh's recasting of the tradition operates at the level of name-reformations (Thraētaona → Frēdōn → Fereydun; Aži Dahāka → Dahāg → Zahhāk), at the level of narrative-amplification (the dragon-tyrant is given a distinctive serpent-shoulders motif in the Shāhnāmeh that is a Firdowsi-period elaboration), and at the level of literary register (the New Persian *masnavi* verse-form rather than the Pahlavi prose-compilation of the *Bundahišn*).footnote:[Per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the onomastic chains; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Shāhnāmeh recasting at multiple levels.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

Because the Shāhnāmeh's elaborations are post-medieval Islamic-era literary productions, and because the brief stipulates that the chapter flags rather than narrates the reception, the chapter does not treat the Zahhāk-of-Firdowsi material as a primary witness to the Iranian tradition. It is a reception-witness that the present book reserves for its concluding reception coda rather than admitting into the Part I story-chapter on the Avestan-Pahlavi material.footnote:[Per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the chapter's flagging-rather-than-narrating treatment; per chaoskampf scope.md on the reception coda for post-medieval material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V ch. 46

The chapter's discipline on the Shāhnāmeh boundary is therefore that the New Persian Fereydun-Zahhāk material is named only where its existence is structurally relevant to the Avestan-Pahlavi material's reception-history, that the Avestan-Pahlavi diacritical forms (Thraētaona and Aži Dahāka) are used throughout the body, and that the New Persian forms (Fereydun and Zahhāk) appear only in this brief reception note.footnote:[Per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the Avestan-Pahlavi-vs-New-Persian onomastic discipline; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Shāhnāmeh boundary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=passim

The Shāhnāmeh reception is one of several long-trajectory reception threads that the Avestan-Pahlavi Aži Dahāka material has carried into later Iranian-and-related cultural spheres. The dragon-tyrant motif has further reflexes in Armenian, Caucasian, and Central Asian traditions (Russell 1987 on the Armenian reception is the chaoskampf scope.md's Vahagn / Vishaps anchor), and the chapter notes the existence of these threads as a forward-link to the Vahagn-and-Vishaps family-chapter elsewhere in the book.footnote:[Per chaoskampf scope.md on the Vahagn / Vishaps family-chapter as a related-reception locus; Watkins 1995, Part V ch. 46, on the long-trajectory reception of the Aži Dahāka material.]
