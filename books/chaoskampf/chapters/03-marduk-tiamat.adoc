== Marduk vs. Tiamat: Enūma Eliš

=== Source overview

Enūma Eliš is a Babylonian creation epic of seven tablets. By the Akkadian convention of naming a composition from its opening words, the title comes from its first phrase, "When on high." That phrase opens both the text itself and any modern reading of it, and it has come to function as the work's standard label.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction; Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The text is the canonical case of what Hermann Gunkel in 1895 named Chaoskampf — the combat-with-chaos pattern that organises one strand of ancient Near Eastern cosmology. When the comparative literature speaks of Chaoskampf as a category, this is the work it has chiefly in mind.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, with Marduk vs. Tiamat as the central case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The critical edition used throughout this chapter is W. G. Lambert's *Babylonian Creation Myths*, published in 2013 as volume sixteen in the Mesopotamian Civilizations series. Lambert's volume gives the cuneiform transliteration, an English translation, the manuscript score that integrates the witnesses tablet by tablet, and a running commentary. It is the apparatus on which any reading of Enūma Eliš now depends.footnote:[Lambert 2013, whole volume.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=whole volume

Lambert's edition replaces Philippe Talon's 2005 edition and the older King 1902 edition. It is the standard critical text of Enūma Eliš, and earlier editions are now read against it rather than alongside it.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The working English translation cited in this chapter is Benjamin Foster's "Epic of Creation," published in the third edition of *Before the Muses* in 2005, on pages 436 to 486.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 436–486, "Epic of Creation."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-486

Foster's translation is more narratively fluid than Lambert's conservative rendering. It is therefore preferred here for body prose, while Lambert is preferred for textual and philological matters. The two translations agree on the substance and disagree mainly in their English register.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote on translation principles; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 436

Lambert's 2013 manuscript catalogue lists more than seventy witnesses to Enūma Eliš, drawn from Assyrian and Babylonian libraries. By the standards of cuneiform literature, this is a great many copies, and the unusually rich attestation is itself part of the story: the text was widely transmitted because the institutions that copied it had reasons to keep it alive.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The bulk of these witnesses come from Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian copies of the first millennium BCE. The composition itself is older — the dating debate is taken up below — but the surviving manuscripts are mostly from the centuries that followed the first millennium BCE rise of the great Mesopotamian libraries.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on witness distribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

Significant Neo-Assyrian copies were recovered from the libraries of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh and from the temple libraries at Aššur, Kalhu, and Sultantepe. The seventh-century Assyrian crown gathered Babylonian literature as a matter of imperial policy, and Enūma Eliš was among the texts that travelled north for that reason.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the Neo-Assyrian witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

Neo-Babylonian witnesses come from Babylon, Borsippa, and other centres, and continue into the late Achaemenid and Seleucid periods. The text continued to be copied in Babylonia long after political independence had been lost, which says something about the depth of its embedding in the local religious life.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the Neo-Babylonian and later witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The composition's date of authorship is debated. The prevailing view places it in the late second millennium BCE under Nebuchadnezzar I, who reigned roughly between 1125 and 1104 BCE; both earlier and later dates have been argued, and the question is taken up again below in the discussion of variants and reception.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the dating debate; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis ties the composition to the recovery of the cult-statue of Marduk from Elam and to a programme of Babylonian theological assertion. The hypothesis links text to history at a specific moment: a king who has just won back his city's god commissions the epic that explains why that god outranks all others.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the Nebuchadnezzar I hypothesis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The text was recited liturgically in the Babylonian _akītu_ New Year festival, on the fourth day, by the high priest before the cult-image of Marduk in Esagil. The recitation is not a marginal use; it is the use the surviving record actually attests.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu recitation; Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu ritual recitation is attested in late ritual texts and is the principal performative context of Enūma Eliš in the surviving record. Whatever else the text was — a school exercise, a library exemplar, a royal commission — it was, demonstrably, a New Year liturgy.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the ritual texts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

Bruce Lincoln treats Enūma Eliš as a paradigm case of myth as political-ideological work, with the cosmogony serving the legitimation of Babylonian kingship. Lincoln's reading is one of the principal modern frames for the text, and it sets up a debate that this chapter takes up at length below.footnote:[Lincoln 2012, essays on combat-myth as political ideology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

Debra Scoggins Ballentine's 2015 *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* argues that "chaos" is the wrong analytical category for Tiamat: the adversary is better read as an alternative-sovereign claimant rather than as the embodiment of disorder.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1 on Tiamat as alternative-sovereign rather than chaos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

Ballentine's reading shifts the centre of gravity from cosmology to politics: the question is who reigns, not order versus disorder. The shift is consequential for how one reads the combat itself, and it is the principal alternative to Gunkel's framing in the contemporary literature.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

Scurlock and Beal's 2013 collected volume reassesses Gunkel's Chaoskampf hypothesis across multiple traditions, with several essays treating Enūma Eliš as the type-specimen. The reassessment is not a wholesale rejection of Gunkel; it is a series of qualifications by working specialists.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim

Neil Forsyth places Enūma Eliš within a long combat-myth sequence running from Sumerian Ninurta material through the Hebrew Bible into Christian apocalyptic. Forsyth's frame is the long arc; the present chapter focuses on the Mesopotamian middle of that arc but takes Forsyth's wider sequence as the comparative horizon.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on Enūma Eliš within the combat-myth sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Amar Annus argues that Enūma Eliš is a deliberate literary descendant of the Standard Babylonian Anzû Epic, reusing and outbidding the Ninurta-vs-Anzû combat in a Marduk-centric cosmogony. Chapter 2 of this book treated the Anzû Epic as the antecedent; the present chapter treats Enūma Eliš as the descendant, and Annus's argument is the principal evidence that the relation between the two is conscious literary inheritance rather than general pattern-resemblance. Several scenes the chapter walks through below — the threefold-refusal pattern in Tablet II, the demoted Anzû in Tiamat's army-list, the closing fifty-names doxology — are concrete cases in which Annus's case for inheritance can be checked against the text.footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction on the Anzû-Marduk genealogy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

=== Setting and context

Marduk is the patron god of Babylon. He was originally a city-god of relatively limited regional importance, and rose in the second millennium to become the chief god of the Babylonian pantheon. The arc of his career as a deity, from local figure to head of a pantheon, is the religious correlate of his city's political rise.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on Marduk's rise; Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

Marduk's elevation in Enūma Eliš is the narrative complement of the historical elevation of Babylon as imperial capital. The text and the political claim are co-constituted: the epic explains why Marduk reigns, and the city's pre-eminence is the practical fact that the explanation underwrites. The mutual constitution is part of why both Lincoln and Ballentine treat the epic as political theology rather than as cosmology in the abstract; the text exists because a city had to make a claim on the cosmos, and the cosmos in Enūma Eliš has the shape that the city's claim required.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

In the Mesopotamian theogonic background, Marduk's father is Ea, god of wisdom and the deep fresh waters of the Apsû. Ea is the figure on whom much of the older Mesopotamian theology of cunning and creation already rests, and Enūma Eliš leans on that prior theology in fashioning Marduk's pedigree. The new god is given a known father, in a known generation, so that his rise can be charted against the older system rather than presented as a break with it.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, pp. 439–440.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Ea in Akkadian context corresponds to Sumerian Enki, but Enūma Eliš is an Akkadian composition and uses the Akkadian theonym throughout. The point is one of usage rather than substance: the same god goes by Ea in this text, and the chapter follows that usage.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, p. 439.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Marduk's mother is Damkina, Ea's consort, and his birth at the centre of the Apsû is signalled by elaborate praise-formulae. The praise-formulae are themselves a feature of the composition, marking the new god's arrival as an event of cosmic consequence.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Tiamat is the primordial salt-sea, the cosmogonic mother who, with Apsû the fresh-water, generates the first gods. She is, in the opening pages, the universe's first parent on the mother-side of the pairing — and the role she occupies at the start is one of the points the Ballentine reading uses to argue that what she becomes in the second half of the text is a function of provocation and politics rather than primordial nature.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, pp. 436–438.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

The Akkadian noun *tiāmtu* / *tâmtu* (sea) is etymologically related to Tiamat as a personal-divine name. Gunkel's 1895 argument turns on the cognancy with Hebrew _tehôm_, the deep of Genesis 1:2 — a linguistic fact that, since Gunkel, has been the principal evidence for a connection between the Babylonian and Israelite traditions. The cognancy is etymologically secure; what remains contested is what to do with it, and that question belongs to Chapter 8's treatment of the Hebrew material rather than to the present chapter's reading of the Babylonian text.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I on the *tehôm*-Tiamat cognancy; Day 1985 for the later debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

Apsû is the personified primordial fresh-water, Tiamat's consort and male counterpart in the opening generation. The two waters together — salt and fresh — are the entire universe at the moment the text begins.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, pp. 436–437.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

The Akkadian noun *apsû* names the cosmic fresh-water region beneath the earth. The same word in Sumerian context is Abzu, but the Akkadian form with circumflex is the one used in Enūma Eliš, and the chapter follows it.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

The Anzû Epic is the most direct Mesopotamian antecedent of Enūma Eliš. The scholarship treats Marduk's combat as a deliberate rewriting of Ninurta's combat against Anzû (cross-ref: Ch. 2). The relation is one of literary inheritance: the Marduk poet had the older epic in front of him.footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction on the Anzû-to-Enūma-Eliš genealogy; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

The Tablet of Destinies, the cosmic decree-token familiar from the Anzû Epic, returns in Enūma Eliš as the object Tiamat bestows on Qingu and that Marduk reclaims after the combat. The Tablet's reappearance is one of the clearest signs of the inheritance: the same artefact migrates from the older epic into the new one, with its narrative function preserved.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Foster 2005, p. 459.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet IV

The akītu festival, at which Enūma Eliš was recited, was the Babylonian New Year ritual celebrated in the spring month of Nisan, and centred on the cult of Marduk in his Babylon temple Esagil. The festival and the text are bound together; what one is for the other helps define.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu New Year festival.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

Esagil is Marduk's temple in Babylon, named in the epic as founded after the combat. In Babylonian theology it is the cosmic axis where heaven and earth meet — and the epic itself accounts for that status by recounting the temple's origin. The temple, the city, the kingship, and the text are bound together in a single religious-political system, and Enūma Eliš is one of the documents that holds that system together by giving it a foundational story.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet VI; Foster 2005, pp. 472–473.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet VI

With the source overview and the setting in place, the chapter now turns to the text itself. The discussion follows the seven tablets in three movements: Tablets I and II for the theogony and the killing of Apsû; Tablets III through V for the assembly's elevation of Marduk and the combat with Tiamat; and Tablets VI and VII for the creation of humans, the founding of Babylon, and the fifty-names ceremony that closes the epic. After the textual walk-through, the chapter takes up the Gunkel/Ballentine debate, the akītu ritual frame, and the variants and reception of the work — three discussions for which the previous narrative is the necessary background.

=== Movement I — Theogony and the killing of Apsû

==== Tablet I: primordial waters, the first gods, the killing of Apsû

Tablet I opens with the celebrated incipit "When on high" (*enūma eliš*). The setting is primordial: no gods, no world, nothing yet distinguished. The text begins, in the literal sense, before the beginning, and that emptiness is the surface against which the rest of the action takes place.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 436, Tablet I opening; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 436

The opening describes the mingling of two primordial waters, Apsû the fresh and Tiamat the salt, undifferentiated into gods, lands, or names. The two waters are not metaphors; they are the material of the universe in its undeveloped state, the substance from which everything else will be derived.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 436–437, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-437

The first generation of gods is born from this mingling. Lahmu and Lahamu are identified as a paired male-female deity-couple at the dawn of differentiation. With them, the universe acquires its first inhabitants and its first distinction between persons.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 437, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 437

The second generation is Anšar and Kišar, sky-horizon and earth-horizon, who in turn beget Anu, the sky-god. The names themselves carry a programme: a sky-horizon paired with an earth-horizon describes the geometry the cosmos is about to acquire.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 437, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 437

Anu begets Nudimmud, the alternative name for Ea in this composition, characterised by surpassing wisdom. The wisdom that will save the younger gods, when the crisis comes, is named here at the moment of Ea's birth.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 437–438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 437-438

The theogonic sequence — Apsû / Tiamat → Lahmu / Lahamu → Anšar / Kišar → Anu → Ea — establishes a four-tier descent from the primordial waters to the wisdom-god who will become Marduk's father. The genealogy is the spine of the rest of the chapter: every one of these names will matter when the assembly is convened.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 436–438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-438

The younger gods, born within Apsû and Tiamat, are noisy. They disturb the repose of their parents. The disturbance is not yet a moral failing; it is a fact of generational difference, but it sets up the conflict that follows.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 438

The text gives Apsû's perspective as exasperation. The noise of the younger gods makes rest impossible, and Apsû resolves to destroy them so that quiet may return. The motive is given without disguise: he wants peace, and the cost is to be paid by his own offspring.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 438-439

Apsû takes counsel with his vizier Mummu, and Mummu encourages the plan: the younger gods are to be annihilated. The vizier's role is the bureaucratic one — he ratifies and amplifies the decision rather than initiating it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 438

Apsû then approaches Tiamat and proposes the destruction of their offspring. Tiamat refuses. She will not destroy what she has herself brought forth. The refusal matters: it places her, at the start, on the side of the children rather than the parental impulse to silence them.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 438-439

Tiamat's refusal is the first divergence between Apsû and Tiamat in the text. It is later cited as evidence that her motive in the second movement is grief and obligation rather than primordial malice — a textual datum on which the Ballentine reading of Tiamat-as-rival-sovereign turns.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The younger gods learn of Apsû's plan and are paralysed with fear. They sit silent. The reaction is the first of several scenes in which the older generations of gods cannot move, while the younger Ea is the only one who finds an answer.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Ea, the wise one, alone among the younger gods devises a counter-strategy. He composes a powerful incantation, recites it over Apsû, and casts him into a sleep. The mode of the action is significant: Ea defeats the primordial father by speech and ritual, not by force.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

With Apsû asleep, Ea binds him, strips him of his crown and divine radiance, and kills him. The sequence — bind, strip, kill — will be echoed in Marduk's later treatment of Tiamat, and the parallel is part of how the epic structures its two combats.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Ea also captures Mummu, Apsû's vizier, binds him, and takes his nose-rope as a leash. The image of the leashed vizier is concrete and humiliating, and the text dwells on it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Ea then establishes his dwelling on top of the slain Apsû's corpse, founding what becomes the cosmic Apsû — the abode of the wisdom-god above the primordial fresh-water. The defeated body becomes the floor of a divine residence; the territory of the slain enemy becomes habitable real estate.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 439–440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 439-440

The killing of Apsû is the first combat in the epic. It establishes the pattern: the threatened younger god uses incantation and binding to subdue and dismember a primordial parent. Forsyth treats the episode as a structural rehearsal for the Marduk-Tiamat combat that follows: same shape, smaller scale.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Within Ea's new dwelling, in the Apsû, Marduk is born to Ea and Damkina. The text describes his birth in terms of unprecedented brilliance and divine perfection. The poet means the new god to be visibly different from the gods who have come before; the description is not a mere flourish but a thematic setup.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Marduk is described as having four eyes and four ears, of surpassing physical proportions, and as radiant from birth. The catalogue of features is meant to mark him as outside the ordinary divine type, and the image becomes the standard portrait of Marduk in later iconography.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Anu, Marduk's grandfather, fashions the four winds as a gift, and gives them to Marduk to play with. The winds will later prove decisive in the combat. What looks like a child's toy in Tablet I is in fact the principal weapon Marduk will use in Tablet IV — the text plants its weapons in advance, and the audience is meant to recognise the gift as foreshadowing.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Marduk's playing with the winds disturbs Tiamat in her primordial waters. The disturbance compounds the grievance of the older gods who have been agitating Tiamat to act. What had been the noise of the younger gods has now become a more pointed disturbance, and the texture of the conflict tightens.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 440–441, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 440-441

A faction of gods aligned with the slain Apsû now reproaches Tiamat for her passivity. She did not stand up for Apsû when he was killed, and now her offspring's noise compounds the insult. The reproach is what changes Tiamat's posture: it is the political pressure of an aggrieved party, not a primordial impulse.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 441, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 441

The reproach faction's demand is explicit: Tiamat must avenge Apsû. The terms are vengeance-terms, and the moral framing is theirs, not the text's omniscient voice.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 441, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 441

Tiamat is moved to action by the reproach. She resolves to make war on the younger gods and to set the cosmos in order through the destruction of those she now reframes as rebels. The shift is consequential: she stops being the mother who refused destruction and starts being the leader of the war that the older faction wanted.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442

The reframing of Tiamat's posture between her refusal in Tablet I and her later mobilisation is one of the textual hinges on which the alternative-sovereign reading turns. The text gives the audience two scenes — refusal first, then anger after reproach — and the order matters: the war is what the older faction wants, not what Tiamat originally wanted. The interpretive caveat that follows acknowledges that the reading itself goes a step beyond what the text explicitly says.

[INFERENCE: Tiamat's reframing of the younger gods as rebels rather than her offspring is read in the scholarship as a transformation of obligation into adversariality | basis: Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, and Ballentine 2015, ch. 1, both note that Tiamat's anger is provoked, not primordial | risk: the surviving text does not explicitly mark this as a reframing; this reading is interpretive.]

Tiamat brings forth a host of monstrous creatures to fight on her side: serpents with venom for blood, Mušhuššu-dragons, lahmu-monsters, lion-demons, scorpion-men, fish-men, bull-men, mighty winds, and other composite beasts. The catalogue is concrete and varied — the poet wants the reader to see the army before the battle is joined.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442

The text enumerates eleven monstrous champions. The catalogue becomes a recurring formula in the epic, repeated four times across Tablets I–IV. The repetition is part of how the composition keeps the army-list in front of the audience: Tiamat's host is one of the things the text wants the listener to remember.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I, on the eleven-monsters formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442

Anzû, the lion-headed eagle of the earlier Anzû Epic, appears in the catalogue of Tiamat's monsters: he has been demoted from rebel-protagonist to subordinate creature. The detail is not incidental — it is one of the clearest signals of Enūma Eliš's literary relation to the Anzû Epic, since the figure who carried the older combat is now a footnote in the new one.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I, on Anzû's appearance among Tiamat's brood; Annus 2001, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Tiamat appoints Qingu, identified in the text as her firstborn or as her chosen consort after Apsû, as commander-in-chief of her host. The double identification — firstborn or chosen consort — is itself a feature of the text, and it is one of the points Marduk will later seize on in the verbal challenge before the combat.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

Tiamat invests Qingu with the Tablet of Destinies, fastening it to his chest, and thereby confers on him the cosmic decree-power. The Tablet is the artefact whose possession determines who can issue binding cosmic commands; with it on his chest, Qingu's word becomes effective in the way the assembly's word will need to be effective when its own champion is named.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

The Tablet's transfer to Qingu is the structural inheritance from the Anzû Epic, where the Tablet had been seized by a usurper. Here Tiamat herself bestows it. The shift in mode of transfer — theft versus gift — is one of the smaller adjustments Enūma Eliš makes to its source, and the change has consequences for how the combat is framed.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Annus 2001, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Tiamat declares Qingu her husband and supreme among the gods of her party, raising him above all others. The declaration has the form of a counter-coronation: Tiamat now has her own king, and her own court, and the conflict becomes a contest between two organised divine assemblies.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

The fashioning of Tiamat's host and the elevation of Qingu close Tablet I. The first tablet has done its work: it has assembled an army, named a commander, and bestowed the Tablet of Destinies. Everything Marduk's side will have to overcome has now been put on the field.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet I closing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

==== Tablet II: Ea's report and the search for a champion

Tablet II opens with Ea learning of Tiamat's preparations and her appointment of Qingu. The intelligence flows where it would be expected to flow: to the wisdom-god, who already once defeated a primordial parent and who is the obvious first point of contact.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 443–444, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 443-444

Ea brings the news to his father Anšar, reporting Tiamat's mobilisation and the catalogue of her monsters in formal repetition. The catalogue is delivered, this time, by an informant rather than the narrator, and the form of the repetition belongs to the genre — the news is news of a kind the audience already knows.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 443–444, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 443-444

Anšar receives the report with distress, striking his thigh and biting his lip in formulaic gestures of dismay. The gestures are stock and the audience is meant to recognise them as such; their formulaic character does not lessen them but locates the action within a familiar register of divine response.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 444, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 444

Anšar dispatches Ea against Tiamat. As the slayer of Apsû, Ea is the first candidate for the new combat: precedent recommends him. The qualification that worked once is asked to work again.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 444–445, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 444-445

Ea sets out, but turns back. Faced with Tiamat directly, he cannot prevail and returns to Anšar without engaging. The text gives no extended explanation; Ea finds the task is beyond him, and the failure is its own datum.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 445, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 445

Anšar then dispatches Anu against Tiamat, with the same outcome: Anu also turns back unable to confront her. Two of the most senior figures in the divine genealogy have now failed to face her, and the failure is becoming a problem for the assembly's whole position.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 445–446, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 445-446

The threefold-refusal pattern of the Anzû Epic — three candidates decline before the eventual champion is named — is structurally inherited here, with two failed embassies before Marduk is proposed. Annus reads the structural inheritance as deliberate: the new poet is making the audience hear the older composition's pattern even as he gives it a new resolution.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 444–446, Tablet II; Annus 2001, introduction; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

Anšar despairs. No god of the older generations can confront Tiamat, and the divine assembly is left without a champion. The despair is structural — it sets up the moment when a god from outside the older generations will be needed.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 446, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 446

Ea then proposes Marduk: his young son, born in the Apsû, surpasses all the other gods in strength and is the only candidate who can face Tiamat. The proposal comes from the wisdom-god, which is the proper origin for it; Ea who solved the first crisis solves the second by nominating his son.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 446–447, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 446-447

Ea brings Marduk before Anšar, and Anšar receives him with relief and instruction. The relief is part of the rhetorical work — the eldest gods having failed, the youngest god is going to be asked to do what they could not — and the instruction sets up the negotiation that follows.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 447, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 447

Anšar instructs Marduk to face Tiamat, and Marduk responds by declaring his readiness — but with a condition that closes Tablet II and dominates Tablet III. The condition is the structural pivot: nothing in what follows works without it.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 447–448, Tablet II closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 447-448

Marduk's condition is that, if he is to fight Tiamat as the champion of the gods, his decree must be supreme. Whatever he commands must come to pass, and his word must take precedence over the assembly's. The condition is a constitutional demand, not a tactical one: Marduk asks for the office before he agrees to do the work.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 448, Tablet II closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 448

The condition transforms the combat from a defensive engagement into a kingship-acquisition. Marduk's victory will entail his elevation to supreme rule. Both Lincoln and Ballentine read this as the text's central political move: the combat is the occasion for the kingship, and the text knows it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 448, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II; Lincoln 2012; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

By the close of Tablet II, the structural premises of the rest of the text are in place. Tiamat has an army, a champion, and the Tablet of Destinies. The younger gods have a candidate of their own — Marduk — but his agreement to fight has been made conditional on a constitutional change. The combat that the second movement narrates is therefore prepared on both sides, and what remains is the formal acceptance of Marduk's terms, the combat itself, and the cosmogony that follows from it.

=== Movement II — Marduk's elevation and the combat with Tiamat

==== Tablet III: the assembly is convened

Tablet III opens with Anšar dispatching his vizier Kaka to Lahmu and Lahamu, the eldest gods, to summon them to the assembly. The text moves up the genealogical ladder: the senior figures of the first divine generation are now to be brought into the deliberations.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 449–450, Tablet III; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 449-450

Kaka delivers the news to the elder gods in formal repetition: Tiamat's mobilisation, Qingu's commission, the failed embassies of Ea and Anu, and Marduk's condition. The vizier's speech is the third major restatement of the army-list and the third major restatement of the diplomatic situation, and the audience is meant to hear them as the same speech each time.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 450–453, Tablet III; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 450-453

The repetition is structurally important. The catalogue of monsters and the substance of Marduk's terms are repeated multiple times across the first four tablets, a feature of the composition's oral-performance heritage. The text was meant to be heard, and a heard text uses repetition the way a written text uses cross-references.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442 and 443–444 (Anšar's report and Ea's report on Tiamat's army) and pp. 450–453 (the assembly's confirmation); Lambert 2013, Tablet III commentary on the repetition pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442, 450-453

Lahmu and Lahamu, hearing the report, gnash their teeth in distress and depart to the assembly. Their distress is the proper reaction at this point in the narrative and authorises the formal gathering that follows.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 453, Tablet III; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 453

The assembly of the great gods, the Igigi and the Anunnaki, gathers and feasts. They drink beer, they sit, they eat bread. The detail is concrete and unromantic; the gods feast in the way Babylonian assemblies are pictured feasting, and the divine council is given the texture of a real political body.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 453–454, Tablet III closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 453-454

The assembly's ceremonial drinking and feasting close Tablet III, with the formal decision on Marduk deferred to Tablet IV. The deferral is structural: the ceremony of gathering precedes the ceremony of decision, and the next tablet will give the second of those.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 453–454, Tablet III closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 453-454

==== Tablet IV: the assembly's elevation, the test of the cloth, the combat

Tablet IV opens with the assembly's formal decision. Marduk is granted the supremacy he demanded, and his decree is elevated above all others. The condition Marduk laid down at the close of Tablet II is now formally accepted: the assembly grants what he asked.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 455–456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 455-456

The gods construct a princely throne for Marduk, set him upon it, and acclaim him with the formula "your decree is supreme; your word is Anu." The throne, the seating, and the acclamation are all enthronement-elements; the audience is meant to recognise the ceremony as a coronation.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The formula equates Marduk's word with the decree of Anu, the sky-father — the highest divine authority in the older Mesopotamian theology. To say Marduk's word is Anu's word is to say the new god has acquired the office that older theology placed at the top of the pantheon.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The assembly tests Marduk's new power by placing a cloth or garment in their midst and asking him to destroy it and then restore it by command alone. The test is a public demonstration; the assembly will not let him fight as champion until they have seen for themselves that the office they have just granted has its proper effect.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

Marduk speaks; the cloth vanishes. He speaks again; the cloth is restored intact. The two acts are deliberate: the test verifies destruction and restoration, undoing and remaking, the two operations that will matter for the cosmogony to come.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The cloth-test confirms that Marduk's word now possesses the cosmic decree-power that the older gods recognise as a property of supreme rule. The Tablet of Destinies is across the field, on Qingu's chest, but the decree-power that the Tablet conveys is now in Marduk's voice.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The assembly, satisfied, hails Marduk as king: "Marduk is king." The acclamation is brief and direct, and it is the formal output of the cloth-test.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The acclamation establishes the kingship before the combat. Marduk fights as already-king, and his victory ratifies rather than acquires the office. The point is one Lincoln and Ballentine both insist on: the kingship is the precondition of the combat, not its prize.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Lincoln 2012; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The gods then arm Marduk for the combat. He takes a bow, an arrow, a mace, lightning, a flaming flame held in his lips, and a net to entrap his foe. The catalogue of weapons is once again concrete; the new king goes to war kitted out as a warrior.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 456–457, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 456-457

Marduk fashions the four winds his grandfather Anu had given him into weapons, and he raises in addition seven evil winds — the Wind, the Tempest, the Whirlwind, the Hurricane, the Cyclone, the Imhullu (literally "evil wind"), and others — that he holds in reserve for the combat. The toy of Tablet I has now become an arsenal, and the audience is meant to remember its origin.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 456–457, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 456-457

Marduk mounts his storm-chariot, drawn by four named horses whose names are variously translated. Foster 2005 renders them "Killer, Pitiless, Trampler, Fleet"; other English versions diverge in the rendering of the fourth name. The chariot is the standard equipment of a Mesopotamian war-god, and Marduk is being equipped accordingly.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 457, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 457

Marduk draws near to Tiamat, and the two engage in a verbal challenge before the physical combat begins. The verbal challenge belongs to the form: heroic combats in this literature regularly open with speech-exchanges, and the present case follows the convention.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 457–458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 457-458

Marduk taunts Tiamat with the charge of injustice. She had refused to destroy her offspring with Apsû, yet now she elevates Qingu, who is not her firstborn by right, to a kingship that does not belong to him. The taunt is not an aside; it is a legal indictment of Tiamat's appointment of Qingu, and it presses on exactly the point at which her case is weakest.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Tiamat answers, and the text describes her as enraged, taking up the combat: she opens her mouth wide. The image is the threshold between the verbal challenge and the physical engagement, and the text dwells on the open mouth because the next move depends on it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Marduk drives the Imhullu, the evil wind, into Tiamat's open mouth so that she cannot close her lips. The tactic is precise: the wind that was once a child's plaything is now a battlefield weapon, and it functions by preventing speech.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Other winds rush in after Imhullu, distending Tiamat's belly and rendering her immobile. The combat-tactic is unusually concrete in its physiology, and the text gives the sequence in step-by-step terms.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

With Tiamat unable to close her mouth, Marduk shoots an arrow into her open belly, splitting her heart. The kill is direct, technically efficient, and explicitly anatomised: the text does not abstract the act.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Tiamat falls dead, and Marduk stands upon her corpse. The image of the victor standing on the body is, in this literature, the standard sign of victory and possession.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 458–459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 458-459

The host of monstrous creatures Tiamat had fashioned scatters in flight, but Marduk's net catches them. He binds them and treads them under foot. The army that took a tablet to assemble is dispatched in a few lines: the catalogue's narrative function was to be defeated.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 459

Qingu, Tiamat's general, is captured among the monsters. Marduk binds him and seizes from his chest the Tablet of Destinies that Tiamat had bestowed. The Tablet, as in the Anzû Epic, returns to its proper holder by combat — though here the prior holder was the rebel coalition's appointed champion rather than a thief.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 459

Marduk seals the Tablet of Destinies and presses it to his own chest, transferring the cosmic decree-power to himself. The transfer is physical and the gesture is unmistakable: what was on Qingu's chest is now on Marduk's, and the decree-power has changed hands.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 459

Marduk returns to Tiamat's corpse and rests his foot upon it. He then turns to the work of cosmogony. The combat is over; the cosmogony is a separate act, and the text marks the transition with the foot-on-corpse image.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 459–460, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 459-460

Marduk splits Tiamat's body lengthwise like a flat fish, into two halves. The simile is borrowed from food-preparation, and that is part of its force: the body of the defeated cosmogonic mother is processed like a fish on a slab.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 459–460, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 459-460

He raises the upper half of Tiamat's body and sets it as the sky, fixing it in place with bars and a watchman so that her waters cannot escape. The sky is made of Tiamat's matter, and the bars-and-watchman provision is in the text as a security arrangement: the new sky is also a containment for the cosmogonic waters.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 460, Tablet IV closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 460

The use of Tiamat's body as the material from which the sky is made establishes the cosmogony as combat-cosmogony: the world is the slain adversary's matter, ordered by the victor. Forsyth treats this as one of the defining features of the type — the cosmogony is not made from nothing but from the body of what had to be defeated first.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 460, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

==== Tablet V: the cosmogony, the calendar, the stations of the sky

Tablet V opens with Marduk continuing the cosmogonic work. He establishes the stations of the great gods in the sky, fixing them as constellations. The astronomy of Tablet V is precise where Tablet IV's body-dismemberment was visceral: the new cosmos is being equipped with an ordered observational schema.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 461–462, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 461-462

Marduk fixes the year of twelve months, with three stars assigned to each month and the sequence of stations governing the calendar. The calendar that the text describes is recognisably the calendar of Mesopotamian observational astronomy, and the cosmogony is being made to issue in the local timekeeping.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 461–462, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 461-462

He establishes the place of Nēberu, the crossing-place — identified with Marduk's own astral station — at the heart of the heavens to fix the boundaries of the year. Nēberu is Marduk's signature in the sky, and its placement at the centre means the new king's name is written across the year.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 462, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 462

Marduk creates the moon, gives it a crown, and assigns it the office of regulating the night and marking the months. The grant is administrative: the moon is given a portfolio, and the portfolio is the lunar month.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 462–463, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 462-463

The text gives explicit instructions for the moon's monthly cycle: at the new moon it is to shine as a horn; at the seventh it is to appear as a half-disk; at the fifteenth, the full moon, it is to face the sun directly; and thereafter to wane. The instructions read as a job-description, and the level of detail is striking — the cosmogony is being made to lay down the working schedule of the visible heavens.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 462–463, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 462-463

Marduk creates the sun and assigns it the office of regulating the day. Sun and moon together divide cosmic time. The pair is the standard pair of Mesopotamian time-keeping, and the cosmogony has now produced both halves of the daily and monthly cycle.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 463, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 463

The reading of Tablet V that the chapter has just given depends in places on Lambert's reconstruction of partly broken passages, particularly in the calendrical sections. The lacuna marker that follows registers that dependence: where the surviving witnesses are damaged, the score-text used here integrates the available manuscripts to fill the gap.

[LACUNA: Tablet V contains small gaps in the description of the stations of the moon and the organization of time, around the calendrical instructions for the lunar cycle | source: Lambert 2013, Tablet V apparatus | scholarly_reconstruction: Lambert's critical score integrates Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian witnesses to reconstruct the affected passages.]

Marduk takes the lower half of Tiamat's body and uses it for the earth-cosmogony. From her thighs he makes the great mountains; her eyes he opens for the sources of the Tigris and Euphrates; her nostrils become springs. The body-to-landscape mapping is anatomically specific: each feature of the cosmos has its origin in a named feature of Tiamat's corpse.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 463–464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 463-464

He piles the mountains on Tiamat's head and bores wells through her skull. The image is grim and concrete; the cosmogony does not abstract the body it is processing.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 464

He coils her tail to form the cosmic bond that links the heavens and the earth at the horizon. The horizon, in this cosmology, is a literal cord, and Tiamat's tail is what makes it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 464

He arches her crotch to support the heavens, an architectural element of the new cosmos. The detail is part of the cosmogony's architectural concreteness: the sky needs a support, and one of Tiamat's body-parts provides it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 464

The total cosmogony from Tiamat's body provides sky, mountains, rivers, springs, the cosmic bond, and the architectural support of the heavens — a comprehensive ordering of the visible world from the corpse of the slain adversary. The completeness of the mapping is what makes this combat-cosmogony rather than just combat: every part of the cosmos is accounted for as a feature of Tiamat.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 459–464, Tablets IV–V; Lambert 2013, Tablets IV–V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 459-464

Marduk presents his completed work to the assembled gods, and the gods respond with celebration. The presentation is a public review: the gods see the work and approve it, and the approval is the second formal moment of acclamation.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 464–465, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 464-465

The gods give Marduk the insignia of kingship: a sceptre, a throne, and a royal robe, marking the ratification of his rule over the new cosmos. The kingship Marduk had received in advance, before the combat, is now ratified after the cosmogony, with the standard regalia.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 464–465, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 464-465

The gods also pledge their submission to Marduk and make a formal commitment to obey his decree. The pledge completes the constitutional move that Marduk's condition in Tablet II had begun: the assembly's word was supreme; now Marduk's word is supreme over the assembly, and the assembly has agreed.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 465, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 465

Tablet V closes with Marduk preparing to receive the gods at his side and with the gods' acclamation of his sovereignty over the ordered cosmos. The first half of the epic — combat and cosmogony — is now complete; the second half will turn to humans, the city, and the names.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 465–466, Tablet V closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 465-466

By the close of Tablet V, the cosmos is in place. The kingship that Marduk was granted before the combat has been ratified after the cosmogony, and the assembly has pledged its submission. What remains is what the third movement will give: the creation of humans, the founding of the city and its temple, and the fifty-names ceremony in which the assembly's praise becomes the text's doxological closure.

=== Movement III — Creation of humans, founding of Babylon, the fifty names

==== Tablet VI: humans from Qingu's blood, the building of Babylon and Esagil

Tablet VI opens with Marduk announcing his plan to relieve the gods of their labour. He will create humans to perform the work of maintaining the cosmos, freeing the gods for repose. The plan is presented as a benefit to the gods, and the human race comes into existence as a labour-saving measure for the divine assembly.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 467–468, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 467-468

Marduk consults Ea on the means: blood is required, blood from a god who instigated the rebellion of Tiamat. The means are not arbitrary; the construction of humans is to be paid for by a specific party's blood, and the specification matters.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 468, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 468

The assembly identifies Qingu as the responsible party. He held the Tablet of Destinies, he led Tiamat's host, he is the one whose blood will pay. The identification is the assembly's own; Qingu is named for the role on the basis of his actions in the war.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 468, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 468

Qingu is bound, brought before Ea, and his blood is opened. From his blood the first humans are fashioned. The act is sacrificial in form: a god is killed, his blood is shed, and humanity is the product.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 468–469, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 468-469

The aetiology is explicit: humans are made from the blood of a rebel god so that they may bear the labour-burden of the gods. The text does not soften the point — humans exist because the gods needed someone to do the work, and the substance of humanity is the blood of a defeated rebel.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 468–469, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

The aetiology gives religious sanction to the corvée-and-temple-service economy of Babylonian temple-states. Humans are constituted as labour for the gods at their inception, and Lincoln's reading of the text is that the social order of the temple-state is being projected back into the cosmogony as its origin.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 468–469, Tablet VI; Lincoln 2012; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

Marduk then divides the gods, allotting three hundred to the heavens above and three hundred to the earth below, fixing the divine assembly's distribution in the new cosmos. The numbers are formulaic, and the division is a constitutional move: the divine bureaucracy is now organised into upper and lower chambers.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 469, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 469

In gratitude for their relief from labour, the gods undertake to build a sanctuary for Marduk in Babylon. They will construct Esagil, the temple-house, and the city around it. The construction is presented as a voluntary return-gift: humans took on the gods' labour, and the gods reciprocate by building Marduk's house.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 470–471, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 470-471

The Anunnaki, the gods of the underworld, undertake the construction. They make bricks, raise walls, and build Esagil over the course of two years. The detail of brick-making and the two-year timeframe locate the cosmogony's final architectural act in the same idiom as a real Babylonian building campaign.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 471–472, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 471-472

The cosmic-architectural reading of Esagil that the chapter is about to mark with an inference token is a standard piece of scholarly synthesis: the temple's pairing with Anu's Esarra above, and with Ea's Apsû below, gives a three-tier picture in which the new temple sits at the centre of a cosmography drawn from the older theogony. The text describes the pairing; the three-tier theogonic reading goes one step further, and the marker that follows registers that step.

[INFERENCE: Esagil is constructed as the cosmic counterpart of the Apsû above and the Esarra of Anu — the three-tier structure mirrors Anšar / Anu / Ea theogonically | basis: Foster 2005 pp. 471–472 and Lambert 2013 Tablet VI describe Esagil's foundation as cosmic counterpart-architecture; the theogonic three-tier reading is a standard scholarly synthesis | risk: the text states the architectural pairing but does not explicitly assert the Anšar/Anu/Ea correspondence as a named structural feature]

Esarra, the earthly counterpart of the heavenly Esagil-above, is established as Anu's temple, paralleling Marduk's seat below. The pairing puts Marduk's house in a cosmological architecture: not a single temple but one of a tier of temples that organise the new cosmos.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 471, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 471

When Esagil is complete, the gods enter and feast at Marduk's house. The sanctuary becomes the venue of the divine assembly, with Marduk presiding. The feast at Esagil is the counterpart of the feast in Tablet III: there the assembly was about to grant Marduk the kingship; here it is celebrating the kingship as accomplished.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 472–473, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 472-473

The founding of Babylon and Esagil establishes the city as the cosmic centre. The temple is the axis where heaven, earth, and Apsû meet under Marduk's rule. The cosmography is centred on Babylon, and the centring is the text's principal theological claim about the city.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 471–473, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

The text identifies Babylon as "the house where the great gods reside," establishing its theological status as the seat of the cosmic kingship. The phrasing is direct, and the text uses it as a name-of-status rather than a description.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 472, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 472

Within Esagil, Marduk takes his seat upon the throne, and the gods kneel before him in homage. The image of the seated king with kneeling subordinates is the standard picture of Mesopotamian sovereignty, and the text is closing the kingship-acquisition arc that began with Marduk's condition in Tablet II.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 472–473, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 472-473

The gods then begin the ceremonial naming of Marduk. The assembly will pronounce his fifty names, each marking an aspect of his divinity, his combat, or his rule. The naming is the text's last major movement, and it is a deliberate piece of doxological architecture.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–474, Tablet VI closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-474

The fifty-names ceremony begins in Tablet VI and continues throughout Tablet VII. Structurally it functions as the doxological closure of the epic. The names do for Marduk in praise what the combat and cosmogony did for him in action: they account for what he is, in liturgical form.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–486, Tablets VI–VII; Lambert 2013, Tablets VI–VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-486

==== Tablet VII: the fifty names

Tablet VII is given over almost entirely to the fifty names of Marduk and their explications, with each name accompanied by interpretive lines that gloss the name's meaning. The form is litanic: name, then gloss, then the next name. The text is its own commentary, and the audience is taught what each name means as it is pronounced.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–486, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-486

The first names cluster in Tablet VI: Marduk, Marukka, Marutukku, Mershakushu, Lugaldimmerankia (king of the gods of heaven and earth), Nari-Lugal-dimmer-an-ki-a, Asalluhi, and others associated with rule, exorcism, and creation. This first cluster gathers the names that connect Marduk to his older Babylonian identities and to the practical theological functions he had already accumulated before Enūma Eliš was written.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–476, Tablets VI–VII; Lambert 2013, Tablets VI–VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-476

A second cluster of names emphasises Marduk's combat and victory: titles like Asaru, Tutu, and the storm-god names that recall his defeat of Tiamat. The cluster keeps the combat present in the doxology — the names of victory remind the audience that praise rests on a specific feat.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 476–479, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 476-479

A third cluster emphasises Marduk's cosmic ordering work: names that bind him to the calendar, the constellations, and the regulation of cosmic time. The names make Tablet V's astronomy doxological — the calendar Marduk fixed becomes the calendar his names reflect.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 479–482, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 479-482

A fourth cluster identifies Marduk with the prerogatives of older gods. He absorbs functions and epithets of Enlil, Ea, Anu, Adad, and others, becoming a supreme deity who concentrates the older pantheon's offices in himself. The text is performing, in the form of names, the same theological consolidation that the combat performed in the form of action: older offices belong to Marduk now.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 482–485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 482-485

Anu bestows the number fifty on Marduk, equating Marduk numerologically with Enlil — whose sacred number was fifty in the older theology. The numerology matters: in the older system, fifty was Enlil's number and the highest in the conventional ordering, and the bestowal here is therefore a transfer of rank.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 485

The bestowal of Enlil's number on Marduk is theologically decisive. It affirms that Marduk has supplanted Enlil in the supreme office, completing the displacement that the combat had begun. Annus reads the moment as the conceptual capstone of the whole composition: the older pantheon has been rearranged so that Marduk sits at its top, with the number that used to belong to its previous head.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII; Annus 2001, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet VII

The closing portion of the fifty-names passage is the second of the two damaged sections that affect this chapter's narrative. Where Tablet V's gaps fell in the calendrical section, Tablet VII's gaps fall in the doxological closure, and the second lacuna marker registers the dependence on Lambert's reconstructed score.

[LACUNA: Tablet VII closing fifty-names passage has some name-explications partially broken | source: Lambert 2013, Tablet VII apparatus | scholarly_reconstruction: Assyrian and Babylonian manuscripts overlap, allowing Lambert's critical score to reconstruct most of the affected passages.]

Tablet VII closes with a doxological hymn that calls on humans to remember and recite Marduk's fifty names, with the explicit liturgical exhortation that the names be passed from father to son and from elder to junior. The text is reaching past its own ending to the conditions of its own future use: it tells the audience how to keep the text alive.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 485–486, Tablet VII closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 485-486

The closing hymn frames Enūma Eliš as a recitation-text. It is meant to be spoken aloud, learned by heart, and transmitted through the generations. The form Enūma Eliš took in its surviving witnesses is partly a function of this self-instruction; the manuscripts are tools for the kind of transmission the text itself prescribes.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 485–486, Tablet VII closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 485-486

The closing hymn is consistent with the akītu-festival liturgical use of the text. The epic is composed to be performed. The performative dimension is not an extra-textual fact about its reception; it is what the text says it is for.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 486, Tablet VII closing; Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu recitation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

With the seven tablets now walked through, the chapter turns from narrative description to interpretive debate. Three discussions follow: the Gunkel/Ballentine question about whether "chaos" is the right category for Tiamat; the akītu ritual frame in which the text was actually used; and the variants and reception of the work in its later Babylonian and Assyrian afterlife.

=== The Gunkel and Ballentine debate

Hermann Gunkel's 1895 *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* identified Enūma Eliš as the type-specimen of a religious-historical pattern he named Chaoskampf, in which a sovereign deity defeats the adversary of order and establishes the cosmos. Gunkel's coining of the German term has shaped the field since, and it is the term this book inherits, even as it inherits the contemporary qualifications that have grown up around it.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, with Marduk vs. Tiamat as the central case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

Gunkel's argument was developed in part to illuminate the Hebrew Bible. He proposed that Genesis 1's _tehôm_ (the deep) was cognate with Tiamat and that Israelite cosmogony preserved a demythologised version of the Babylonian combat. The argument was historical-religious in its orientation: Gunkel was trying to show that the biblical opening had a Mesopotamian background, and Enūma Eliš was the Mesopotamian background he had in mind.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I; Day 1985 for the inheriting debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The Gunkel reading dominated the twentieth-century literature on Enūma Eliš and on the Hebrew Bible's _tehôm_ / Leviathan / Rahab corpus. It remains the framework against which contemporary alternatives are argued. To call a contemporary reading "alternative" is, in this field, to position it against Gunkel; the framework has not been displaced, even where it has been qualified.footnote:[Day 1985 on the dominant Chaoskampf reading; Scurlock & Beal 2013 on the reassessment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=passim

Debra Scoggins Ballentine's 2015 alternative-sovereign reading argues that "chaos" mistranslates the conceptual work the Mesopotamian text is doing. Tiamat is not undifferentiated disorder but a rival claimant to divine sovereignty. Ballentine's argument turns on what Tiamat actually does in the text: she organises an army, appoints a champion, distributes a token of authority, and conducts a war. None of those actions is well described by the word "chaos."footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

On Ballentine's reading, the conflict in Enūma Eliš is between two competing political orders — Tiamat's coalition with Qingu and the assembly's elevation of Marduk — and not between order and disorder. Both sides have an order; the question is which order will prevail. The shift in framing changes how one reads almost every scene of the epic, and Ballentine's chapter is principally an exercise in showing that the change is textually warranted.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

Ballentine's reading is anticipated by Bruce Lincoln's argument that combat myths function as political-ideological work, legitimating the victor's regime by recasting the loser's claim as illegitimate. Lincoln's general thesis applies to a wider corpus than Enūma Eliš, but the Babylonian text is one of his clearest examples, and Ballentine's specific reading of Tiamat-as-rival-sovereign is recognisable as Lincoln's general framework applied to the particular case.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The Scurlock and Beal 2013 collected volume gathers a range of essays that, in different ways, qualify or reject Gunkel's framing while preserving the recognition that Enūma Eliš is centrally about combat and cosmic order. The volume is not a manifesto; it is a collection of working specialists adjusting Gunkel's category from inside the field, with different essays offering different qualifications.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim

The book's working position, following Scurlock and Beal and Ballentine, is that "combat myth" is a more accurate descriptive term than "Chaoskampf," but that "Chaoskampf" is retained as the term of art that the scholarly conversation continues to use. The terminology is therefore a compromise: this book uses "combat myth" as its descriptive default and "Chaoskampf" when speaking with the inherited literature.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim

The Tiamat-as-mother-against-her-children reading is supported by the textual evidence that her initial impulse is refusal of Apsû's destruction-plan and that her later anger is provoked by reproach rather than primordial. The reading does not require dissolving the cosmogonic dimension that Gunkel correctly identified. The cosmos is in fact made from Tiamat's body; the text's interest in cosmogony is real. What changes, on the alternative-sovereign reading, is how the combat itself is to be characterised — as a political contest with cosmogonic results, rather than as a fight between order and primordial chaos. The two readings can be held together without contradiction: Tiamat is a rival sovereign in the political register and the cosmogonic mother in the cosmological register, and Enūma Eliš works with both registers at once.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–442, Tablet I; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The chapter does not adjudicate finally between Gunkel and Ballentine; the variant marker that follows records the decision to carry both readings in the body, on the grounds that the principal interpretive crux of the text is best presented as a debate rather than as a settled question.

=== The akītu festival context

The akītu New Year festival is the principal performative context of Enūma Eliš. The text was recited on the fourth day of the festival before the cult-image of Marduk in Esagil. This is the practical setting that the surviving record gives the text — not a school exercise, not a private read, but a liturgy spoken before the god's image inside his own temple.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu recitation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu festival was celebrated in Nisan, the spring month, and structured the Babylonian liturgical year around the renewal of Marduk's kingship and the king's submission to him. The festival's annual character is part of how it works: each year the kingship is renewed, and each year the human king has to stand again before the god whose subordinate he is. The cosmogony is therefore not a fact in the past tense; it is a fact that has to be re-affirmed each spring, and the recitation is one of the means by which the affirmation is done.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The recitation of Enūma Eliš at the akītu was performed by the high priest before Marduk's image, and the text functioned as the liturgical re-presentation of the cosmogony. The recitation makes the text into an event: the cosmogony is not only described but re-enacted in the temple, with the priest as the performer and the cult-image as the audience.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu also included a ritual humiliation and reinstatement of the king. The king was stripped of his insignia, struck on the cheek, and restored — a performance that subordinated the human king to Marduk's cosmic kingship. The ritual is concrete: the human king is, for a moment, made into a humiliated subject before being given his crown back; the gesture stages the asymmetry between the god's kingship and the king's.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The combat-narrative and the ritual frame are mutually constitutive. Enūma Eliš is not a narrative that happens to be recited at the akītu; it is co-extensive with the akītu's annual reactivation of the cosmogony. To separate text and ritual would be to misdescribe the function of either; the two are aspects of the same event.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Lincoln 2012; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu context establishes that the text was understood, in its Babylonian context, as a performative re-creation of the order it narrates — not a one-time historical event but an annually renewed reality. The cosmos is not made once and left to run; it is made again every spring, and the recitation is part of how the making is done. The text and the festival together constitute a single piece of religious work; reading the text apart from the festival is a modern operation, and the modern reader who wants to understand the text in its Babylonian setting has to keep the festival in view.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

=== Variants and reception

The Enūma Eliš witnesses preserved across the Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian copies show overall textual stability, with localised variants in spelling, divine epithets, and minor phrasing rather than competing recensions. The text is one of the more stable cases in the cuneiform tradition: the version recited in Esagil and the version copied at Nineveh were, by and large, the same text.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the manuscript stability.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

Lambert's critical score for each tablet integrates the variant readings. The standard text presented in Lambert 2013 and in Foster 2005 is a composite drawn from this integration. The composite is the working object of contemporary scholarship; reading "Enūma Eliš" today means reading Lambert's reconstructed text in Foster's English.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15 and per-tablet apparatus; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The Sultantepe tablet group preserves an Assyrian recension in which Aššur, the chief Assyrian deity, replaces Marduk as the cosmic champion in some passages. The substitution is a political reuse: the Assyrian crown took the Babylonian text and adjusted its leading deity to match its own pantheon.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the Aššur substitution in the Assyrian recension.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Aššur substitution attests to the political portability of the narrative. The same combat-and-cosmogony structure could be retooled to centre on a different patron deity when the political centre shifted. The recension is therefore evidence that the text was understood as a piece of political theology, capable of being adapted to whichever capital was reading it.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

Royal inscriptions from Nebuchadnezzar I onward invoke Marduk's victory over Tiamat as the charter for the king's own legitimacy. The king reigns as Marduk's terrestrial counterpart. The pattern of citation is consistent: the epic's combat is cited as the warrant for the king's office, and the office is described as a downward-projection of the god's.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the royal-inscription tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis ties the composition to the recovery of Marduk's cult-statue from Elam after a period of foreign captivity, framing the epic as theological response to that historical episode. On this reading the text is occasional in origin: it was written for a specific moment in Babylonian history, when a king brought his city's god back from exile and needed an account of why that god had always belonged at the centre of the cosmos.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis is the prevailing scholarly view but it is not universally accepted, and the inference marker that follows acknowledges that the dating debate is real and that this chapter's framing rests on the consensus position rather than on unanimity.

[INFERENCE: the Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis is the prevailing scholarly view but not universally accepted | basis: Lambert 2013 introduction surveys the dating debate and presents Nebuchadnezzar I as the likely commissioning patron | risk: alternative dates (earlier Kassite, later Neo-Babylonian) have been argued; the commissioning hypothesis is consensus but not unanimous.]

The fifty-names ceremony shows continuity with the older Sumerian and Akkadian tradition of compiling divine names. The closer antecedent is the honorific names bestowed on Ninurta at the close of the Anzû Epic (cross-ref: Ch. 2). The continuity is one of the most concrete pieces of evidence for the literary genealogy: the closing-doxology form in which Ninurta is praised in the Anzû Epic is the same form in which Marduk is praised at the close of Enūma Eliš.footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction and Tablet III commentary; Lambert 2013, Tablets VI–VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

The reuse and outbidding of the Anzû Epic in Enūma Eliš is the clearest documented case of combat-myth rewriting within Mesopotamian literature, with the older Ninurta narrative restructured as a more comprehensive cosmogony with Marduk in the warrior-role. Annus's case for deliberate authorial reuse rests on a string of correspondences — the threefold-refusal pattern, the Tablet of Destinies, the closing-name doxology, the demoted Anzû — and the case is now broadly accepted in the field.footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

In the Mesopotamian reception, Enūma Eliš circulated as a pedagogical text in the scribal curriculum, with copies and excerpts attested from school contexts. The text was used in scribal training; learning to read and write Akkadian meant, among other things, copying out passages of Enūma Eliš.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the manuscript distribution including school contexts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The text continued to be copied and recited into the late Achaemenid and Seleucid periods, attesting its centrality in Babylonian religion across more than a millennium. Long after Babylonian political independence had been lost to successive Persian and Greek-speaking empires, the local temples kept copying the epic, and that fact is itself a measure of the text's depth in the local religious life.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the late witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The post-canonical reuses include the incorporation of Marduk's combat and cosmogony into late Babylonian and Hellenistic-period theological compendia, where the fifty-names tradition continued to develop. The names did not stop accumulating once the text was fixed; later scholarly compendia kept working on the name-list as a doxological resource.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the post-canonical reception.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

Foster identifies Enūma Eliš as the most widely studied text of the Akkadian literary corpus and as the central case for any treatment of Mesopotamian cosmology. The judgement is the working assumption of the field, and it is the assumption this chapter has acted on throughout.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 436

The text's reception in the modern study of religion runs through Gunkel 1895 and into all subsequent treatments of the combat-myth pattern, making Enūma Eliš the type-specimen of the comparative category that this book examines. Whatever else this book argues about the combat-myth as a category, the Babylonian text is the standard against which the rest of the corpus has been read for more than a century, and the rest of the volume's chapters return to it implicitly even when they treat other traditions on their own terms.
