=== Source overview

The chapter treats the Nyanga oral epic in which the hero Mwindo, after a sequence of feats and journeys, kills the dragon Kirimu in the forest and is then struck down by the sky-god Nkuba and taken up to the celestial realm where he is humiliated, instructed in the limits of his power, and returned to the human world chastened — a narrative in which the killing of the dragon is not the establishment of cosmic order or sovereignty but a transgression that the sky-god punishes, and in which the moral-didactic point of the episode is the hero's maturation through humiliation rather than the defeat of the dragon as such.footnote:[Per brief 29-mwindo-kirimu.yaml summary on Mwindo's killing of Kirimu, on Nkuba's subsequent punishment of Mwindo for the killing, on the inverted moral valence of the dragon-slaying as hubris rather than order-making, and on the hero's required humiliation and re-integration as the narrative's didactic centre; Biebuyck, D. and Mateene, K.C. (trans. and eds.), *The Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (Zaire)* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1969; ISBN 9780520003767), Kirimu episode and following celestial-journey passages, on the killing of the dragon, the sky-god's lightning-strike, and the celestial humiliation of the hero.]

=== The Banyanga, the forest world, and the epic's setting

The Nyanga (Banyanga) are a Bantu-speaking population of the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo, with their historical heartland in the upper Oso river valley and adjacent forested terrain on the eastern slopes of the Mitumba mountain range and the western fringes of the Kivu rift, in a high-rainfall equatorial-forest zone where forest hunting, banana cultivation, and small-scale ironworking and trade are the economic base; the *Mwindo Epic* is one of the principal narrative-and-performance traditions of this community and is, per current African-oral-epic scholarship, the flagship recorded epic of the eastern central-African forest-zone Bantu corpus.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, translators' introduction on the Nyanga (Banyanga) as a Bantu-speaking population of the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo with historical heartland in the upper Oso river valley and adjacent forested terrain on the eastern slopes of the Mitumba range and the western fringes of the Kivu rift, on the high-rainfall equatorial-forest zone in which they live, on forest hunting / banana cultivation / small-scale ironworking and trade as the economic base, and on the *Mwindo Epic* as one of the community's principal narrative-and-performance traditions; Belcher 1999, ch. on Mwindo, on the *Mwindo Epic* as the flagship recorded epic of the eastern central-African forest-zone Bantu corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The Nyanga oral-tradition's framing of the *Mwindo Epic* is a connected narrative of the hero's life rather than a primarily cosmogonic narrative: the cosmos and the human world are already in place when the epic begins, and the narrative's interest lies in the hero's miraculous birth, his disputes with his kin, his journeys across the human and spirit worlds, his testing by the powers above and below, and his eventual chastened return as a properly-formed chief — with the epic's compositional centre being the hero's biographical-and-moral arc rather than the establishment of the cosmos.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, translators' introduction on the *Mwindo Epic*'s framing within Nyanga oral tradition as a connected narrative of the hero's life rather than a cosmogonic narrative, on the already-in-place cosmographic framing of the epic, on the narrative's compositional centre as the hero's biographical-and-moral arc, and on the moral framing of the chastened-return-as-properly-formed-chief outcome; per brief 29-mwindo-kirimu.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the didactic (the hero's maturation through humiliation) and social-moral (reciprocal balance between human and spirit worlds) functions of the narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The epic's principal protagonists are Mwindo, the miraculously-born hero whose name is conventionally rendered with the *karisi* honorific (the praise-and-narrative singer-tradition's title) and whose proper recitation is the epic itself; Mwindo's father Shemwindo, the chief of Tubondo whose hostility to his son's birth precipitates the early action of the epic; Mwindo's paternal aunts (especially Iyangura, the river-spirit aunt) who shelter and aid him; Nkuba, the sky-god / lightning-bringer who intervenes against Mwindo at the Kirimu episode and who is one of the epic's principal divine figures; Muisa, the underworld lord whose realm Mwindo also visits in the journey-sequence; and Kirimu, the dragon whose killing the chapter's narrative-of-interest takes as its centre.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, translators' introduction and Kirimu episode passages on the epic's principal protagonists — Mwindo as miraculously-born hero (with the *karisi* honorific tradition behind the proper recitation of the epic); Shemwindo as Mwindo's hostile chiefly father at Tubondo whose opposition precipitates the early action; Iyangura as Mwindo's paternal aunt and river-spirit shelterer; Nkuba as sky-god and lightning-bringer who intervenes at the Kirimu episode; Muisa as underworld lord; and Kirimu as the dragon at the chapter's narrative centre; per glossary 'Mwindo', 'Kirimu', 'Nkuba' first_mention_glosses on the principal protagonists.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The Nyanga cosmographic framing within which the epic's action unfolds is a layered world in which the human world (the forest-and-banana-grove realm of the Nyanga settlements) is bounded above by the sky-realm of Nkuba and the celestial powers, below by the underworld-realm of Muisa and the chthonic powers, and laterally by the river-and-water realm of Iyangura and the riverine spirits; Mwindo's journeys across these realms in the course of the epic are presented within an already-existing layered cosmography rather than as cosmogonic establishment of the layers themselves.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, translators' introduction on the Nyanga cosmographic framing of the epic — the human world of the forest-and-banana-grove Nyanga settlements bounded above by Nkuba's sky-realm and the celestial powers, below by Muisa's underworld-realm and the chthonic powers, and laterally by Iyangura's river-and-water realm and the riverine spirits — and on Mwindo's journeys across these realms in the course of the epic as movements within an already-existing layered cosmography rather than as cosmogonic establishment of the layers themselves; Belcher 1999, ch. on Mwindo, on the layered-cosmography framing of the epic's action.]

=== Mwindo's birth, growth, and early feats

Mwindo's birth, in the recorded performance, is presented as miraculous in several distinct registers: he is born already speaking and walking, born from his mother's middle finger after his father has forbidden the birth of any son and ordered the killing of any male child his wives bear, and born already in possession of his characteristic implements — a *conga*-scepter (a flywhisk-and-spirit-implement that is in the epic the hero's principal magical-practical instrument) — with the multiple-register miraculous birth supplying the epic's opening situation in which the hero is exceptional from his first moment.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, opening passages on Mwindo's miraculous birth — born already speaking and walking, born from his mother's middle finger after his father Shemwindo had forbidden the birth of any son and ordered the killing of any male child his wives bore, and born already in possession of the *conga*-scepter (the flywhisk-and-spirit-implement that is the hero's principal magical-practical instrument throughout the epic); on the multiple-register miraculous birth supplying the epic's opening situation of the hero's exceptionality from his first moment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

Mwindo's early action in the epic is structured around his pursuit of recognition by his hostile chiefly father Shemwindo and around the conflict between father and son: Shemwindo, on learning of his son's birth, attempts to have Mwindo killed by various means (sealing him in a drum and casting him into the river is the most widely cited episode of the early epic), and Mwindo survives each attempt and pursues his father across the human and spirit worlds, with the early epic's narrative work being the establishment of the hero's character through the father-son conflict rather than through any combat with a serpentine adversary.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, early passages on the conflict between Mwindo and his father Shemwindo, on Shemwindo's attempts to have Mwindo killed (with the drum-and-river episode the most widely cited of the early-epic episodes), on Mwindo's survival of each attempt, and on his subsequent pursuit of Shemwindo across the human and spirit worlds; on the early epic's narrative work as the establishment of the hero's character through the father-son conflict rather than through combat with a serpentine adversary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

Mwindo's journeys, in the middle sections of the epic, take him through the river-realm (where he is sheltered by Iyangura), through the underworld of Muisa (where he is tested and where he eventually defeats or compels Muisa), and through further regions of the spirit-and-forest world, with the journey-sequence being a sequence of trials each of which adds to the hero's accumulated power and reputation but each of which also occasions warnings, debts, and obligations that the hero accumulates alongside his power.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, middle-epic passages on Mwindo's journeys through the river-realm (where he is sheltered by his aunt Iyangura), through the underworld of Muisa (where he is tested and where he eventually defeats or compels the underworld lord), and through further regions of the spirit-and-forest world; on the journey-sequence as a sequence of trials each of which adds to the hero's accumulated power and reputation but each of which also occasions warnings, debts, and obligations alongside the power; on the hero's accumulating-power-and-accumulating-obligations pattern as a load-bearing feature of the epic's middle sections.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

Mwindo's accumulation of power is checked, in the epic's framing, by the warnings he receives from his aunts and from various other figures along the way: the recorded performance repeatedly registers in formulaic refrains and direct narrative comment that Mwindo's growing power is dangerous, that his hubris will require correction, and that the powers above and below are watching the hero's career with a view to the eventual moment at which the correction will fall — a foreshadowing pattern that the narrative develops across multiple episodes before the Kirimu episode arrives.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, throughout the middle-epic passages on the formulaic refrains and direct narrative comments through which Mwindo's accumulating power is repeatedly registered as dangerous, on the warnings the hero receives from his aunts and from other figures, on the announced expectation that the hubris will require correction, and on the powers above and below being presented as watching the hero's career with a view to the eventual moment of correction; Belcher 1999, ch. on Mwindo, on the foreshadowing-of-correction pattern as a load-bearing feature of the epic's middle sections preparing the Kirimu episode and its consequences.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

By the time the Kirimu episode arrives, in the recorded performance's overall structure, Mwindo has already established his exceptional power, defeated multiple adversaries human and spirit, and accumulated reputation as a hero whom the powers above and below regard with concern; the Kirimu episode is therefore not the establishment of the hero's status but a moment within an already-developed heroic career, and the killing of the dragon is presented within a narrative arc that has been preparing the audience for the consequences of the hero's overreach throughout the preceding sections.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, on the structural placement of the Kirimu episode within the overall epic — arriving after Mwindo has already established his exceptional power, defeated multiple adversaries human and spirit, and accumulated heroic reputation — and on the killing of the dragon as a moment within an already-developed heroic career rather than as the establishment of the hero's status; on the narrative arc's preparation of the audience for the consequences of the hero's overreach across the preceding sections.]

=== The Kirimu episode: provocation, hunt, and killing

Kirimu, the dragon at the chapter's narrative centre, is in the recorded performance a creature of the deep forest with multiple heads (the recorded text gives seven in the principal passages, with formulaic-passage variation registered in the editorial apparatus), a long body, and a fire-and-darkness aura; the dragon dwells away from the human settlements in a forest fastness and is not, in the framing the narrator gives the audience, an adversary of the cosmic order or of the human community as such — Kirimu is rather a powerful spirit-being of the forest world whose existence is part of the already-established Nyanga cosmography rather than a primordial chaos against which the hero must establish order.footnote:[Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, Kirimu episode passages on Kirimu as a creature of the deep forest with multiple heads (seven in the principal passages, with formulaic-passage variation registered in the editorial apparatus), a long body, and a fire-and-darkness aura; on the dragon's dwelling in a forest fastness away from the human settlements; on Kirimu's narrative framing as a powerful spirit-being of the forest world whose existence is part of the already-established Nyanga cosmography rather than as a primordial chaos against which the hero must establish order; per glossary 'Kirimu' first_mention_gloss on the Nyanga dragon slain by Mwindo.]

=== The maturation-through-humiliation reading: the tradition's own framing

The Nyanga oral tradition's own framing of the Mwindo / Kirimu episode is, per brief 29-mwindo-kirimu.yaml summary and cultural_relevance, *maturation-through-humiliation* rather than *cosmogonic combat*: the narrative is told as the story of an exceptional hero whose accumulated power outruns his moral-and-cosmographic discipline, who commits a transgressive killing in the dragon-hunt, who is corrected by the sky-god, and who returns to the human world as a properly-formed chief by virtue of having undergone the correction — with the moral-didactic kernel that the narrative communicates to its hearers being the structural necessity of the correction and the limits of unsanctioned heroic power against the cosmographic order.footnote:[Per brief 29-mwindo-kirimu.yaml summary on the inverted moral valence of the dragon-slaying as hubris rather than order-making, and on the hero's required humiliation and re-integration as the narrative's didactic centre, with the cultural_relevance.function entry registering didactic (maturation through humiliation), social-moral (reciprocal balance between human and spirit worlds), and religious-political (sky-god's authority over the hero) functions; Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, throughout the Kirimu episode and following passages, on the maturation-through-humiliation framing as the Nyanga tradition's own narrative-structural register; Belcher 1999, ch. on Mwindo, on the moral-didactic kernel and on the limits-of-heroic-power register of the episode within the Nyanga tradition's own framing.]
