=== Source overview

The chapter surveys the Greek productive reworking of the dragon-combat pattern in the wake of the flagship Zeus-Typhon narrative treated in the preceding chapter, organising six post-Olympian dragon-combats by function rather than by hero.footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml; Ogden 2013, *Drakōn: Dragon Myth and Serpent Cult in the Greek and Roman Worlds*, ch. 2 (Greek dragon-combats).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The scholarly anchor for the chapter is Daniel Ogden's *Drakōn: Dragon Myth and Serpent Cult in the Greek and Roman Worlds*, published by Oxford University Press in 2013, with chapter 2 surveying the principal Greek dragon-combats and chapter 11 treating the *Ketos* and its Christian reception.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2 (Greek dragon-combats) and ch. 11 (Ketos and Christian reception).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=chs. 2 and 11

Ogden's monograph is printed-only; in this chapter it is cited at the chapter level rather than at sub-page granularity, in line with the established citation discipline for printed comparative anchors in the present book.footnote:[Ogden 2013, chs. 2 and 11 (printed only; cited at chapter level per brief).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=chs. 2 and 11

The specialist anchor for the Apollo-Python material is Joseph Fontenrose's *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, published by the University of California Press in 1959, with chapters 1 to 3 treating the Delphic combat, the *drakaina*-Python tradition, and the Septerion festival.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, chs. 1–3 on Apollo-Python.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Fontenrose's monograph is printed-only and is cited at chapter level in the present chapter, with sub-page references reserved for the small number of points where the Septerion-festival argument is load-bearing.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3 (printed only; cited at chapter level per brief).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The specialist anchor for the Geryon material — and, by extension, for the Indo-European cattle-raid argument that links Geryon to Hercules-Cacus — is Calvert Watkins's *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics*, published by Oxford University Press in 1995, whose Part V treats the Geryon combat as an Indo-European cattle-raid reflex.footnote:[Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics*, Part V on Geryon as an Indo-European cattle-raid reflex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Watkins's monograph is printed-only and is cited at section level in the present chapter, with explicit reference to Part V as the locus of the Geryon argument.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V (printed only; cited at section level per brief).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The specialist anchor for the Hercules-Cacus material is M.L. West's *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, published by Oxford University Press in 2007, whose chapter 6 places Cacus in the Indo-European storm-god / cattle-raid complex alongside the Vedic and Iranian comparanda.footnote:[West 2007, *Indo-European Poetry and Myth*, ch. 6 on Cacus as an Italic Indo-European cattle-raid reflex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The secondary survey-anchor for the Greek combat-myth corpus is Neil Forsyth's *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth*, published by Princeton University Press in 1987, whose chapter 4 treats Greek combat narratives as part of the long-trajectory combat-myth survey.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth*, ch. 4 on Greek combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 4

The Greek primary texts on which the chapter relies — the Homeric Hymn to Apollo, Hesiod's *Theogony*, Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca*, the *Iliad*, Pausanias, Ovid's *Metamorphoses* and *Fasti*, and Virgil's *Aeneid* — are all extant in standard scholarly editions, and there are no load-bearing textual lacunae of the kind that affects, for instance, the Hittite Illuyanka tablets or the Akkadian Labbu fragment.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the textual condition of the principal Greek combat sources; Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the Apollo-Python textual corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The chapter is a family-chapter (related-tier) treatment, organising six dragon-combat sub-cases by combat-function — cult-foundation, hero-labour, and Indo-European cattle-raid reflex — rather than by giving each of the six hero-figures a thin full chapter.footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, family-chapter framing with six sub-cases organised by function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The chapter's framing premise is that the six combats inherit the Greek dragon-combat template — the template whose flagship instance is the Zeus-Typhon combat treated in the preceding chapter — and rework it in productive directions that are no longer cosmogonic but variously cult-foundational, hero-initiatory, and Indo-European-cattle-raid-reflexive.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-Typhonomachy Greek dragon-combat corpus; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the Greek combat-pattern's productive reworkings.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

=== Apollo vs. Python — cult-foundation at Delphi

The earliest extant narrative attestation of the Delphic dragon-combat is the Homeric Hymn to Apollo, in which the young Apollo, in search of an oracle-site, slays a monstrous serpent guarding a spring on the slopes of Parnassus.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, ch. 1, on the Homeric Hymn to Apollo as the earliest narrative attestation of the Delphic combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=ch. 1

In the Homeric Hymn the serpent is unnamed and female — a *drakaina* — and her slaying establishes the site at Pytho, the older name of what later tradition calls Delphi.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–2, on the unnamed female *drakaina* at Pytho in the Homeric Hymn; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the *drakaina* designation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-2

The later tradition, principally Ovid's *Metamorphoses* book 1 and the prose mythographers, names the slain dragon Python and presents the figure as male, recasting the Hymn's *drakaina* under the masculine name that Joseph Fontenrose's monograph takes as its title.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the renaming of the Hymn's *drakaina* as Python in the later tradition; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same renaming.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

[VARIANT: primary=Homeric Hymn to Apollo (the slain serpent is an unnamed female *drakaina*) | alt=Ovid *Metamorphoses* 1 and later mythographers (the slain dragon is male and named Python); Fontenrose 1959 chs. 1–3 surveys the gender variation across the corpus | chosen: present-both | reason: the Hymn and the later tradition both circulate as load-bearing witnesses; the chapter treats the gender variation as a documented feature of the Delphic corpus rather than collapsing it.]

In the Hymn the *drakaina* is associated with the local nymph-tradition and with a separate monster-rearing episode involving the giant Typhaon, an episode that Fontenrose treats as an aetiological doublet of the principal combat.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, ch. 1, on the Hymn's monster-rearing episode and the Typhaon doublet.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=ch. 1

The Ovidian Python is described as an enormous earth-born serpent that emerges from the post-flood mud at the foot of Parnassus, an aetiological frame that locates the dragon's origin in the chthonic-fertile substrate that Apollo's killing then purifies.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Ovid's earth-born Python and the post-flood frame; Fontenrose 1959, ch. 3, on the chthonic-purification reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Apollo's weapon in both the Hymn and the Ovidian narrative is the bow and arrows — the divine archery that becomes the iconographic emblem of Apollo as dragon-slayer in the later sculptural and ceramic tradition.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the bow-and-arrows of Apollo in the Delphic combat-iconography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The killing of the dragon at Pytho is the foundational act of the Delphic oracle: in the Hymn, Apollo establishes his cult and his prophetic seat at the site of the combat, and the place-name Pytho itself is treated as derived from the rotting (*pythein*) of the slain dragon's body.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–2, on the cult-foundation function and the place-name etymology; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same etymology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-2

The Apollo-Python combat is therefore cult-foundational rather than cosmogonic: the cosmos has already been established under Zeus, and the combat does the order-making work of installing a new cult at a specific terrestrial site.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the cult-foundation function of the Apollo-Python combat; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-Olympian-establishment dating of the episode.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Septerion festival, attested in Plutarch and discussed at length in Fontenrose's monograph, was a Delphic ritual held every eight years that re-enacted Apollo's killing of the dragon, with a hut representing the dragon's lair burned and a procession of purification staged through the sanctuary.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the Septerion festival as eightfold-cyclical re-enactment of the Python-slaying.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Septerion's eight-year periodicity gives the Apollo-Python combat a cyclical-maintenance reflex alongside its primary cult-foundational function: the original killing is a one-off founding event, but its ritual re-enactment makes the dragon's threat a recurrent feature of the Delphic religious calendar.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the cyclical-maintenance reflex of the Septerion alongside the cult-foundation function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Pausanias 10.6.5–6, in the second-century CE *Description of Greece*, preserves a local Delphic version of the combat-narrative and a brief account of the cult-aetiology, providing a third independent ancient witness alongside the Homeric Hymn and Ovid.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, ch. 1, on Pausanias 10.6.5–6 as a third witness to the Delphic combat-tradition; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Pausanias's account.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=ch. 1

Fontenrose's broader argument — that the Delphic combat is the type-specimen for a wider Greek and Near-Eastern combat-myth pattern — is the methodological reason the Apollo-Python episode is a load-bearing case for the comparative chapter on cyclical-maintenance combat (Part II C3).footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on Apollo-Python as a comparative type-specimen.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The *Septerion* festival is therefore the chapter's principal forward-link to Part II C3 (cyclical-maintenance combat), where it joins the Egyptian Ra-Apep ritual cycle and the Hittite *purulli* festival as the three principal cyclical-maintenance reflexes treated in the comparative section.footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, Septerion as forward-link to Part II C3; Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the cyclical-maintenance reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

Apollo's epithets *Pythios* and *Pythoktonos* — "of Pytho" and "Python-slayer" — are documented in the Greek cult-corpus as the two principal cult-titles that record the combat in the lived ritual language of Delphic worshippers.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the *Pythios* and *Pythoktonos* epithets; Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the cult-titles in the Delphic ritual language.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Forsyth's chapter 4 places the Apollo-Python combat alongside Zeus-Typhon as the two principal Greek instances of the long combat-myth pattern, with Apollo-Python representing the cult-foundational function-type and Zeus-Typhon the sovereignty-cosmogonic type.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on Apollo-Python alongside Zeus-Typhon in the Greek combat-myth corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 4

The Apollo-Python episode is post-Olympian: the cosmogony is already complete, Zeus is already the sovereign, and Apollo is going out as a young Olympian to install his own cult-site within the already-ordered world that the Typhonomachy has secured.footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the post-Olympian dating of the combat; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-cosmogonic frame of the episode.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

=== Herakles vs. the Hydra, Ladon, and Geryon — three labours

The Lernaean Hydra is described in Hesiod's *Theogony* 313–318 as a many-headed serpent reared in the marshes of Lerna in the Argolid, and named there as one of the monstrous offspring of Typhon and Echidna.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Hesiod *Theogony* 313–318 as the early attestation of the Lernaean Hydra; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the Hydra in the Greek combat-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* 2.5 is the principal mythographic source for the labour-sequence, presenting the killing of the Hydra as the second of the twelve labours that Herakles undertakes for King Eurystheus.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Apollodorus *Bibliotheca* 2.5 as the principal mythographic source for the labours; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the labour-sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Hydra in the mythographic tradition is many-headed — the count varies across sources from seven to nine to a hundred — and the central narrative motif is regenerative: when one head is cut, two grow back in its place, so that the killing requires a sustained tactical solution rather than a single decisive stroke.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the head-count variation and the regeneration motif; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the Hydra's regrowth as the narrative crux.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The tactical solution is supplied by Herakles's nephew and companion Iolaos, who cauterises each neck-stump with fire as Herakles severs it, preventing the regrowth and converting an otherwise interminable combat into a finite victory.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Iolaos's cauterisation as the tactical resolution of the Hydra combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

One of the Hydra's heads is described in Apollodorus as immortal, and after its severance Herakles buries it under a heavy stone — a narrative detail that converts the killing into an act of containment rather than annihilation.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the immortal head and its containment under stone in Apollodorus 2.5.2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

After the killing Herakles dips his arrows in the Hydra's venomous blood, and the venom-arrows then function as a recurring narrative instrument across the later labours and across the eventual death-of-Herakles tradition.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the venom-arrows as a recurring instrument in the Heraklean cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Hydra combat is hero-labour rather than cult-foundational: the killing earns Herakles credit toward the completion of the twelve-labour sequence imposed by Eurystheus and does not establish a cult-site or rework the cosmic order.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the hero-labour function of the Hydra combat; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the labour-sequence framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 4

The dragon Ladon, the second serpent-adversary in the Heraklean cycle treated by the chapter, guards the apples of the Hesperides at the western edge of the world and is killed (or, in some versions, simply circumvented) when Herakles undertakes the labour of fetching the apples.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Ladon as the serpent-guardian of the Hesperidean apples; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the Ladon labour.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Hesiod's *Theogony* describes Ladon as the serpent who guards the golden apples in the garden of the Hesperides at the world's western verge, and the mythographic tradition consolidates this guardianship into the eleventh of the labours.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Hesiod's *Theogony* on Ladon and on the eleventh labour in Apollodorus 2.5.11.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Hesperides themselves are nymphs of the western horizon, daughters in the standard tradition of Atlas or of Night, and their garden is located beyond the known world at the place where the sun sets.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Hesperides and the western-horizon location of their garden.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Ladon's role in the labour is iconographic as much as narrative: in the surviving artistic record the dragon is regularly depicted coiled around the apple-tree, and the labour-image of Herakles confronting a tree-coiled serpent becomes one of the most widely reproduced Heraklean scenes.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Ladon-and-tree iconography in Heraklean art.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The third serpent-adversary in the Heraklean cycle treated by the chapter is Geryon, the triple-bodied warrior whose cattle Herakles drives away from the western island of Erytheia in the tenth labour.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on Geryon as the triple-bodied adversary of the tenth labour; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Erytheia setting in Apollodorus 2.5.10.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Geryon is not strictly a serpent in the iconography but a triple-bodied warrior — three heads, three torsos, three pairs of legs — and his inclusion in a dragon-combat chapter is justified by the structural function of the combat rather than by the surface morphology of the adversary.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on Geryon's triple-bodied form and the structural classification of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Watkins's argument in Part V of *How to Kill a Dragon* is that the Geryon combat is an Indo-European cattle-raid reflex: the hero crosses a boundary, takes the cattle of an other-worldly adversary, and brings them back across the same boundary to the human world.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on Geryon as an Indo-European cattle-raid reflex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Watkins reads the Geryon combat as a Greek instance of the same Indo-European pattern that produces, in different traditions, Indra's cattle-rescues from the Paṇis and the Vedic Trita-Āptya cattle-rescues — a structural family that the comparative chapter on the Indo-European cattle-raid (Part II C5) treats together.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on the cross-tradition Indo-European cattle-raid pattern; per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, forward-link to Part II C5.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

[INFERENCE: the chapter's grouping of the Hydra, Ladon, and Geryon labours under a single sub-section, although mythographically natural and supported by Apollodorus's labour-sequence, is the chapter's editorial framing rather than an unmediated feature of the Greek tradition; the three labours are scattered across Apollodorus 2.5.2 (Hydra), 2.5.10 (Geryon), and 2.5.11 (Ladon) and are linked here by their common serpent / monstrous-adversary feature. | source: Watkins 1995 Part V; Ogden 2013 ch. 2; Apollodorus 2.5 labour-sequence | scope: editorial grouping for the family-chapter format, per brief.]

The Geryon combat is therefore Indo-European-cattle-raid-reflexive in function, distinct from the hero-labour function of the Hydra and Ladon combats and distinct again from the cult-foundational function of the Apollo-Python combat at Delphi.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on the cattle-raid function distinct from cult-foundation and from generic hero-labour.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* 2.5 is the standard mythographic source for the full labour-sequence and supplies the working narrative skeleton for all three of the Heraklean serpent-combats treated here.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Apollodorus 2.5 as the standard mythographic source for the labour-sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Hesiod's *Theogony* 313–318 names the Hydra alongside Ladon (described in adjacent lines) and the Chimaera as members of the same monstrous brood descended from Typhon and Echidna, providing an early genealogical pre-grouping of the dragon-adversaries the labours later confront.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Hesiod's *Theogony* 313ff. as the early genealogical pre-grouping of the monstrous brood.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The post-Typhonic genealogical placement of the Hydra, Ladon, and the Chimaera in Hesiod is itself a feature of the Greek productive reworking of the dragon-combat pattern: the Typhonomachy has installed Zeus's sovereignty, but Typhon's monstrous progeny survive him and become the adversaries that the post-Olympian heroes must dispatch.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-Typhonic genealogical placement and the productive reworking it enables.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

=== Perseus vs. Ketos — and the Andromeda rescue

Perseus is the son of Zeus and the mortal Danaë, and his career as a slayer of monstrous adversaries opens with the beheading of Medusa, the Gorgon whose gaze petrifies, in the labour imposed by King Polydektes of Seriphos.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Perseus cycle and the Medusa-beheading as its opening combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Medusa episode is treated in the chapter as the cycle-opening combat that supplies the iconographic equipment — the winged sandals, the *kibisis* (the bag for the head), the cap of invisibility — that Perseus then carries into the Andromeda rescue.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the iconographic equipment carried over from the Medusa episode to the Andromeda rescue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Andromeda rescue is the chapter's principal Perseus combat for the comparative argument: returning from the Medusa-beheading, Perseus encounters the Aithiopian princess Andromeda chained to a sea-cliff as a sacrifice to a sea-monster (the *Ketos*) sent by Poseidon to ravage the coast.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the Andromeda rescue and the Ketos in Apollodorus 2.4.3 and Ovid *Metamorphoses* 4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

The narrative cause of the Ketos's appearance is the boast of Andromeda's mother Cassiopeia, who claims to be more beautiful than the Nereids; Poseidon, offended on the Nereids' behalf, sends the *Ketos* against the coast and an oracle declares that Andromeda's exposure will appease the god.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on Cassiopeia's boast, Poseidon's punishment, and the oracle in Apollodorus 2.4.3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

The *Ketos* is a sea-monster — the Greek term denotes a generic large sea-creature, and the iconographic tradition consolidates the figure as a serpentine sea-dragon with a long body and a fanged head — and the iconographic stability of the type is what makes the Andromeda rescue the principal Greek pattern-source for the medieval St. George rescue.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the *Ketos* iconography and the Andromeda rescue as iconographic pattern-source.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* 2.4.3 supplies the prose-mythographic narrative of the rescue, naming Cassiopeia, Cepheus, Andromeda, and Perseus, and presenting the killing of the *Ketos* and the marriage of Perseus and Andromeda as the resolution of the episode.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on Apollodorus 2.4.3 as the prose-mythographic narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

Ovid's *Metamorphoses* book 4 supplies the principal poetic narrative of the rescue, expanding the prose mythographers' account into a full episode with extended description of Andromeda's exposure, Perseus's aerial approach, and the *Ketos*-combat itself.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on Ovid *Metamorphoses* book 4 as the principal poetic narrative of the rescue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

In the Ovidian version Perseus engages the *Ketos* aerially, using the winged sandals from the Medusa episode, and kills the monster with the sword that has already beheaded Medusa, transferring the iconographic equipment of one combat directly into the next.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the Ovidian version's aerial combat and the equipment-transfer.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

The Andromeda rescue is the iconographic archetype of the medieval St. George princess-and-dragon scene: a chained royal woman, a dragon-monster, a hero arriving from outside the local order to kill the monster and free the woman.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the Perseus-Andromeda rescue as iconographic archetype of the medieval St. George rescue-scene; per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, Ch10 cross-reference.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

Ogden's chapter 11 of *Drakōn* makes the Perseus-Andromeda iconographic-substrate argument explicitly, tracking the visual continuity from the Greco-Roman rescue-scenes to the late-antique-and-medieval St. George rescue iconography in mosaic, fresco, manuscript illumination, and panel painting.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the visual continuity from Perseus-Andromeda to St. George.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

The Perseus-Ketos combat is hero-labour-rescue in function: the killing does not establish a cult-site, does not rework the cosmic order, and does not recover divine cattle, but rescues a specific imperilled human and so structurally resembles the hero-initiation labours rather than either the cult-foundation or the cattle-raid types.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the rescue function of the Perseus-Ketos combat; Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the hero-labour-rescue framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 4

The marriage of Perseus and Andromeda following the rescue is the standard narrative resolution and supplies the pre-existing-betrothal complication (Andromeda's prior promise to her uncle Phineus) that Ovid develops into a second combat at the wedding-feast.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the Phineus-wedding-feast complication in Ovid *Metamorphoses* 5.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

The Perseus cycle as a whole — Medusa-beheading and Andromeda-rescue together — supplies the Greek tradition with two iconographically complementary combats: the head-trophy-and-petrifying-monster combat (Medusa) and the rescue-from-sea-monster combat (Ketos), with the latter the load-bearing case for Christian-reception cross-references.footnote:[Ogden 2013, chs. 2 and 11, on the iconographic complementarity of the two Perseus combats.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=chs. 2 and 11

The chapter's principal forward-link from the Perseus-Ketos material is therefore to chapter 10 of the present volume on the Christian reception (St. George and St. Michael), where the Perseus-Andromeda iconographic substrate is registered as the documented Greco-Roman background for the high-medieval princess-and-dragon episode.footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, forward-link to Ch10 (Christian reception); Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the substrate-relation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

Pausanias and other ancient witnesses locate variants of the Andromeda story at Joppa (Jaffa) on the Levantine coast, where local tradition pointed to the chains and the rocks of the rescue-site, providing a topographical anchor for the episode that may have facilitated its later transmission into Christian-era contexts.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the Joppa localisation of the Andromeda rescue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

=== Kadmos vs. the dragon of Ares — foundation of Thebes

Kadmos is the son of Agenor of Tyre, sent out by his father to find his sister Europa after Zeus has abducted her, and the Greek tradition recasts his unsuccessful search as the foundation-narrative of Thebes.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Kadmos foundation-narrative and Apollodorus *Bibliotheca* 3.4.1–2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* 3.4.1–2 is the principal prose-mythographic source for the Kadmos foundation-narrative, and Ovid's *Metamorphoses* book 3 lines 1–130 supplies the principal poetic narrative, with substantial agreement between the two on the major narrative elements.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Apollodorus 3.4.1–2 and Ovid *Metamorphoses* 3.1–130 as the principal sources.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Following the Delphic oracle's instruction, Kadmos abandons the search for Europa and follows a cow until it lies down at the site of the future Thebes, where he intends to sacrifice the animal and found the city.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Delphic oracle, the cow-following, and the cow's collapse at the future site of Thebes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The site is occupied by a great serpent — the dragon of Ares — guarding a spring sacred to the war-god, and when Kadmos's companions go to the spring to fetch water for the sacrifice the dragon kills them.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the dragon of Ares at the spring of Ares and the killing of Kadmos's companions in Apollodorus 3.4.1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Kadmos kills the dragon in single combat, and in the standard mythographic tradition the killing is performed with a great stone or with a spear, depending on the source and the iconographic moment depicted.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Kadmos's killing of the dragon in single combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the iconographic-moment variation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

After the killing the goddess Athena instructs Kadmos to sow half of the dragon's teeth in the earth, and from the sown teeth there spring up armed warriors — the *Spartoi*, the "Sown Men" — fully grown and battle-ready.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Athena-instruction, the tooth-sowing, and the *Spartoi* in Apollodorus 3.4.1; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the *Spartoi* tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Spartoi*, on first emerging from the soil, immediately fall to fighting one another; in the standard tradition only five of them survive, and these five become the founding patrilineal lineages of the Theban aristocracy.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the *Spartoi* mutual combat and the five surviving founding-lineages of Thebes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Kadmos combat is therefore cult-and-city-foundational: the killing of the serpent and the sowing of its teeth produce both the population and the political genealogy of the new city, with the dragon's body literally generative of the founding aristocracy.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the foundation-function of the Kadmos combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the city-foundation reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The dragon of Ares is post-Olympian: the cosmos is established under Zeus and the Theban region is already inhabited by Greek-speaking populations, but the city of Thebes itself is not yet founded, and the serpent's killing supplies the foundational moment for the polis.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-Olympian framing of the Kadmos combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Because the slain dragon was sacred to Ares, Kadmos must serve a long expiation to the war-god — eight years of servitude in the standard tradition — before being granted the kingship of Thebes and the marriage to Harmonia, the daughter of Ares and Aphrodite.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Kadmos's eight-year expiatory service to Ares and the marriage to Harmonia.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Kadmean tooth-sowing has a structural near-parallel in the Argonautic tradition, where Jason sows the teeth of a different dragon at Kolchis and confronts a similar crop of armed earth-born men, suggesting that the Greek tradition treats serpent's-teeth-as-warriors as a transferable narrative motif rather than a Theban exclusive.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Argonautic parallel of the dragon's-teeth-as-warriors motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Kadmos combat shares with the Apollo-Python combat the cult-foundation function-type, but differs in a structurally important respect: the Apollo-Python combat installs a god's cult, while the Kadmos combat installs a city's founding aristocracy, and the chapter treats these as two adjacent foundation-types rather than as a single category.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the cult-foundation versus city-foundation distinction within the Greek combat-corpus; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same distinction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 4

Ovid's *Metamorphoses* 3.1–130 expands the prose-mythographic combat into an extended poetic narrative, with the dragon described in catalogue-mode physical detail and the *Spartoi* episode given a dramatic mutual-combat finale before the five-survivor resolution.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Ovid *Metamorphoses* 3.1–130 as the expanded poetic narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

=== Bellerophon vs. the Chimaera — Lycian hero-labour

Bellerophon is a Greek hero exiled to Lycia in southwest Anatolia, where King Iobates receives him at the request of Proitos and, by way of disposing of an unwanted guest, commissions him to slay the Chimaera that ravages the country.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Bellerophon-Iobates frame and the Chimaera commission in Iliad 6.179–183.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The earliest extant attestation of the combat is *Iliad* 6.179–183, in the speech in which Glaucus narrates the deeds of his ancestor Bellerophon, and the passage is the principal Homeric witness to the Lycian dragon-combat tradition.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Iliad 6.179–183 as the earliest attestation; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the Homeric witness to Bellerophon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Iliad* describes the Chimaera as a divinely-bred composite monster — *prosthe leōn, opithen de drakōn, messē de chimaira*, "in front a lion, behind a serpent, and in the middle a she-goat" — fixing the canonical tripartite morphology that the later iconographic tradition standardises.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Iliad 6.181 description of the Chimaera's tripartite morphology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Iliad* further specifies that the Chimaera breathes fire, a feature taken up by the entire later tradition and consolidated in the iconographic record as one of the visual identifiers of the Lycian monster.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the fire-breathing motif in Iliad 6 and the iconographic tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Hesiod's *Theogony* in the lines following the Hydra (in the same monstrous-genealogy passage) places the Chimaera among the offspring of Echidna and Typhon, attaching it to the post-Typhonic monstrous brood that supplies the post-Olympian dragon-adversaries treated across the chapter.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Hesiod *Theogony* on the Chimaera's post-Typhonic genealogy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Bellerophon undertakes the Chimaera-killing on the back of Pegasus, the winged horse born from the blood of the slain Medusa — and the Pegasus-mount is the principal narrative innovation of the Bellerophon tradition, supplying the aerial-combat solution to a fire-breathing ground-monster.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Pegasus as the Bellerophon-mount and the aerial-combat solution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The killing-method in the post-Homeric tradition involves a lump of lead fixed to Bellerophon's spear: the lead melts in the Chimaera's fiery breath and the molten metal is forced down the monster's throat, killing it from within.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the lead-on-spear killing-method in the post-Homeric Bellerophon tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

Apollodorus's *Bibliotheca* 2.3 supplies the prose-mythographic narrative of the combat alongside the *Iliad*'s embedded narrative, and the two principal witnesses agree on the killing's basic structure while differing on the killing-method itself.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Apollodorus 2.3 as the prose narrative and on the witness-agreement on basic structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Chimaera is regularly cited in scholarly discussion as a documented Anatolian or Lycian regional monster: ancient witnesses associate the figure with a fire-vent (a natural gas seep) on a mountain in Lycia, and the local topographical association strengthens the case for treating Bellerophon-Chimaera as a Lycian regional combat-tradition mediated through Greek epic.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Lycian fire-vent association of the Chimaera and the regional-tradition reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Bellerophon-Chimaera combat is hero-labour-and-initiation in function: the killing is undertaken at the commission of a foreign king, earns Bellerophon Iobates's respect (and, eventually, his daughter), and so structurally resembles the Heraklean labour-cycle's commissioned-killings rather than the cult-foundation combats.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the hero-labour function of the Bellerophon combat; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the commissioned-killing structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 4

Bellerophon's later career — his unsuccessful attempt to ride Pegasus to Olympus and his subsequent fall — is a hubris-narrative pendant to the Chimaera-killing and lies outside the chapter's combat-myth scope; the chapter limits its Bellerophon material to the combat itself.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Pegasus-and-fall pendant as outside the combat-myth scope of the Bellerophon material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

=== Hercules vs. Cacus — Italic IE cattle-raid reflex

The Hercules-Cacus combat is the chapter's one Latin-tradition combat, and the Latin name *Hercules* is used throughout this section in distinction to the Greek-tradition *Herakles* used in the labour-cluster section above, in line with the chapter's glossary discipline.footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml glossary discipline, Latin *Hercules* in the Cacus context and Greek *Herakles* elsewhere; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Latin-tradition designation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The principal Latin-tradition source for the Cacus combat is Virgil's *Aeneid* book 8 lines 190–279, embedded in the narrative of Aeneas's reception by Evander on the future site of Rome.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Virgil *Aeneid* 8.190–279 as the principal Latin source for Hercules-Cacus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The narrative frame is that Hercules, returning from the western edge of the world with the cattle of Geryon — that is, on the homeward leg of the cattle-raid that the Heraklean cluster section above treats as Geryon's tenth labour — drives the herd through the future site of Rome and stops to rest on the Aventine.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the homeward-leg framing in the Aeneid 8 and the connection to the Geryon labour.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The narrative connection between the Cacus episode and the Geryon labour is therefore not editorial but textual: Virgil and Ovid both situate the Cacus combat as the Italian sequel to the Geryon cattle-taking, with the same cattle re-stolen on Italian soil and re-recovered by the same hero.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the textual continuity of the Geryon-cattle frame across the Greek Geryon labour and the Latin Cacus episode.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Cacus is described in Virgil as a fire-breathing giant — half-human, monstrous, *semihominis Caci facies*, and his cave on the Aventine is described as smoke-blackened from his fire-breath and from the bones of his victims.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Virgilian description of Cacus and the Aventine cave.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

While Hercules sleeps after driving the cattle to the Aventine, Cacus steals four bulls and four heifers, dragging them backwards by their tails into his cave so that the trail of hoofprints leads outward from the cave rather than inward, in an attempt to disguise the theft.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the backwards-dragging cattle-theft motif in the Aeneid 8.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Hercules discovers the theft when the remaining cattle low at dawn and the stolen cattle low back from the cave, and he tracks the lowing to its source, breaks open the cave, and confronts Cacus inside.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the lowing-recognition motif and the cave-breaking in the Aeneid 8.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The combat in Virgil is described in storm-cloud-and-fire terms: Cacus belches fire and smoke from the cave, Hercules tears off the cave's stone roof and rains rocks and projectiles down on Cacus, and the killing is achieved by strangulation, with Hercules's hands at the giant's throat.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the storm-and-strangulation combat-mechanics in the Aeneid 8.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Ovid's *Fasti* book 1 lines 543–582 supplies the second principal Latin-tradition narrative of the combat, agreeing with Virgil on the basic narrative shape (cattle-theft, lowing-discovery, cave-combat, strangulation) and differing in tone (Ovid's account is more compact and aetiologically focused on the Roman cult of Hercules at the *Ara Maxima*).footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Ovid *Fasti* 1.543–582 as the second principal Latin-tradition narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

[VARIANT: primary=Virgil *Aeneid* 8.190–279 (extended epic narrative; storm-cloud combat mechanics; substantial monster-description) | alt=Ovid *Fasti* 1.543–582 (compact aetiological narrative; cult-foundation focus on the Ara Maxima) | chosen: present-both | reason: West 2007 ch. 6 treats both as load-bearing witnesses to the Italic Cacus tradition; Virgil supplies the combat-mechanics, Ovid supplies the cult-aetiology, and the chapter cites both for the Italic combat-narrative as it survives.]

The killing of Cacus is followed in both Virgil and Ovid by the foundation of the *Ara Maxima* — the Greatest Altar — at the site, where Hercules institutes a cult that Roman tradition then continued; the Cacus-killing is therefore both cattle-raid resolution and Roman cult-foundation in a single combined narrative beat.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Ara Maxima* cult-foundation following the Cacus-killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

West's chapter 6 of *Indo-European Poetry and Myth* makes the principal scholarly argument that Cacus is an Italic Indo-European cattle-raid reflex, structurally cognate to the same Indo-European pattern that Watkins reads in the Greek Geryon combat and that the comparative chapter on the Indo-European cattle-raid (Part II C5) treats together with the Vedic Indra-Paṇis material.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Cacus as Italic Indo-European cattle-raid reflex; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the Greek Geryon side of the same pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Cacus combat shares with the Geryon combat the cattle-raid function-type — the recovery of cattle taken from a hero by a monstrous adversary — and in this respect Hercules's two cattle-related combats (Geryon and Cacus) bracket the cattle-raid material on its outward and homeward legs.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the bracketing structure of Geryon and Cacus around the cattle-raid material; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the same bracketing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Hercules-Cacus combat is therefore the chapter's principal forward-link to the comparative chapter on the Indo-European cattle-raid (Part II C5), where it joins the Geryon material from Watkins's Part V and the Vedic Indra-cattle-rescues from Jamison and Brereton's Rigveda translation in a single comparative section.footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, forward-link to Part II C5; West 2007, ch. 6, on the comparative position of Cacus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Italic localisation of the Cacus combat — the Aventine, the future site of Rome, the *Ara Maxima* — gives the episode a topographical specificity that ties the Indo-European cattle-raid pattern to the Roman urban landscape, and that turns the inherited combat-pattern into a foundation-aetion for a specific Roman cult.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the topographical specificity of the Cacus episode and its link to the Roman urban landscape.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

=== Family pattern and forward links

The six combats treated in the chapter share a structural combat-pattern that is recognisably the same template as the Zeus-Typhon flagship of the preceding chapter: a champion-figure confronts a monstrous serpent-or-composite adversary in single combat, defeats it, and produces some form of order-effect from the killing.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the structural continuity of the post-Typhonomachy Greek combat-pattern; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the same structural continuity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The six combats differ functionally, however, in productive directions that diverge from the cosmogonic-and-sovereignty function-type of the Zeus-Typhon flagship: cult-foundation (Apollo-Python, Kadmos), hero-labour (Hydra, Ladon, Perseus-Ketos, Bellerophon), and Indo-European cattle-raid reflex (Geryon, Hercules-Cacus).footnote:[Per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, functional differentiation of the six combats; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the same functional spread.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

[INFERENCE: the chapter's three-way functional grouping (cult-foundation / hero-labour / cattle-raid) is a synthetic categorisation that, while well-supported by Ogden 2013, Watkins 1995, West 2007, and Fontenrose 1959 individually, does not appear in any single source as a formalised tripartite scheme; the tripartite framing is the chapter's editorial synthesis. | source: Ogden 2013 ch. 2; Watkins 1995 Part V; West 2007 ch. 6; Fontenrose 1959 chs. 1–3 | scope: tripartite functional scheme synthesised for the family-chapter's organising frame.]

None of the six combats is cosmogonic: each takes place after the Olympian sovereignty has been established by Zeus's victory over Typhon, and each does its order-making work within an already-ordered cosmos, so that the chapter is consistent with the book's exclusion of cosmogonic-reframings of post-Olympian Greek hero-myth.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-cosmogonic dating of the six combats; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the same dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The Apollo-Python combat carries an additional cyclical-maintenance reflex via the Septerion festival, which re-enacts the killing every eight years and so converts the founding combat into a recurrent ritual event; this is the chapter's principal forward-link to Part II C3 (cyclical-maintenance combat).footnote:[Fontenrose 1959, chs. 1–3, on the Septerion as cyclical-maintenance reflex; per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, forward-link to Part II C3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=fontenrose-1959 ; loc=chs. 1-3

The Perseus-Andromeda rescue supplies the iconographic substrate for the medieval Christian St. George rescue-scene, and is the chapter's principal forward-link to chapter 10 of the present volume (the family-chapter on the Christian reception).footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 11, on the Perseus-Andromeda iconographic substrate; per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, forward-link to Ch10.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 11

The Geryon labour and the Hercules-Cacus combat are read by Watkins and West as Indo-European cattle-raid reflexes, and together with the Vedic Indra-cattle-rescues and the Iranian comparanda they constitute the chapter's principal forward-link to Part II C5 (Indo-European *HERO SLAY SERPENT* and the cattle-raid pattern).footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on Geryon as Indo-European cattle-raid reflex; West 2007, ch. 6, on Cacus as Italic Indo-European cattle-raid reflex; per brief 12-greek-dragons-after-typhon.yaml, forward-link to Part II C5.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The chapter's overall claim about the Greek tradition is therefore that it is productively reworking the inherited dragon-combat template in three different directions at once: into cult-foundation aetiologies for specific oracle-sites and cities, into hero-labour-and-rescue narratives for individual heroes, and into Indo-European cattle-raid reflexes whose comparanda lie east in the Vedic and west in the Italic material.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the productive Greek reworking of the dragon-combat template; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the productive-reworking framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2

The combat-pattern's persistence across the six episodes — across cult-foundation, hero-labour, and cattle-raid — is itself the comparative observation that justifies the family-chapter format: the surface-level diversity of the six episodes is held together by a shared structural template that the post-Typhonic Greek tradition reuses for divergent functional ends.footnote:[Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the combat-pattern's structural persistence under functional diversification; Forsyth 1987, ch. 4, on the family-chapter's organising premise.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2
