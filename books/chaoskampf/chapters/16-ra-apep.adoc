== Ra vs. Apep: The Nightly Combat

Ra travels his solar barque through the *Duat* — the night-journey netherworld — and meets Apep, a great serpent who tries to halt the barque before it reaches the eastern horizon. This is the canonical cyclical-maintenance case: fought every night, never closed, never narrated as a single cosmogonic event.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml; Assmann 2001, _The Search for God in Ancient Egypt_, on the daily-combat structure of the Egyptian solar theology; te Velde 1967, _Seth, God of Confusion_, chs. 3–4, on Apep as Ra's nightly adversary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and maʿat/isfet

The combat is not one narrative in one text. It is a tradition distributed across multiple Egyptian textual corpora over roughly two thousand years, from the Pyramid Texts of the late Old Kingdom (c. 2400 BCE) to the Bremner-Rhind Papyrus of the Ptolemaic period (c. 310 BCE).footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the composite-editorial classification; te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the distribution of the Apep material across the Egyptian textual corpora; Assmann 2001 on the long attestation arc of the solar combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4

This composite character forces the chapter to organise by corpus-stratum rather than by linear narrative. The chapter traces Apep across six layers — Pyramid Texts, Coffin Texts, Book of the Dead, Amduat, Book of Gates, and Bremner-Rhind Papyrus — and periodises each where the periodisation is load-bearing.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the composite-editorial classification and the by-corpus structure; te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the same by-corpus organisation; Assmann 2001, on the diachronic stratification of the Egyptian funerary-and-solar corpora.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4

The primary anchor is Herman te Velde's _Seth, God of Confusion_ (Brill, Leiden, 1967, Probleme der Ägyptologie 6; 2nd ed. 1977). The earliest layer is the Pyramid Texts, on the interior walls of late Old Kingdom royal pyramids at Saqqara — Unas (Fifth Dynasty, c. 2350 BCE), then Teti, Pepi I, Merenre, Pepi II, and several queens of the Sixth Dynasty — conventionally dated c. 2400–2200 BCE.footnote:[Faulkner 1969, _The Ancient Egyptian Pyramid Texts_, introduction on the late Old Kingdom royal-pyramid attestation of the corpus; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Pyramid Texts as the earliest attested layer of the Apep material.]

The standard English edition is Raymond O. Faulkner's _The Ancient Egyptian Pyramid Texts_, Oxford University Press, 1969, in which the corpus is presented at numbered-utterance level. The chapter cites at utterance level accordingly.footnote:[Faulkner 1969 edition of the Pyramid Texts, cited at utterance-number level per brief; per the citation-discipline established in prior CORE chapters of the present book.]

The earliest allusions cluster in the apotropaic utterances. Utterances 226, 232, 236, 298, 378, and 385 are among those in which serpent-adversaries — Apep on some readings, other rebellious serpents on others — are commanded to fall back, to be cut, or to be turned aside.footnote:[Faulkner 1969 edition of the Pyramid Texts, on utterances 226, 232, 236, 298, 378, and 385 as the principal apotropaic / serpent-rebuffing utterances; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Pyramid Texts apotropaic stratum as the earliest layer of the Apep material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Pyramid Texts apotropaic stratum)

The Coffin Texts are the Middle Kingdom layer, inscribed mostly on the interior surfaces of wooden coffins from c. 2050–1650 BCE. They develop Apep further: spells 757 and 885 name him explicitly in contexts of binding, cutting, or rebuffing.footnote:[Faulkner 1973–78, _The Ancient Egyptian Coffin Texts_, on Coffin Texts spells 757 and 885 in the Apep cycle; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Coffin Texts development of the Apep material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Coffin Texts stratum)

The standard English edition is Raymond O. Faulkner's _The Ancient Egyptian Coffin Texts_, three volumes from Aris and Phillips, Warminster, 1973–78. The chapter cites at spell-number level.footnote:[Faulkner 1973–78 edition of the Coffin Texts, cited at spell-number level per brief.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Coffin Texts stratum)

The Book of the Dead — _Spells for Coming Forth by Day_ — succeeds the Coffin Texts and continues through the Late Period and Ptolemaic period. Attested mainly on papyri from non-royal burials, it is the layer holding the principal Apep-rebuffing spells (7, 39, 108).footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), introduction on the Book of the Dead as the New-Kingdom-and-later funerary corpus; on spells 7, 39, and 108 as the principal Apep-rebuffing spells.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=introduction; spells 7, 39, 108

Of these, spell 39 is the most explicit. Faulkner titles it "of repelling the *rerek*-snake in the necropolis"; it rebuffs the serpent and identifies the deceased's protective magic with the magic that defends Ra against Apep nightly.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Book of the Dead spell 39 ("of repelling the *rerek*-snake in the necropolis") as the most explicit Apep-rebuffing spell of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 39

Spell 108 places Apep on the western horizon-mountain in the cosmography of the deceased's nightly transit — one of the principal loci for Apep's localisation within the *Duat*.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Book of the Dead spell 108 and the western-horizon-mountain localisation of Apep.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 108

Spell 7 is shorter; it rebuffs Apep in the deceased's solar-barque journey to keep the afterlife passage clear, and belongs to a cluster of short apotropaic spells that thread the Apep-rebuffing motif through the corpus.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Book of the Dead spell 7 as a short apotropaic Apep-rebuffing spell within the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 7

The Amduat ("Book of What Is in the Duat") is a New Kingdom royal funerary text inscribed in the Valley of the Kings from the Eighteenth Dynasty (c. 1500 BCE). It narrates Ra's nightly journey through twelve hours; the canonical Apep-combat hour is hour 7.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Amduat as the Eighteenth-Dynasty-onward royal funerary text and on hour 7 as the canonical Apep-combat hour; Assmann 2001, on the Amduat and the twelve-hour nocturnal structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat hour 7)

In hour 7, Apep blocks Ra's barque on the sandbanks of the *Duat* — a stretch of shallow water in which the barque can be grounded. The barque-crew dispatches the serpent with knives and cutting magic, and the barque continues toward the eastern horizon.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the seventh-hour combat in the Amduat (Apep on the sandbanks of the Duat); Assmann 2001, on the cosmographic structure of the Amduat's seventh hour.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat hour 7)

The Book of Gates succeeds and supplements the Amduat in the same royal-tomb tradition. It too narrates twelve nocturnal subdivisions, but organised by gates rather than hours; its canonical Apep-combat scene is at gate 4.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Book of Gates as the New-Kingdom royal funerary text and on gate 4 as the Apep-combat scene; Assmann 2001 on the Book of Gates and the twelve-gate nocturnal structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Book of Gates gate 4)

The Bremner-Rhind Papyrus (BM EA 10188) is a late-Ptolemaic hieratic papyrus (c. 310 BCE). Its second portion preserves the _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ — the daily liturgical text from the temple of Ra at Heliopolis, the chapter's principal documentary witness to the ritual embedding of the combat in priestly practice.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind Papyrus (British Museum EA 10188) and the _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ as the daily liturgical text from the temple of Ra at Heliopolis; Faulkner's editions in JEA 22–24 (1936–38) on the philological reconstruction of the papyrus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind P. BM 10188)

The principal edition is Faulkner's _JEA_ series: Faulkner 1936 ("The Bremner-Rhind Papyrus I", JEA 22) and Faulkner 1937–38 ("II–IV", JEA 23–24). These present the philology separately from Faulkner's Book of the Dead translation; the chapter cites at JEA-volume level (Faulkner 1936–38, JEA 22–24).footnote:[Faulkner's editions of the Bremner-Rhind Papyrus in JEA 22 (1936) and JEA 23–24 (1937–38), as referenced in te Velde 1967, ch. 3, and in the per-brief lacuna note.]

=== Cosmological frame — *maʿat* versus *isfet*

The framework within which the Apep combat is read is the *maʿat*/*isfet* opposition, treated at length in Assmann's _The Search for God in Ancient Egypt_ as the central cosmological-ethical category-pair of Egyptian religion.footnote:[Assmann 2001, _The Search for God in Ancient Egypt_, on the *maʿat*/*isfet* opposition as the central cosmological-ethical category-pair of Egyptian religion; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on *maʿat* and *isfet* as the locked terms.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

*maʿat* — written with the *ʿayin* per the locked glossary — designates "truth, order, justice" simultaneously. The Egyptian term names neither abstract metaphysical order alone nor ethical justice alone but a single integrated condition: cosmic order, social justice, ritual correctness, and truth-of-speech as inseparable aspects.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on *maʿat* as the integrated truth-order-justice category; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the locked rendering of *maʿat* with the *ʿayin*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat

*isfet* — its antithesis — designates "disorder, injustice, chaos" with the same simultaneity. It covers cosmic disorder, social injustice, ritual transgression, and falsehood-of-speech as inseparable aspects of a single condition, and it is the condition Apep personifies in the Egyptian theological imagination.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on *isfet* as the integrated disorder-injustice-falsehood category; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the locked rendering of *isfet*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on isfet

The opposition is not a dualism of equally-positive principles. *maʿat* is the positive condition Ra's daily journey preserves; *isfet* is its privation or violation that Apep's nightly assault threatens to impose. The two are not co-equal cosmic principles but order and its violation.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the asymmetric structure of the *maʿat*/*isfet* opposition; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same asymmetry as it underlies the Apep combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

This asymmetry grounds compliance with the Ballentine 2015 critique. Per scope.md, Apep is *isfet* — disorder, injustice, violation of order — not "chaos" in the Western-philosophical sense. The chapter calls Apep the personification of *isfet* and an agent of disorder, avoiding the umbrella term banned for this figure.footnote:[Per chaoskampf scope.md on the Ballentine critique and on *isfet* as the appropriate category-term for Apep; Ballentine 2015, _The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition_, on the inadequacy of "chaos" as a category-term for the combat-myth adversaries; Assmann 2001 on *isfet* as a moral-political rather than metaphysically-inchoate category.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=passim (chaos-category critique)

Alongside the abstract noun, the pantheon supplies a personified goddess Maʿat — capitalised when the personified figure is meant. She is the goddess of truth, order, and justice; iconographically she wears the ostrich-feather of *maʿat* on her head, and she is conceived as the daughter of Ra.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the personified goddess Maʿat as the daughter of Ra and the patron of truth-order-justice; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the capitalisation discipline distinguishing the personified Maʿat from the abstract *maʿat*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat (personified)

Pharaonic legitimacy is figured as the maintenance of *maʿat* on earth. The pharaoh's principal liturgical function is to "present *maʿat* to the gods" — offering a small image of the goddess — and the obligation to preserve order in the polity is inseparable from the obligation to preserve cosmic order.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the pharaonic offering of *maʿat* to the gods as the central liturgical act of Egyptian kingship; on the inseparability of the cosmological and political-religious dimensions of *maʿat*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat (pharaonic-liturgical)

Apep's role is therefore not that of a primordial-cosmogonic adversary — Tiamat (_Enūma Eliš_, Ch. 3) is the standard contrast — but a perpetual *isfet*-personification: an agent of moral-cosmic disorder the daily round of *maʿat*-maintenance has to keep at bay, structurally a moral-political adversary rather than an inchoate primordial.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on Apep as *isfet*-personification rather than as cosmogonic-primordial figure; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same; Ballentine 2015, on the same point as a critique of the "chaos" category-term.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on isfet and Apep

This distinction — Apep-as-*isfet*-personification versus the cosmogonic adversaries (Tiamat, Yamm, Vṛtra) — is load-bearing for the book's typology. The cosmogonic combat establishes the cosmos for the first time; the cyclical-maintenance combat preserves an already-established cosmos against ongoing threat. *maʿat*/*isfet* is the Egyptian theological articulation of this distinction.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the cyclical-maintenance structure of the Egyptian solar theology; per chaoskampf scope.md §"Subject definition" on the cosmogonic-vs-cyclical-maintenance distinction; Lambert 2013 on Tiamat as cosmogonic adversary, by contrast.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology

The cosmos is conceived as already in place from creation — Atum-Ra's emergence, the differentiation of sky and earth, the ordering of the gods — so Ra's daily journey through sky and *Duat* is the maintenance of an already-established order, not its fabrication.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the already-in-place character of the Egyptian cosmos and on Ra's daily journey as maintenance rather than fabrication; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and cosmogony

Within this cosmos Ra's daily journey has two halves: the *manḏet*-barque (day-barque) traverses the sky east to west, and the *mesektet*-barque (night-barque) traverses the *Duat* west to east. The Apep combat belongs to the nocturnal half.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the day-barque and night-barque structure of Ra's daily journey; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same and on the location of the Apep combat in the nocturnal half.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology

The *Duat* is conceived as a region underneath or behind the visible cosmos through which the sun-god passes between sunset and sunrise. It is populated by both blessed and dangerous beings; Apep is the principal dangerous one, whose threat is to halt the barque and end the daily round.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Duat as the nocturnal region of Ra's transit and on Apep as the principal dangerous being there; Assmann 2001 on the cosmographic structure of the Duat; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the locked rendering of *Duat*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Duat cosmography)

The wager of the Egyptian solar tradition is that without Ra's nightly victory the sun does not rise. The rising each morning is not a natural-process given but a contingent outcome of the combat, which is therefore the ongoing condition for the continuation of the visible cosmos.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the contingency of sunrise upon Ra's nightly victory and on the daily combat as the ongoing condition for the visible cosmos; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the cosmological function of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology

This contingency distinguishes Egyptian solar theology from a deterministic-naturalistic celestial mechanics. The sun rises because the gods, the ritual liturgy, and the magical power of priestly utterance all collaborate to defeat Apep nightly, and the collaboration is constitutive rather than incidental.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the constitutive character of the daily-combat collaboration between gods, liturgy, and magic; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same constitutive character.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and ritual

The combat has no eschatological closure. The corpus projects no final defeat of Apep at the end of cosmic time; the combat is continuous, and the silence on a closure scenario is itself a meaningful structural feature of the tradition.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the absence of eschatological closure in the Egyptian Apep corpus; Assmann 2001 on the continuous-combat structure; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the eschatological function as deliberately absent in the cyclical-maintenance subtype.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (no eschatological closure)

[INFERENCE: the absence of an eschatological closure in the Egyptian Apep corpus is a structural-typological feature of the cyclical-maintenance subtype rather than a contingent silence in the surviving sources | basis: te Velde 1967 ch. 3 reads the recurrence as the constitutive structure rather than as a placeholder pending a closure scenario; Assmann 2001 on the daily recurrence as constitutive; the Pyramid-Texts-to-Bremner-Rhind two-thousand-year arc consistently presents the combat as recurrent without any narrative gesture toward closure | risk: the inference is structural-typological rather than directly attested as a stated theological doctrine; the surviving Egyptian corpus is large but not exhaustive, and the inference is in principle subject to revision should a future textual discovery present an eschatological closure scenario for Apep.]

This contrasts with the eschatological closure of Aži Dahāka (Ch. 13). The Iranian dragon is bound under Damāvand by Thraētaona, escapes at the end of time, and is killed for the second and final time by the revived Kərəsāspa at *Frashokereti* — a closure-narrative the Egyptian corpus lacks.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the contrast between cyclical-maintenance (Egypt) and eschatological-closure (Iran); Watkins 1995 Part V chs. 44–48 on the Iranian closure-narrative; cross-ref Ch. 13.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (cyclical-maintenance)

It contrasts also with the once-only cosmogonic victory of Marduk-Tiamat (Ch. 3). Marduk's victory establishes the cosmos for the first time and is not nightly re-enacted; Ra's victory preserves an already-established cosmos and must be re-enacted every sunrise. Per the brief, this contrast is load-bearing for the book's typology.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the contrast between cyclical-maintenance (Egypt) and once-only cosmogonic victory (Mesopotamia); Lambert 2013 on the Marduk-Tiamat material as once-only cosmogonic; cross-ref Ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology (cyclical-maintenance)

The personification of *isfet* in Apep is a principal Egyptian articulation of moral-political evil. Apep is not merely a natural-cosmic obstacle but morally adversarial — a hostile, malicious agent whose intent is to halt the sun, overturn order, and plunge the cosmos into the privation *isfet* names.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Apep as morally-adversarial agent rather than natural-cosmic obstacle; Assmann 2001, on the moral-political dimension of *isfet*-personification.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (moral-adversarial Apep)

This adversarial character is reflected in the apotropaic register of the spell-corpus. The Apep-rebuffing spells command the serpent to fall, to be cut, to be turned aside, and invoke the magical-protective power of the gods against him — a register of moral-magical aggression rather than cosmological-explanatory description.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the apotropaic-imperative register of Book of the Dead spells 7, 39, 108; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the magical-aggression register of the Apep spells more generally.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spells 7, 39, 108

The deceased's role aligns structurally with Ra's. In the funerary corpus the deceased identifies with Ra in the nightly journey and rebuffs Apep alongside him, so the afterlife passage and the sun-god's nightly transit are the same combat — the afterlife as participation in the daily *maʿat*-preservation.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the deceased's identification with Ra in the funerary corpus; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same identification.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spells 7, 39, 108 (deceased-with-Ra identification)

The pharaoh's role aligns similarly. Pharaonic legitimacy as guarantor of *maʿat* on earth is explicitly figured as participation in Ra's daily *maʿat*-preservation in the cosmos, and the pharaoh's ritual-political function is theologised as the human-political analogue of the divine combat against Apep.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the pharaoh as guarantor of *maʿat* in alignment with Ra's daily combat against *isfet*; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same alignment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat (pharaonic)

The frame *maʿat*/*isfet* is therefore both ontological — how the cosmos is — and ethical-political — how human society and pharaonic rule must be. The Apep combat is the cosmological articulation of an integrated theological-ethical-political project rather than an isolated mythological narrative.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the integrated character of the *maʿat*/*isfet* framework as ontological-ethical-political; per chaoskampf scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" on opening with the tradition's own framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

This integrated character gives the Egyptian material its distinctive position within the book's comparative typology: the Egyptian tradition supplies the most explicit theological articulation of the cyclical-maintenance subtype's ethical-political dimension, and the chapter therefore opens with this conceptual frame rather than with the textual corpus.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml special_instructions on opening with the cosmological frame; per chaoskampf scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" on opening with the tradition's own framing; Assmann 2001 on the ethical-political dimension of *maʿat*/*isfet*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on maʿat and isfet

=== Textual corpus — Pyramid Texts through Bremner-Rhind

The corpus spans two thousand years in six principal layers — Pyramid Texts (c. 2400 BCE), Coffin Texts (c. 2050–1650 BCE), Book of the Dead, Amduat, Book of Gates, Bremner-Rhind Papyrus (c. 310 BCE) — each adding to and reworking Apep without superseding earlier layers.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the six principal layers of the corpus; te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the six-layer stratification; Assmann 2001 on the diachronic stratification of the funerary-and-solar corpora.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4

The Pyramid Texts are inscribed exclusively on the interior walls of late Old Kingdom royal pyramids at Saqqara, beginning with Unas (Fifth Dynasty, c. 2350 BCE). Distribution to non-royal contexts is a Middle Kingdom phenomenon tied to the Coffin Texts; the Apep allusions originate in a royal funerary context.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the royal-funerary context of the Pyramid Texts and the Apep allusions therein; Faulkner 1969 introduction on the Saqqara royal-pyramid attestation of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Pyramid Texts royal context)

In the Pyramid Texts the serpent-adversary is not yet centralised under "Apep" with the consistency of later corpora. Serpent-adversaries appear under several names and morphologies, and Apep is one among a class of rebellious serpents commanded to fall back, to be cut, or to be turned aside.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the diffuse serpent-adversary class in the Pyramid Texts and the gradual centralisation of the figure under the name "Apep" in the later corpora.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Pyramid Texts diffuse serpent-class)

The apotropaic spells — utterances 226, 232, 236, 298, 378, 385 among others — are short imperative formulas. They command the serpent to fall, to spit out his fangs, to retreat, or to be cut, and invoke the magical-protective power of named gods (Atum, Geb, Horus, Re-Atum) against him.footnote:[Faulkner 1969 edition of the Pyramid Texts, on utterances 226, 232, 236, 298, 378, 385 as the principal apotropaic / serpent-rebuffing utterances; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the imperative-formulaic register of these utterances.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Pyramid Texts apotropaic stratum)

These spells presuppose, but do not narrate at length, the cosmographic frame within which the serpent-adversary acts. The deceased king's posthumous ascent to join Ra in the sky-barque is the wider liturgical context, and the rebuffing-spells are protective accompaniments to that ascent rather than free-standing combat-narratives.footnote:[Faulkner 1969 introduction on the deceased-king's-ascent context of the Pyramid Texts; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the protective-accompaniment function of the apotropaic spells within that context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Pyramid Texts ascent context)

The Coffin Texts extend the funerary spells beyond the royal context and inscribe them on the interior surfaces of wooden coffins of high-status non-royal burials. The Apep material is correspondingly extended and elaborated, with new spells (notably 757 and 885) developing the combat further.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Coffin Texts extension of the funerary corpus to non-royal contexts and on the Apep material elaboration; Faulkner 1973–78 introduction on the Coffin Texts corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Coffin Texts stratum)

The Coffin Texts give Apep a more centralised and consistent identity than the diffuse serpent-class of the Pyramid Texts. By the Middle Kingdom "Apep" carries the principal weight of the serpent-adversary tradition, and the cosmographic frame of the nightly combat is more articulated than in the Old Kingdom corpus.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the centralisation of the figure of Apep in the Coffin Texts and on the more articulated cosmographic frame of the Middle Kingdom material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Coffin Texts centralisation)

The Book of the Dead is preserved principally on funerary papyri from non-royal burials and is the layer through which the Apep combat is most familiar to modern readers and Egyptological synthesis. Faulkner's 1972 (rev. 1985) translation is the standard English edition.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), introduction on the New-Kingdom-onward Book-of-the-Dead corpus and its papyrus attestation; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Book of the Dead as the most-readable layer of the Apep tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=introduction

Spell 39 is, in Faulkner's translation, "of repelling the *rerek*-snake in the necropolis". It explicitly names the serpent-adversary, addresses him in apotropaic-imperative register, and identifies the deceased's protective magic with the magic that defends Ra against Apep.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Book of the Dead spell 39 ("of repelling the *rerek*-snake in the necropolis") as the most explicit Apep-rebuffing spell in the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 39

In spell 39 the deceased addresses the serpent in the second person, commanding him to be cut, turned aside, and made ineffective against the afterlife passage. The spell asserts a magical equivalence between the deceased's protective utterance and the utterances spoken in defence of Ra by his barque-crew nightly.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the second-person imperative register of Book of the Dead spell 39 and on the magical-equivalence assertion within it.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 39

This identification is the principal mechanism by which the funerary corpus draws the deceased into the cosmological combat. The deceased is not merely petitioning Ra for protection but is theologised as participating in the combat Ra fights, the deceased's own utterance theologised as having combat-efficacy.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the deceased's-participation theology of Book of the Dead spell 39; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same theology in the Book of the Dead corpus more broadly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (deceased's participation theology)

Spell 108 places Apep on the western horizon-mountain. The western horizon is the entry-point of the solar barque into the *Duat*, and Apep is localised at this entry-point as the cosmographic obstacle whose threat must be overcome at the threshold of the nocturnal journey.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Book of the Dead spell 108 and the western-horizon-mountain localisation of Apep; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the cosmographic structure of the localisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 108

The localisation in spell 108 supplies one of the principal cosmographic anchors of the Apep tradition: Apep's locus is at the threshold between the visible cosmos and the *Duat*, and this threshold-locus is a recurrent feature of the figure across the corpora.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the threshold-locus of Apep in Book of the Dead spell 108; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the threshold-locus as a recurrent feature of the figure across the corpora.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (threshold-locus)

Spell 7 is among the shorter apotropaic spells and belongs to a cluster that threads the Apep-rebuffing motif through the deceased's afterlife journey at multiple liturgical points: at the entry to the *Duat*, again during the transit, and again at the eastern horizon at the threshold of dawn.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Book of the Dead spell 7 as part of the cluster of short apotropaic Apep-rebuffing spells; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the multi-point threading of the Apep-rebuffing motif through the deceased's journey.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spell 7

This repetitive threading is itself a structural reflection of the cyclical-maintenance subtype. The combat is encountered repeatedly within a single nocturnal journey just as it is repeated nightly across cosmic time, and the spell-cluster recapitulates the repetitive structure of the wider tradition.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the recapitulative structure of the Book-of-the-Dead Apep-rebuffing cluster; Assmann 2001 on the repetitive-liturgical structure of Egyptian solar theology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (recapitulative spell-cluster)

The Amduat (Egyptian *Imy-Duat*) is a principal New Kingdom royal funerary text, inscribed in the Valley of the Kings from the Eighteenth Dynasty (c. 1500 BCE). It narrates Ra's nightly journey through the twelve hours of the night in the *Duat* as a continuous twelve-section cosmographic narrative.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Amduat as the New Kingdom royal funerary text and on its twelve-hour structure; Assmann 2001 on the Amduat and the twelve-hour nocturnal cosmography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat structure)

The twelve-hour structure is a liturgical-cosmographic conceit: each hour corresponds to a cosmographic region of the *Duat*, and the journey of Ra's barque through the twelve hours is the journey of the sun-god through the twelve regions, with named beings and named events characterising each hour.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the liturgical-cosmographic structure of the Amduat's twelve hours; Assmann 2001 on the same structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat twelve-hour structure)

Hour 7 is the canonical Apep-combat hour: it is here that Apep blocks the barque on the sandbanks of the *Duat*, and the combat is narrated as the central event of the nocturnal journey rather than a peripheral detail.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the seventh hour of the Amduat as the canonical Apep-combat hour; Assmann 2001 on the centrality of the seventh-hour combat within the Amduat narrative structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat hour 7)

The combat is described in cosmographic-spatial terms. The barque encounters the sandbanks — a stretch of shallow water in which it can be grounded — and Apep coils across the channel to block its progress, the combat staged in the moment of encounter with the obstacle.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the cosmographic-spatial description of the seventh-hour combat (sandbanks; coiling across the channel); Assmann 2001 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat hour 7 spatial structure)

The dispatch is conducted by the gods of the barque-crew, who use knives and cutting magic to dismember the serpent's coils and clear the channel. It is collective rather than carried out by a single hero, and magical-cutting is the principal narrative beat.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the collective dispatch of Apep by the barque-crew with knives and cutting magic; Assmann 2001 on the magical-cutting register of the Amduat seventh-hour combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat hour 7 dispatch)

The Amduat does not present this combat as the conclusive defeat of Apep. The dispatch enables the barque to continue, and the combat resumes the next night. The structure is recurrent rather than terminal, and the Amduat projects no final hour of cosmic time after which the combat ceases.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the recurrent-rather-than-terminal structure of the Amduat seventh-hour combat; Assmann 2001 on the daily-recurrence structure of the Egyptian solar theology more broadly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Amduat recurrence)

The Book of Gates also narrates twelve nocturnal subdivisions, but is organised by gates between successive cosmographic regions rather than by hours within them.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Book of Gates as the New-Kingdom royal funerary text and its twelve-gate structure; Assmann 2001 on the gate-structuring of the Book of Gates.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Book of Gates structure)

The fourth gate contains the canonical Apep-combat scene within that corpus. Apep blocks the barque's passage at the gate-threshold, and the barque-crew dispatch him with knives, ropes, and binding-spells before the barque can continue.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the fourth gate of the Book of Gates as the Apep-combat scene; Assmann 2001 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Book of Gates gate 4)

The fourth-gate scene differs from the seventh-hour scene principally in cosmography: the Book of Gates locates Apep at the gate-threshold rather than at the sandbanks-channel, but the figure of the serpent and the dispatching-by-the-barque-crew remain consistent across the two corpora.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the threshold-cosmography of the Book of Gates fourth gate by contrast with the sandbanks-channel of the Amduat seventh hour; Assmann 2001 on the consistency of the figure of Apep across the two corpora.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Book of Gates threshold cosmography)

The Bremner-Rhind Papyrus (P. BM EA 10188) is a Ptolemaic hieratic papyrus of c. 310 BCE. Its second portion contains the _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ — the daily liturgical text from the temple of Ra at Heliopolis and the chapter's principal witness to the ritual embedding of the combat.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind Papyrus and the _Book of Overthrowing Apep_; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the dating and provenance of the papyrus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind P. BM 10188)

The _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ is structured as a liturgical manual: it contains spells, ritual prescriptions, and apotropaic instructions that accompany the daily priestly ritual at the temple of Ra, and it is the principal surviving witness to the priestly liturgy as practised in the Ptolemaic period.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind text as a liturgical manual for the daily priestly Apep-overthrowing rite; Faulkner's JEA editions on the philological structure of the manual.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind liturgical manual)

The text gives explicit instructions for producing effigies of Apep — modelled in wax, written on papyrus, drawn on the ground — and ritually destroying them: cut with knives, spat upon, trampled, burned. The destruction of the effigy serves as the ritual enactment of the cosmological combat.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind effigy-destruction instructions; Faulkner's JEA editions on the philological detail of the rubrics.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind effigy-destruction)

In te Velde's reading these rituals constitute a sustained ritual-magical attack on Apep. The priest's daily destruction of the wax or papyrus image is ritually equivalent to Ra's daily destruction of Apep in the *Duat*, and the temple liturgy is theologised as participation in the cosmological combat.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the ritual-magical equivalence between effigy-destruction and the cosmological Apep-combat; Assmann 2001 on the ritual-magical-cosmological theology of the Egyptian temple liturgy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind ritual-magical equivalence)

The _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ is the latest in-scope primary source for the chapter. Dating from c. 310 BCE, it marks the late-Ptolemaic terminus of the two-thousand-year attestation arc; the chapter's primary-source horizon does not extend further into Hellenistic or Roman-period material.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml and per chaoskampf scope.md §"Period" on the Bremner-Rhind Papyrus as the latest in-scope primary source for the chapter; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind dating)

Across the six layers Apep is morphologically stable: a great serpent, of vast size, of unspecified-but-monstrous coils, capable of spitting venom, of swallowing the barque, and of regenerating after each apparent dispatch.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the morphological stability of the figure of Apep across the six layers of the corpus; Assmann 2001 on the same stability.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (morphological stability)

This regenerative capacity grounds the cyclical-maintenance structure. The dispatch at hour 7 of the Amduat or gate 4 of the Book of Gates does not annihilate the serpent, who returns the next night — the regenerative capacity is the textual articulation of the recurrence-structure.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Apep's regenerative capacity as the textual articulation of the recurrence-structure; Assmann 2001 on the recurrence-structure of the Egyptian solar theology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (regenerative Apep)

Apep is therefore typologically distinct from once-killed cosmogonic adversaries — Tiamat, killed once and dismembered into the cosmos in _Enūma Eliš_ — and from once-bound-and-eschatologically-killed adversaries — Aži Dahāka, bound under Damāvand and killed for the second and final time at *Frashokereti*. He belongs to the unkillable-because-recurrent class.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Apep as unkillable-because-recurrent; Assmann 2001 on the cyclical-maintenance structure; Lambert 2013 on Tiamat as once-killed cosmogonic adversary; Watkins 1995 Part V chs. 44–48 on Aži Dahāka as eschatologically-killed; cross-refs Ch. 3 and Ch. 13.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (unkillable-because-recurrent)

The diachronic continuity of Apep across the six-layer corpus — from late Old Kingdom Pyramid Texts to Ptolemaic Bremner-Rhind — is among the most striking features of the tradition: one of the longest unbroken attestation arcs of any combat-myth figure in the book's catalogue.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the two-thousand-year unbroken attestation arc of the Apep figure; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the centrality-evidence of the long attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (long attestation arc)

[INFERENCE: the six-layer corpus's morphological and theological stability across two thousand years reflects an unusually robust ritual-cultic continuity rather than the persistence of a literary-poetic motif | basis: the corpora are predominantly funerary or temple-liturgical (Pyramid Texts, Coffin Texts, Book of the Dead are funerary; Amduat and Book of Gates are royal-funerary; Bremner-Rhind is daily temple-liturgical), so the persistence reflects continuous ritual performance contexts; te Velde 1967 ch. 3 reads the textual continuity as ritual-cultic continuity rather than as literary-tradition continuity; Assmann 2001 reads the Egyptian funerary-and-temple corpora as ritual-textual rather than purely literary | risk: the inference is structural-historical rather than directly attested as a stated theological doctrine; alternative readings (e.g., "literary archaism preserves the figure across changing ritual contexts") remain available, though they are less natural fits for the funerary-and-temple corpus that the chapter treats.]

The two-thousand-year textual arc is paralleled by a continuous iconographic record. The serpent-figure being knifed, cut, or trampled is attested on royal tomb walls (Eighteenth Dynasty onward), coffins (Middle Kingdom onward), funerary papyri, and temple reliefs (New Kingdom and later), with stable pictorial vocabulary across the corpus.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the continuous iconographic record of Apep-combat imagery across funerary and temple media.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (iconographic continuity)

=== Ritual embedding — Bremner-Rhind and the daily temple liturgy

The third section treats the ritual embedding of the Apep combat in priestly practice. The Bremner-Rhind _Book of Overthrowing Apep_ is the principal documentary witness, and the daily temple liturgy at Heliopolis is the liturgical context within which the cosmological combat was ritually re-enacted.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml special_instructions on the ritual embedding as the third structural section of the chapter; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind text and the Heliopolis temple liturgy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (ritual embedding)

The temple of Ra at Heliopolis (Egyptian Iunu, Greek "city of the sun") was the leading solar-cult centre throughout pharaonic history. The city lay in the northern Nile delta near modern Cairo; the temple is poorly preserved archaeologically, but the textual evidence for its theological influence is extensive.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Heliopolis as the principal cult-centre of Ra and the locus of the daily Apep-overthrowing liturgy; Assmann 2001 on Heliopolis as the leading solar-theological centre.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Heliopolis)

The Bremner-Rhind text is internally framed as the liturgical manual of the Heliopolis temple: its rubrics refer to the daily performance, to the priestly officiant, to the time of day at which particular rubrics are to be performed, and to the specific liturgical implements (wax, papyrus, fire, knives).footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the internal framing of the Bremner-Rhind text as a liturgical manual; Faulkner's JEA editions (1936–38) on the rubrics' philological structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Bremner-Rhind framing)

On te Velde's reading and on the philological evidence of the rubrics, the rite was performed daily — every day at the temple. Its regularity is a structural feature of the liturgy that mirrors the regularity of the cosmological combat it re-enacts.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the daily-performance frequency of the Apep-overthrowing rite; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the daily ritual as the cyclical-maintenance liturgical anchor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (daily performance)

The implements include wax effigies, papyrus images, ground-drawings in some rubrics, and knives, fire, and spit for destroying them. The implements vary; the logic does not — the effigy is identified with the cosmological serpent, and its destruction is the destruction of the cosmological serpent.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the ritual implements of the Bremner-Rhind text and on the ritual-logical identification between effigy and cosmological serpent.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (ritual implements)

The destruction-rubrics prescribe a sequence of magical-aggressive actions: the effigy is bound with cords, cut with knives, trampled underfoot, spat upon, and finally burned. The sequence is structured to enact the multi-stage dispatch of the cosmological serpent that the Amduat and Book of Gates describe.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the multi-stage destruction-sequence of the Bremner-Rhind rubrics; Assmann 2001 on the ritual-cosmological structural correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (destruction sequence)

Spitting on the effigy is a particularly notable feature. The ritual gesture is one of the most consistent apotropaic elements of the rite, carrying a magical-aggressive significance distinct from cutting and burning while reinforcing their cumulative force.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the spitting-upon as a distinctive feature of the Bremner-Rhind apotropaic rite; Assmann 2001 on apotropaic ritual gestures more broadly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (spitting apotropaic)

Alongside the destruction-rubrics the text contains a substantial body of recitative spells: hymns praising Ra's victory, narrative recitations of the combat, and apotropaic-imperative formulas commanding Apep to fall back, to be cut, to be turned aside. Spells and destruction-rubrics together constitute the full ritual apparatus.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the spell-corpus of the Bremner-Rhind text alongside the destruction-rubrics; Faulkner's JEA editions on the philological detail of the recitative material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (recitative spells)

The recitative spells include hymns that recite the moments at which Apep falls — structured as a recapitulation of the cosmological combat in priestly recitation.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the recitative hymns of the Bremner-Rhind corpus that recapitulate the cosmological combat; Faulkner's JEA editions on the philological structure of these hymns.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (recitative hymns)

The ritual-aetiological function is explicit on te Velde's reading. The daily ritual preserves *maʿat* and prevents Apep's victory: the ritual is the combat made recurrent, theologised as continuous with the cosmological combat. Priestly recitation and effigy-destruction are theologised as combatant acts.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the ritual-aetiological function of the Bremner-Rhind liturgy as preserving *maʿat* and preventing Apep's victory; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the ritual-aetiological function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (ritual-aetiological function)

This reading is performative in the strong sense: the rite does not merely commemorate the combat but constitutes one moment of it, and the priestly officiant is theologised as a combatant alongside the gods of the barque rather than a mere observer.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the strong-performative reading of the Bremner-Rhind liturgy and on the priestly officiant as combatant; Assmann 2001 on the strong-performative theology of the Egyptian temple liturgy more broadly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on temple liturgy and combat

The strong-performative reading is the principal Egyptian articulation of the cyclical-maintenance subtype's ritual-cultic dimension: the combat is re-enacted every day, the re-enactment is theologised as a moment of the combat, and the daily liturgy is the structural hinge between the ritual-cultic and cosmological dimensions.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the strong-performative reading as the Egyptian articulation of the cyclical-maintenance subtype's ritual dimension; Assmann 2001 on the ritual-cosmological hinge.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (cyclical-maintenance ritual)

In the funerary corpora — Book of the Dead especially — the afterlife journey is theologised as participation in the same daily combat the Heliopolis liturgy enacts. The deceased's protective magic, the rebuffing-spells of the papyri, and the apotropaic gestures of the funerary cult are funerary articulations of that combat.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the deceased's funerary-cult participation in the daily-combat structure; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same participation; Assmann 2001 on the integrated funerary-and-temple cultic structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=faulkner-1972-bod ; loc=spells 7, 39, 108 (deceased participation)

The Apep combat is therefore ritually present in two distinct but co-ordinated cultic registers: the daily temple liturgy at Heliopolis (Bremner-Rhind) and the funerary cult of the deceased's afterlife passage (Book of the Dead spells 7, 39, 108). Both are theologised as participations in the same cosmological combat.footnote:[Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on the funerary-cult register; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the temple-liturgy register; Assmann 2001 on the integration of the two registers within the Egyptian solar theology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and ritual

The brief identifies this ritual embedding as one of the principal features making the Apep tradition canonical for cyclical-maintenance. It is not merely that the combat recurs nightly in the cosmographic frame; the liturgy explicitly enacts the recurrence as a ritual programme, and this articulation is structurally distinctive.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the ritual embedding as a centrality-evidence kind for the cyclical-maintenance subtype; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the structural distinctiveness of the explicit ritual articulation; Assmann 2001 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (ritual embedding centrality)

This distinctiveness can be measured against the Hittite *purulli* festival, the only other comparably-explicit cyclical-maintenance ritual (Ch. 6). The *purulli* re-enacts the Illuyanka combat annually; the Bremner-Rhind liturgy re-enacts the Apep combat daily. Both are cyclical-maintenance programmes; the Egyptian runs on the more rapid cycle.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the Hittite *purulli* parallel; Beckman 1982 on the *purulli* festival; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Egyptian daily liturgy by contrast; cross-ref Ch. 6.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (cyclical comparison)

On the philological evidence of the rubrics, the Heliopolis officiants were a daily-rotated body of priests who performed the rite at prescribed hours. The rubrics specify the time of day for particular acts, and the temple's daily liturgy was therefore a sustained programme rather than an isolated rite.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the priestly-rotation structure of the Heliopolis daily liturgy; Faulkner's JEA editions on the philological detail of the time-prescription rubrics.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (priestly rotation)

The Heliopolis daily liturgy is therefore the principal Egyptian institutional form preserving the cyclical-maintenance combat. It is not a popular festival, an annual or seasonal observance, or a personal devotional practice — it is a sustained temple-priestly programme on the daily solar cycle.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the temple-priestly institutional form of the Heliopolis daily liturgy; Assmann 2001 on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (temple-priestly institution)

On Assmann 2001's reading, this wager is theologically explicit in the Egyptian sources. The sun's rising is contingent on the ritual's success, the ritual's success on correct performance of the rubrics, and correct performance is the priesthood's principal cosmological obligation.footnote:[Assmann 2001, on the explicit theological character of the priestly cosmological obligation in the Egyptian sources; te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=assmann-2001-search ; loc=chs. on solar theology and priestly cosmology

[INFERENCE: the daily-liturgical performance of the Apep-overthrowing rite at Heliopolis was theologised as constitutive of the rising of the sun in a non-trivial sense — that is, the sun's rising was understood to depend on the correct performance of the rite, not merely to be commemorated or reflected by it | basis: the strong-performative reading of the Bremner-Rhind rubrics in te Velde 1967 ch. 3, in which the priestly officiant is theologised as a combatant rather than as an observer; Assmann 2001's strong-performative reading of the Egyptian temple liturgy more broadly; the rubrics' specification of daily-time-of-day performance and the cosmographic correspondence between liturgical sequence and solar-journey sequence | risk: the inference is theological-interpretive rather than directly attested in a prescriptive theological treatise; alternative readings (in which the rite is commemorative-symbolic rather than constitutive-performative) remain in principle available, though they are less natural fits for the priestly-cosmological-obligation theology that the chapter draws on Assmann 2001's reading to articulate.]

The Bremner-Rhind text is therefore the principal documentary anchor for the ritual-cultic dimension of the Apep combat, and the daily Heliopolis liturgy it manuals is the principal institutional form within which the cyclical-maintenance subtype's ritual-aetiological articulation is preserved across two thousand years of Egyptian religious history.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the Bremner-Rhind text as the principal documentary anchor and on the daily Heliopolis liturgy as the principal institutional form; Assmann 2001 on the same; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the ritual-embedding section.]

=== Seth at the prow — the ambivalent Apep-slayer

The fourth section treats Seth's ambivalent role as Apep-slayer at the prow of the solar barque. te Velde 1967 chs. 3–4 are the principal scholarly anchor, and the section sets up the Ch. 17 edge-case treatment of the Horus-Seth contention.footnote:[Per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml special_instructions on Seth's ambivalent role as the fourth structural section; te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on Seth as Apep-slayer; cross-ref Ch. 17 (Horus-Seth).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4

Seth — Egyptian Setekh, alternative form Sutekh in Ramesside diplomatic contexts — is the god of storms, deserts, and disorder. He is a complex and ambivalent figure whose role varies across texts and centuries; this chapter and Ch. 17 together cover the principal divisions of his dossier.footnote:[te Velde 1967, _Seth, God of Confusion_, on Seth as the god of storms, deserts, and disorder and on the complexity-and-ambivalence of his mythological dossier; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the locked rendering "Seth" with "Sutekh" reserved for Hittite-treaty contexts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=passim

Seth's principal positive role is Apep-slayer at the prow. In the record he stands at the prow of Ra's nocturnal barque, harpoon or spear in hand, and dispatches Apep before the serpent can halt the barque. te Velde 1967 ch. 4 is the principal scholarly synthesis.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 4, on Seth as Apep-slayer at the prow of the solar barque; on the iconographic and textual record of the prow-position dispatch.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 4 (Seth at the prow)

The prow-position role is iconographically distinctive. From the New Kingdom onward, Egyptian funerary and temple imagery consistently shows Seth — recognisable by his *Seth-animal* head, a composite-creature head whose biological identification remains philologically debated — at the front of the barque with weapon raised, dispatching the coiling serpent.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 4, on the iconographic distinctiveness of the prow-position Seth and on the *Seth-animal* head as the iconographic identifier; on the philological debate about the biological identification of the *Seth-animal*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 4 (Seth-animal iconography)

In the texts that narrate Amduat hour 7 and Book of Gates gate 4, Seth is named among the barque-crew who dispatch Apep. The dispatch is collective, but Seth is the single most-named individual combatant in the texts that name combatants by name.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 4, on Seth as the single most-named individual combatant against Apep in the Amduat seventh-hour and Book of Gates fourth-gate texts; on the collective character of the dispatch.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 4 (Seth named in dispatch texts)

Seth's Apep-slayer role is theologically distinct from his role as Osiris's murderer. The two belong to different dossiers; the sources do not regularly attempt reconciliation. Seth is at once Apep-slayer at the prow and brother-murderer in the Osiris cycle, and the apparent contradiction stands without theological harmonisation.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the theological distinction between Seth's positive Apep-slayer role and his negative Osiris-murderer role; on the Egyptian sources' allowance of the apparent contradiction without harmonisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (Seth's two dossiers)

This unharmonised character is one of the principal phenomena te Velde's monograph addresses. The title _Seth, God of Confusion_ reflects the scholarly judgment that the Egyptian tradition's handling of Seth is itself characterised by deliberate ambivalence rather than clear positive or negative valuation.footnote:[te Velde 1967, _Seth, God of Confusion_, title and introduction on the deliberate ambivalence of the Egyptian tradition's handling of Seth; chs. 3–4 on the unharmonised character of the two dossiers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=introduction (ambivalence)

The historical-political dimension is complex. In the Ramesside period (Nineteenth Dynasty, c. 1292–1186 BCE) Seth held a positive royal-cult position; in the Late Period and Ptolemaic period his valuation shifted toward the negative, with the Osiris-murderer dossier coming to dominate his theological reception.footnote:[te Velde 1967, on the Ramesside positive-cult position of Seth and the late-period shift to predominantly-negative valuation; on the historical-political dimension of the ambivalence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=passim (Ramesside cult; late-period shift)

The Ramesside position is reflected in the diplomatic record. The Hittite-Egyptian treaty of Ramesses II (c. 1259 BCE) names Seth — in the Ramesside contact-context name *Sutekh* — as the Egyptian divine guarantor alongside the Hittite storm-god; the equivalence Sutekh ↔ Tarḫunna is itself evidence of Seth's high-cult standing.footnote:[te Velde 1967, on the Ramesses II / Hittite treaty and on the diplomatic equivalence Sutekh / Tarḫunna; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the rendering "Sutekh" reserved for Ramesside diplomatic contexts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=passim (Sutekh diplomatic)

The Apep-slayer role is documented across both the Ramesside positive-cult period and the later periods of negative valuation. The prow-position dispatch is preserved in the Amduat, the Book of Gates, and continues into Late Period and Ptolemaic temple iconography even as Seth's wider valuation becomes more negative.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the diachronic continuity of the Apep-slayer role across the Ramesside positive-cult period and the later periods of negative valuation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (Apep-slayer continuity)

This continuity under shifting valuation is principal evidence for te Velde's reading of Seth as deliberately ambivalent rather than univocally evaluated. The same god is throughout both Apep-slayer (positive cosmological function) and Osiris-murderer (negative theological evaluation), with the two roles persisting in parallel.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the diachronic continuity-of-both-roles as evidence for the deliberate-ambivalence reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (deliberate ambivalence)

The Apep-slayer role is, on te Velde's reading, theologically necessary. The combat requires a divine combatant whose force is structurally suited to dispatching the violent serpent-adversary; Seth's storm-and-desert violence is the qualification — his disorder is the storm-god disorder that can match the *isfet*-personification's onslaught.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the theological necessity of Seth's storm-and-desert violence for the Apep-slayer role; on the structural fit between Seth's morphology and Apep's onslaught.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (theological necessity)

This reading is theologically subtle: it implies that the dispatching of *isfet* requires a divine agent whose own character includes the violent and the disorderly, and that the cosmological-combat's structural logic recognises a positive cosmological function for divine violence that is itself morally ambivalent.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the theologically-subtle implication of Seth's structural fit; on the recognition of a positive cosmological function for ambivalent divine violence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (subtle theology)

[INFERENCE: the Egyptian theological tradition's recognition of a positive cosmological function for Seth's ambivalent violence is one of the principal points at which the *maʿat*/*isfet* framework reveals its conceptual sophistication: the framework distinguishes between *isfet* as moral-political evil (Apep) and the violence-of-the-storm-god as cosmologically-functional even where it carries moral ambivalence (Seth) | basis: te Velde 1967 chs. 3–4 on the theological subtlety of Seth's role; Assmann 2001 on the *maʿat*/*isfet* framework's conceptual sophistication; the Egyptian sources' allowance of the unharmonised two-dossier structure for Seth as evidence of the framework's tolerance of moral ambivalence within cosmological function | risk: the inference is interpretive-theological rather than directly attested as a stated theological doctrine; alternative readings (in which Seth's two roles reflect different historical-political evaluations rather than a deliberate theological subtlety) remain available, though they are less natural fits for the diachronic continuity of both roles in parallel that te Velde 1967 documents.]

Mehen — a serpent-coil deity who encircles and protects Ra — is, with Seth, one of the principal attendants in the prow-and-cabin scenes. Seth stands at the prow with weapon raised; Mehen coils around Ra in the cabin. Together they form the barque's principal protective apparatus against Apep.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Mehen as the serpent-coil protector of Ra in the solar barque; on the prow-and-cabin protective apparatus formed by Seth and Mehen together; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the locked rendering "Mehen".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Mehen protector)

Mehen is iconographically a serpent — a serpent-bodied deity whose form is paradoxically continuous with Apep's morphology, the very serpent Mehen and the barque-crew defend Ra against. The Egyptian tradition deploys serpent-imagery on both sides of the cosmological combat, and the serpent-form is therefore not univocally aligned with *isfet*.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Mehen's serpent-form as paradoxically continuous with Apep's morphology; on the Egyptian deployment of serpent-imagery on both sides of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Mehen serpent-form)

This non-univocal alignment of serpent-imagery is a further subtlety the Apep material contributes to the book's typology. Serpents are not categorically aligned with *isfet*; the combat is not a war between non-serpentine order and serpentine disorder but between specific serpent-figures occupying different positions in the *maʿat*/*isfet* economy.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the non-univocal alignment of serpent-imagery; per chaoskampf scope.md on the diversity of adversary morphologies across traditions; per glossary.yaml on the "adversary" category-term as morphologically neutral.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (non-univocal serpent imagery)

Isis — the magic-working goddess, well known from the Osiris cycle as wife-of-Osiris and mother-of-Horus — also appears in the Apep material as a magical combatant. In several spells she is named binder-of-Apep, who binds the serpent with magical formulas and reduces him to immobility for the dispatch.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on Isis as the binder-of-Apep with magical spells; per chaoskampf glossary.yaml on the locked rendering "Isis"; Faulkner 1972 (rev. 1985), on Isis-named binding spells in the Book of the Dead corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Isis binder)

Isis's binder-of-Apep role is magical-aggressive, distinct from her better-known maternal-protective and resurrection-magical roles in the Osiris cycle. The register the Apep-binding spells call for is consistent with her wider reputation as the goddess "great of magic", but is aligned with the cosmological-combat dossier rather than the Osiris dossier.footnote:[te Velde 1967, ch. 3, on the distinction between Isis's Apep-binding role (cosmological-combat dossier) and her Osiris-cycle roles; on Isis's wider reputation as the goddess "great of magic".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=ch. 3 (Isis dossiers)

The collaborative cast — Seth at the prow, Mehen in the cabin, Isis with bindings, the wider barque-crew — gives Apep a richer ensemble than several comparands. Marduk fights Tiamat principally alone (Ch. 3); Indra fights Vṛtra principally alone (Ch. 14); Apep is fought by a divine collective.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the collective-divine-combatant character of the Apep dispatch; Lambert 2013 on Marduk's solo combat against Tiamat (cross-ref Ch. 3); Jamison and Brereton 2014 on Indra's solo combat against Vṛtra (cross-ref Ch. 14).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=te-velde-1967 ; loc=chs. 3-4 (collective combatants)

This collective structure is a further feature that fits the cyclical-maintenance subtype. The combat is not the singular triumph of a sovereignty-establishing or cosmogonic champion but the daily collaborative work of a divine ensemble — order-preservation, unlike order-establishment, requires ongoing collaboration among many.footnote:[te Velde 1967, chs. 3–4, on the collaborative-ensemble structure of the Apep dispatch; Assmann 2001 on the collaborative theology of Egyptian solar religion; per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml on the cyclical-maintenance subtype's structural features.]

// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: Iran (Aži Dahāka and Frashokereti; Ch. 13) — the Egyptian Apep tradition and the Iranian Aži Dahāka tradition (Ch. 13) sit at opposite poles of how combat-myth handles cosmic time: the Apep combat is perpetually-recurrent without eschatological closure (Egyptian *maʿat*/*isfet* maintenance), while the Aži Dahāka combat is bound-and-eschatologically-killed (Thraētaona binds Aži Dahāka under Damāvand; the rebound dragon escapes at the end of time and is killed for the second and final time by the revived Kərəsāspa at *Frashokereti*). The Iranian eschatologically-closed structure and the Egyptian perpetually-recurrent structure are the two limit-cases of the cosmic-time-handling spectrum that Part II C4 (Eschatological Displacement) develops, and per brief 16-ra-apep.yaml the contrast is load-bearing for the chapter's positioning. Treated in detail in Part II C4 (assmann-2001-search; benveniste-renou-1934; watkins-1995; cross-ref Ch. 13).
// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: Christian apocalyptic (Revelation 12; Ch. 9) — the Egyptian Apep tradition contrasts also with the Christian-apocalyptic combat tradition of Revelation 12 (Ch. 9) at the level of eschatological structure: the Egyptian combat has no eschatological closure and is perpetually-recurrent, while Revelation 12's combat between Michael and the dragon is presented as a moment within a wider eschatological narrative whose closure is the elimination of the dragon and the establishment of the new heavens-and-earth. Forsyth 1987 and Yarbro Collins 1976 treat the Revelation 12 combat as part of the longue-durée trajectory in which combat-myth motifs reach Christian apocalyptic via Hebrew-Bible mediation; the Egyptian Apep material, on the present chapter's reading, is structurally distinct from this trajectory in lacking the eschatological closure that the Hebrew-Bible-to-Christian-apocalyptic line develops. Treated in detail in Part II C4 (forsyth-1987; yarbro-collins-1976; cross-ref Ch. 9).
// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: Ballentine 2015 critique (Part II C8) — the Egyptian *maʿat*/*isfet* framework's philosophical sophistication offers Ballentine 2015 a non-chaos counter-model: Apep is explicitly not "chaos" in the Western-philosophical sense of inchoate primordial unformedness but *isfet* — disorder, injustice, falsehood, the violation of order — a moral-political category whose theological articulation in Assmann 2001 supplies the wider book with one of its strongest pieces of evidence for the inadequacy of "chaos" as a cross-tradition category-term. The chapter's compliance with the locked glossary discipline (the banned umbrella category-term is avoided throughout; *isfet* and "agent of disorder" are used instead) is itself a marker of this counter-model interface. Treated in detail in Part II C8 (assmann-2001-search; ballentine-2015; cross-ref Part II C8).
// COMPARATIVE-HOOK: Combat as ambivalent-divine-collective (Seth, Mehen, Isis at the barque) — the Egyptian Apep combat is fought by a divine collective with a structurally-ambivalent leading combatant (Seth at the prow, with his unharmonised positive-Apep-slayer-and-negative-Osiris-murderer two-dossier structure), in contrast to several of the comparand cases in which the combat is fought by a single sovereignty-establishing or cosmogonic champion (Marduk in Ch. 3; Indra in Ch. 14; Zeus in Ch. 11). The collective-and-ambivalent character of the Egyptian divine combatants is one further structural feature that fits the cyclical-maintenance subtype and that distinguishes it from the cosmogonic and the sovereignty subtypes; the unharmonised theological character of Seth's role in particular sets up the Ch. 17 edge-case, in which Seth's negative dossier becomes the principal subject. Treated in detail in Ch. 17 and in Part II C3 (te-velde-1967; cross-refs Chs. 11, 14, 17, and Part II C3).
