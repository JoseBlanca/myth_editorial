=== Source overview

The chapter treats the Mexica cosmogonic-and-sovereignty combat in which Huitzilopochtli is born fully armed from his mother Coatlicue at the Mountain of Serpents (Coatepec), and at the moment of his birth kills his elder sister Coyolxauhqui and the Centzon Huitznahua, his four hundred stellar brothers, who had come up the mountain to kill the pregnant Coatlicue and the not-yet-born god they regarded as a shame upon their lineage.footnote:[Per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml; Matos Moctezuma 1988, *The Great Temple of the Aztecs: Treasures of Tenochtitlan* (London: Thames & Hudson; ISBN 9780500390245), interpretive chapters on Coatepec as Templo Mayor, on the Florentine Codex Book 3 narrative as the fullest single Coatepec account, and on the cosmogonic-and-sovereignty function of the combat within Mexica state religion.]

The Coatepec narrative has no pre-conquest written-text witness in the strict sense, since Mesoamerican writing-systems before 1521 CE were primarily pictographic-logographic and the Mexica did not record their narrative literature in continuous alphabetic prose; the surviving textual witnesses to the Coatepec story are post-conquest, written down by Nahua informants and Spanish friars in the generation after the fall of Tenochtitlan in 1521 CE.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, *City of Sacrifice: The Aztec Empire and the Role of Violence in Civilization* (Boston: Beacon Press; ISBN 9780807046425), chapter on Coatepec, on the post-conquest character of the surviving textual witnesses to the Coatepec narrative and on the absence of pre-conquest continuous-prose attestations of Mexica narrative literature; Matos Moctezuma 1988, on the same post-conquest textual situation; per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml lacunae entry.]

The *Florentine Codex* is a bilingual codex written in Classical Nahuatl in the left column and in sixteenth-century Spanish in the right column, with hundreds of pictorial illustrations executed by Nahua artists trained in the Franciscan school at Tlatelolco, and presents the Coatepec narrative as part of the wider encyclopaedic survey of pre-conquest Mexica religion, cosmology, history, and natural history that Sahagún and his collaborators undertook in the second half of the sixteenth century.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the *Florentine Codex* as a bilingual Nahuatl-Spanish encyclopaedic codex compiled by Sahagún with Nahua collaborators trained at the Tlatelolco school, and on the pictorial illustrations executed by Nahua artists; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the encyclopaedic character of Sahagún's project and on the place of the Coatepec narrative within Book 3 ("The Origin of the Gods").]

The post-conquest textual witnesses to the Coatepec narrative include, alongside the *Florentine Codex* Book 3, the anonymous early-colonial Nahua-Spanish compilation *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas* (compiled c. 1530s–1540s and preserving an earlier-stratum Mexica account with slightly different kinship details for the Centzon Huitznahua), the *Crónica Mexicáyotl* of Hernando Alvarado Tezozómoc (early seventeenth century), the *Anales de Cuauhtitlán* (sixteenth-century Nahuatl annals), and the *Historia de las Indias de Nueva España* of the Dominican friar Diego Durán (compiled c. 1581 and giving the Coatepec narrative its fullest ritual-context treatment).footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the post-conquest textual corpus for the Coatepec narrative including the *Historia de los Mexicanos por sus pinturas*, the *Crónica Mexicáyotl*, the *Anales de Cuauhtitlán*, and Durán's *Historia*; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same corpus and on Durán's c. 1581 *Historia* as the fullest ritual-context source; per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml lacunae entry on the corroborative material from these post-conquest sources.]

=== The Mexica state, the festival, and the architecture

The Mexica were the dominant Nahuatl-speaking polity of the Basin of Mexico in the late pre-conquest period (c. 1325–1521 CE), with their capital at Tenochtitlan (founded c. 1325 CE on an island in Lake Texcoco) and their tributary empire extending across central Mexico from the Pacific coast in the west to the Gulf coast in the east at its greatest extent under the rulers Moctezuma I, Axayácatl, Ahuítzotl, and Moctezuma II.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the Mexica polity of the Basin of Mexico, on Tenochtitlan as its capital founded c. 1325 CE on an island in Lake Texcoco, and on the tributary empire's greatest extent under the late pre-conquest rulers; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the Mexica imperial context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

Huitzilopochtli — whose name is conventionally glossed in modern scholarship as "Hummingbird-of-the-South" or "Hummingbird-on-the-Left" — is the patron god of the Mexica state, the sun-warrior whose daily rising in the east is read in the Mexica theological tradition as the daily re-enactment of his Coatepec victory over his lunar sister and his stellar brothers, and whose tutelary relationship to the Mexica polity is co-constitutive with the political identity of the state itself.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on Huitzilopochtli as patron god of the Mexica state, on the conventional gloss of his name as "Hummingbird-of-the-South" / "Hummingbird-on-the-Left", and on the daily-rising solar reading of the Coatepec victory; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the co-constitutive relation between Huitzilopochtli's tutelary role and the political identity of the Mexica state; glossary 'Huitzilopochtli' first_mention_gloss.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=The Great Temple of the Aztecs, interpretive chapters

The Templo Mayor, the great double-stair pyramid at the geographical and ritual centre of Tenochtitlan, was the principal sanctuary of Huitzilopochtli — the southern (left-hand) summit-shrine on the pyramid's twin platforms was dedicated to the god, with the northern (right-hand) summit-shrine dedicated to the rain-god Tlaloc — and the structure was rebuilt and enlarged at least seven times over the polity's two-century lifespan, with each successive enlargement encasing the earlier structure within the new one in a stratigraphic sequence that the 1978-onward excavations have recovered.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the Templo Mayor as the principal sanctuary of Huitzilopochtli, on the southern Huitzilopochtli summit-shrine paired with the northern Tlaloc summit-shrine on the pyramid's double-stair platform, on the seven principal building stages identified by the excavations, and on the stratigraphic sequence of nested structures recovered from 1978 onward.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=The Great Temple of the Aztecs, interpretive chapters

The Templo Mayor's architectural identification as Coatepec — the Mountain of Serpents on which Huitzilopochtli is born and fights — is the load-bearing scholarly argument of Matos Moctezuma 1988 and is supported by multiple converging lines of iconographic and architectural evidence: the great serpent-headed balustrades flanking the southern (Huitzilopochtli) staircase, which give the structure its character as a serpent-mountain; the great Coyolxauhqui stone discovered in 1978 at the foot of that staircase, which depicts the dismembered moon-goddess at the base of Coatepec; and the structural design of the double pyramid as a four-tiered cosmic mountain with the god's shrine at its summit.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the Templo Mayor as Coatepec, on the converging lines of evidence (the serpent-headed balustrades flanking the southern staircase, the great Coyolxauhqui stone at the foot of that staircase, and the four-tiered cosmic-mountain structural design) supporting the architectural identification; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same architectural identification.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=The Great Temple of the Aztecs, interpretive chapters

The great Coyolxauhqui stone — a roughly circular monolith approximately 3.25 metres in diameter, sculpted in low relief on its upper face — was discovered on 21 February 1978 by electrical-utility workers digging a trench at the corner of Calle Guatemala and Calle de la Argentina in central Mexico City, lies at the foot of the southern (Huitzilopochtli) staircase of the Stage IV phase of the Templo Mayor, and depicts the moon-goddess in the moment of her dismemberment, with her head, arms, and legs severed and arranged around her nude torso, the bells of her cheeks visible on her face.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the Coyolxauhqui stone (approximately 3.25 metres in diameter) discovered on 21 February 1978 at the corner of Calle Guatemala and Calle de la Argentina in central Mexico City, on its placement at the foot of the southern (Huitzilopochtli) staircase of the Stage IV Templo Mayor, and on its iconographic depiction of the dismembered moon-goddess with the bells on her cheeks visible; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same iconography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=The Great Temple of the Aztecs, interpretive chapters

The Coyolxauhqui stone's placement at the foot of the southern staircase is iconographically and ritually consequential: the stone depicts the dismembered moon-goddess at the base of the cosmic mountain Coatepec, and the bodies of sacrificial victims cast down the staircase from the summit-shrine of Huitzilopochtli at the top of the pyramid would have come to rest on or beside the stone, completing the architectural-and-ritual re-enactment of the moment in the narrative when the newly born god flings the body of his sister down the side of the mountain.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the architectural-and-ritual re-enactment in which sacrificial victims cast down the southern staircase from the Huitzilopochtli summit-shrine come to rest on or beside the Coyolxauhqui stone at the foot of the staircase, completing the re-enactment of the goddess's dismemberment at the base of Coatepec; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same architectural-and-ritual re-enactment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

The Panquetzaliztli ("Raising of Banners") festival, the Mexica winter-solstice festival held in the corresponding twenty-day veintena of the eighteen-month Mexica calendrical year (the European-calendar dates fall around the winter solstice in late November to mid-December in the Julian system used in the early colonial sources, with the precise correspondence varying with the Mexica calendrical drift), was the annual ritual context in which the Coatepec narrative was publicly re-enacted at the Templo Mayor, and was one of the two principal annual festivals of the Mexica state ceremonial calendar.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the Panquetzaliztli ("Raising of Banners") winter-solstice festival as the annual ritual context in which the Coatepec narrative was re-enacted at the Templo Mayor and as one of the two principal annual festivals of the Mexica state ceremonial calendar, on its placement in the corresponding twenty-day veintena of the Mexica calendrical year, and on the Julian-calendar date-correspondence in the early colonial sources; glossary 'Panquetzaliztli' first_mention_gloss.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

The Panquetzaliztli festival included a sequence of public ritual elements that re-enacted components of the Coatepec narrative: the fashioning of a dough-image (*tzoalli*) of Huitzilopochtli that was processed through the city; mock combats between pairs of warriors representing Huitzilopochtli's forces and the Centzon Huitznahua; the casting of sacrificial victims' bodies down the southern staircase of the Templo Mayor onto the Coyolxauhqui stone; and the ritual feasting on portions of the dough-image of the god himself by the assembled celebrants, in a re-enactment-and-communion sequence whose structural-symbolic logic Carrasco 1999 reads as the ritual armature of the chapter's narrative material.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the Panquetzaliztli festival's ritual sequence including the dough-image (*tzoalli*) of Huitzilopochtli processed through the city, the mock combats between pairs of warriors representing Huitzilopochtli's forces and the Centzon Huitznahua, the casting of sacrificial victims onto the Coyolxauhqui stone, and the ritual feasting on the dough-image; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same festival sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

The Mexica practice of human sacrifice at the Templo Mayor — chiefly of war-captives taken in the so-called "flowery wars" (*xōchiyāōyōtl*) waged against neighbouring polities such as Tlaxcala — is interpreted in Carrasco 1999's reading as the ritual performance of the Coatepec narrative on a cosmic-political scale, with the prisoners' deaths and the casting of their bodies down the staircase functioning as the perpetual feeding of Huitzilopochtli's solar-warrior energy and as the perpetual re-enactment of the moment when the newly born god dismembers his lunar-stellar siblings.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the Mexica practice of human sacrifice at the Templo Mayor as the ritual performance of the Coatepec narrative on a cosmic-political scale, on the principal source of war-captives in the "flowery wars" (*xōchiyāōyōtl*) waged against neighbouring polities such as Tlaxcala, and on the ritual logic of the casting of bodies down the southern staircase as perpetual re-enactment of the dismemberment of the lunar-stellar siblings; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same ritual logic.]

=== The Coatepec narrative: Coatlicue and the conception of Huitzilopochtli

The Coatepec narrative as preserved in the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 opens with the goddess Coatlicue ("She-of-the-Serpent-Skirt"), the earth-goddess mother of Huitzilopochtli, sweeping at the holy mountain Coatepec ("Mountain of Serpents") near Tula in the pre-Mexica era, in the company of her daughter Coyolxauhqui and her four hundred sons the Centzon Huitznahua.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative opening with Coatlicue ("She-of-the-Serpent-Skirt") sweeping at the holy mountain Coatepec ("Mountain of Serpents") near Tula in the pre-Mexica era, in the company of her daughter Coyolxauhqui and her four hundred sons the Centzon Huitznahua; glossary 'Coatlicue' / 'Coatepec' / 'Coyolxauhqui' / 'Centzon Huitznahua' first_mention_gloss entries.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=The Great Temple of the Aztecs, interpretive chapters

Coatlicue's iconographic identity is monumentally captured in the great Coatlicue statue in the Museo Nacional de Antropología in Mexico City, an Aztec-period basalt sculpture approximately 2.6 metres tall that depicts the goddess with a skirt of intertwined serpents, a necklace of human hands and hearts, two outward-facing serpent-heads as her own face (formed where her decapitated head would otherwise be), and clawed feet — the iconography supplying a parallel non-textual witness to her character as earth-and-death-and-fertility goddess.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the great Coatlicue statue in the Museo Nacional de Antropología in Mexico City, an Aztec-period basalt sculpture approximately 2.6 metres tall depicting the goddess with serpent-skirt, necklace of human hands and hearts, twin outward-facing serpent-heads in place of her decapitated head, and clawed feet, as a parallel non-textual witness to her identity as earth-and-death-and-fertility goddess; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same iconography.]

=== The combat at Coatepec

The combat at Coatepec begins, in the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative, at the moment Huitzilopochtli is born — fully grown, fully armed, and equipped for war — emerging from Coatlicue's womb at the summit of the holy mountain just as Coyolxauhqui and the Centzon Huitznahua reach the top, with the timing of the birth being itself the narrative's first decisive movement against the conspirators.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative of Huitzilopochtli's birth at Coatepec, on his emergence fully grown, fully armed, and equipped for war from Coatlicue's womb at the moment Coyolxauhqui and the Centzon Huitznahua reach the summit, and on the timing of the birth as the narrative's first decisive movement against the conspirators; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same birth-as-combat-opening.]

Huitzilopochtli's birth-arms include the *xiuhcōātl* (the turquoise-serpent weapon — a fire-serpent of solar character whose iconographic appearance in Mexica art is a serpent-shaped firebrand or fire-spear with a recurved snout) which the newly born god wields as his decisive weapon against Coyolxauhqui, and a body-paint and feathered war-regalia which the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative describes in detail at the moment of his appearance at the mountain's summit.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on Huitzilopochtli's birth-arms in the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative, on the *xiuhcōātl* (turquoise-serpent / fire-serpent weapon) as a serpent-shaped firebrand or fire-spear with recurved snout in the Mexica iconographic tradition, on its decisive role in the killing of Coyolxauhqui, and on the body-paint and feathered war-regalia described in the narrative; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same iconography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=matos-moctezuma-templo-mayor ; loc=The Great Temple of the Aztecs, interpretive chapters

The fully-armed-from-birth pattern is theologically load-bearing for the chapter's reading of Huitzilopochtli as a sun-warrior whose combat with Coyolxauhqui is enacted at the moment of his coming-into-being rather than at a later mature-stage moment of his career: the god's identity and the combat are inseparable, since the very emergence of the sun-warrior IS the killing of the lunar-stellar siblings, with no narrative gap between birth and combat in which the god might be characterised independently of the killing.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the theological load-bearing of the fully-armed-from-birth pattern for the reading of Huitzilopochtli as a sun-warrior whose identity is inseparable from the combat, on the simultaneity of his coming-into-being with the killing of Coyolxauhqui and the Centzon Huitznahua, and on the absence of a narrative gap between birth and combat in the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 account; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same theological reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

The killing of Coyolxauhqui is, in the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative, immediate and total: the newly born Huitzilopochtli strikes off Coyolxauhqui's head with the *xiuhcōātl* at the summit of Coatepec, then dismembers her body — severing her arms and legs from her torso — and rolls or flings the dismembered body down the side of the holy mountain so that it comes to rest at the foot of Coatepec, with the dismemberment being the most iconographically explicit moment of the narrative.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative of Huitzilopochtli's killing of Coyolxauhqui as an immediate and total dismemberment at the summit of Coatepec, on the use of the *xiuhcōātl* to strike off her head, on the severing of her arms and legs from her torso, and on the rolling or flinging of the dismembered body down the side of the holy mountain; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same dismemberment narrative.]

The Coyolxauhqui stone at the foot of the southern Templo Mayor staircase is the architectural-iconographic re-enactment of this exact moment of the narrative: the stone depicts the dismembered moon-goddess at the base of Coatepec, head severed, arms and legs disarticulated and arranged radially around her nude torso, and is positioned on the ground at the foot of the staircase up which the worshippers and sacrificial victims would have processed toward the summit-shrine of Huitzilopochtli — the stone is the narrative-moment frozen in basalt at the precise location where the narrative-moment occurs.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the Coyolxauhqui stone as the architectural-iconographic re-enactment of the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 dismemberment-moment, on its iconographic depiction of the moon-goddess with severed head and disarticulated limbs arranged radially around her nude torso, and on its placement at the foot of the southern staircase as the precise location at which the narrative-moment occurs in the architectural re-enactment; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same architectural-iconographic logic.]

The killing of the Centzon Huitznahua follows in the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative immediately after the dismemberment of Coyolxauhqui: Huitzilopochtli pursues the four hundred southern stellar brothers across the mountain, around its slopes, and (in some strata of the narrative) four times in succession around the summit, killing them as they flee — the chase imagery being the load-bearing narrative figure for the daily-rising-sun-pursuing-and-extinguishing-the-stars cosmological reading of the combat.footnote:[Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative of Huitzilopochtli's pursuit of the Centzon Huitznahua across the slopes of Coatepec following the dismemberment of Coyolxauhqui, on the four-times-around-the-summit chase pattern in some strata of the narrative, and on the chase imagery as the load-bearing figure for the daily-rising-sun-pursuing-and-extinguishing-the-stars cosmological reading; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the same chase narrative.]

A small remnant of the Centzon Huitznahua is in some strata of the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative permitted to escape — fleeing south to a region whose name is rendered in some translations as Huitzlampa ("place of thorns" or "south") — and the survival of this remnant as the visible southern stars in the night sky is the narrative's aetiology for the persistence of stars in the Mexica heaven despite the daily victory of the sun-warrior god.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the *Florentine Codex* Book 3 narrative-stratum in which a small remnant of the Centzon Huitznahua is permitted to escape, fleeing south to a region named in some translations as Huitzlampa ("place of thorns" or "south"), and on the aetiological reading of this remnant as the visible southern stars in the night sky despite the daily victory of the sun-warrior god; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same aetiological reading.]

The combat at Coatepec is therefore in the Mexica theological reading not a one-time foundational combat that establishes the cosmos and recedes into the past, but a daily-recurring cosmic event whose primordial-narrative form (Huitzilopochtli's birth at Coatepec) is the perpetual archetype of the daily-recurring fact (the sun's rising in the east, the moon's setting, the stars' fading) — the combat is inscribed in the daily course of the sky, with each dawn re-enacting the killing of Coyolxauhqui and the pursuit of the Centzon Huitznahua.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the Mexica theological reading of the combat at Coatepec as a daily-recurring cosmic event whose primordial-narrative form is the perpetual archetype of the daily fact of the sun's rising and the moon's and stars' fading, and on the inscription of the combat in the daily course of the sky as continuous re-enactment; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same daily-cosmic reading; per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml cultural_relevance.function entry on the cosmological function: "the diurnal solar-kingship of Huitzilopochtli over the defeated moon (Coyolxauhqui) and stars (Centzon Huitznahua) is performed every day: the sun's rising IS the victory".]

=== The cosmogonic and sovereignty work of the combat

The combat at Coatepec performs simultaneous cosmogonic and sovereignty work in the Mexica theological reading: the establishment of the proper order of the Mexica cosmos (sun-and-day above, moon-and-stars-and-night below; solar-warrior-god as the ruling celestial body) is the cosmogonic outcome of the combat, while the establishment of Huitzilopochtli's sovereignty over his lunar-and-stellar siblings — and, by extension, over the wider Mexica pantheon — is the sovereignty outcome, with the two functions inseparable in the Mexica theological tradition.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the simultaneous cosmogonic and sovereignty work of the combat at Coatepec, on the establishment of the proper order of the Mexica cosmos as the cosmogonic outcome and on the establishment of Huitzilopochtli's sovereignty over his lunar-and-stellar siblings and the wider Mexica pantheon as the sovereignty outcome; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same dual function; per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml cultural_relevance.function entries on royal-legitimation and cosmological functions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=carrasco-1999-city ; loc=City of Sacrifice, ch. on Coatepec

The Mexica state's claim to the Coatepec narrative as its founding combat is explicit in the political-ritual record: Tenochtitlan as the political centre of the Mexica polity is architecturally identified with Coatepec via the Templo Mayor at its centre, and the Mexica royal-legitimation narrative traces the polity's authority to the patron god whose Coatepec victory established his sovereignty and (by extension) the sovereignty of the Mexica state that he favours.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the Mexica state's explicit claim to the Coatepec narrative as its founding combat, on Tenochtitlan as architecturally identified with Coatepec via the Templo Mayor at its centre, and on the Mexica royal-legitimation narrative that traces the polity's authority to the patron god's Coatepec victory; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same political-ritual claim; per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml cultural_relevance.function royal-legitimation entry on Tenochtitlan's political centre as architecturally the cosmic mountain of the god's victory.]

=== The chapter's place in the typology and the comparative reading

The Coatepec narrative is in the present book's typology a cosmogonic-and-sovereignty combat — that is, a combat that does both kinds of order-making work simultaneously rather than performing a single one — with the cosmogonic work consisting in the establishment of the proper celestial order (the regnant sun above the defeated moon and stars) and the sovereignty work consisting in Huitzilopochtli's establishment as the patron deity of the Mexica state and the legitimating reference for the polity's political-ritual order.footnote:[Per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml cultural_relevance.function entries on royal-legitimation and cosmological functions; scope.md §"Subject definition" 1 (cosmogonic) and 2 (sovereignty / kingship) on the two order-making subtypes that the Coatepec narrative simultaneously instantiates; Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the simultaneous cosmogonic and sovereignty work of the combat.]

The chapter's relation to the post-conquest filtering of the source-record is methodologically central: every textual claim about the Coatepec narrative in the present chapter is built on post-conquest Nahuatl-and-Spanish texts compiled in a context of active suppression of Mexica narrative-religion, and the chapter's reading of the narrative as a combat-cosmogony — and as an unambiguous one — must be held alongside the recognition that the texts on which the reading depends were shaped by Spanish friars whose conceptual frames included pre-existing notions of "war in heaven" derived from biblical-and-medieval Christian sources, with the consequent risk of category-import.footnote:[Carrasco 1999, ch. on Coatepec, on the methodological centrality of the post-conquest filtering of the source-record, on the consequent risk of category-import from biblical-and-medieval Christian "war in heaven" notions in the Spanish-friar redaction of the Mexica narrative, and on the methodological discipline of holding the combat-cosmogony reading alongside this filtering recognition; Matos Moctezuma 1988, interpretive chapters on the same methodological situation; per brief 24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.yaml lacunae entry on the post-conquest character of the textual record.]
