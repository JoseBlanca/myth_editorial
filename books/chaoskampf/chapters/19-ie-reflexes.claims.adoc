=== Source overview

The chapter surveys six Indo-European post-Vedic, post-Iranian, and post-Anatolian dragon-combat sub-cases that Calvert Watkins's *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* identifies as reflexes of the reconstructed Indo-European poetic formula HERO SLAY SERPENT, ordered in the present chapter from the strongest philological evidence (the Old Norse Sigurd-Fáfnir corpus) to the most fragmentary (the Armenian Vahagn material).footnote:[Per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics*, Part V on the IE reflexes of HERO SLAY SERPENT, with ch. 42 specifically on the Old Norse Sigurd material.]

=== Sigurd vs. Fáfnir — strongest philological evidence

The Sigurd-Fáfnir narrative is preserved principally in the Eddic poem *Fáfnismál* in the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE), one of three closely-grouped poems of the Sigurd-cycle in the Codex (with *Reginsmál* preceding and *Sigrdrífumál* following in the manuscript order), and is also attested in the prose *Völsunga saga* (c. 1270 CE) and in the Middle High German *Nibelungenlied* (c. 1200 CE) as the related Germanic witness with Sigurd in the German form Siegfried.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* and the surrounding Codex Regius poems (*Reginsmál* preceding, *Sigrdrífumál* following) on Sigurd; *Völsunga saga* c. 1270 CE; *Nibelungenlied* c. 1200 CE as the related Middle High German Siegfried witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the related-Germanic-witness status of the Nibelungenlied.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

Sigurd is the son of Sigmund of the Völsung lineage, raised in the household of the smith Regin after his father's death, and the *Fáfnismál* presents him as a young hero on the threshold of his first major heroic deed, the killing of the dragon Fáfnir on the heath of Gnitaheiðr.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* and the surrounding Codex Regius poems (with *Reginsmál* on Sigurd's upbringing and Regin's role); on the Völsung lineage and Gnitaheiðr setting.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Reginsmál; Fáfnismál

Fáfnir is a dwarf transformed into a dragon by his guardianship of the cursed gold of Andvari, gold that Loki had earlier extorted from the dwarf Andvari to pay a wergild to Hreidmar (Fáfnir's and Regin's father) for the killing of Otr, and the curse on the gold is the load-bearing motif that drives the Sigurd-cycle from the killing of the dragon to Sigurd's own death.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* and *Fáfnismál*, on the cursed gold of Andvari, Loki's extortion of the hoard, the wergild for Otr, Fáfnir's transformation into a dragon, and the curse-driven trajectory of the cycle; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the cursed-gold and Sigurd-death motifs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Reginsmál; Fáfnismál

Regin, Fáfnir's brother and the smith who fosters Sigurd, forges the sword Gram from the fragments of Sigmund's broken sword and incites Sigurd to ambush Fáfnir on the heath, and the *Fáfnismál* opens with Sigurd at the moment of the killing-stroke, having dug a pit on the dragon's path to the water and lying in wait below.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* on the forging of Gram from Sigmund's sword-fragments and Regin's incitement; *Fáfnismál* opening on the pit-and-ambush mechanism.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál opening

The killing-stroke itself is delivered with the sword Gram from the pit: Sigurd thrusts upward into Fáfnir's underbelly as the dragon passes over the pit on his way to the water, and the *Fáfnismál* presents the killing-stroke as the central act around which the poem's dialogue is organised rather than as an extended combat-narrative.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* on Sigurd's upward thrust with Gram from the pit and on the structure of the poem as dialogue around the central killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

The post-killing dialogue between Sigurd and the dying Fáfnir occupies the bulk of the *Fáfnismál*: Fáfnir, mortally wounded, asks Sigurd's name, warns him of the curse on the gold, and supplies cosmological-and-genealogical lore in the wisdom-dialogue register that the *Vafþrúðnismál* and *Grímnismál* establish elsewhere in the Eddic corpus.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál*, on the post-killing wisdom-dialogue between Sigurd and Fáfnir, including the curse-warning and the cosmological-and-genealogical exchange; on the Eddic wisdom-dialogue register paralleled in *Vafþrúðnismál* and *Grímnismál*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál stanzas (post-killing dialogue)

After Fáfnir's death Regin requests that Sigurd roast the dragon's heart, and when Sigurd touches the cooking heart with his finger and licks the burned blood from his finger he gains understanding of the speech of birds, and the nuthatches in the surrounding trees warn him that Regin plans to kill him for the treasure.footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* on the heart-roasting, the blood-tasting, the finger-burn, the bird-speech understanding, and the nuthatches' warning; on the resulting threat from Regin.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál (heart-roasting and bird-speech)

Sigurd, warned by the birds, kills Regin in turn — beheading him with Gram — and then takes Fáfnir's hoard onto his horse Grani, with the *Fáfnismál* ending on Sigurd in possession of the cursed gold and on the way to the next stage of the cycle (his encounter with the valkyrie Sigrdrífa / Brynhild in the *Sigrdrífumál*).footnote:[Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* concluding stanzas and *Sigrdrífumál* opening, on Sigurd's killing of Regin, the loading of the hoard onto Grani, and the bridge to the Sigrdrífa / Brynhild encounter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál concluding stanzas; Sigrdrífumál opening

The Sigurd-cycle's outcome is ambiguous in a way that distinguishes it from the cosmogonic and sovereignty-establishment subtypes of the combat myth: Sigurd kills the dragon and gains the hoard, but the curse on the gold drives the long-arc tragedy of the cycle (Brynhild's death, the Burgundian disasters, Sigurd's own death at the hands of Gunnar's brother Gutthorm) and the killing therefore destroys rather than secures the hero's prosperity.footnote:[Larrington 2014, on the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle from *Fáfnismál* through *Sigrdrífumál* and the Burgundian poems to Sigurd's death; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the ambiguous-outcome reading and on the forward-link to Part II C7 (inversion problem).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-cycle long arc

[INFERENCE: the reading of the Sigurd-cycle's outcome as ambiguous — the killing brings the hero the treasure but also the curse that will destroy him — is the chapter's editorial reading of the long-arc tragedy of the cycle, drawn from Larrington 2014 on the Codex Regius poems and from Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the diction of the killing, and is presented in the chapter as the principal forward-link to Part II C7 of the present book (the inversion problem); the *Fáfnismál* itself does not in so many words frame the killing as destroying-rather-than-securing prosperity, but the curse-warning of Fáfnir to Sigurd and the long-arc trajectory of the surrounding Eddic poems supply the basis for the inference. | basis: Larrington 2014 on the curse-driven trajectory of the Sigurd-cycle including Fáfnir's curse-warning at *Fáfnismál*; Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the philological diction of the killing; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the C7-inversion forward-link | risk: the framing as "ambiguous outcome" is a comparative-typological reading rather than a direct attestation, and the *Fáfnismál* presents the killing as a heroic deed in its own right within the poem's own register.]

The Sigurd-cycle is also attested in the prose *Völsunga saga* (c. 1270 CE), which gives a continuous-narrative version of the cycle including the Fáfnir-killing episode, and the prose witness is treated by Old Norse scholarship as preserving older oral-tradition material alongside the Eddic poetic witnesses; the present chapter cites the *Völsunga saga* as a parallel witness rather than as the primary text for the *Fáfnismál* episode itself.footnote:[*Völsunga saga* c. 1270 CE as continuous-narrative parallel witness to the Sigurd-cycle including the Fáfnir-killing episode; Larrington 2014 on the Eddic-and-prose witness relationship.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-cycle parallel witnesses

The Middle High German *Nibelungenlied* (c. 1200 CE) is the related Germanic witness to the Sigurd-cycle, with Sigurd in the German form Siegfried and the dragon-killing transposed into a brief retrospective narrative episode rather than treated as a focal scene of the poem; the *Nibelungenlied* is cited in the present chapter at corpus level as the related Germanic witness rather than as a primary text for the Fáfnir-episode.footnote:[*Nibelungenlied* c. 1200 CE on Siegfried's dragon-killing in retrospective narrative; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the related-Germanic-witness status of the Nibelungenlied as parallel to the Old Norse Sigurd material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd / Siegfried Germanic parallel

In Watkins's reading in *How to Kill a Dragon* chapter 42, the Eddic *Fáfnismál* diction of Sigurd's killing is a direct reflex of the reconstructed Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula: the verb of killing (Old Norse *vega*, "to kill in combat"), its subject (the named hero), and its object (the named dragon) instantiate in Old Norse the same syntactic-and-semantic structure that Vedic *áhann áhim* ("he slew the serpent") instantiates in Vedic Sanskrit and that Hittite *kuenta . . . illuyanka* ("he killed the serpent") instantiates in Hittite.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on the *Fáfnismál* diction of Sigurd's killing as a direct reflex of the reconstructed IE HERO SLAY SERPENT formula; Vedic *áhann áhim* (RV 1.32) and Hittite *kuenta . . . illuyanka* (CTH 321) as the comparative philological exhibits of the same formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42

Watkins's argument in chapter 42 is that the *Fáfnismál* preserves the IE poetic formula at the level of the verbal diction of the killing-stroke itself, not merely at the level of the structural-typological pattern, and that the Norse sub-case is therefore among the strongest philological evidence in the post-Iranian, post-Vedic, post-Anatolian corpus for the reconstructed formula's status as Indo-European poetic inheritance rather than as accidental cross-tradition convergence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on the philological strength of the Norse evidence as inheritance rather than convergence; on the *Fáfnismál* diction of the killing-stroke as the load-bearing locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42

The Sigurd-Fáfnir episode is also one of the iconographically densest in the post-Vedic IE corpus, with at least eleven Scandinavian rune-stones from the 11th and 12th centuries CE depicting Sigurd's killing of Fáfnir (the Ramsund stone in Södermanland c. 1030 CE is the principal-and-best-preserved witness), and the iconographic dossier supplements the Eddic and prose textual witnesses as a third register of attestation alongside the verbal-narrative and the philological-formulaic.footnote:[Larrington 2014 on the Sigurd-Fáfnir iconographic tradition; Ramsund stone (Södermanland c. 1030 CE) as principal Scandinavian rune-stone witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the iconographic dossier as a third register of attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Sigurd-Fáfnir iconographic dossier

[INFERENCE: the identification of the Sigurd-Fáfnir Eddic-iconographic-philological triple-witness pattern as parallel to the Thor-Jörmungandr triple-witness pattern of chapter 18 of the present volume is a comparative reading drawn from the wider Norse evidence, presented as a parallel between two Old Norse cases rather than as a feature of either case in isolation; the parallel is presented in the chapter as a comparative observation rather than as a direct claim of the *Fáfnismál* itself. | basis: Watkins 1995 ch. 42 on the *Fáfnismál*-diction strength; Larrington 2014 on the Sigurd-iconographic dossier; brief 18-thor-jormungandr.yaml on the Thor triple-witness pattern; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the back-link to Ch. 18 | risk: the parallel is typological-comparative rather than evidence of direct connection between the two cycles.]

=== Beowulf vs. Grendel and the dragon

The Old English poem *Beowulf* survives in a single manuscript witness — London, British Library, Cotton Vitellius A.xv (the Nowell Codex), c. 1000 CE — and is conventionally assigned to a composition-period bracketed somewhere in the 7th to 10th centuries CE, with the dragon-episode forming the third and final movement of the poem after the Grendel-episode and the Grendel's-mother-episode that dominate the poem's first two movements.footnote:[*Beowulf*, single-manuscript witness London BL Cotton Vitellius A.xv (Nowell Codex), c. 1000 CE; composition 7th–10th c. CE in standard scholarly bracketing; three-movement structure (Grendel / Grendel's mother / dragon) with the dragon-episode as third and final movement; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the manuscript and composition-dating conventions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf is a Geatish hero — a member of the Gēatas, a southern Scandinavian people — who in the poem's first movement travels to the Danish court of king Hrothgar to defend the hall Heorot from the man-eating monster Grendel, the descendant of Cain who has been killing Hrothgar's warriors at Heorot for twelve years.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 86–193 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on the Geatish identity of Beowulf, the Danish setting, Hrothgar's court at Heorot, Grendel's twelve-year depredations, and Grendel's descent from Cain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

Beowulf kills Grendel by tearing off his arm in a wrestling-match in the dark hall of Heorot, refusing weapons because Grendel uses none, and Grendel flees mortally wounded to die in the fenland-mere where his mother also dwells; the killing-encounter is at lines 710–836 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 710–836 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on the wrestling-match killing of Grendel, the arm-tearing, the weaponless contest, and Grendel's flight to the fen-mere.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The next night Grendel's mother attacks Heorot to avenge her son, and Beowulf descends into the underwater hall of the fen-mere to kill her with a giant-forged sword that he finds in the underwater hall itself; the encounter is at lines 1251–1887 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering.footnote:[*Beowulf*, lines 1251–1887 in the standard Klaeber-edition line-numbering, on Grendel's mother's revenge-attack, Beowulf's descent into the fen-mere, the underwater-hall combat, and the giant-forged sword.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

=== Lugh vs. Balor — the Second Battle of Mag Tuired

The Lugh-Balor narrative is preserved principally in the Middle Irish prose narrative *Cath Maige Tuired* ("The Battle of Mag Tuired"), transmitted in manuscripts from the 12th to the 16th century CE and presenting the Second Battle of Mag Tuired as the decisive combat between the Tuatha Dé Danann (the Irish gods-and-ancestors) and the Fomorians (a monstrous-and-oppressive people associated with the sea and with tyranny over the Tuatha Dé).footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired*, manuscripts 12th–16th c. CE; standard critical edition Gray 1982 (Irish Texts Society) — to be pinned at glossary-lock per inventory.approved.yaml identifier_missing flag; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Tuatha Dé Danann / Fomorians combat-axis; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the Second Battle of Mag Tuired as the decisive Tuatha-Dé combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Lugh is a young warrior-hero of the Tuatha Dé Danann, the son of Cian (a Tuatha Dé) and of Ethniu (the daughter of the Fomorian Balor), and his hybrid lineage — Tuatha Dé father and Fomorian mother — is the load-bearing genealogical motif of the *Cath Maige Tuired* narrative since it positions Lugh as the only figure who can kill his Fomorian grandfather Balor and end the Fomorian tyranny.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Lugh's hybrid lineage (Cian / Ethniu / Balor) and on the kill-the-grandfather motif; West 2007 ch. 6 on the same lineage as the IE-warrior structural-genealogical pattern; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the hybrid-lineage motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Balor is the king (or principal warrior) of the Fomorians and is named in the *Cath Maige Tuired* as "Balor of the Evil Eye" (Balor Birug-derc, "Balor of the piercing eye") because of his single enormous destructive eye that can incinerate any host on which it is opened, and the tactical problem of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired is the problem of how to bring the eye down before it is opened on the Tuatha Dé host.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Balor's title "Balor of the Evil Eye" / Balor Birug-derc; on the destructive eye and the tactical-narrative problem it poses; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the evil-eye motif.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The killing-stroke at the Second Battle of Mag Tuired is delivered by Lugh with a sling-stone that strikes Balor's eye at the moment of the eye's opening, driving the eye through the back of Balor's head so that its destructive gaze is turned on the Fomorian host behind Balor and breaks their order; the *Cath Maige Tuired* narrates the killing-stroke as the decisive moment of the battle that ends the Fomorian tyranny and inaugurates Tuatha Dé Danann rule.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on Lugh's sling-stone killing of Balor, on the eye-driven-through-the-head reversal, on the destruction of the Fomorian host by the redirected eye, and on the inauguration of Tuatha Dé rule; West 2007 ch. 6 on the same narrative-structural pattern.]

The *Cath Maige Tuired* narrative is structured around the wider battle-context of the Second Battle of Mag Tuired, in which the Tuatha Dé and the Fomorians fight a decisive engagement on the plain of Mag Tuired (in modern County Sligo), with the Lugh-Balor encounter as the decisive single-combat at the centre of the wider battle and with Lugh's status as battle-leader of the Tuatha Dé established by the prelude of the prose narrative.footnote:[*Cath Maige Tuired* on the wider battle-context, on the plain of Mag Tuired (County Sligo) as the setting, and on Lugh's battle-leader role; West 2007 ch. 6 on the centred-single-combat structure of the IE warrior / dragon-slayer narrative.]

=== The Red Dragon and the White Dragon — Welsh combat

The Welsh Red-and-White Dragon narrative is preserved in two principal primary witnesses with distinct framings: the Latin *Historia Brittonum* (the Nennius-recension, conventionally dated c. 828 CE) gives a politicised version in which the two dragons are interpreted as the Britons and the Saxons, and the Middle Welsh prose tale *Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (manuscripts from c. 1350 CE onward) preserves an older, less politicised version in which the combat is an annual cosmological-and-territorial disturbance to be ended by the king Lludd at the urging of his brother Llefelys.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* c. 828 CE (Nennius recension) on the Britons-vs-Saxons political reading; *Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (manuscripts White Book of Rhydderch c. 1350 CE; Red Book of Hergest c. 1382–1410 CE) on the annual-disturbance reading; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the two-recension distinction; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the two-witness framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Historia Brittonum* the combat-narrative is set within the Vortigern-and-Ambrosius episode: Vortigern, the British king, attempts to build a fortress at Dinas Emrys, but the foundations sink each night; Ambrosius (the boy-prophet who is, in the narrative, the figure who later becomes Merlin in the post-Geoffrey-of-Monmouth tradition) reveals that beneath the foundation-site is a hidden underground pool in which a red dragon and a white dragon fight, and the political-allegorical reading is that the red dragon is the Britons and the white dragon is the Saxons.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* on the Vortigern-Ambrosius episode at Dinas Emrys, on the foundation-sinking, on the underground pool, on the red-dragon-vs-white-dragon combat, and on the Britons-vs-Saxons political-allegorical reading; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

The political-allegorical reading of *Historia Brittonum* casts the Welsh Red-and-White Dragon combat as an early-medieval prophetic-and-political narrative — the red Welsh dragon will eventually defeat the white Saxon dragon, and the political-allegorical frame is load-bearing for the early-medieval recension of the narrative — and the red-dragon symbol enters the long-arc British (and specifically Welsh) political iconography from this *Historia Brittonum* moment onward.footnote:[*Historia Brittonum* on the political-allegorical frame and on the prophetic outcome; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the long-arc Welsh-political iconographic reception of the red dragon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Lludd and Llefelys* the combat-narrative is one of three plagues that afflict the Island of Britain in the time of king Lludd: the first plague is the Coraniaid (a magical foreign people who hear all conversations), the second plague is the dragon-combat (an annual scream that destroys crops, terrifies pregnant women, and devastates the realm), and the third plague is a magical thief who steals the king's stores; Lludd, on his brother Llefelys's advice, addresses each of the three plagues in turn.footnote:[*Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* on the three-plague structure (Coraniaid; dragon-combat; magical thief); on Lludd's address to each plague on his brother Llefelys's advice; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the three-plague structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

In *Lludd and Llefelys* the dragon-combat is presented as an annual cosmological-and-territorial disturbance: every May Eve the two dragons rise from beneath the centre of Britain and fight in the air with a scream that shakes the realm and destroys crops, and the resolution prescribed by Llefelys is to find the centre of the Island, dig a pit, fill the pit with mead, lure the two dragons into the pit, capture them when they are drunk on the mead, and bury them in a stone chest at Dinas Emrys.footnote:[*Lludd and Llefelys* on the May-Eve annual disturbance, on the central location of the combat, on the mead-pit-and-burial resolution, and on the Dinas Emrys burial-site; Ogden 2013 ch. on Celtic material on the resolution-mechanism.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. on Celtic material

[VARIANT: primary=*Historia Brittonum* (red-and-white-dragon political allegory; Britons-vs-Saxons reading; the dragons fight beneath Dinas Emrys and Vortigern's fortress sinks; the prophetic-political resolution) | alt=*Lludd and Llefelys* in the *Mabinogion* (annual May-Eve disturbance; the dragons are buried at Dinas Emrys after being lured into a mead-pit; cosmological-territorial resolution rather than political-allegorical) | chosen: present-both | reason: the two recensions present two distinct framings of the same underlying combat-tradition, with *Historia Brittonum* the political-allegorical reading of c. 828 CE and *Lludd and Llefelys* the older non-political reading of the *Mabinogion* tradition; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the two-witness side-by-side presentation.]

=== Batraz and the Nart combats — Ossetic

The Batraz narrative is preserved in the Ossetic Nart saga corpus, an oral-epic tradition recorded in the 19th and 20th centuries CE from Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen narrators across the North Caucasus, with the principal scholarly anchor Georges Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments and the principal IE-comparative apparatus West 2007's chapter 6.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus, 19th–20th c. CE oral-epic recordings from Ossetian, Circassian, and Chechen narrators; Dumézil's twentieth-century treatments as the principal scholarly precedent for the IE-comparative reading; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Scytho-Alanic IE warrior / dragon-slayer reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Narts are the heroic figures of the North Caucasian epic tradition — a category-class of warrior-figures who occupy the epic narrative-space and who are the principal characters of the Ossetic Nart saga corpus and of the cognate Circassian and Chechen Nart traditions — with Batraz one of the principal Nart warrior-figures in the Ossetic version of the corpus.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on the Narts as heroic figures of the North Caucasian epic tradition; West 2007 ch. 6 on Batraz as a principal Ossetic Nart warrior-figure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Batraz is distinguished within the Ossetic Nart corpus by his steel body — he is born from a tumour or boil on the back of his father Khamits, plunges himself into a furnace, and emerges with a body of steel — and the steel-body motif is the principal physical-and-narrative attribute that distinguishes Batraz from other Narts within the corpus.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on Batraz's steel-body origin (born from Khamits's back; furnace-tempering; steel-body status); West 2007 ch. 6 on the steel-body motif as IE-warrior structural attribute.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Batraz fights monstrous giants and dragons across multiple episodes of the Nart corpus: against the giant Mukara (or Bedzenag), against various dragons-and-monstrous-figures, and against opposing Nart-clans within the Ossetic tradition; the Nart corpus is not a single continuous narrative but a cluster of episodic combats, with each combat-episode preserving the warrior-pole of the IE pattern in distinctive Ossetic morphology.footnote:[Ossetic Nart saga corpus on Batraz's combats against Mukara / Bedzenag and against various dragons-and-monstrous-figures; West 2007 ch. 6 on the episodic-cluster structure of the Nart corpus and on the warrior-pole preservation; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the episodic-cluster structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

=== Vahagn and the vishaps — Armenian (fragmentary)

The Vahagn material is the most fragmentary of the six sub-cases of the present chapter, and the Vahagn sub-section is presented with explicit fragmentary framing throughout: the principal hymn-text to Vahagn is lost, and what survives is only Movses Khorenatsi's late paraphrase of an earlier Armenian hymn in book I chapter 31 of the *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* ("History of the Armenians").footnote:[Khorenatsi *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* book I.31 on the Vahagn paraphrase; Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the lost-hymn-and-late-paraphrase status of the Vahagn material; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the explicit fragmentary framing required for the Vahagn sub-section.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=russell-zoroastrianism-armenia ; loc=Vahagn chapter

[LACUNA: the principal hymn-text to Vahagn is lost; what survives is only Khorenatsi's late paraphrase of the hymn in book I chapter 31 of the *Patmut'iwn Hayots'*. | source: Khorenatsi *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* I.31 (preserving the late paraphrase); the hymn-text itself is not extant in any manuscript witness; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the lost-hymn status | scholarly_reconstruction: Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter reads Khorenatsi's paraphrase as preserving the substantive content of the lost hymn at the level of the dragon-slayer-epithet (Vishapakagh, "dragon-reaper") and the storm-god combat-frame, with the specific verbal-poetic diction of the lost hymn unrecoverable; Petrosyan's Armenian-mythology scholarship corroborates the Russell reading at the level of substantive content while flagging the verbal-philological limits of the reconstruction.]

Vahagn is the Armenian storm-god whose epithet *Vishapakagh* — "dragon-reaper" — preserves in nominal form the dragon-slayer combat-pattern and identifies him as Armenia's storm-god dragon-slayer in the same conceptual category-position that Indra occupies in the Vedic tradition, Thraētaona in the Iranian tradition, and Thor in the Norse tradition.footnote:[Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on Vahagn as the Armenian storm-god and on the *Vishapakagh* "dragon-reaper" epithet; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the storm-god dragon-slayer category-position.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=russell-zoroastrianism-armenia ; loc=Vahagn chapter

Khorenatsi's *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* — conventionally dated to the 5th to 9th century CE in successive scholarly assessments, with the precise dating contested in twentieth-century Armenian scholarship — is itself a relatively-late and historiographical witness to the pre-Christian Armenian religious culture, and Khorenatsi's paraphrase of the Vahagn hymn at I.31 is presented within his work as a document of Armenian pagan-religious tradition on which his own Christian-historiographical narrative depends for its pre-Christian background.footnote:[Khorenatsi *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* on the historiographical-and-Christian framing of the work; Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the dating-contested status of Khorenatsi's composition (5th–9th c. CE bracketing) and on the historiographical-paraphrase status of the Vahagn passage at I.31.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=russell-zoroastrianism-armenia ; loc=Vahagn chapter

Khorenatsi's paraphrase at *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* I.31 describes Vahagn's birth from the heavens, the sea, and a reed — a triple-birth motif in which the heavens labour, the sea labours, the purple reed labours, and Vahagn emerges from the smoke of the reed with sun-like hair and beard and fire-like eyes — and the paraphrase preserves this birth-narrative without supplying a continuous narrative of Vahagn's combat with the *vishaps* themselves.footnote:[Khorenatsi *Patmut'iwn Hayots'* I.31 on the triple-birth motif (heavens / sea / reed; sun-like hair; fire-like eyes); Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the birth-narrative as preserved in the paraphrase and on the absence of continuous combat-narrative; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the paraphrase contents.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=russell-zoroastrianism-armenia ; loc=Vahagn chapter

The Vahagn combat-with-the-*vishaps* is therefore not preserved as a continuous narrative in any extant primary witness, and the chapter's combat-content for the Vahagn sub-case is reconstructed from the *Vishapakagh* dragon-reaper epithet, from the storm-god category-position of Vahagn within the Armenian pantheon, from the *vishapakar* stelae of the second millennium BCE (which document the cultic-and-iconographic context of the dragon-figure within Armenian pre-Christian religion), and from comparative-philological readings of the surviving paraphrase against the Vedic, Iranian, and Anatolian comparanda.footnote:[Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the reconstruction-from-epithet, from-category-position, from-cultic-stelae, and from-comparative-philology methodology for the Vahagn combat-content; *vishapakar* stelae as the cultic-iconographic-context evidence; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the reconstruction methodology and on the explicit fragmentary framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=russell-zoroastrianism-armenia ; loc=Vahagn chapter

The *vishaps* are the dragons of the Armenian tradition — the term *vishap* is the Armenian common noun for "dragon", attested both as a narrative-mythological category and as the name given to the second-millennium-BCE stone stelae (the *vishapakar* or "dragon-stones") that survive in the highlands of historical Armenia — and the *vishapakar* stelae are the principal pre-Christian-period material attestation of the dragon-figure within the Armenian cultic-archaeological context.footnote:[Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the *vishap* as Armenian common noun for "dragon" and on the *vishapakar* stelae of the second millennium BCE as the principal pre-Christian-period material attestation; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the *vishapakar* stelae as cultic-iconographic-context evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=russell-zoroastrianism-armenia ; loc=Vahagn chapter

The *vishapakar* stelae are dated by Armenian archaeologists to the second millennium BCE and are concentrated in the high pastures of the Armenian highlands (principally on the slopes of Mount Aragats and in the Geghama mountains), with the stelae's morphology featuring fish-like or serpentine-like carvings on tall stone shafts; the cultic-iconographic context of the stelae is inferred from their location and morphology, and the Armenian-archaeological literature reads the stelae as cultic objects associated with water, fertility, and dragon-figures.footnote:[Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the second-millennium-BCE dating, on the Aragats / Geghama-mountains location, on the fish-and-serpentine morphology, and on the cultic-iconographic reading of the *vishapakar* stelae; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the *vishapakar* archaeological context.]

[INFERENCE: the reading of Vahagn as the Armenian Indo-European reflex of HERO SLAY SERPENT — given the lost-hymn status of the principal narrative source and the reconstruction of the combat-content from epithet, category-position, *vishapakar* archaeological context, and comparative-philological readings — is a heavily-comparative reading whose evidential basis is fragmentary in a way that none of the other five sub-cases of the present chapter is; the chapter presents the Vahagn reading with explicit fragmentary framing and refrains from reconstructing combat-content beyond what Khorenatsi's paraphrase, the *Vishapakagh* epithet, and the *vishapakar* stelae jointly support. | basis: Russell 1987 Vahagn chapter on the four-source reconstruction methodology (paraphrase / epithet / stelae / comparative); Petrosyan's Armenian-mythology scholarship on the same; brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the explicit-fragmentary-framing requirement | risk: the reading depends on a much-thinner evidential base than the Norse, Old English, or Celtic sub-cases, and the chapter's presentation of the Vahagn material is correspondingly more circumspect about combat-content claims.]
