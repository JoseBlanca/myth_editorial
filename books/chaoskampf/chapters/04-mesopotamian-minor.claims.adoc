== Minor Mesopotamian Combats (Marduk/Labbu) — Claims

=== Source overview

The Akkadian text known to scholars as the "Slaying of Labbu" survives on a single fragmentary cuneiform tablet, with the working English translation in Foster's *Before the Muses* running to about two pages.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, "The Slaying of Labbu" (full translation).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

Foster prefaces the text with a headnote describing it as a fragmentary myth-fragment and treats it as belonging to the Akkadian combat-myth corpus alongside Anzû and *Enūma Eliš*.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote on the Labbu fragment as a fragmentary Akkadian combat narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The classical scholarly treatment of the fragment is Mary K. Wakeman's *God's Battle with the Monster* (1973), which devotes a section of its first chapter to the Labbu material as part of the Mesopotamian combat-myth background to biblical imagery.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–26, on the Labbu fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 21-26

Wakeman uses Foster's predecessor translations and the underlying Akkadian editions as the basis for her discussion; she does not produce a new edition but works through the interpretive questions raised by the surviving lines.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–26, citing earlier published Akkadian editions in her treatment of the fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 21-26

The fragment is short: even with breaks restored, the surviving prose translation in Foster occupies only about two manuscript pages, and there are no complete columns or fully preserved tablets.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, where the entire surviving translation fits on roughly two pages with multiple internal breaks indicated.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

The chapter relies on Foster 2005 for the working English translation and on Wakeman 1973 for the interpretive scholarship; supplementary placement of the fragment within the Mesopotamian combat corpus draws on Lambert 2013 and Forsyth 1987.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582; Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–26; Lambert 2013, introduction on the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on Mesopotamian combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

=== The fragment as it survives

The fragment opens with a short cosmological setting in which the gods are troubled by some disturbance, and an appeal goes out for a champion.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, opening lines on the divine disturbance and the call for a champion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The adversary in the fragment is named Labbu, a serpent-dragon described in monstrous physical dimensions.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, on Labbu as the named adversary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The text gives Labbu a length of fifty leagues, a measurement that places the dragon on the same scale of cosmic monstrosity as Tiamat in *Enūma Eliš* but without the cosmogonic framing.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, on Labbu's measured length of fifty leagues; Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–22, on the dimensions as a marker of the dragon's cosmic scale.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The name *Labbu* itself is the Akkadian word for "lion" or, on alternative readings, "raging one," and the fragment uses it as a proper name for the dragon throughout.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote on the name Labbu; Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–22, on the lexical range of the name.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

Despite the leonine name, the creature in the surviving lines is described in serpentine terms — the text speaks of its tail and of its movement through the waters or the deep.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on Labbu's serpentine description and the references to its tail; Wakeman 1973, pp. 22–23, on the tension between the leonine name and the serpentine description.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

A council of the gods convenes in response to Labbu's threat, and the gods seek out a champion willing to face the dragon.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the divine assembly seeking a champion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

The text contains a major lacuna at the precise point where the divine name of the chosen champion appears, so that the surviving manuscript does not preserve, in legible form, the name of the god who actually goes out to fight Labbu.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, on the break at the divine name of the champion; Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the lacuna and the question of whom it concealed.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

[LACUNA: the divine name of the champion who fights Labbu is broken in the surviving manuscript and cannot be read from the tablet itself | source: Foster 2005 p. 581; Wakeman 1973 pp. 23–25 | scholarly_reconstruction: Marduk (Wakeman 1973), Tishpak, and Nergal have each been proposed; no consensus reading exists from the surviving signs alone.]

The chosen god is given counsel about how to fight the dragon, and instructions are issued involving cloud, storm, and wind as the means of attack.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the storm-and-wind instructions to the champion; Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–24, on the storm-god instrumentation of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

The champion is told to ride upon a storm-cloud and to use the winds as his weapons against Labbu, a tactical pattern recognisable from other Mesopotamian combat narratives.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the cloud-and-wind tactic; Wakeman 1973, pp. 24–25, on the storm-god tactical pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582

The combat is described in the surviving lines as conducted from the storm, with the champion striking the dragon and slaying it.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the slaying of Labbu in the surviving lines.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582

The text indicates that the dragon's blood flows for some distance after the killing — a motif of monstrous excess attested in the surviving lines.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the blood-flow motif following the slaying.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582

The fragment ends, in the surviving translation, before any extensive aftermath is preserved: there is no surviving cosmogonic conclusion of the kind found in *Enūma Eliš*, no preserved kingship-acclamation, and no clearly preserved cult-foundation passage.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the fragment's truncated end; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26, on the absence of a preserved aftermath.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582

Foster's translation indicates additional small breaks within the surviving lines beyond the central lacuna at the divine name; the fragment is broken in multiple places, not only at one point.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, where breaks are indicated by ellipses and bracketed restorations throughout.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

=== Identification of the champion

Wakeman's reading of the fragment makes the case that the slaying god, in the manuscript she works from, is to be identified as Marduk, on the basis of the storm-god typology and the parallels with *Enūma Eliš*.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the case for Marduk as the champion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

Wakeman's argument for Marduk is comparative: the cloud-and-wind instrumentation, the divine-assembly framing, and the slaying of a serpent-dragon-of-the-deep all align with the Marduk-Tiamat narrative attested elsewhere.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the structural parallels with the Marduk-Tiamat tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

An alternative reading in the scholarship identifies the slaying god as Tishpak, the patron deity of the city of Eshnunna in the Diyala region, whose mythology is otherwise sparsely attested.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, surveying the Tishpak proposal as one alternative; Lambert 2013, introduction, on the Tishpak-and-Eshnunna context for Mesopotamian combat material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

A further reading identifies the slaying god as Nergal, on the basis of Nergal's broader Akkadian profile as a god of plague, war, and underworld violence.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the Nergal proposal as a third alternative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

The three identifications are not equally weighted in the scholarship: Wakeman favours Marduk on structural grounds, while Tishpak has been preferred by readers who attach the fragment to the Eshnunna-Diyala milieu, and Nergal has been proposed on the lexical strength of his violent profile.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, surveying the three identifications and arguing for Marduk; cf. Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

[VARIANT: primary=Wakeman 1973 pp. 23–25 (Marduk preferred on storm-god structural grounds) | alt=Tishpak proposal (Eshnunna context) and Nergal proposal (violent-god profile), each surveyed in Wakeman 1973 pp. 23–25 | chosen: present-all | reason: the fragment's lacuna at the divine name precludes a secure reading; the brief instructs the chapter to present the textual situation rather than a composite, and the scholarship has no consensus.]

The chapter declines to fill the lacuna from the *Enūma Eliš* Marduk-Tiamat material, even though the structural parallels are real, on the principle that cross-composition reconstruction would manufacture a certainty that the manuscript does not support.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, presenting the break as unfilled; Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–26, on the methodological caution required for fragmentary attribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

=== Place in the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus

The Slaying of Labbu shares with *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), the Anzû Epic (Ninurta vs. Anzû), and *Enūma Eliš* (Marduk vs. Tiamat) the basic combat-myth pattern: a divine assembly, a monstrous adversary, the choice of a champion, and a victorious storm-driven combat.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote on the Labbu fragment as part of the Akkadian combat tradition; Lambert 2013, introduction on the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the Mesopotamian combat sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Labbu fragment differs from the three flagship Mesopotamian combats in that it does not, in the surviving lines, preserve a cosmogonic outcome (as *Enūma Eliš* does) or a sovereignty-token recovery (as the Anzû Epic does), so that the function of the combat in this composition cannot be securely identified.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the absence of a preserved cosmogonic or sovereignty-token aftermath; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26, on the unresolved function of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

Forsyth's *The Old Enemy* treats the Labbu fragment as one of the secondary witnesses in the Mesopotamian background of the combat myth — evidence that the storm-god-slays-dragon pattern circulated in compositions beyond the three primary epics.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus including the Labbu fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Lambert's *Babylonian Creation Myths*, the standard critical edition of *Enūma Eliš*, treats the Labbu fragment as a comparable Akkadian combat-myth witness, separate from but adjacent to the cosmogonic line that runs from the Anzû Epic into *Enūma Eliš*.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the Akkadian combat-myth corpus, including the Labbu fragment as an adjacent witness.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Labbu fragment is a reminder that the three full chapters in this book on Mesopotamian combats — Ninurta vs. Asag, Ninurta vs. Anzû, Marduk vs. Tiamat — are the best-preserved members of a wider corpus rather than the corpus's complete inventory.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote presenting Labbu among the Akkadian combat narratives; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The fragment's value, on the framing adopted in this chapter, is testimonial rather than narrative: the surviving lines are too broken to support a sustained retelling, but they are intact enough to demonstrate that the combat pattern was a productive literary form in Akkadian, generating compositions beyond the canonical three.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the fragmentary state of the surviving translation; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26, on the testimonial value of the fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 25-26
