=== Source overview

The chapter treats the Chinese hero-narrative of Hou Yi, the archer who shoots down nine of ten scorching suns and slays a sequence of monstrous beasts under the sage-king Yao, presented in the present book as a related-tier (short full) chapter rounding out the East Asian cluster by exhibiting a different Chinese combat-pattern from the cosmogonic Gonggong combat of the preceding chapter.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml on the related-tier (short full) classification and on the chapter's role of rounding out the East Asian cluster with a non-cosmogonic Chinese combat-pattern; Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China*, treatment of the Hou Yi / Yao cluster as a distinct Chinese combat-narrative tradition from the cosmogonic Gonggong material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The Hou Yi material has no single continuous primary-source witness in any early Chinese text: the narrative as it is read in modern scholarship is a composite reconstructed from several Warring States and Han-period sources that each preserve different elements of the cycle, and the chapter's textual basis is therefore composite-editorial rather than single-text-anchored, in line with the brief's variant classification.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.classification: co-equal; variants.summary on the composite-editorial character of the Hou Yi material across Chuci 'Tian wen', the Huainanzi, and the Shanhaijing; Lewis 2006, treatment of the Hou Yi / Yao cluster as a multi-source composite within early Chinese mythological literature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The earliest of the principal textual witnesses is the *Chuci* ("Songs of Chu") poem *Tian wen* ("Heavenly Questions"), conventionally dated to approximately the third century BCE and preserved in the wider *Chuci* anthology associated with the southern state of Chu, whose catechetic question-format alludes to Hou Yi and the suns within a longer sequence of cryptic questions about gods, heroes, and cosmic events.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.summary on *Chuci* 'Tian wen' as c. 3rd c. BCE textual witness for the Hou Yi material; Birrell 1993, *Chinese Mythology: An Introduction*, ch. on solar myths, on the *Tian wen*'s catechetic-question form and its place within the *Chuci* anthology associated with the southern state of Chu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The fullest single early-Chinese narrative account of the Hou Yi suns-and-beasts cycle is preserved in the *Huainanzi*, a philosophical compendium composed at the court of Liu An, king of Huainan, in the second century BCE and presenting the Hou Yi material within a wider syncretic cosmological-and-political programme that draws on earlier Warring States traditions.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.summary on the *Huainanzi* (2nd c. BCE) as the principal narrative-form textual witness for the Hou Yi cycle; Lewis 2006, treatment of the *Huainanzi* as the most narratively-developed early Chinese source for the suns-and-beasts cycle; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the *Huainanzi*'s second-century-BCE compositional context at the court of Liu An.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The *Shanhaijing* ("Classic of Mountains and Seas"), an early Chinese geographical-and-mythographic compendium of layered date (with strata reaching from the Warring States through the early Han period), supplies the principal early-Chinese witness for the catalogue of monstrous beasts that Hou Yi confronts and for the geographical placement of the beasts within a fantastical cartography of mountains, rivers, and outer-zones.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.summary on the *Shanhaijing* as the third principal textual witness for the Hou Yi cycle; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the *Shanhaijing*'s mythographic-geographical-compendium form and its layered Warring States and Han-period strata; Lewis 2006 on the *Shanhaijing*'s placement of the Hou Yi beasts within the wider Chinese mythological cartography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The three principal textual witnesses preserve different elements of the cycle with different emphases: the *Chuci* 'Tian wen' alludes to the sun-shooting in catechetic-question form without narrating the episode; the *Huainanzi* gives the narrative-form account of the ten suns and the commission to Hou Yi together with the beast-slaying sequence; and the *Shanhaijing* supplies the catalogue of monstrous adversaries and the geographical placement of the encounters, with no single source preserving the complete cycle as a connected narrative.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.summary on the differential preservation of cycle-elements across the three textual witnesses and on the absence of a single complete-narrative source; Lewis 2006, treatment of the Hou Yi cluster as a multi-source composite where the three principal witnesses preserve distinct cycle-elements; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same multi-source pattern within early Chinese mythological literature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The chapter's principal scholarly anchor is Mark Edward Lewis's *The Flood Myths of Early China*, published by SUNY Press in 2006, which treats the Hou Yi / Yao cluster as one of the early-Chinese order-restoring narrative complexes alongside the Gonggong cosmogonic combat of the preceding chapter and supplies the present chapter's working synthesis across the *Chuci*, *Huainanzi*, and *Shanhaijing* witnesses.footnote:[Lewis 2006, *The Flood Myths of Early China* (Albany: SUNY Press; ISBN 9780791466636), treatment of the Hou Yi / Yao cluster as an order-restoring narrative complex within early Chinese mythological literature; per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml sources.primary on Lewis 2006 as the principal scholarly anchor.]

=== Setting: the Yao era and the ten suns

The narrative's chronological setting in early-Chinese mytho-historical time is the era of the sage-king Yao, one of the legendary pre-dynastic sovereigns whose reign-period in the conventional Chinese mytho-chronology precedes the Xia, Shang, and Zhou dynasties and is associated in the textual tradition with the establishment of the proper ordering of human society and the regulation of the calendar, the seasons, and the cosmos.footnote:[Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the chronological placement of the Hou Yi narrative in the Yao era within the conventional Chinese mytho-chronology of pre-dynastic sage-kings; Lewis 2006 on Yao's place at the head of the legendary pre-dynastic sovereigns associated with the establishment of social, calendrical, and cosmic order.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The narrative's pre-existing cosmic order is significant for the chapter's classification within the present book's typology: Hou Yi acts within an already-ordered cosmos under an already-instituted sage-king, and the crisis the cycle addresses is a perturbation of that order rather than the absence of it — the suns-crisis and the monster-beasts crisis are disturbances within an existing system that need to be removed, not gaps in a not-yet-established system that need to be filled.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml special_instructions on the chapter's order-restoring rather than cosmogonic framing; Lewis 2006 on the pre-existing-cosmic-order setting of the Yao-era narratives within early Chinese mythology; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same pre-existing-order setting.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The narrative's solar premise is that there are ten suns rather than one: the early Chinese textual tradition preserves a mythological cosmology in which ten suns, one for each day of a ten-day week, normally rise in succession from a mulberry tree (the Fusang tree) at the eastern edge of the world, with one sun emerging each day and the other nine remaining in the tree, and the suns' regular alternation supplies the daily and weekly rhythm of cosmic time.footnote:[Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the ten-suns mythological cosmology, the Fusang mulberry tree at the eastern edge of the world, and the regular daily alternation of the suns within the early Chinese textual tradition; Lewis 2006 on the same ten-suns cosmology as the narrative premise of the Hou Yi cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The ten suns are presented in some strata of the early Chinese textual tradition as the children of the high god Di Jun and his consort Xihe, the sun-mother who bathes the suns at the eastern edge of the world before each one's daily rising, with the divine parentage placing the suns within a wider mythological pantheon of cosmic deities and legitimating their place in the regular celestial order.footnote:[Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the divine parentage of the ten suns as children of Di Jun and Xihe within the early Chinese textual tradition and on Xihe's role as sun-mother bathing the suns at the eastern edge of the world; Lewis 2006 on the same divine-parentage tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The narrative's precipitating crisis is that the ten suns, contrary to the regular daily alternation, on a single occasion rise together: all ten leave the Fusang tree at once and appear simultaneously in the sky, and the resulting heat scorches the earth, dries the rivers, withers the crops, and exposes both the natural environment and human society to a catastrophic disturbance of the proper cosmic order.footnote:[Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the precipitating crisis in which the ten suns rise together against the regular alternation, with the consequent scorching of the earth, the drying of rivers, and the withering of crops within the early Chinese textual tradition; Lewis 2006 on the same precipitating crisis within the Hou Yi narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The crisis's character is environmental-and-political rather than cosmogonic-and-foundational: the existing world is at risk of destruction by overheating rather than not-yet-existing-and-needing-to-be-formed, and the political order under Yao is at risk of collapse because the climatic perturbation makes human existence within the king's domain unsustainable, with the framing aligning the Hou Yi cycle with the present book's "order under threat" subtype rather than with the cosmogonic-combat subtype.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml on the cosmological function of the Hou Yi cycle as solar-plurality rectification and environmental restoration; Lewis 2006 on the environmental-and-political character of the suns-crisis within early Chinese narratives of Yao-era order-restoration; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same environmental-political framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

=== Hou Yi and the shooting of the suns

The figure of Hou Yi is introduced into the narrative as a divine archer of unparalleled skill: in the strata of the textual tradition that preserve his celestial origin, Hou Yi is sent down from the heavens by the high god Di Jun (the father of the ten suns themselves) to deal with the crisis caused by his own children, and the commission places the archer in the position of a divine agent whose authority for the killing comes from the heavens rather than from the human sage-king alone.footnote:[Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on Hou Yi's celestial origin and on his commission by Di Jun to address the suns-crisis caused by Di Jun's own children within the early Chinese textual tradition; Lewis 2006 on the divine-agent framing of Hou Yi within the Yao-era narratives.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The archer's principal attribute is his bow, with which he is equipped — in the textual strata that supply this detail — by Di Jun himself at the moment of his commission, with red corded arrows in some accounts; the bow-and-arrows are the narrative's load-bearing combat-implement and the archery-as-method is what defines the cycle's characteristic combat-pattern as distinct from the close-combat patterns of other order-restoring narratives in the present book.footnote:[Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on Hou Yi's bow as the load-bearing combat-implement of the cycle and on the red corded arrows tradition within the early Chinese textual material; Lewis 2006 on the archery-as-method that defines the Hou Yi cycle's combat-pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=birrell-1993 ; loc=ch. on solar myths

The act of shooting the suns is presented in the *Huainanzi* and the wider tradition as a sequential drawing-and-loosing of the bow against the simultaneously-risen suns: Hou Yi shoots one sun, then the next, then the next, with each arrow striking its mark and bringing down a sun in turn, until nine of the ten suns have been shot and only one remains in the sky.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the sequential sun-shooting sequence in the *Huainanzi* and the wider tradition with nine of the ten suns brought down in turn; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same sequential pattern of the suns-shooting within the early Chinese textual material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The narrative's nine-of-ten outcome is significant for the cycle's classification within the present book's combat-typology: Hou Yi is not eliminating the suns as a class — the sun as a celestial body remains in the sky and continues to provide the daily light and warmth that the cosmos requires — but is correcting an excess, restoring the proper one-sun-at-a-time alternation that the Fusang-tree cosmology presupposes, with the killing-arithmetic itself encoding the order-restoration logic of the cycle.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the order-restoration logic encoded in the nine-of-ten killing-arithmetic of the Hou Yi cycle and on the contrast with class-elimination patterns in other combat-narratives; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the post-shooting cosmos in which the surviving single sun continues the regular daily cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The killing of the nine suns immediately ends the scorching crisis: with only one sun remaining in the sky, the proper daily alternation is in principle restored, the temperature returns to a survivable level, the rivers begin to recover, and the human order under Yao is preserved from collapse, with the archery-act functioning as a single decisive intervention that resolves the environmental-and-political emergency.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the resolution of the scorching crisis through the killing of the nine suns and the restoration of the proper daily alternation within the early Chinese narrative tradition; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the post-shooting recovery of the environmental and human order under Yao.]

=== The monstrous beasts

The Hou Yi cycle's second narrative component is the sequence of monstrous-beast slayings that the archer carries out either after or in parallel with the killing of the suns, with the textual tradition presenting the beast-slayings as a series of further order-restoring acts that address other disturbances of the proper cosmic and human order beyond the suns-crisis itself.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the monstrous-beast sequence as the second narrative component of the Hou Yi cycle and on its order-restoring function within the early Chinese narrative tradition; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the relation between the suns-episode and the beast-slayings within the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The principal early-Chinese textual witnesses do not preserve a single fixed list of the monstrous beasts: different sources name different combinations, with the *Huainanzi* preserving the most narratively-developed enumeration and the *Shanhaijing* supplying the geographical placement and the iconographic descriptions of the individual beasts within its wider mythographic-cartographical compendium; the present chapter accordingly follows Lewis 2006's synthesis of the beast-catalogue across the witnesses.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the synthesis of the monstrous-beast catalogue across the early-Chinese textual witnesses with the *Huainanzi* as the principal narrative-form source and the *Shanhaijing* as the principal geographical-and-iconographic source; per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.summary on the variation in the nine-beasts subnarrative across the textual witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

[VARIANT: primary=Lewis 2006's synthesis of the *Huainanzi*'s narratively-developed beast-enumeration and the *Shanhaijing*'s geographical-and-iconographic catalogue of the beasts associated with Hou Yi | alt=individual textual witnesses preserve different combinations of the beasts with differing names and differing geographical placements; no single source preserves the same complete list as another | chosen: present-the-Lewis-synthesis-skeleton | reason: per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml variants.classification: co-equal with Lewis 2006 as the scholarly synthesis across the *Chuci*, *Huainanzi*, and *Shanhaijing* witnesses; per brief variants.summary on the textual variation in the nine-beasts subnarrative.]

One of the named beasts in the cycle is the Chiseltooth (Chinese *Zaochi*), a tusked monstrous figure whose name in the early-Chinese tradition refers to a chisel-like protruding tooth and who is presented as one of the order-disturbing beasts that Hou Yi confronts and kills in the course of the wider beast-slaying sequence.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the Chiseltooth (Chinese *Zaochi*) as one of the named monstrous beasts that Hou Yi confronts and kills in the early-Chinese narrative tradition; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same Chiseltooth figure with the chisel-like-tooth iconography that gives the figure its name.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

A second of the named beasts is the Nine-headed Monster (Chinese *Jiuying*), a multi-headed creature whose multi-headedness is the iconographic marker of its monstrous status within the early-Chinese mythological tradition and whose killing by Hou Yi forms one of the most structurally-significant of the beast-slayings for the cycle's chaoskampf-comparative reading.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the Nine-headed Monster (Chinese *Jiuying*) as one of the named monstrous beasts that Hou Yi confronts and kills, with the multi-headedness as the iconographic marker of monstrous status; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same multi-headed-monster figure within the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

A third of the named beasts is the Long Serpent (Chinese *Xiushe* 修蛇, sometimes also referred to as *Bashe* 巴蛇 in different strata of the textual tradition), a giant serpentine figure whose serpentine morphology and large size place it within the cross-tradition family of monstrous serpents that hero-figures across many of the present book's chapters confront; in the *Huainanzi* ch. 8 (*Benjing xun*) beast-catalogue Hou Yi kills the Long Serpent at Dongting Lake, and the early-Chinese material's treatment of the killing is heroic-combat-pattern rather than cosmogonic-combat-pattern.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on Xiushe (the Long Serpent, with *Bashe* as an alternative name in different strata of the textual tradition) as the serpentine adversary among the named monstrous beasts that Hou Yi confronts and kills, with the *Huainanzi* ch. 8 (*Benjing xun*) locating the killing at Dongting Lake; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same giant-serpent figure within the cycle and on the heroic-combat-pattern character of the killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The early-Chinese tradition's wider beast-catalogue for the Hou Yi cycle includes additional figures named in the *Huainanzi* and in the *Shanhaijing* — among them a wind-causing giant bird, a giant boar, and the figure named *Yayu* — with the precise combination of beasts varying across the textual witnesses and the present chapter naming the Chiseltooth, the Nine-headed Monster, and the Long Serpent (*Xiushe*) as the three brief-named representatives of the wider sequence.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the wider beast-catalogue across the *Huainanzi* and the *Shanhaijing* including the wind-causing giant bird, the giant boar, and the figure named *Yayu* as additional members of the sequence; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same wider beast-catalogue and on its variation across the textual witnesses; per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml summary on the three brief-named representatives (Chiseltooth, Nine-headed Monster, Long Serpent / *Xiushe* — substituted from the brief's "Zhuyin serpent" framing per FC-01, since Zhuyin / Zhulong is a Shanhaijing cosmic deity rather than a Hou Yi adversary, and Xiushe is the attested Hou Yi serpent-adversary in the *Huainanzi* ch. 8 catalogue).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

The geographical placement of the beast-encounters in the *Shanhaijing* mythographic-cartographical apparatus locates each killing in a specific named region of the fantastical early-Chinese cartography, with the encounters distributed across the four directional zones of the world and the killings together constituting a comprehensive sweep across the geography of the inhabited cosmos that complements the celestial sweep of the suns-shooting in the same cycle.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the *Shanhaijing*'s geographical placement of the beast-encounters across the four directional zones of the early-Chinese mythological cartography and on the geographical-comprehensiveness pattern that the placement encodes; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same geographical-distribution pattern of the encounters.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lewis-2006-flood ; loc=Hou Yi / Yao cluster

[INFERENCE: the chapter's reading that the celestial sweep of the suns-shooting and the geographical sweep of the beast-killings together constitute a comprehensive cosmic-and-terrestrial order-restoration pattern is an editorial synthesis of Lewis 2006's treatment of the cycle rather than a single source-attested formulation; Lewis 2006 surveys the suns-episode and the beast-sequence as the two narrative components of the cycle without supplying this exact celestial-and-terrestrial-comprehensive-sweep phrasing, and the synthesis is offered for transparency. | basis: Lewis 2006 treats the suns-episode and the beast-sequence as both order-restoring components of a single Hou Yi cycle, and the *Shanhaijing*'s geographical apparatus places the beast-encounters across the directional zones of the cartography. | risk: alternative readings are possible — a stronger reading would treat the two components as more loosely connected episodes within a wider Yao-era narrative-cluster; the chapter's celestial-plus-terrestrial reading is a reasonable but not the only available synthesis.]

The killing-method varies across the beast-slayings: in some encounters Hou Yi uses his bow and arrows in the same archery-pattern as in the suns-shooting; in others he uses close-combat weapons; the variation in killing-method across the beast-sequence distinguishes the Hou Yi cycle from sequences in other traditions in which the hero uses a single signature weapon throughout, although the bow-and-arrows remain the cycle's iconic implement.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the variation in killing-method across the Hou Yi beast-sequence with archery and close-combat patterns both attested in the early-Chinese textual witnesses; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the bow-and-arrows as the cycle's iconic implement despite the variation in method across individual beast-encounters.]

The narrative outcome of the beast-sequence is the comprehensive removal of the named monstrous adversaries from the inhabited zones of the Yao-era cosmos: with the beasts killed and the suns reduced to one, the environmental-and-political crisis that the cycle had presented as its precipitating problem is fully resolved, and the proper Yao-era order is restored across both the celestial and the terrestrial dimensions of the world.footnote:[Lewis 2006 on the comprehensive resolution of the environmental-and-political crisis through the combined suns-shooting and beast-slaying sequences within the Hou Yi cycle; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the post-cycle restoration of the proper Yao-era order.]

=== Aftermath: the relation between the components and Hou Yi's later fate

The relation between the suns-episode and the beast-sequence is one of co-occurrence rather than strict dependence within the early-Chinese textual tradition: the two components are routinely presented together in the principal witnesses (most prominently in the *Huainanzi*'s narrative account) but are also distinguishable as separate order-restoring acts, with each component being independently capable of being treated as a free-standing episode within the wider Yao-era narrative-cluster.footnote:[Per brief 22-hou-yi.yaml special_instructions on the chapter's task of presenting both components and noting their relation; Lewis 2006 on the co-occurrence-rather-than-strict-dependence relation between the suns-episode and the beast-sequence in the *Huainanzi* and the wider tradition; Birrell 1993, ch. on solar myths, on the same distinguishable-but-co-occurring relation.]
