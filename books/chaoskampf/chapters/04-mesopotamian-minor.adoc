== Minor Mesopotamian Combats (Marduk/Labbu)

=== Source overview

The text at the centre of this chapter is a single fragmentary cuneiform tablet in Akkadian. Benjamin Foster prints the working English translation in *Before the Muses* under the title "The Lion-Serpent," grouping it with the "Monster Serpents," and that translation runs to about two manuscript pages.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, "The Lion-Serpent" (full translation); Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365, on the same fragment under the title "The Slaying of Labbu".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582
W. G. Lambert, in his *Babylonian Creation Myths*, refers to the same composition as "The Slaying of Labbu." Foster's headnote treats the tablet as a fragmentary myth and places it inside the Akkadian combat-myth corpus, alongside the Anzû Epic and *Enūma Eliš*.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote on the Labbu fragment as a fragmentary Akkadian combat narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The classical interpretive treatment is Mary K. Wakeman's *God's Battle with the Monster* (1973), which devotes a section of its first chapter to Labbu within a wider survey of the Mesopotamian combat material that lies behind biblical imagery.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–26, on the Labbu fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 21-26
Wakeman works from previously published Akkadian editions and from the predecessor English translations that Foster later refined; she does not produce a fresh edition, but she walks through the interpretive questions raised by the surviving lines.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–26, citing earlier published Akkadian editions in her treatment of the fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 21-26

The fragment is short. Even with breaks restored on the page, Foster's translation occupies only about two pages, and there are no complete columns or fully preserved tablets behind it.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, where the entire surviving translation fits on roughly two pages with multiple internal breaks indicated.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582
For the working English the chapter relies on Foster 2005; for interpretive scholarship it relies on Wakeman 1973; and for the placement of the fragment within the wider Akkadian combat corpus it draws on Lambert 2013 and Forsyth 1987.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582; Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–26; Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365 on the Labbu fragment within the Akkadian combat corpus; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on Mesopotamian combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

The tablet's most consequential feature, for the reader's purposes, is a break at the very point where the divine name of the slaying god would have appeared. There is, in consequence, no scholarly consensus on which god kills the dragon. The chapter handles that uncertainty straightforwardly rather than papering over it.

=== The fragment as it survives

The opening lines set a short cosmological scene. The gods are troubled by some disturbance, the nature of which the surviving tablet does not fully specify, and an appeal goes out across the divine assembly for a champion willing to act.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, opening lines on the divine disturbance and the call for a champion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581
The adversary is named almost at once. He is Labbu, a serpent-dragon described in monstrous physical dimensions.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, on Labbu as the named adversary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The text gives Labbu a measurement that places him on a scale of cosmic monstrosity comparable to Tiamat in *Enūma Eliš*, though without the cosmogonic framing. The surviving witnesses give his length variously: fifty leagues in the manuscripts that read his species as _bašmu_, sixty leagues in those that read it as _mušḫuššu_.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, on Labbu's measurement; Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–22, on the dimensions as a marker of the dragon's cosmic scale and on the witness-dependent length.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581
The name Labbu itself is the Akkadian word for "lion," with an alternative reading of "raging one"; the text uses it as a proper name for the dragon throughout.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote on the name Labbu; Wakeman 1973, pp. 21–22, on the lexical range of the name.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

The leonine name does not match the body. In the surviving lines the creature is described in serpentine terms — the text speaks of his tail and of his movement through the waters or the deep — and the tension between the name and the description is one of the things later scholarship has tried to account for.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on Labbu's serpentine description and the references to its tail; Wakeman 1973, pp. 22–23, on the tension between the leonine name and the serpentine description.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

A council of the gods convenes in response to Labbu's threat, and the assembly seeks out a champion willing to face the dragon.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the divine assembly seeking a champion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582
At precisely this point — where one of the gods steps forward, or is named by the assembly, as the chosen champion — the manuscript breaks. The signs that would have given the name are not legible. The surviving tablet does not preserve, in any readable form, the identity of the god who actually goes out to fight Labbu.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, on the break at the divine name of the champion; Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the lacuna and the question of whom it concealed.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581

[LACUNA: the divine name of the champion who fights Labbu is broken in the surviving manuscript and cannot be read from the tablet itself | source: Foster 2005 p. 581; Wakeman 1973 pp. 23–25 | scholarly_reconstruction: Tishpak (the patron deity of Eshnunna, in the Neo-Assyrian witness) and Nergal (in earlier reconstructions) have been the main proposals; Marduk has also been suggested as a structural parallel, since the storm-god typology and the divine-assembly framing align with the Marduk-Tiamat material; no consensus reading is recoverable from the surviving signs alone.]

What survives, after the lacuna, are the instructions. The chosen god, whoever he is, is given counsel about how to fight the dragon, and the means of attack are specified: cloud, storm, and wind.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the storm-and-wind instructions to the champion; Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–24, on the storm-god instrumentation of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582
He is told to ride upon a storm-cloud and to use the winds as his weapons against Labbu, a tactical pattern that the reader has already met in other Mesopotamian combat narratives.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the cloud-and-wind tactic; Wakeman 1973, pp. 24–25, on the storm-god tactical pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582

The combat itself is described, in the surviving lines, as conducted from the storm. The champion strikes the dragon and slays him.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the slaying of Labbu in the surviving lines.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582
The text then gives the dragon's bleeding a temporal duration: three years, three months, one day, and one night. The figure is a motif of monstrous excess, attested in the surviving lines.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the duration of the blood-flow following the slaying.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582

After this, the fragment runs out. In the surviving translation no extensive aftermath is preserved. There is no surviving cosmogonic conclusion of the kind found in *Enūma Eliš*, no preserved kingship-acclamation for the victorious god, and no clearly preserved passage establishing a cult.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 582, on the fragment's truncated end; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26, on the absence of a preserved aftermath.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 582
The central lacuna at the divine name is not the only break, either. Foster's translation indicates additional small breaks throughout the surviving lines, marked with ellipses and bracketed restorations. The fragment is broken in multiple places, not only at one.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, where breaks are indicated by ellipses and bracketed restorations throughout.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

=== Identification of the champion

Three candidates have been put forward in the scholarship for the god whose name the manuscript no longer preserves. They are not equally weighted, and the order in which they are sometimes mentioned in older surveys can mislead.

Marduk has been proposed as a structural parallel for the slaying god, on the basis of the storm-god typology and the parallels with *Enūma Eliš*.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, surveying the proposed identifications including the Marduk parallel.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25
The case for Marduk is comparative rather than textual. The cloud-and-wind instrumentation, the divine-assembly framing, and the slaying of a serpent-dragon-of-the-deep all align with the Marduk-Tiamat narrative attested elsewhere, and a reader who knows *Enūma Eliš* will recognise the family resemblance at every step.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the structural parallels with the Marduk-Tiamat tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

The most strongly supported reading in the recent scholarship is different. It identifies the slaying god as Tishpak, the patron deity of the city of Eshnunna in the Diyala region, and it does so particularly with reference to the Neo-Assyrian witness, where the surrounding context fits Tishpak's profile.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, surveying the Tishpak proposal; Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365, on the Tishpak-and-Eshnunna context for Mesopotamian combat material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25
A third proposal identifies the slaying god as Nergal, on the strength of Nergal's broader Akkadian profile as a god of plague, war, and underworld violence — a profile that fits a story whose adversary leaves a three-year flow of blood.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, on the Nergal proposal as a third alternative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

These three identifications are not equally supported. Tishpak is the leading candidate in the Neo-Assyrian recension on contextual grounds. Nergal has been proposed in earlier reconstructions on the strength of his violent profile. Marduk has been suggested as a structural parallel rather than as a textually-grounded reading.footnote:[Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–25, surveying the three identifications; Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 23-25

[VARIANT: primary=Tishpak (the Neo-Assyrian-context reading, supported by Wakeman 1973 pp. 23–25 and the Eshnunna milieu argument in Lambert 2013) | alt=Nergal (proposed in earlier reconstructions on the violent-god profile) and Marduk (proposed as a structural parallel with Enūma Eliš) | chosen: present-all | reason: the fragment's lacuna at the divine name precludes a secure reading; the brief instructs the chapter to present the textual situation rather than a composite, and the scholarship has no consensus.]

The chapter declines to fill the lacuna by importing the Marduk-Tiamat material from *Enūma Eliš*, even though the structural parallels are real. The reason is methodological. To borrow a divine name from one composition in order to plug a hole in another would manufacture a certainty that the manuscript itself does not support.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, presenting the break as unfilled; Wakeman 1973, pp. 23–26, on the methodological caution required for fragmentary attribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581
What can be said with confidence is that the slayer was a storm-using champion of the divine assembly. What cannot be said is his name.

=== Place in the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus

The Slaying of Labbu shares with three better-preserved compositions — *Lugal-e* (Ninurta vs. Asag), the Anzû Epic (Ninurta vs. Anzû), and *Enūma Eliš* (Marduk vs. Tiamat) — the basic combat-myth pattern: a divine assembly, a monstrous adversary, the choice of a champion, and a victorious storm-driven combat.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote on the Labbu fragment as part of the Akkadian combat tradition; Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365 on the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the Mesopotamian combat sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

It also differs from those three in an important respect. In the surviving lines, Labbu does not preserve a cosmogonic outcome of the kind that closes *Enūma Eliš*, and it does not preserve the recovery of a sovereignty-token of the kind that closes the Anzû Epic. Because the aftermath is lost, the function of the combat in this composition cannot be securely identified.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the absence of a preserved cosmogonic or sovereignty-token aftermath; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26, on the unresolved function of the combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 581-582

Forsyth's *The Old Enemy* treats the Labbu fragment as one of the secondary witnesses in the Mesopotamian background of the combat myth — evidence that the storm-god-slays-dragon pattern circulated in compositions beyond the three primary epics.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the Mesopotamian combat-myth corpus including the Labbu fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2
Lambert's *Babylonian Creation Myths*, the standard critical edition of *Enūma Eliš*, treats the Labbu fragment as a comparable Akkadian combat-myth witness — separate from, but adjacent to, the cosmogonic line that runs from the Anzû Epic into *Enūma Eliš*.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365 in Part III on the Akkadian combat-myth corpus, treating the Labbu fragment as an adjacent witness.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

This is why the chapter exists. The Labbu fragment is a reminder that the three full chapters in this book on Mesopotamian combats — Ninurta vs. Asag, Ninurta vs. Anzû, Marduk vs. Tiamat — are the best-preserved members of a wider corpus rather than the corpus's complete inventory.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 581, headnote presenting Labbu among the Akkadian combat narratives; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26; Lambert 2013, pp. 361–365.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 581
The fragment's value, on the framing adopted here, is testimonial rather than narrative. The surviving lines are too broken to support a sustained retelling. They are intact enough to demonstrate that the combat pattern was a productive literary form in Akkadian, generating compositions beyond the canonical three.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 581–582, on the fragmentary state of the surviving translation; Wakeman 1973, pp. 25–26, on the testimonial value of the fragment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=wakeman-1973 ; loc=pp. 25-26

That, in the end, is the point of the Labbu chapter. The composition existed; it was copied; it was read; and a god whose name we cannot now recover defeated a dragon fifty leagues long with the help of a storm. The rest is broken tablet.
