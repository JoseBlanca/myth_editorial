=== Source overview

This chapter treats the Mapuche cosmogonic narrative in which two serpent-beings — Kai-Kai, who lives in the sea, and Tren-Tren, who lives on the mountain — raise water and earth against one another, with the resulting flood-and-mountain rivalry generating the elevated southern-cone landscape that the Mapuche inhabit and saving the ancestors of the Mapuche people from extinction in the sea.footnote:[Per brief 27-mapuche-kai-tren.yaml summary on the marine serpent Kai-Kai raising the sea to drown the earth, on the mountain serpent Tren-Tren raising the mountains in response to save the Mapuche, and on the two-serpent cosmogony preserving a flood-and-height memory; Koessler-Ilg 1962, *Cuentan los araucanos: Mitos, leyendas y tradiciones* (Buenos Aires: Editorial Mar Océano, 1962; OCLC 4124869), sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony as the principal Spanish-language narrative anchor for the cycle.]

The narrative survives in oral tradition rather than in any pre-conquest written record: the Mapuche of present-day south-central Chile and southern-cone Argentina did not develop an alphabetic writing system before contact with Spanish-speaking colonisers, and the principal record of the Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle is the body of nineteenth- and twentieth-century ethnographic compilations recorded from Mapudungun-speaking informants by Spanish- and German-speaking ethnographers and folklorists working in Chile and Argentina from approximately the 1860s onward.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, introductory matter on the oral-tradition character of the Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle and on the body of Mapuche oral narrative she records from Mapudungun-speaking informants in the 1920s-30s; Foerster 1993, *Introducción a la religiosidad mapuche* (Santiago: Editorial Universitaria, 1993; ISBN 9789561109315), introductory matter on the textual situation of Mapuche cosmogonic narrative and on the body of nineteenth- and twentieth-century ethnographic compilations.]

=== The Mapuche cosmographic frame: Wenu Mapu, Naq Mapu, Minche Mapu

The narrative's geographical and cultural setting is the Mapuche homeland of present-day south-central Chile (between the Bío-Bío and Toltén rivers, with adjacent areas to the north and south) and southern-cone Argentina (the Andean foothills of Neuquén, Río Negro, and Chubut), a temperate-rainforest landscape of forested mountain slopes, intermontane valleys, lakes, and a long Pacific coastline, with the cycle rooted in specific named landscape features within this geography that figure in the narrative as the bodies and the sites of the two named serpent-beings.footnote:[Foerster 1993, introductory matter on the Mapuche homeland of south-central Chile and southern-cone Argentina, on the temperate-rainforest landscape of forested mountain slopes, intermontane valleys, lakes, and a long Pacific coastline, and on the rooting of Mapuche cosmogonic narrative in specific named landscape features that figure as the bodies and the sites of the named serpent-beings; Koessler-Ilg 1962 on the regional geography of the cycle's narrative settings.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foerster-religiosidad-mapuche ; loc=Introducción a la religiosidad mapuche, on the Wenu / Naq / Minche Mapu cosmographic system and ngillatun ritual

The cycle's principal cosmographic framework is the Mapuche vertical-cosmographic system in which the world is layered into the *Wenu Mapu* (the upper land, sky, and the dwelling of *ngen* and beneficent powers), the *Naq Mapu* (the middle land, the surface of the earth where humans live), and the *Minche Mapu* (the under-land, the realm of the *wekufü* and other adversarial powers), with Kai-Kai and Tren-Tren operating across the boundary between *Naq Mapu* and the surrounding water-and-mountain registers rather than as figures of a primordial chaos preceding cosmos.footnote:[Foerster 1993, on the Mapuche vertical-cosmographic system of *Wenu Mapu* (upper land), *Naq Mapu* (middle land, the surface where humans live), and *Minche Mapu* (under-land), on the *ngen* (place-spirits / owners) of the upper land and the *wekufü* (adversarial powers) of the under-land; Course 2011, on the cosmological framework underpinning Mapuche ritual practice and the personhood-cosmography integration; per glossary 'Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren' first_mention_gloss on the paired Mapuche marine and mountain serpents.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foerster-religiosidad-mapuche ; loc=Introducción a la religiosidad mapuche, on the Wenu / Naq / Minche Mapu cosmographic system and ngillatun ritual

Kai-Kai (also rendered Kaikai-filu in some twentieth-century compilations, with *filu* the Mapudungun word for serpent) is in the cycle the marine serpent of the deep ocean, associated with the destructive power of the sea — tidal flooding, cataclysmic wave-action, the rising of waters that threatens to overwhelm the land — and is presented in the narrative not as an unmotivated primordial chaos but as a named serpent-being acting within an already-existing Mapuche cosmographic order, with Kai-Kai's flood-action being the cycle's principal cosmogonic-cataclysm rather than a chaos-versus-cosmos opening.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on Kai-Kai (also Kaikai-filu) as the marine serpent of the deep ocean, on the destructive power of the sea associated with the figure (tidal flooding, cataclysmic wave-action, the rising of waters), and on Kai-Kai as a named serpent-being within an already-existing Mapuche cosmographic order rather than as an unmotivated primordial chaos; Foerster 1993 on the cosmogonic-cataclysm reading of the Kai-Kai flood-action.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=koessler-ilg-cuentan ; loc=Cuentan los araucanos, sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony

Tren-Tren (also rendered Trentren-filu in some compilations) is in the cycle the mountain serpent of the high land, associated with the protective and uplifting power of the earth — the rising of mountains, the sheltering of the Mapuche on high ground, the provision of refuge from the rising sea — and is presented as the protective counterpart to Kai-Kai rather than as a hero who slays Kai-Kai, with the resolution of the cycle being a mutual-rising stalemate rather than a kill-and-eradicate combat-outcome.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on Tren-Tren (also Trentren-filu) as the mountain serpent of the high land, on the protective and uplifting power of the earth associated with the figure (the rising of mountains, the sheltering of the Mapuche on high ground), and on Tren-Tren's role as the protective counterpart to Kai-Kai rather than as a hero who slays Kai-Kai; Foerster 1993 on the mutual-rising stalemate as the cycle's resolution rather than a kill-and-eradicate combat-outcome.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=koessler-ilg-cuentan ; loc=Cuentan los araucanos, sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony

=== The flood: Kai-Kai raises the sea

The cycle's narrative crisis begins with Kai-Kai's raising of the sea: the marine serpent rises from the ocean depths and causes the waters to swell, with tidal floods overwhelming the coastal lowlands and rising progressively up the river valleys and onto the inland plains, threatening to drown all of the Naq Mapu and to extinguish the human and animal populations of the Mapuche homeland.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on the cycle's narrative opening with Kai-Kai's raising of the sea, on the marine serpent rising from the ocean depths and causing the waters to swell, on tidal floods overwhelming the coastal lowlands and rising progressively up river valleys and onto inland plains, and on the threat to drown all of the *Naq Mapu* and extinguish the human and animal populations of the Mapuche homeland.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=koessler-ilg-cuentan ; loc=Cuentan los araucanos, sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony

The flood's narrative function within the cycle is cosmogonic-cataclysm rather than cosmogonic-establishment-from-nothing: the Mapuche world is already populated and ordered before Kai-Kai's flood begins, and the flood is a near-extinction crisis within an existing cosmos rather than the cosmos's first ordering, with the cycle's load-bearing narrative work being the preservation and reconfiguration of an already-existing world rather than the establishment of cosmos out of chaos.footnote:[Foerster 1993, on the cosmogonic-cataclysm character of the Kai-Kai flood as a near-extinction crisis within an already-existing Mapuche cosmos rather than as cosmogonic establishment from nothing, and on the cycle's load-bearing narrative work as the preservation and reconfiguration of an already-existing world; Course 2011 on the cosmological framework underpinning the cycle's interpretation as cataclysm-within-cosmos rather than cosmos-from-chaos.]

=== The mountain rising: Tren-Tren raises the land

The cycle's narrative resolution begins with Tren-Tren's response: as Kai-Kai's waters rise from the sea, the mountain serpent rises from beneath the land and causes the mountains to lift, with high ground rising progressively higher to outpace the rising waters and the Mapuche people fleeing upslope onto the rising peaks where Tren-Tren's body provides shelter and refuge from the sea.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on Tren-Tren's response with the mountain serpent rising from beneath the land, on the mountains lifting progressively higher to outpace the rising waters, on the Mapuche people fleeing upslope onto the rising peaks, and on Tren-Tren's body providing shelter and refuge from the sea; Faron 1964 on the protective-mountain-serpent figure as a structural feature of the Mapuche cosmogonic narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=koessler-ilg-cuentan ; loc=Cuentan los araucanos, sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony

The two serpents' opposing motions — Kai-Kai pushing the waters up, Tren-Tren pushing the mountains up — produce the cycle's principal combat-image: a vertical race between sea and mountain in which neither serpent kills or is killed, with the flood-and-height engagement being a sustained mutual-rising rather than a single decisive blow, and with the Mapuche on the rising peaks being the narrative's centre of attention as the cycle's beneficiary-survivors of a combat between two non-human powers.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on the two serpents' opposing motions as the cycle's principal combat-image, on the vertical race between sea and mountain as a sustained mutual-rising rather than a single decisive blow, and on the Mapuche on the rising peaks as the cycle's beneficiary-survivors of a combat between two non-human powers; Bacigalupo 2007 on the two-serpent structural pattern as distinctive to Mapuche cosmogonic material relative to the hero-versus-monster pattern of much Eurasian combat-narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=koessler-ilg-cuentan ; loc=Cuentan los araucanos, sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony

The cycle's outcome is not the killing of Kai-Kai by Tren-Tren or the killing of Tren-Tren by Kai-Kai but a stable equilibrium: the waters subside, the mountains remain elevated, and the Mapuche descend from the high peaks onto the now-permanently-elevated and rugged landscape that the rising-mountain action has produced — a landscape-aetiological resolution in which the southern-cone Andean topography is itself the cycle's load-bearing trace and the Mapuche territorial occupation of that elevated terrain is the cycle's narrative point.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on the cycle's outcome as stable equilibrium rather than killing — waters subside, mountains remain elevated, the Mapuche descend onto the permanently-elevated and rugged landscape — and on the southern-cone Andean topography as the cycle's load-bearing landscape-aetiological trace; Foerster 1993 on the landscape-aetiological resolution and on the Mapuche territorial occupation of the elevated terrain as the cycle's narrative point.]

=== Regional variants: Nguluche, Huilliche, Pehuenche

The cycle is attested across the principal regional Mapuche groupings — Nguluche of the central Mapuche heartland between the Bío-Bío and the Toltén; Huilliche of the southern Mapuche territories south of the Toltén into the Chiloé archipelago; and Pehuenche of the Andean cordillera and adjacent eastern slopes — with the common narrative core (Kai-Kai's marine flood; Tren-Tren's mountain rising; the Mapuche refuge on the rising peaks; the landscape-aetiological resolution) shared across the regional variants, and with regional-specific narrative elements (specific named mountain refuges; particular animal companions of the human survivors; details of Kai-Kai's defeat or pacification) varying among the groupings.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962, sections on the cycle's attestation across the principal Mapuche regional groupings — Nguluche of the central Mapuche heartland, Huilliche of the southern territories and Chiloé, Pehuenche of the Andean cordillera and eastern slopes — and on the shared common narrative core (Kai-Kai's marine flood, Tren-Tren's mountain rising, the Mapuche refuge on the rising peaks, the landscape-aetiological resolution); per brief 27-mapuche-kai-tren.yaml variants entry on the co-equal regional variation across Nguluche, Huilliche, and Pehuenche.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=koessler-ilg-cuentan ; loc=Cuentan los araucanos, sections on Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren and the flood-and-height cosmogony

The regional-specific variants exhibit characteristic localised performance-anchoring rather than disagreement about overall narrative structure: Huilliche material identifies the named refuge-mountain with specific peaks of the southern Andean cordillera and integrates the cycle with further Huilliche-specific cosmogonic-and-aetiological material on the local landscape, while Pehuenche material (recorded on both Argentine and Chilean sides of the cordillera) integrates the cycle with the Pehuenche cultural-and-ritual association with the *pehuén* (Araucaria araucana / monkey-puzzle tree), whose seeds are the staple food of the Pehuenche economy, with some recordings tying the post-flood landscape's establishment to the founding of the *pehuén* groves on the rising mountains.footnote:[Koessler-Ilg 1962 on Huilliche-specific variants identifying the refuge-mountain with specific peaks of the southern cordillera, and on Pehuenche-specific recordings tying the post-flood landscape's establishment to the founding of *pehuén* (Araucaria araucana) groves on the rising mountains; Foerster 1993 on the regional-specific identifications as localised performance-anchoring; Course 2011 on the Pehuenche subsistence base and the integration of cosmogonic narrative with subsistence-aetiological material.]

=== Ngillatun and the cycle's ritual context

The Mapuche cosmogonic material does not function in isolation from ritual but is embedded in the *ngillatun* communal ritual — a multi-day public ceremonial gathering held at named ritual sites (the *nguillatuwe*) on a calendrical schedule, presided over by the *machi* (the Mapuche shaman, traditionally though not exclusively a woman) and incorporating prayers, sacrificial offerings, dances, and ritualised speech directed to the *ngen* of the upper land — within which the cosmogonic narratives are performed, transmitted, and re-actualised across generations.footnote:[Foerster 1993, on the *ngillatun* communal ritual as a multi-day public ceremonial gathering held at named ritual sites (*nguillatuwe*) on a calendrical schedule, on its presidency by the *machi* (the Mapuche shaman), on its incorporation of prayers, sacrificial offerings, dances, and ritualised speech directed to the *ngen* of the upper land, and on the embedding of Mapuche cosmogonic narratives in the *ngillatun* ritual context for performance and transmission across generations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foerster-religiosidad-mapuche ; loc=Introducción a la religiosidad mapuche, on the Wenu / Naq / Minche Mapu cosmographic system and ngillatun ritual

The Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle's ritual function within *ngillatun* and within the wider Mapuche ritual life is the foundational-charter function: the cycle establishes the legitimacy of Mapuche occupation of the elevated southern-cone landscape (Tren-Tren's rising mountains as the place where the Mapuche took refuge and from which they descended to populate the present-day terrain), the legitimacy of the *ngillatun* ritual itself (as the continuing communal address to the powers whose combat established the Mapuche cosmographic situation), and the ritual register within which the *machi* operates as an intermediary between *Naq Mapu* and the powers above and below.footnote:[Bacigalupo 2007, on the foundational-charter function of the Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle within Mapuche ritual life — establishing the legitimacy of Mapuche occupation of the elevated southern-cone landscape, the legitimacy of the *ngillatun* ritual, and the ritual register of the *machi* as intermediary between *Naq Mapu* and the powers above and below; Course 2011 on the foundational-charter function of cosmogonic narrative in Mapuche ritual life and on the integration of the Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle into the contemporary *ngillatun* practice.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bacigalupo-shamans-foye ; loc=Shamans of the Foye Tree, on machi and contemporary Mapuche ritual

The cycle's continuing ritual function in still-active Mapuche communities of Chile and Argentina is significant for the chapter's status: where most of the Eurasian combat-myth material treated in the present book survives only in the archival record of an antique tradition no longer in continuous performance, the Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle remains in active oral and ritual transmission within communities that perform *ngillatun* on a continuing basis and within which contemporary *machi* maintain the lineage of ritual practice, with the modern ethnographic record (Bacigalupo 2007; Course 2011) being a record of a still-living tradition rather than of an archival reconstruction.footnote:[Bacigalupo 2007, on the continuing ritual function of Mapuche cosmogonic narrative in still-active Mapuche communities of Chile and Argentina, on the contemporary practice of *ngillatun* and the contemporary *machi* lineage; Course 2011 on the still-living-tradition character of the Mapuche ethnographic record relative to the archival situation of most Eurasian combat-myth material.]

=== Typology, area-specialist framing, and forward-links

The Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle's place within the present book's chaoskampf typology is — when taken in the area-specialist reading — a related-tier (rather than core-tier) case in which a combat-pattern is structurally present (two named serpent-beings opposing one another with sustained vertical action) but the cosmogonic-establishment-ex-novo and order-versus-chaos load-bearing functions of Eurasian chaoskampf are not load-bearing for the Mapuche material, with the cycle's principal narrative work being cosmogonic-cataclysm-within-existing-cosmos and *ngillatun*-ritual-foundation rather than cosmos-versus-chaos combat.footnote:[Per brief 27-mapuche-kai-tren.yaml summary on the cycle's two-serpent structure not mapping cleanly onto chaoskampf and on the related-tier classification; scope.md §"Inclusion / exclusion criteria" on the related-tier criteria where the cosmogonic-combat work is weaker; Foerster 1993, Bacigalupo 2007, Course 2011, Faron 1964 on the area-specialist consensus reading of the cycle as cosmogonic-cataclysm-and-ngillatun-ritual-foundation rather than as cosmos-versus-chaos combat.]

The substance of the area-specialist objection is that the cycle's two-serpent structure (with no hero-figure and no kill-and-eradicate outcome, and with a non-divine human population as the narrative's beneficiary-centre) and the cycle's cataclysm-within-already-existing-cosmos framing (with the Mapuche world already populated and ordered before Kai-Kai's flood begins) do not match the cosmos-versus-chaos opposition that the Gunkel-tradition chaoskampf-typology presupposes; the cycle's narrative work is the foundational-charter establishment of the *ngillatun* ritual life and the legitimisation of Mapuche territorial occupation of the elevated southern-cone landscape rather than the cosmogonic establishment of cosmic order from primordial chaos. The area-specialist objection-strength is comparable to the Ch26 Pariacaca case (where Salomon, Urioste, Taylor, and Dean read the cycle as wak'a-succession rather than as chaos-versus-order combat) — both are mid-spectrum objections in which the area-specialist reading does accommodate some combat-myth framing while objecting to the chaos-order framing — and milder than the Ch32 Nanabozho case (where Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, and Vizenor reject combat-and-defeat-of-the-other framing entirely in favour of necessary-counterpart-dialectic framing). Part II C7 (inversion / transformation; ch. 40 of the present volume) and Part II C8 (refusal and appropriation; ch. 41) are the loci where the comparative analysis must address this objection rather than paper over it; the present chapter functions as a worked instance of the appropriation-protocol's outside-framing requirement at scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" point 3, and forwards the objection to C7 and C8 for the comparative-engagement work that scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" point 2 requires of the comparative chapters.]

The cycle's distinctive contribution to the present book's wider comparative argument is the two-serpent-with-human-beneficiary-centre pattern: where Eurasian chaoskampf-tradition narratives typically oppose a single sovereign or champion deity to a single serpentine adversary and place the deity's victory at the narrative's centre, the Kai-Kai / Tren-Tren cycle opposes two serpent-beings to one another and places the survival of a non-divine human population on the rising mountains at the narrative's centre — a structural pattern that the methods sidebar (chapter 33) and Part II C7-C8 will engage with as one of the present book's load-bearing examples of the spectrum of non-Eurasian combat-narrative architectures.footnote:[Per brief 27-mapuche-kai-tren.yaml summary on the two-serpent-with-human-beneficiary-centre pattern as distinctive to the Mapuche material; Faron 1964 on the structural-ritual framework within which the human-beneficiary-centre operates; Course 2011 on the contemporary anthropological reading of the human-beneficiary-centre as integrated with Mapuche personhood and ritual.]
