== Slavic and Baltic Combat (Perun/Veles, Dobrynya byliny)

=== Source overview

This chapter takes up two East Slavic dragon-combat sub-cases that share a contested scholarly link but sit on radically different evidential ground. The first is the reconstructed pre-Christian combat between the storm-god Perun and the earth-serpent Veles — Perun's adversary in the 'basic myth' reconstruction. The second is the East Slavic *bylina* (the Russian-epic oral song; plural *byliny*) tradition's combat between the *bogatyr* (the *bylina* warrior-hero) Dobrynya Nikitich and the three-headed dragon Zmey Gorynych.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml; West 2007, ch. 6 ("Storm and Stream"), on the Perun-Veles reconstruction within the wider Indo-European storm-god / water-serpent complex; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the Slavic combat-myth material as a comparative witness to the Indo-European dragon-combat pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The first move of the chapter is to be explicit about the asymmetry between the two cases. The Perun-Veles combat survives only as a 20th-century scholarly reconstruction drawn from folklore fragments, medieval chronicles, and toponyms, with no continuous primary text in any Slavic language. The Dobrynya combat, by contrast, survives in dozens of recorded *bylina* variants from 19th-century Russian oral performance, with a textual record that is robust even though late.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling, on the asymmetry between reconstructed Perun-Veles and recorded Dobrynya material; West 2007, ch. 6, on the reconstructed Slavic combat-myth as evidence for the IE complex; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the Slavic combat-myth corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

There is no Slavic equivalent of the Rigveda, the Theogony, the Edda, or the Baal Cycle preserving a Perun-Veles combat as a connected narrative. The reconstruction is built up instead from medieval Christian-period chronicles' brief god-list mentions, place-names whose etymologies are read back into the deity-pair, and folklore items collected centuries after the formal Christianisation of the Slavic-speaking peoples.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the absence of a continuous Slavic primary-text witness for the Perun-Veles combat and on the reconstruction's reliance on chronicles, toponyms, and folklore; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the indirect character of the Slavic evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The principal scholarly construct that organises the Perun-Veles material is the 'basic myth' (Russian *основной миф*) reconstruction of Vyacheslav Ivanov and Vladimir Toporov, published in a sequence of monographs and articles between 1965 and 1974 in the Soviet structural-semiotic tradition, and now widely cited in English-language Slavic-studies and Indo-European-studies scholarship as the standard reconstruction of the pre-Christian Slavic storm-god-vs-serpent combat.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Ivanov and Toporov's 1965–1974 'basic myth' reconstruction as the standard Slavic reconstruction in current Indo-European-studies scholarship; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same dating; glossary 'basic myth (Ivanov-Toporov)' entry on the standard English rendering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The reconstruction is at the same time real, influential, and seriously contested. Methodological objections have been voiced by Roman Jakobson, by Patrice Lajoye in the French Slavic-studies tradition, by Leszek Moszyński in the Polish Slavic-studies tradition, and by Mate Kapović in the Croatian Indo-European tradition, on grounds including circular reasoning between toponym and combat-narrative, the late and Christianised character of the chronicle attestations, and over-interpretation of folklore fragments.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the methodological objections to the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction in subsequent Slavic and Indo-European scholarship; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the named critics (Jakobson, Lajoye, Moszyński, Kapović) and the load-bearing requirement that the chapter present the objections fairly; glossary 'basic myth (Ivanov-Toporov)' first_mention_gloss on the criticism of methodological circularity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The chapter accordingly presents the Perun-Veles combat as reconstruction throughout. Every plot-element claim about Perun's pursuit of Veles is co-located with the [RECONSTRUCTION] marker that identifies its evidential status, and the chapter uses language such as "on Ivanov and Toporov's reconstruction" or "if the reconstruction holds" rather than asserting the reconstructed plot as attested fact, in line with scope.md's reconstruction policy and the brief's special_instructions.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the explicit-reconstruction-framing requirement; scope.md §"Reconstruction policy" on [RECONSTRUCTION] markers naming donor sources; glossary 'basic myth (Ivanov-Toporov)' on the contested status that motivates the framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Dobrynya Nikitich material, by contrast, survives in the East Slavic *bylina* tradition: a corpus of orally-transmitted heroic epic-songs collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers, principally in the Russian North (Onega, Pinega, Mezen', White Sea coast), with later collections from Siberia and other regions. The tradition is conventionally taken to reach back to the medieval Kievan period in some form, even though the recorded texts are demonstrably late.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina* tradition as the recorded medium for the East Slavic dragon-combat material; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the East Slavic *bylina* corpus, recorded 19th c., tradition medieval.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina tradition overview)

The corpus's principal 19th-century collectors are P. N. Rybnikov and A. F. Hilferding (Gilferding), whose mid- and late-19th-century field collections supply the bulk of the Dobrynya texts, with later 20th-century collections by Soviet-period folklorists supplementing the material. Per brief, edition-pinning for the working English-language translations (Oinas, Propp) is deferred to the glossary-lock stage and the chapter cites at corpus level rather than at variant-and-line granularity.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the 19th-century *bylina* collection history (Rybnikov, Hilferding) and on the supplementary Soviet-period collections; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling and special_instructions on the deferral of Oinas / Propp edition-pinning to glossary-lock and on corpus-level citation discipline accordingly.]

=== Perun and Veles — the contested 'basic myth'

The pre-Christian East Slavic religious tradition has no continuous primary-text witness in any Slavic language. The surviving direct attestations of its deities are confined to brief and largely god-list-style mentions in medieval Christian-period chronicles, together with a heterogeneous corpus of folklore fragments, ritual survivals, and toponyms collected from the medieval period through the 20th century.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the absence of a continuous primary-text witness for pre-Christian Slavic religion and on the chronicle / folklore / toponym character of the surviving evidence; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the indirectness of the Slavic evidence relative to the better-attested Indo-European traditions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The principal medieval witness to a pre-Christian Slavic pantheon is the Old East Slavic *Primary Chronicle* (the *Povest' vremennykh let*, conventionally dated to the early 12th century CE). It records that Vladimir of Kiev set up six idols on a hill in Kiev in 980 CE — including idols of Perun and of other Slavic deities — before himself converting to Byzantine Christianity in 988 CE and ordering Perun's idol thrown into the Dnieper.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Primary Chronicle*'s 980 CE Vladimir-pantheon entry and on Perun's place at the head of the list; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Slavic chronicles as one of the three classes of evidence for the reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The *Primary Chronicle* gives Perun's idol a silver head and a gold moustache and identifies him at the head of the Vladimir-pantheon list. This is the principal direct medieval attestation of Perun as a deity-name and of his prominence in the late-pre-Christian East Slavic religious culture, but the entry does not narrate any combat or any plot-content involving Perun.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Primary Chronicle*'s description of Perun's idol with silver head and gold moustache and on the absence of any combat-narrative in the entry; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the chronicle's god-list-style witness without combat-content.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

Veles (East Slavic variant *Volos*) is attested separately in the *Primary Chronicle* and in the 12th-century epic *Slovo o polku Igoreve* (*The Tale of Igor's Campaign*) as a god of cattle and of poets. The 945 CE Russo-Byzantine treaty's oath-formula names both Perun and Volos, and the *Slovo* calls the bard Boyan "the grandson of Veles." The surviving attestations characterise Veles as a cattle-and-wealth deity with poetic-and-oracular associations, but, again, do not narrate any combat between Veles and Perun.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the *Primary Chronicle* and *Slovo o polku Igoreve* attestations of Veles / Volos as cattle-god and patron of poets and on the 945 CE oath-formula naming Perun and Volos; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same chronicle-and-epic attestations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The crucial point about the medieval Slavic chronicle attestations is therefore a negative one. Although Perun and Veles are both directly named in 11th- and 12th-century East Slavic Christian-period sources, no surviving medieval Slavic source narrates a combat between them. The combat as a story is absent from the direct medieval record.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the absence of any surviving medieval Slavic narrative of a Perun-Veles combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the negative state of the direct medieval evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the no-direct-attestation premise that the [RECONSTRUCTION] marker addresses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Ivanov-Toporov 'basic myth' reconstruction proposes nonetheless that the pre-Christian East Slavic religious tradition contained a structurally central combat-narrative: the storm-god Perun pursues the cattle-stealing earth-serpent Veles through a sequence of refuges (a tree, a rock, a body of water, and, finally, livestock) before striking him with a lightning-bolt and releasing rain, and the combat constitutes the structural core of the pre-Christian Slavic mythology.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction of the Perun-Veles combat including the four-refuge sequence (tree, rock, water, livestock) and the lightning-strike with rain-release; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same plot-elements; glossary 'basic myth (Ivanov-Toporov)' first_mention_gloss on the same reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

[RECONSTRUCTION: the connected narrative in which Perun, the East Slavic storm-god, pursues Veles, the cattle-stealing earth-serpent, through tree, rock, water, and livestock before striking him with lightning and releasing rain | gap_source: there is no surviving Slavic primary-text witness for the combat-narrative as a connected story; the medieval *Primary Chronicle*, *Slovo o polku Igoreve*, the 945 CE Russo-Byzantine treaty, and the other direct medieval attestations name Perun and Veles separately but narrate no combat between them | fill_source: Ivanov and Toporov 1965–1974 (cited via West 2007 ch. 6), drawing on three classes of indirect evidence — Slavic folklore fragments collected in the 19th and 20th centuries, medieval Slavic Christian-period chronicles, and Slavic toponyms (place-names whose etymologies are read as preserving the deity-pair); the four-refuge sequence (tree → rock → water → livestock) is reconstructed from folklore items and from cross-comparative Indo-European parallels surveyed in West 2007 ch. 6 | confidence: low — the reconstruction is contested by Jakobson, Lajoye, Moszyński, and Kapović on grounds of circular reasoning between toponym and combat-narrative, the late and Christianised character of the chronicle attestations, and over-interpretation of folklore fragments; per brief special_instructions and scope.md §"Reconstruction policy", every plot-element claim about Perun's pursuit of Veles is presented within or co-located with this marker and the chapter does not assert the reconstructed plot as attested fact.]

The reconstruction draws on three classes of indirect evidence. First, Slavic folklore items (songs, sayings, ritual-survivals, weather-charms) collected by 19th- and 20th-century ethnographers and fieldworkers and read as preserving fragments of the pre-Christian combat-narrative under their Christianised surface. Second, the medieval chronicle and treaty attestations — the *Primary Chronicle*, the *Slovo o polku Igoreve*, the 945 CE oath-formula — that name Perun and Veles separately as deities. Third, Slavic toponyms whose etymologies link to the names Perun and Veles and which are read as marking sites associated with the combat-narrative.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the three classes of indirect evidence (folklore, chronicles, toponyms) on which the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction draws; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same three classes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The folklore-evidence class includes ritual-survival items such as harvest-songs in which a bearded figure (read as Veles) is hidden in the last sheaf, weather-charms naming Perun (with East Slavic variants such as the Belarusian and Ukrainian *peryn*-type), and folktale-fragments in which a thunder-figure pursues a serpent-figure that hides successively in a tree, a stone, water, and the body of an animal or person — items collected in the 19th and 20th centuries from East and South Slavic informants.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the folklore-evidence class for the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction including the four-refuge folktale type; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the folklore fragments as one of the three evidence classes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The toponym-evidence class includes Slavic place-names whose etymologies are read as preserving Perun's name (Russian *Perun'*, Croatian *Perun-* and *Perunkovac*, Slovenian *Perun-* place-names, the *Peryn* sanctuary site near Novgorod) and place-names whose etymologies are read as preserving Veles's name (the Bulgarian town *Veles*, Russian *Volos-* place-names, and the Croatian-Bosnian *Vol-* / *Vel-* type), with the geographical patterning of the two name-classes interpreted as marking the distribution of the deity-pair's cult.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the toponym-evidence class including the Russian *Peryn* sanctuary, Croatian / Slovenian Perun-toponyms, and the Bulgarian town *Veles*; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the toponyms as the third evidence class.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

On the Ivanov-Toporov reading, the reconstructed combat's narrative core is a four-stage pursuit. Veles steals the cattle (or the rain, or the waters, on alternative phrasings of the reconstruction) and conceals himself in a tree; Perun's lightning-bolt splits the tree and Veles flees to a rock; the rock is split and Veles flees to water; the water is dispersed and Veles flees into livestock or a human victim; and the final lightning-strike kills Veles and releases the rain.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the four-stage pursuit and the lightning-strike-with-rain-release in the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the four-refuge sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The cattle-theft motif is one of the load-bearing features of the reconstruction for its Indo-European-comparative function. Veles's role as cattle-stealer aligns the reconstructed Slavic combat with the wider Indo-European cattle-raid complex (Greek Geryon, Italic Cacus, Indra's release of the cattle in the Vedic Vala-cave myth and elsewhere), and the alignment is one of the principal reasons the reconstruction is influential within Indo-European-studies scholarship.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the cattle-theft motif's Indo-European-comparative alignment with Geryon, Cacus, and the Vedic Vala-cave material; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml cross_cultural_parallels on the cattle-theft motif as parallel to IE cattle-raid reflexes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The lightning-and-rain-release motif similarly aligns the reconstructed combat with the wider Indo-European storm-god / water-serpent complex treated in West 2007 ch. 6 across Vedic, Hittite, Greek, and Norse witnesses: the storm-god strikes the serpent and the obstructed waters are released. The alignment is the second principal Indo-European-comparative argument for the reconstruction's structural shape.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the storm-god / water-serpent complex across Vedic, Hittite, Greek, and Norse witnesses and on the Slavic alignment with the wider type; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the IE storm-god / serpent reflex framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Baltic comparanda are co-located in West 2007 ch. 6's survey. The Lithuanian storm-god Perkūnas and the Latvian Pērkons are name-cognates with Slavic Perun (the Indo-European root *perkw-* / *per-* with Indo-European thunder-association), and the Lithuanian *Velnias* / *Velinas* (a folkloric devil-figure with cattle-and-underworld associations) is sometimes read as a name-cognate with Slavic Veles, although the Baltic combat-evidence is itself indirect and mediated by folklore rather than by primary text.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Baltic Perkūnas / Pērkons and *Velnias* / *Velinas* name-cognates with Slavic Perun and Veles and on the indirect-folklore character of the Baltic evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Baltic comparanda within the Perun-Veles sub-section.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Baltic name-cognancy is one of the load-bearing supports for the Indo-European reading of the Slavic deity-pair. If Perkūnas / Perun and *Velnias* / Veles are inherited Indo-European cognates rather than late parallel developments, then the deity-pair has a depth that the late Slavic chronicle attestations on their own would not establish, and the cognancy is part of the case the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction makes.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Baltic-Slavic name-cognancy as a support for the Indo-European reading; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the IE cognancy framework within which the reconstruction operates.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The methodological objections to the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction are concentrated on three lines of attack, all of which the chapter must present fairly per the brief's special_instructions: circularity in the use of toponym evidence, the late and Christianised character of the chronicle attestations, and over-interpretation of folklore fragments whose connection to the reconstructed combat is itself an inference rather than an attested feature of the items.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the three lines of methodological objection (toponym circularity, late and Christianised chronicles, folklore over-interpretation) voiced by Jakobson, Lajoye, Moszyński, and Kapović; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the load-bearing requirement that the chapter present the objections fairly.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The toponym-circularity objection holds that the etymological identification of a place-name as "Perun-derived" or "Veles-derived" presupposes the deity-pair whose existence the toponym is then taken to support: a place-name like *Volos-* is read as a Veles-toponym because Veles is reconstructed as a deity-name, and the toponym is then cited as evidence for Veles, with the same item entering both ends of the inference and the resulting argument turning back on itself.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the toponym-circularity objection in the methodological critique of the reconstruction; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The late-and-Christianised-chronicles objection holds that the *Primary Chronicle*, the *Slovo o polku Igoreve*, and other medieval Slavic sources are themselves the products of a Christianised literary culture whose representation of pre-Christian deities cannot be taken as direct testimony to pre-Christian belief: the chronicles' brief god-list mentions are informed by the chroniclers' Christian framework and by their literary models, and reading the absent combat-content back into them risks reading a structurally-interesting story into sources that would not have transmitted the story even if it had existed in the pre-Christian tradition.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the late-and-Christianised-chronicles objection; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection within the methodological critique.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The folklore-over-interpretation objection holds that the four-stage pursuit-and-refuge folktale type, the harvest-sheaf ritual, and the weather-charm material are folklore items with their own functions and their own histories within a centuries-long Christian-period folk culture, and that reading them as preserving fragments of a pre-Christian combat-narrative imposes a comparative-mythological frame that the items do not themselves require. The four-refuge folktale, in particular, is an internationally-attested folktale type whose presence in the Slavic-language area does not necessarily indicate inheritance from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the folklore-over-interpretation objection and on the cross-cultural distribution of the four-refuge folktale type; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions naming the objection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The chapter's reading of the methodological objections is that they do not in themselves disprove the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction but that they do establish its evidential floor as low and its evidential ceiling as moderate. The reconstruction can be defended as a structurally-coherent reading of the indirect evidence, but it cannot be defended as a confirmed pre-Christian Slavic narrative on the strength of the surviving direct attestations alone, and the chapter's [RECONSTRUCTION] marker confidence value of "low" reflects this reading.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the standing of the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction within current Slavic-and-Indo-European-studies scholarship as a structurally-coherent but evidentially-limited reading; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the explicit-reconstruction-framing requirement and on the fair presentation of the objections.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

If the reconstruction holds, the reconstructed combat's structural-typological function — on the Ivanov-Toporov reading and the wider Indo-European-comparative reading that West 2007 ch. 6 surveys — is most likely cosmogonic-and-agricultural. === Dobrynya Nikitich and Zmey Gorynych — the East Slavic bylina

The Dobrynya Nikitich combat-narrative is preserved in the East Slavic *bylina* tradition: a corpus of orally-transmitted heroic epic-songs sung by professional and semi-professional reciters and collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers in the Russian North — from the Onega and White Sea regions principally, with later collections supplementing the geographical reach — and whose tradition is conventionally taken to reach back in some form to the medieval Kievan period.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina* tradition as an orally-transmitted heroic epic-song corpus collected from 19th-century Russian peasant performers in the Russian North; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the same tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina tradition)

The principal collectors whose 19th-century field-recordings constitute the working corpus of Dobrynya texts are Pavel Rybnikov (collections published in the 1860s) and Aleksandr Hilferding (Gilferding, with the major Onega collection published in 1873), supplemented by 20th-century collections that extended the geographical and chronological reach of the recorded tradition. The recorded variants are numerous, and the present chapter cites the corpus rather than any one specific recorded variant.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the principal 19th-century *bylina* collectors (Rybnikov 1860s, Hilferding 1873) and on the supplementary 20th-century collections; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling on the multiple-recorded-variants character of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (collection history)

Dobrynya Nikitich is one of the three central *bogatyri* (the heroic warrior-figures) of the *bylina* tradition's so-called Vladimir-cycle. Alongside Ilya Muromets and Alyosha Popovich, Dobrynya is one of the standard companions of the figure Prince Vladimir of Kiev, whose court the *bylina* tradition takes as its narrative centre and whose historical reflex (the Vladimir of the *Primary Chronicle*) places the cycle's narrative setting in the late 10th and early 11th centuries CE even though the recorded songs are 19th-century.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction and Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on Dobrynya Nikitich as one of the three central *bogatyri* of the Vladimir-cycle alongside Ilya Muromets and Alyosha Popovich; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the medieval setting and the 19th-century recording.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction and Dobrynya-cycle headnotes

The Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting is the court of Prince Vladimir at Kiev, and the cycle's standard plot-elements include the *bogatyri*'s service at the court, their patrols of the steppe-frontier and the rivers, their combats with hostile foreigners and supernatural adversaries, and their feasts and quarrels at the prince's table. The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* sits within this wider cycle as one of Dobrynya's standard combat-narratives.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting at Kiev and its standard plot-elements; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the *bylina* corpus's heroic-epic register.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Vladimir-cycle setting)

The adversary of the Dobrynya combat is Zmey Gorynych, a three-headed dragon (*zmey*, "serpent" / "dragon") whose epithet *Gorynych* is a patronymic derived from *gora* ("mountain") and is conventionally read as "Mountain-son" or "Son of the mountain", marking the dragon's mountain-residence in the eastern frontier-territory beyond the Russian-settled lands.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on Zmey Gorynych as a three-headed dragon with the patronymic *Gorynych* derived from *gora* ("mountain"); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the three-headed character of the dragon and the mountain-residence; glossary 'Zmey Gorynych' first_mention_gloss on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (Zmey Gorynych)

The standard plot-skeleton of the Dobrynya-vs-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina*, as it appears across the recorded variants, is a three-stage narrative: an initial encounter at a river (typically the Puchai-river or a similar named river) in which the young Dobrynya defeats the dragon in a first combat without killing him; a treaty-and-truce in which the dragon promises to leave the Russian lands alone; and a second combat occasioned by the dragon's breach of the truce — typically by abducting a princess (often Vladimir's niece Zabava Putyatichna) — in which Dobrynya kills the dragon, releases the princess and the other captives, and returns to Vladimir's court.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the standard three-stage plot-skeleton of the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* across the recorded variants; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the kills-the-dragon and rescues-the-princess outcome.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (plot-skeleton)

The first combat takes place when the young Dobrynya, against his mother's warning, bathes in the Puchai-river (or its variant equivalents). The dragon emerges from the river or descends from the mountain and attacks, and Dobrynya — armed in some variants with his cap (*kolpak*) of folkloric-hyperbolic weight as an improvised weapon, having no other arms to hand — defeats the dragon and forces the truce.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the first combat at the Puchai-river and the cap-as-weapon detail (the precise pud-count specification flagged for verification at glossary-lock against Oinas / Propp or equivalent specialist edition); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the standard combat-mechanics of the *bylina*'s first combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (first combat)

The second combat is occasioned by the dragon's abduction of the princess and is resolved by Dobrynya's killing of the dragon. In the standard variants, Dobrynya rides out from Kiev armed with a cudgel or sword, fights the dragon at the dragon's mountain-lair over a multi-day combat, and finally kills the dragon — typically by severing the dragon's heads — before releasing the princess and the other captives held in the dragon's caves and returning to Vladimir's court.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the second combat's multi-day duration, the killing of the dragon by head-severing, and the rescue of the princess and other captives (the precise day-count and head-severing-sequence variants flagged for verification at glossary-lock); brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the kills-the-dragon-and-rescues-the-princess resolution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (second combat)

The recorded variants of the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* differ in multiple specific details — the name of the river of the first combat, the precise weapon Dobrynya uses in the killing, the exact number of heads severed and the order of the severing, the identity of the abducted princess, the duration and details of the second combat — but agree on the three-stage plot-skeleton (first combat at river → truce → second combat with abduction-and-killing) that the present chapter takes as the corpus's common narrative core.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, Dobrynya-cycle headnotes on the inter-variant differences and on the agreement of the recorded variants on the three-stage plot-skeleton; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling on the chapter's presentation of the common narrative skeleton.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=Dobrynya-cycle headnotes (variants)

[VARIANT: primary=the recorded *bylina* corpus (Rybnikov 1860s; Hilferding 1873; later 20th-c. collections; multiple variants agreeing on the three-stage plot-skeleton — first combat at the river, truce, second combat with princess-abduction and dragon-killing) | alt=individual variants differ in the river-name (Puchai- and others), the weapon (cap, cudgel, sword), the precise head-severing sequence, and the identity of the abducted princess (Zabava Putyatichna in many; other names in some) | chosen: present-the-common-skeleton | reason: per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml shared_variant_handling, the chapter presents the common narrative skeleton across the corpus rather than privileging one specific recorded variant; per brief special_instructions, primary-text edition-pinning (Oinas, Propp) is deferred to glossary-lock and the chapter cites at corpus level rather than at variant-and-line granularity.]

The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina* is, in its surviving 19th-century-recorded form, on firmer attestation ground than the reconstructed Perun-Veles combat. The recorded songs are direct primary-text witnesses to a living oral-epic tradition, with multiple independent variants from multiple performers and multiple collectors, and the textual record's robustness is not in dispute — only its dating and its relationship to older traditions are.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the robustness of the *bylina* corpus's textual record relative to the indirectness of the Perun-Veles evidence; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the firmer attestation ground of the Dobrynya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (textual-record robustness)

The Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych *bylina*'s historical-cultural setting in the Vladimir-cycle places its narrative reference in the late-10th- and early-11th-century Kievan-Rus' period. The conventional reading within Slavic-folklore scholarship is that the *bylina* tradition in some form reaches back to the Kievan period even though the recorded texts are 19th-century, with the long oral-transmission gap accounting for both the variants' specific differences and their structural agreement.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the Vladimir-cycle's late-Kievan reference and on the conventional medieval-tradition reading of the *bylina* corpus; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the medieval-tradition / 19th-century-recording two-tier dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Vladimir-cycle dating)

The Christian conversion of Kievan Rus' in 988 CE is the principal historical-cultural caesura that separates the Vladimir-cycle's narrative setting from any pre-Christian religious frame. The *bylina* tradition as it survives is wholly Christian-period in its religious frame, with Vladimir himself remembered as the Christianising prince, and the Christian frame is one of the principal complications for any reading of the tradition as preserving pre-Christian combat-myth content.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the 988 CE Christian conversion as the historical-cultural caesura between the *bylina* setting and any pre-Christian religious frame; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Christian-period character of the recorded tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (Christian-period frame)

Ivanov and Toporov's specific comparative claim about the Dobrynya material is that the *bylina* combat is the later epic reflex of the reconstructed pre-Christian Perun-Veles 'basic myth' combat, with the *bogatyr* Dobrynya inheriting the role of the storm-god Perun and Zmey Gorynych inheriting the role of the cattle-stealing earth-serpent Veles, and with the *bylina*'s Christian-period epic register replacing the pre-Christian myth's religious register but preserving the structural shape of the combat.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on Ivanov and Toporov's claim that the Dobrynya *bylina* is the later reflex of the reconstructed Perun-Veles combat; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the Ivanov-Toporov comparative claim about the Dobrynya material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

[INFERENCE: the Ivanov-Toporov claim that the Dobrynya *bylina* is a later reflex of the reconstructed pre-Christian Perun-Veles combat is itself a comparative argument rather than a directly attested feature of the Slavic textual record; the recorded *byliny* do not name Perun or Veles, do not invoke the deity-pair, and do not present themselves as preserving older mythological material, and the reflex-claim is therefore made on structural-comparative grounds (storm-figure-pursues-serpent / cattle-and-frontier-themes / dragon-with-multiple-heads) by reading the Christian-period epic against the reconstructed pre-Christian myth that the chapter has already framed as contested. | basis: West 2007 ch. 6 surveys the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim as a comparative argument; the *bylina* texts themselves (Forsyth 1987 ch. 2's overview of the corpus) make no reference to Perun or Veles; the reflex-claim is doubly mediated, depending both on the contested pre-Christian reconstruction and on the structural reading of the Christian-period epic. | risk: the reflex-claim's evidential weight is therefore sensitive to two prior questions — whether the Perun-Veles reconstruction itself holds, and whether structural-comparative reading is sufficient warrant for inheritance-claims of this kind — and the chapter presents the claim as a comparative argument within Ivanov and Toporov's framework rather than as a fact about the *bylina* tradition's textual history.]

The structural-parallel observations on which the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim rests include the storm-figure / serpent-figure pairing (Dobrynya as warrior-hero / Zmey Gorynych as serpent-adversary, parallel to Perun / Veles); the multi-headed character of the serpent-adversary (Zmey Gorynych three-headed; Veles in some folklore-fragments connected to multi-headed motifs); the water-and-mountain setting (the Puchai-river first-combat / mountain second-combat in the *bylina*; the water-and-rock refuges in the reconstructed Perun-Veles pursuit); and the rescue-and-restoration outcome (the *bylina*'s rescue of the princess and captives parallel to the reconstruction's release of the rain).footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the structural parallels that support the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml family_members entry on the structural-parallel basis for the comparative claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The reflex-claim's evidential limits are however significant, and the chapter must register them. The *bylina*'s genre is heroic epic rather than cosmogonic-and-agricultural myth, the dragon's role is as enemy-of-the-state rather than as cattle-stealer-and-rain-blocker, the resolution is dragon-killing-and-princess-rescue rather than rain-release, and the Christian-period frame may itself have generated the structural-parallel features as part of the international hagiographic-and-romance dragon-slaying type rather than inherited them from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-narrative.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the *bylina*'s heroic-epic genre and its differences from the reconstructed cosmogonic-and-agricultural Perun-Veles material; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml special_instructions on the comparative-argument-rather-than-fact framing for the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=bailey-ivanova-1998 ; loc=introduction (bylina genre vs. reconstructed myth)

The international-hagiographic-and-romance-type complication is significant. The medieval-and-early-modern Christian-period dragon-slaying corpus — the St. George cycle, the romance-tradition's princess-rescuing dragon-slayers, the wider European folktale-dragon-slayer type — supplies a well-attested international type that could plausibly have shaped the Dobrynya *bylina*'s combat-and-rescue plot directly, without any inheritance from a pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth, and the chapter does not adjudicate between the inheritance-and-international-type-borrowing readings.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the international Christian-period dragon-slaying type as a competing-or-supplementary explanation for the *bylina*'s combat-and-rescue plot; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the comparative-argument framing that leaves the question open.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Dobrynya *bylina*'s position within the present book's typology is therefore as a heroic-epic combat-narrative whose link to the reconstructed pre-Christian Slavic combat-myth is itself contested, and whose principal evidential value for the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula argument depends on the prior acceptance of the Ivanov-Toporov reflex-claim that the chapter has flagged as a comparative argument.footnote:[Per brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml on the Dobrynya *bylina*'s position within the disputed-evidence framing; West 2007, ch. 6, on the *bylina*'s evidential dependence on the prior Perun-Veles reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Dobrynya *bylina*'s cultural-historical importance within the East Slavic literary culture is at the same time central and uncontested. The Vladimir-cycle is one of the foundational corpora of pre-modern East Slavic oral literature, the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych narrative is one of its best-known and most-recorded individual *byliny*, and the cycle has been a load-bearing cultural reference within Russian, Ukrainian, and Belarusian literary, scholarly, and popular culture from the 19th-century collection onwards.footnote:[Bailey and Ivanova 1998 (eds.), *An Anthology of Russian Folk Epics*, introduction on the central cultural-historical importance of the Vladimir-cycle and the Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych narrative within East Slavic literary culture; brief 20-slavic-baltic.yaml cultural_relevance on the *bylina* corpus's centrality.]

=== Family pattern and forward links
