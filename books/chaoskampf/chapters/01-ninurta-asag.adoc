== Ninurta vs. Asag: The Exploits of Lugal-e

=== The physical evidence

The Sumerian poem that this chapter retells has no original title. Like most Sumerian literary compositions, it is known instead by its opening phrase — its incipit — and modern scholarship has kept that convention. The incipit is _lugal-e ud me-lám-bi nir-ĝál_, "O king, storm of majestic splendour," and on the strength of those first words the composition is catalogued as _Lugal-e_. The Electronic Text Corpus of Sumerian Literature, the digital scholarly standard produced at Oxford, lists it as text 1.6.2. Its working scholarly title, when scholars need an English label, is "Ninurta's Exploits." A reader will see both forms in the scholarly literature, depending on whether the citing author is following the indexing conventions of the field or the thematic handle the composition has acquired in English-language discussion.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text (Black, Cunningham, et al.); the composition is titled from its opening line _lugal-e ud me-lám-bi nir-ĝál_, "O king, storm of majestic splendour."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text header

_Lugal-e_ is not a short poem. It is one of the longest surviving Sumerian literary compositions. The ETCSL composite text ends at line 726, and van Dijk's two-volume critical edition, published by Brill in 1983, numbers some witnesses as high as line 728 depending on how certain subscript lines are counted. Van Dijk's edition gives the composition a French subtitle that tells the reader what kind of poem he took it to be: _Le récit épique et didactique des Travaux de Ninurta, du Déluge et de la Nouvelle Création_ — "the epic and didactic account of Ninurta's Labours, of the Flood, and of the New Creation." Readers used to the New Testament Gospels or Virgil's _Aeneid_ will find _Lugal-e_ shorter than either, but by the standards of the Sumerian literary corpus it is a very long poem.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 composite, ending at line 726; van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction); the edition's full title names the composition by its first line and subtitles it "Le récit épique et didactique des Travaux de Ninurta, du Déluge et de la Nouvelle Création."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, introduction

The physical evidence for it is tablets. Dozens of them, almost all from scribal-school contexts, and almost all dating to the Old Babylonian period — the first few centuries of the second millennium BCE, when Sumerian had already been displaced as a spoken language by Akkadian but was still being taught and copied as a literary and liturgical prestige tongue. The bulk of the witnesses come from Nippur, the Sumerian temple-city where Enlil's temple stood and where scribal training was concentrated; a smaller cluster was recovered from the city of Ur. This is the provenance profile of a text used as a teaching composition. Young scribes copied it to practise their Sumerian; the copies they left behind — the exercises, the partial drafts, the fragments that broke during firing and were discarded in the school's rubbish dump — are the witnesses from which the modern composite text is reconstructed.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (manuscript catalogue and introduction to the transmission); ETCSL 1.6.2 editorial notes list the Old Babylonian witnesses that underlie the composite.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, manuscript catalogue

No single tablet preserves the whole poem. The text that modern scholars read is a composite — a running, line-numbered edition stitched together from overlapping manuscript witnesses, each of which preserves a different stretch of the poem with a different pattern of damage. Where two witnesses cover the same passage and agree, the composite follows them. Where they disagree in small ways, the edition records both. Where a stretch is covered by only one witness, that witness is the sole source for those lines. The reader is effectively reading the work of a modern editor as much as of the original poet — although the modern editor's work is philological, not creative.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 composite text, editorial note on manuscript base; van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction to the composite method).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, editorial notes on composite

_Lugal-e_ had a long afterlife as school material. It was excerpted, copied, and eventually bilingualised — arranged in Sumerian-Akkadian interlinear form, with a line of the Sumerian on top and a line-by-line Akkadian translation beneath — for use in temple-school curricula well into the first millennium BCE, long after Sumerian had ceased to be anyone's spoken language. This longevity is why the text survived: it kept being copied, and copying is the only way a cuneiform text outlasts the tablet it was first written on. A composition that fell out of the scribal curriculum vanished. _Lugal-e_ did not; it stayed in the curriculum for the better part of two millennia, and the tablets that document that continuous copying are the raw material of the modern edition.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction on the transmission and the first-millennium bilingual redactions).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, introduction on transmission

The standard critical edition is van Dijk 1983, two volumes from Brill. Volume one contains the introduction, the transliteration of the Sumerian, and a French translation. Volume two contains the cuneiform copies, the manuscript apparatus, and a glossary. This is the philological anchor, and every serious modern discussion of the poem cites it.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, 2 vols. (Leiden: Brill); ISBN 9789004069169.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vols. 1-2

The English translation on which this chapter's narrative is keyed is the ETCSL composite, produced between 1998 and 2006 by Jeremy Black, Graham Cunningham, Esther Flückiger-Hawker, Eleanor Robson, and Gábor Zólyomi. ETCSL follows van Dijk's Sumerian reconstruction closely, with a handful of lexical modernisations where van Dijk used more tentative French readings.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text and English translation; Black et al. 2004 (print edition of the ETCSL translations).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2 front-matter

The story, as the tablets preserve it, goes as follows.

=== Setting: Ninurta at Nippur

The hero of the poem is Ninurta. In the Sumerian pantheon, Ninurta is a warrior-god, the son of Enlil, and the divine patron of the city of Nippur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 (Lugal-e), proem lines 1–19, where the opening epithets identify Ninurta as Enlil's son and as the martial champion of the gods.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1-19

Enlil, Ninurta's father, is the chief god of the Sumerian pantheon. He is the lord of the air and of command, and he resides at the temple-complex called E-kur — "the House that is a Mountain" — in the city of Nippur. Nippur itself was not the political capital of any Sumerian kingdom. It was a religious centre, the place where Enlil's priests certified the legitimacy of whichever human king currently ruled Sumer. The significance of Enlil in the story is therefore institutional as well as familial: he is Ninurta's father, and he is the god whose authorisation ultimately matters.footnote:[Lugal-e references to Enlil throughout (e.g., ETCSL 1.6.2 lines 186–227); for Enlil's profile in the Sumerian pantheon see Forsyth 1987, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 1

Standing behind this story — though he does not act in it as much as Enlil does — is Enki, the Sumerian god of wisdom and fresh water, the counsellor of the gods in the Sumerian corpus. Readers acquainted with later Mesopotamian tradition will recognise him under another name: his Akkadian counterpart is Ea, who reappears in the later Babylonian receptions of this narrative and in the combat myths of the Akkadian-language period. In _Lugal-e_ he is Enki.footnote:[Glossary of Sumerian deities in Black et al. 2004; for the language-conditional pairing Enki (Sumerian) / Ea (Akkadian) see the book's glossary entry.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL glossary of deities

_Lugal-e_ opens with a hymnic proem that addresses Ninurta directly, celebrating his martial prowess and the favour of the higher gods. The opening is not a narrative but a praise-song, and its function is to name the hero and to establish the register in which the story that follows will be told.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 (Lugal-e), proem lines 1–19.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1-19

[VARIANT: primary=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1–19 as a continuous hymnic proem | alt=one Old Babylonian manuscript adds an additional invocation — "An, king of the gods, majestic one" — before the opening line | chosen: primary | reason: the additional invocation is an outlier witness variant and the ETCSL composite follows van Dijk's main reconstruction, which is substantially intact across the Ur and Nippur witnesses]

After this opening, the narrative's action begins. Ninurta is seated in celebration — a feast, the precise occasion of which the text does not specify — when his weapon alerts him to a threat rising in the mountains.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17–23: Ninurta's feast interrupted by Šarur's first report.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17-23

The adversary named in that first report is Asag. Asag is a monstrous being whose parentage is unusual and significant: he is the son of Heaven (An) and Earth (Ki). That genealogy places him, in the Sumerian theogonic family tree, as a sibling of Enlil's — his uncle, if the relationship is read literally, and certainly his peer at the level of cosmic seniority. Asag rules the mountains and is served by an army of stone-warriors.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: Šarur's announcement of Asag's birth and of the stone-warriors in Asag's retinue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

The mountains in this poem are not a neutral landscape. The Sumerian term is _kur_, and its semantic range is wide: it can mean "mountain," "foreign land," or "netherworld" depending on context, and across the Sumerian literary corpus it accumulates a cluster of negative associations. The _kur_ is where things go when they die and where hostile peoples come from, as well as where the physical mountains lie. When Ninurta marches against the _kur_, he is marching against a zone that carries all three of those associations at once. The word does the work of a three-way description without needing three separate terms, and any Sumerian reader — or, later, any Akkadian scribe-apprentice taught the word in its Sumerian context — would have understood that all three connotations were active.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 passim (lines 24ff., 70–118); for the semantic range of _kur_ see Forsyth 1987, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, passim

The weapon that alerts Ninurta is named Šarur, and it is one of the strangest features of the poem. Šarur is not a passive implement. Throughout _Lugal-e_ the mace is an animate, speaking agent — it flies, it scouts, it reports, it argues, it counsels, it praises. The text treats it as a character. This animate-weapon motif is a structural feature of the composition, not an occasional flourish; Šarur speaks more lines than most of the minor deities in the poem, and the combat sequence cannot be followed without paying attention to what the mace says and when.

A reader encountering Šarur for the first time may be tempted to read the speaking weapon as a stylistic convention — a poetic personification, a trick by which the poet dramatises the hero's own deliberations. That reading will run into difficulty. The mace in _Lugal-e_ does not express Ninurta's inner voice; it repeatedly disagrees with him, and the disagreements drive the plot. Šarur is a separate agent. The fact that his voice belongs to the weapon rather than to a separate deity is part of what makes the composition distinctive, and it is part of what makes the poem a natural reference point when later traditions give storm-gods named or speaking weapons of their own.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17–23 and passim: Šarur speaks, flies, reports, and argues. The animate-weapon motif is a structural feature of the composition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17-23 and passim

=== The scouting and the challenge

Šarur, then, is the scout. His first speech to Ninurta delivers the news: Asag has been born from the union of An and Ki, and now reigns among the mountains. The mace reports what it has seen. The report is not diplomatic preamble but raw intelligence: parentage, territory, current status. It is the kind of briefing that a military commander would want from a scout returning from a reconnaissance sweep, and the poem delivers it in those terms.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: Šarur's first speech to Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

Asag does not rule alone. He is surrounded by an army of stone-warriors, and the ETCSL composite names several of the stone-types that serve him. They include _šu_, _saĝkal_, _esi_, _usium_, and _kagena_ — a catalogue of Sumerian stone-names, some of which can be identified with known materials (_esi_ is diorite, _kagena_ is haematite) and some of which remain philological puzzles. The catalogue matters for a reason that will become clear only at the end of the poem, when each of these stone-types is summoned back for judgement.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: the stone-warrior catalogue in Šarur's first report.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

Šarur's first report also gives the reason why Ninurta must act. Asag's forces are terrorising the cities of the land. This is not a distant cosmic threat; the poem frames it as a political-military crisis pressing on the settled Sumerian world from the uplands to the east. The combat, in other words, is not a contest in some abstract zone of the cosmos. It is a response to a concrete emergency affecting the cities whose priests and scribes would eventually copy and recite the poem.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: Šarur's description of Asag's depredations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

The mace then delivers a second speech. Its tone shifts from report to call-to-arms: Šarur urges Ninurta to act, and he frames the threat as a direct challenge to Ninurta's kingship and to the divine powers that support it. The logic is explicit. Asag's rule in the mountains is, by implication, a rival rule. If Ninurta does not respond, the rivalry stands.

This is a significant framing. Šarur is not saying that the cities need rescuing because they are suffering. He says that the adversary's existence is a challenge to the warrior-god's own kingship — the kingship that has been established in the proem and that the poem has already been celebrating. The mace is telling the hero that what is at stake is political, in the specifically theological sense of that word: whose rule, which gods'.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 48–56: Šarur's second speech, a call to arms.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 48-56

A third speech follows, and it is more unsettling than the first two. Šarur now describes Asag's invulnerability. Conventional weapons, the mace warns, cannot penetrate the adversary. Asag's hide is of an order that does not yield to the ordinary instruments of combat. The weapon is telling the warrior-god that the ordinary tools of his trade will not work. It is a strange thing for a weapon to say, and it is meant to be. A mace that reports its own inadequacy against a particular target is a mace that has looked at the target closely, and the report is therefore as credible as any intelligence the hero is going to receive.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 57–69: Šarur on Asag's hide and on the limits of ordinary weaponry.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 57-69

Ninurta, roused by the reports, gathers his army and his storm-weapons and marches toward the mountain lands. The text describes the march as attended by cosmic disturbance: clouds rise around him, lightning accompanies him, and the eight winds come out to walk with him. This is the first indication in the poem that Ninurta is not just a warrior but a storm-warrior, and that his approach to the _kur_ alters the weather over Sumer.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 70–95: Ninurta's mobilisation and the storm-imagery of his approach.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 70-95

A first combat phase follows. Ninurta destroys the enemy cities and rebel settlements of the _kur_ — not Asag himself, but the strongholds subordinate to him. Šarur, meanwhile, flies forward in a separate role: he functions as a reconnaissance weapon, covering ground that his wielder cannot, and returns to Ninurta with reports on Asag's movements. The mace is now acting as a scout as well as a weapon.

This division of labour is one of the features that makes the scouting sequence of _Lugal-e_ structurally unusual. In most warrior-narratives, a hero does his own reconnaissance or gets it from a human subordinate. Here the reconnaissance is done by the weapon, and it is done at a scale that allows the weapon to cover the whole landscape between Ninurta's army and Asag's mountain. The poem is exploiting the animate-weapon motif to solve a narrative problem — how to give the reader access to the adversary without putting the hero in premature contact with him — and the solution is elegant. Šarur is the poem's camera.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 96–118: the first combat phase and Šarur's reconnaissance flight.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 96-118

It is in one of these reconnaissance returns that Šarur does something that sets the poem apart from the cleaner victorious-warrior narratives with which readers may be familiar. The mace delivers a catalogue. It runs through Ninurta's earlier victories — the monsters Ninurta has previously defeated — and the list is long. It includes Kuli-ana, the Dragon, the Gypsum-monster, the Copper-monster, the Six-headed Ram, the Magilum-boat, Saman-ana, the Bison, the Palm-king, Anzud (the lion-headed eagle), and the Seven-headed Snake. This passage is sometimes called the "trophy list" of Ninurta's combats, and it reads, at first, like a catalogue of triumphs — a reminder of the hero's credentials.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119–134: Šarur's catalogue of Ninurta's prior victories, sometimes known as the "trophy list" of Ninurta's combats.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119-134

It is not flattery, though. The catalogue functions as an argument from precedent, and the direction of the argument is not the one a reader expects. Šarur is listing the past victories to measure the gravity of the present challenge, not to cheer his wielder into battle. The weight of the catalogue — eleven defeated adversaries, many of them serious — becomes, in the mace's mouth, a scale on which the new adversary is going to be weighed.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119–150: Šarur's transition from catalogue to warning.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119-150

And Asag, Šarur tells Ninurta, outweighs them all. This is the unusual moment. The mace, having recited the trophy list, urges caution. Asag surpasses every previous foe. Šarur pleads with Ninurta not to engage directly. The weapon is attempting to dissuade its own wielder from combat. It is a scene with no close parallel in the surviving warrior-narrative corpus of the Near East: the hero's own weapon, without ceasing to be loyal, tells the hero that this fight is beyond him.

The sequence of three speeches — report, call-to-arms, warning — and then the trophy-list-and-caution combination together give Šarur a larger speaking role in the first half of the composition than any other figure aside from Ninurta himself. The weapon's speeches are not ornamental interludes between the action scenes. They are the mechanism by which the poem introduces its adversary, calibrates the stakes, and stages a debate about whether the combat should happen at all. A reader who skims over Šarur's speeches will miss the plot.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 135–150: Šarur's caution, an unusual moment in which the weapon dissuades its wielder.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 135-150

Ninurta refuses. He will not withdraw. He advances into the mountain. The text marks the scene with images of day turning to darkness as he marches — the storm-warrior has brought his weather with him, and his approach has dimmed the sun over the _kur_.

The refusal to be counselled by his own weapon is characteristic of the hero and it is necessary for the plot. If Šarur's warnings had worked, the poem would end at line 150 with Ninurta turning back, and there would be no combat, no Tigris, no mountain-naming, no stone-judgements. The hero's refusal to listen is, in the narrative's economy, what permits the rest of the poem to happen. The text does not moralise about it. The refusal is reported and then absorbed, and the narrative moves on.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 151–167: Ninurta's refusal to withdraw and the advance into the kur.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 151-167

When Asag emerges against him, the force of the adversary is described in apocalyptic terms. Cosmic order shakes. The gods of the pantheon are reported as cowering before the confrontation. Whatever Asag is, the text makes him an order of being at which the sky itself flinches.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 168–186: Asag's emergence, cosmic upheaval, and the gods' reaction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 168-186

The narrative cuts, at this pitch, to Enlil in Nippur. The father-god speaks to his consort Ninlil, and what he says is a lament, not a command. "My son is no longer here; what supports me?" Enlil says. His son has gone out of sight into the _kur_, and the pillars of Enlil's own authority — the son who acts for him, the martial arm that executes his will — are shaken. The passage is one of the most psychologically candid in the Sumerian corpus: the chief god, at the crisis point of the combat, is not issuing orders. He is voicing doubt.

That doubt is structurally important. If Enlil were confident, the combat would be a formality and the poem a triumphal march. The lament tells the reader that the outcome of the fight with Asag was, in the narrative's frame, uncertain even to the chief god who had authorised it. It also sets up the intercession scene that follows, because someone — some character — has to resolve Enlil's paralysis, and the poem gives that job not to another deity but to the speaking mace.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 187–190: Enlil's lament to Ninlil.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 187-190

Šarur, at this point, does something remarkable for a weapon. He flies from the battlefield — away from his wielder, in the middle of the combat — all the way back to Enlil at the E-kur in Nippur. The mace is now acting as an intercessor. He goes to Enlil to receive further instruction on behalf of the son who has gone into the _kur_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 191–214: Šarur's errand to Enlil and the intercession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 191-214

Enlil, thus prompted, issues the authorisation that was missing. He blesses Ninurta's mission, but he adds a condition. Ninurta is permitted to slay Asag — the violence is authorised — but the fertility of the land and the species that dwell upon it must be preserved. The charge is not only "defeat the adversary." It is "defeat the adversary without ruining the world you are defending." This conditional structure is important; it shapes everything that follows.

It is also, in a quiet way, the reason the poem has its distinctive aftermath. If Enlil had issued an unconditional authorisation, Ninurta could have killed Asag and walked home. The conditional charge obliges the hero to repair the damage the combat will inevitably cause. The long second half of the poem — the dam, the mountain, the stones, the grain-stewardship — is, in a sense, the execution of that condition. Enlil has asked his son to defend the land in a way that leaves the land still able to feed its people when the fighting is done.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 215–224: Enlil's charge to Ninurta through Šarur.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 215-224

Šarur races back with the mandate. The text marks the mace's speed and joy at bearing the authorising word; the weapon is glad to carry the sanction that will let the combat finish. The mace's mood, it is worth noting, has now shifted. Through the trophy-list and the warnings, the speaking weapon has been trying to prevent the combat. With the authorisation from Enlil in hand, the same weapon is now eager for the combat to proceed. The animate agent has not changed; the conditions under which he was counselling caution have.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 225–227: Šarur returns with Enlil's instructions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 225-227

Upon reaching Ninurta, Šarur delivers a specific battle-directive derived from Enlil's charge. Pierce Asag's liver, he tells the warrior, and bring that organ to the E-kur. The instruction is oddly surgical; it specifies the target, and it specifies what to do with the trophy. The liver, in Mesopotamian divinatory culture, was the seat of vital force, the organ read by priest-diviners for omens. To pierce the liver is to strike the adversary's centre; to carry the liver to Enlil's temple is to deliver the proof of the strike to the god who authorised it.

The narrative has now rounded a full cycle. Šarur opens the poem by alerting Ninurta; he closes the pre-combat sequence by telling Ninurta exactly where to strike. In between, he has reported, urged, warned, scouted, praised the past, and counselled retreat. He has also, in a moment of real structural weight, carried a message across the full length of the narrative geography — from the battlefield in the _kur_ to Enlil's throne at the E-kur — and back again. The combat can begin in earnest only because the weapon has done this preparatory work.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 228–243: Šarur's delivery of Enlil's specific combat instruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 228-243

=== The combat

The combat intensifies. Ninurta's weapons devastate the landscape — the storm-warrior's arsenal is not delicate — but Asag endures. The adversary's invulnerability, which Šarur had warned about in his third speech, is not an exaggeration. Whatever Ninurta does is absorbed. Šarur interjects again. The mace urges retreat.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 244–264: the exchange of blows and Šarur's renewed caution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 244-264

Šarur's final plea frames the adversary in new terms. Asag is storm-like and uncontrollable, the mace says. He is a force of an order unfamiliar to conventional combat — not a warrior who can be out-fought, but a kind of weather-system with hostile intent. The mace's counsel is consistent: this is not a fight Ninurta can win by pressing harder.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 265–280: Šarur's last cautionary speech.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 265-280

Ninurta presses harder anyway, and this time he succeeds. He overpowers Asag and reduces the adversary to a heap of rubble and dust. The slaying fulfils the combat charge from Enlil — the liver has been pierced, the adversary has fallen, the mandate has been executed.

The description of the defeated body as a heap of rubble and dust is the first signal of what the defeated adversary is about to become, even before Ninurta names him Stone. The adversary does not dissolve into sea-foam or into atmospheric elements, as defeated adversaries will in some later combat myths. He falls apart into the kind of matter that is already at work in the Sumerian landscape — rubble and dust, the building material of embankments and of mountain-heaps. The descriptive word-choice anticipates the geographical work that the rest of the poem will carry out.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 281–299: Ninurta's victory over Asag.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 281-299

The scene immediately following the slaying is brief but significant. The gods prostrate themselves before Ninurta. The pantheon that was cowering a few hundred lines earlier is now acknowledging the victor. Šarur, for his part, turns from counsellor back into a voice of praise and sings the victor's praise.

The mace's shift of role here is worth noting. Through the first half of the composition, Šarur's voice has been an agent of restraint — a check on the warrior's impulse to fight. After the slaying, the voice becomes celebratory. The weapon has not changed character; it has moved from one phase of its function to the next. A mace that can speak can also sing, and the poem makes full use of that range.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 300–309: divine homage and Šarur's victory hymn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 300-309

Ninurta then pronounces a decree over the defeated body. Henceforth, he says, the adversary shall be called "Stone." The renaming is not incidental. It transforms the adversary's narrative category. Asag the mountain-demon, son of An and Ki, becomes Stone — the material. And because Asag had an army of stone-warriors as his retinue, that single decree sets up the long aetiological sequence that will occupy the poem's final third: the judgement of each stone-type according to what it did in the battle.

The renaming deserves a moment of attention on its own, because it changes what kind of poem the reader is reading. Up to this point, _Lugal-e_ has been a combat narrative. With the renaming, it becomes a charter for material culture. The adversary, having been defeated as a person, is now available to be treated as a category of matter; and the stone-warriors of his retinue, having been defeated as combatants, are available to be treated as stone-types with destinies that can be fixed. The decree is the pivot on which the second half of the composition turns.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 310–330: the naming of the defeated Asag as Stone, transitioning the narrative toward the lapidary aetiology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 310-330

Before moving on to the stones, Ninurta pronounces a blessing over his own club — a formulaic closure of the combat proper. The weapon that struck the decisive blow receives a brief consecration. The structural cue is that the combat-episode is now over.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 331–333: the mace's blessing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 331-333

=== Ordering the waters

The aftermath of combat, in _Lugal-e_, is not triumph. It is crisis. The landscape has been disturbed. The waters of the mountain are blocked; the fields lie barren; famine threatens the land. Ninurta has killed Asag but, in killing him, has broken the hydraulic order on which the cultivated Sumerian plain depends. Enlil's condition — preserve the fertility of the land — is now a task, not a constraint. The slaying, on its own, has done nothing to feed anyone.

The structural turn here is worth noticing. The slaying of Asag does not produce the cosmos by itself. The combat is step one; everything that makes the world work — the river, the mountain, the material hierarchy, the administrative order — still has to be built afterwards, piece by piece, under the constraint that Enlil has imposed. The poem, in other words, refuses the short cut in which victory and cosmic order are the same event.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 334–346: the post-war ecological crisis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 334-346

Ninurta addresses the water-crisis as an engineer would. He heaps up a great embankment of stones over the mountain. The uncontrolled waters are dammed and channelled. The text describes the construction in technical terms that would be recognisable to any Sumerian farmer who had ever helped clear a canal: the stones are used to build a barrage, the barrage tames the mountain run-off, and the tamed run-off is directed into a single channel — the Tigris.

That the barrage is built out of _stones_ is itself meaningful. The stones that Ninurta uses are, within the narrative logic of the poem, pieces of the defeated adversary and his army. The hero is not calling up new material from nothing. He is taking the body of the defeated combatant, and the bodies of the stone-warriors who fought with the combatant, and using them to build the dam. The poem is careful about this. The material of the landscape the hero now makes is the same material that resisted him a hundred lines earlier.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 347–359: the construction of the stone-barrage and the engineering of the Tigris flow.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 347-359

The Tigris is the great river of eastern Mesopotamia. In _Lugal-e_ it is Ninurta's reordering of its flow — after the combat, as the second half of his cosmogonic work — that establishes the irrigation regime on which the farmland of Sumer depends. The poem is claiming, as a matter of mythic charter, that the hydraulic system which Sumerian farmers actually used owes its existence to Ninurta's engineering after the defeat of Asag.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 347–367: the Tigris reordering; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the cosmological reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

With the Tigris set in order, the consequences unfold immediately. Barley flourishes. Kings rejoice. The trading colonies expand. The text registers, in a few quick lines, a restored economic and agricultural prosperity: the fields are producing, the rulers are benefiting, the commercial networks that the Sumerian city-states ran with their neighbours are once again functioning. The combat's order-making work is beginning to show its dividends.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 360–367: the prosperity that follows the water-ordering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 360-367

=== Naming the mountain

One consequence of the combat has not yet been addressed, and it is at this point that a new character enters the narrative. Ninurta's mother, the goddess variously named Ninmaḫ, comes on stage. She laments the inaccessibility of the mountains where she used to dwell. The landscape that Ninurta has just reshaped includes her old territory, and she has been cut off from it. She travels to her son to make the case for her relationship to the newly reordered _kur_.

The entry of the mother-goddess is a change of register in the composition. Up to this point the poem has been martial and hydraulic. With Ninmaḫ's arrival, a familial note enters. The encounter between son and mother is also the mechanism by which a senior deity of the pantheon receives a new title, which is how the poem performs its second major piece of cosmogonic work — not by renaming the adversary, as before, but by renaming a goddess.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 368–386: Ninmaḫ's lament and journey to Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 368-386

Ninurta honours his mother. He decrees that the stone-heap raised over the defeated Asag shall be called "Mountain" — _ḫursaĝ_ in Sumerian — and that she shall be its divine keeper under the new name Ninḫursaĝa, "Lady of the Mountain." The decree is double: it gives a name to a new feature of the cosmic landscape, and it reassigns the mother-goddess to be the presiding deity of that feature. She enters the poem as Ninmaḫ and leaves it as Ninḫursaĝa.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410: the renaming of the mother goddess as Ninḫursaĝa and the endowment of the mountain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387-410

Ninurta then endows the new Mountain with abundance. Herbs, honey, metals, and wildlife are conferred on it as its produce. The mountain is not to be a dead heap of stones. It is to be a productive ecological zone, and the list of its endowments — aromatic plants, sweetener, ore, game — corresponds to the kinds of goods that the lowland Sumerian cities obtained from the uplands in practice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410: the mountain's endowment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387-410

The renaming episode ties the combat's aftermath directly to the cultic profile of Ninḫursaĝa, one of the senior goddesses of the Sumerian pantheon. The poem is, among other things, offering a narrative etymology for one of her titles — the title that gives her dominion over the mountain that Ninurta has just raised out of Asag's defeated stone-army.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the cosmogonic-royal logic of the renaming.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

=== The judgement of the stones

The mountain has been named. The waters have been ordered. There remains the matter of Asag's defeated army — the stone-warriors who fought on his side. A new divine voice now intervenes to raise this unfinished business. The goddess Aruru, another senior mother-goddess of the Sumerian pantheon, reminds Ninurta that the fates of the slain warriors — now lying as stones at the feet of the newly named mountain — have yet to be fixed.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–434: the opening of the stone-judgements, prompted by Aruru.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411-434

What follows is one of the most remarkable sequences in Sumerian literature. The lapidary aetiology — the judgement of the stones — runs approximately from line 416 to line 647, more than two hundred lines. It assigns each stone-type a destiny: honour or contempt, cultic use or mundane use, reward or punishment, according to what that stone-type did during the battle. Stones that resisted Ninurta are punished. Stones that supported him, or at least stayed neutral, are rewarded. The destinies that Ninurta pronounces are not mythic abstractions; they map, type by type, onto the actual ritual and craft-economic status of those stones in historical Mesopotamian practice.

This is worth pausing on before the individual stone-judgements are walked through, because it is an unusual kind of narrative work for a combat myth to do. Most combat myths in the region and period, when they generate aftermath, generate it in the form of geography and kingship: a mountain is raised, a river is re-routed, a throne is established. _Lugal-e_ does those things too. But it also, uniquely within the corpus, generates a catalogue of materials — a classification of the mineral world into reward-categories and punishment-categories that corresponds precisely to how Sumerian craftsmen actually worked those materials. Temple-grade stones earn their status by loyalty in battle. Abrasive stones earn their abrasive use by resistance. The poem is giving the mineral economy of Sumer a mythic charter, judgement by judgement, over more than two hundred lines. The modern reader may find the sequence long. A Sumerian scribe-apprentice copying it at a tablet-house in Nippur would have found it a working manual for why the objects around him — the temple statue, the king's seal, the grinding-stone in the kitchen — were made of the materials they were made of.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 416–647: the lapidary aetiology as a whole; for the ritual-aetiological reading see Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 416-647

The sequence opens with a curse. The _u_ stone, which modern scholarship identifies as emery — an abrasive mineral used for grinding and polishing — is the first to receive judgement. Ninurta curses it. Its resistance in the battle, the text says, is punished by an unfavourable destiny.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–434: Ninurta's curse on the emery stone (_u_).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411-434

The second judgement is a reward. The _šu_ and _gasura_ stones are assigned to be shaped by the goldsmith to form a matrix for ritual creations. They will be used, the decree says, to hold "the first fruits of the gods" at the time of the new moon. This is a specific cultic privilege: these stones will be in direct contact with the offerings that mark the lunar calendar, and the goldsmith — a high-status craftsman in the Sumerian economy — will be the artisan who shapes them.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 435–447: "you shall be shaped by him to form a matrix for his creations" and "people shall place the first fruits of the gods on you at the time of the new moon."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 435-447

The _saĝkal_, _gulgul_, and _saĝĝar_ stones are judged next, and together. They are condemned to contempt, poverty, and servitude. The triple judgement suggests that in Ninurta's assessment they acted together against him, and their punishment is correspondingly collective.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 448–462: the punitive judgement on _saĝkal_, _gulgul_, and _saĝĝar_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 448-462

Then comes one of the longer individual judgements, and one of the most important for what it tells the reader about how this poem thinks about materials. The _esi_ stone — diorite — receives a destiny of high honour. It is to be quarried from Magan, the land across the Persian Gulf from which Sumer imported its hard stone, and it is to be shaped for temple use. Specifically, it is to be made into statuary and libation-vessels. Diorite was, historically, the preferred material for royal statues and for ritual bowls in the Sumerian and Akkadian temple corpus; the poem is giving that high cultic status a narrative origin. A reader with access to a museum collection of Sumerian art will have seen the working-out of the verdict the poem issues here.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 463–478: the judgement on _esi_ (diorite), and the Magan sourcing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 463-478

The _na_ stone fares badly. It is cursed: to be broken up, its fragments discarded. The judgement is terse, and the punishment is total.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 479–486: the punitive judgement on _na_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 479-486

The _elel_ stone, by contrast, is rewarded. It is to be set on a pedestal and praised by all the lands. Where _na_ is broken and discarded, _elel_ is displayed and honoured. The opposition is deliberate: the poem is drawing contrasts between successive judgements.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 487–496: the honorific judgement on _elel_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 487-496

The _kagena_ stone — haematite, a dense iron-oxide mineral used in antiquity for seals, weights, and polished objects — receives a destiny of high status. It is blessed to serve as a judge-stone in foreign lands and to be valued alongside gold. The detail that it functions as a judge-stone is not incidental. Haematite weights were used to standardise commercial transactions in the Mesopotamian economy; the stone's juridical function in the narrative maps onto its role in the actual regulation of trade.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 497–511: the judgement on _kagena_ (haematite).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 497-511

The _ĝišnugal_ stone — alabaster, a soft white stone used for seals and for small prestige vessels — is honoured for loyalty and is assigned the role of treasury seal-keeper. Alabaster seals are documented in the Mesopotamian archaeological record, and they were used precisely to authenticate access to valuables. The poem is again giving a historical craft-use a mythic pedigree.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 512–521: the judgement on _ĝišnugal_ (alabaster).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 512-521

The _algameš_ stone is cursed. It is to be consumed by the smiths' fires as a daily sacrifice — used up in metalworking, not preserved. Whatever _algameš_ was, the text is assigning it the role of a consumable flux-material or a tempering stone, its value realised only in its destruction.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 522–527: the punitive judgement on _algameš_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 522-527

A cluster of precious stones is then judged together. _Nir_, _gug_ (carnelian, a red-orange variety of chalcedony) and _zagin_ (lapis lazuli, the deep-blue stone that Mesopotamian craftsmen imported from the east) receive a collective honour. They are to be mounted in precious metal and displayed at libations. Carnelian and lapis lazuli were the two signature jewelry-stones of the Sumerian craft tradition, and the poem's judgement of this cluster gives their high status a mythic rationale.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 528–542: the honorific judgement on the precious-stone cluster.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 528-542

The _ĝir-zu-ĝal_ stone — flint — is cursed. Its horns are split; it is reduced to powder under the hands of metalworkers and carpenters. Flint, in the Sumerian craft economy, had once been a tool-stone for blades and cutting implements; by the second millennium BCE, as metalwork displaced stone-blade cutting, it had been progressively relegated to ancillary uses such as abrasive powder and scraper-chips. The judgement captures the material's declining cultic status.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 543–553: the punitive judgement on _ĝir-zu-ĝal_ (flint).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 543-553

The _iman_ and _alliga_ stones are punished together. Their cries go unheeded, and they are barred from the palace. The image is striking: the stones are given voices, and those voices are then refused an audience. The punishment is social as much as material — these stones will not be allowed into the royal court.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 554–565: the punitive judgement on _iman_ and _alliga_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 554-565

The _mašda_, _dubban_, and _urutum_ stones are condemned collectively and in violent terms. They are to be slaughtered, crushed, sharpened for weapons, and battered. The vocabulary of the judgement is almost butcher's-shop: these stones are not just demoted, they are rendered into working-material and worn out in service.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 566–578: the punitive judgement on _mašda_, _dubban_, and _urutum_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 566-578

The _šagara_ stone is punished in a more ritually specific way. It is cast on the dais and fed malt like a sheep. The image is a parody of a cultic offering-ritual: a stone being treated as though it were a sacrificial animal, and by implication a deliberately low-status one. The judgement is a calibrated lowering of the stone's cultic status.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 579–591: the punitive judgement on _šagara_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 579-591

Then a reward. The _marḫuša_ stone is rewarded: it becomes a filter-bowl for sacred libations and an inlay-work ornament. Both roles are high-status craft applications. A filter-bowl was used to strain offerings before they were poured; inlay-work is the specialist technique of embedding small pieces of coloured stone into a substrate to make decorative panels.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 592–599: the honorific judgement on _marḫuša_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 592-599

A different kind of judgement follows. The _ḫaštum_ stone is not rewarded or cursed; it is renamed for its function. Henceforth, Ninurta decrees, it is to be known as "Ditch" — _ḫaštum_. Its name is derived from the trench-like use to which Ninurta assigns it. The judgement is an aetiology of the stone's own Sumerian name.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 600–608: the renaming of _ḫaštum_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 600-608

The _durul_ stone is honoured in a manner that picks up the international-trade theme again. Ninurta magnifies its name and decrees that foreign nations shall seek it out. The judgement imports a real feature of the Sumerian stone-trade — that certain stones were in demand across the wider region — and gives it a narrative warrant.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 609–617: the honorific judgement on _durul_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 609-617

The _šegšeg_, _engen_, and _ezinum_ stones receive a more complicated set of judgements. The passage in which they occur is dense and difficult, and the outcomes vary from stone to stone. Some of the imagery here is opaque even to modern Sumerologists, and the text's language outruns our ability to map it cleanly onto known craft-uses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 618–633: the mixed judgements on _šegšeg_, _engen_, and _ezinum_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 618-633

The final group of judgements in the sequence is a cultic consecration. The _kurgaranum_, _bal_, and _šembi_ stones — the last of which is kohl, the black pigment used around the eyes in Near Eastern cosmetics and in some cultic contexts — are assigned to the cult of Ninḫursaĝa. In her festivals, they will be honoured. The sequence closes by returning the stones to the goddess who was installed over the mountain a hundred lines earlier, completing a narrative loop.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 634–644: the judgement on _kurgaranum_, _bal_, and _šembi_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 634-644

Taken together, the lapidary aetiology is a retributive structure. Its logic is simple once the reader sees it. Stones that took Asag's side are punished; stones that stayed neutral or favoured Ninurta are rewarded; and the destinies decreed correspond to the actual ritual and craft-economic status of each stone-type in historical Mesopotamian practice. The poem is not inventing a hierarchy of stones. It is narrating the hierarchy that the Sumerian craft economy already used, and providing a mythic rationale for why that hierarchy is the hierarchy it is.

The pattern of the sequence — punishment, reward, punishment, reward, with occasional reuse-and-rename judgements breaking the alternation — gives the lapidary aetiology a shape that rewards careful reading. It is not a bare list. It is a sequence of courtroom verdicts, each handed down over a defeated combatant and each producing a specific working relationship between that combatant and the human world. Some of the stones are assigned to the goldsmith's workshop, some to the palace treasury, some to the temple, some to the smith's forge, some to the cosmetic kit. The verdicts read, in places, like a job-placement programme for a demobilised army.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–644, as a unit; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the lapidary aetiology's mapping onto Sumerian craft-hierarchies.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

[INFERENCE: The lapidary aetiology is simultaneously a narrative reward-and-punishment sequence and an aetiological explanation of the hardness, colour, and ritual status of individual stone-types as used by Sumerian craftsmen. | basis: The stone-judgements correspond closely to the actual uses of these materials documented in the Mesopotamian craft-record (diorite for statuary, carnelian and lapis for jewelry, flint knapped to powder in metalworking). | risk: The one-to-one correspondence is an interpretive reading; the text itself presents the judgements in mythic register.]

=== The triumphant return

With the stone-judgements concluded, the poem turns to its final movement: Ninurta's return to Nippur. The warrior-god embarks on a barge named Ma-kar-nunta-ea for the return voyage. The naming of the barge is typical of Sumerian literary practice — significant vessels receive proper names — and the embarkation is the formal beginning of the triumphal passage home.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 645–651: the triumphal boarding of the barge and the beginning of the return voyage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 645-651

The boatmen sing. Their hymn exalts Ninurta as the unrivalled king of the Anuna, the Sumerian divine assembly. This is the first time in the poem that Ninurta is addressed in a direct royal register by a chorus of voices; the position he has earned is now being sung back to him. The claim that he is unrivalled is, in the poem's logic, now defensible: the rival sovereign from the mountains has been defeated, the stone-warriors have been judged, and the cosmic consequences of the fight have been brought under order. The boatmen's hymn is the first piece of evidence the poem offers that the claim has taken.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 652–661: the boatmen's hymn to Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 652-661

The text then shifts register once more, away from narrative and into prayer. A short passage blesses the devotee who maintains Ninurta's temple. The shift is characteristic of Sumerian hymnic literature, which often embeds prayer-passages addressed to the worshipper inside larger narrative frames.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 662–668: the prayer for the devotee.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 662-668

A brief invocation follows, calling upon the sky-god An and the healing-goddess Bau to look favourably upon the worshipper. The pairing enlists both the highest-ranking deity of the Sumerian theogony and a senior healing-deity in the blessing of the person who keeps Ninurta's cult.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 669–671: the invocation of An and Bau.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 669-671

Ninurta then arrives home. The Anuna — the divine assembly — receive him with prayers and praise. The homecoming is framed as a ritual reception: the pantheon that was shaken at the combat's mid-point, and that prostrated itself immediately after the slaying, now receives the victor formally, in its collective capacity.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 672–680: the Anuna's reception of Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 672-680

Enlil, Ninurta's father, now bestows the final blessing. The content of the blessing is threefold: eternal life, a celestial mace, and an unchanging rule. Each of the three elements corresponds to something Ninurta has earned. The life is the reward for having survived the combat. The mace is the consecration of the weapon-role that the composition has been exploring throughout. The unchanging rule is the formal confirmation of the kingship-status that the boatmen and the Anuna have just sung to him.

The three-part blessing closes, at the level of narrative mechanics, the conditional charge that Enlil issued back at the mid-point of the combat. Enlil had told Šarur that Ninurta could slay the adversary provided the land and its species were preserved. Ninurta has done both. He has killed Asag, and he has ordered the landscape so that it can support life. The final blessing is Enlil's acknowledgement that the condition has been met.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 681–697: Enlil's blessing of Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 681-697

The closing movement of the poem brings one more figure into the ordered cosmos. Ninurta appoints the wise goddess Nisaba as guardian of the granaries and of agricultural order, entrusting her with the stewardship of grain. Nisaba is the Sumerian goddess of writing, accounting, and the grain-harvest — a surprisingly tight overlap, from a modern perspective, but one that makes sense in a culture where literacy was first and most durably a technology for recording barley-yields. The stewardship is a deliberate choice: the god who has just reordered the Tigris now passes the ongoing administration of grain to the deity of writing.

The appointment closes the practical loop that the combat opened. Water was the first problem after the slaying. Stone was the second. Grain — how to grow it, how to store it, how to record it — is the third, and the one that will matter most in the day-to-day working life of the Sumerian cities that the combat was fought to protect. Nisaba's portfolio ties the mythic charter to the bureaucratic machinery that made the cities run.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 698–711: the grain-stewardship entrusted to Nisaba.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 698-711

Extended epithets follow, celebrating Nisaba as judge, as keeper of records, and as divine administrator. These epithets do work the reader might otherwise miss: they tie the post-combat order directly into the scribal-administrative life of the Sumerian city. The poem does not end with military victory and a feast. It ends with the goddess of bureaucracy receiving her portfolio.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 712–723: the praise of Nisaba.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 712-723

The composition closes with a brief invocation calling for the praise of Ninurta and of Enlil. A doxology — the technical term for a short concluding formula of praise — returns the poem to the hymnic register with which it began.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 724–725: the closing doxology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 724-725

The colophon of the composite tablet-tradition identifies the poem as a _šir-sud_ (?) to Ninurta — a classification of Sumerian hymnic song whose exact performance context is uncertain. The question mark in the transliteration is the editors' own, and it signals that the reading of the genre-label is not entirely secure. Whatever _šir-sud_ meant to a Sumerian scribe, it placed _Lugal-e_ in the larger family of praise-songs directed at named gods.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, line 726 (colophon); van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (discussion of the _šir-sud_ classification).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, line 726

=== What the combat makes

Reading the poem as a whole, what is striking is how much order-making work the combat has done by the time the doxology closes it. The fight with Asag occupies perhaps a quarter of the composition; the remaining three-quarters are given over to consequences. This ratio is itself a statement about what kind of poem _Lugal-e_ is. It is not a duel-narrative with ritual footnotes. It is a charter for an entire cosmos — geographical, cultic, economic, royal — in which the duel is the hinge that makes the charter possible. Four separate orderings can be listed.

The combat reorders the flow of the Tigris and establishes the irrigation regime that underwrites Sumerian agriculture. The cosmogonic work, in other words, is hydraulic. The river that Sumerian farmers used is the river whose course Ninurta set after the slaying of Asag. This is the poem's concrete geographical claim: the landscape of settled Sumer exists in the shape it does because of a combat.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 334–367; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on Ninurta's hydraulic ordering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The combat renames the stone-heap as the Mountain (_ḫursaĝ_) and creates the cultic profile of the mother-goddess Ninḫursaĝa. A new geographical feature, a new divine title, and a new cultic portfolio all emerge out of a single decree over the defeated body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The combat fixes the destinies of the stones, assigning to each stone-type its ritual status, its craft-use, and its social value within the Sumerian economy. The mineral hierarchy of Sumerian craft — why diorite is temple-grade, why flint is ground to dust, why carnelian and lapis are jewelry-grade — is given a narrative pedigree.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–644; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

And Ninurta's victory in _Lugal-e_ provides a model of the victorious king. This model becomes the template for Sumerian and later Akkadian royal ideology of the warrior-ruler. Kings who want to claim the authority of a Ninurta-like figure — who want to claim that their military success has established an order that the scribes and priests of Enlil can certify — have a narrative charter in this poem. The charter outlives the Sumerian language itself.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the royal-legitimation function of the Ninurta narratives.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Each of these four is a separate kind of work — hydraulic, geographical, economic, political — and all four are generated by the same combat. The combat myth, in the form _Lugal-e_ gives it, is a multi-purpose instrument. It can be read as cosmogony, as royal legitimation, as craft-economic aetiology, or as a narrative about divine family relations, and none of those readings exhausts it. This multi-functionality is part of why the poem remained useful to Mesopotamian scribal culture for two thousand years.

=== Place in the corpus

_Lugal-e_ has a particular position in the history of scholarship on combat myths, and it is worth naming briefly. Hermann Gunkel's 1895 thesis — the book that gave the category "Chaoskampf" its name and that is the starting point for this volume's introduction — already identified the Sumerian Ninurta narratives as important antecedents to the combat-myth pattern he was reconstructing for Genesis 1 and Revelation 12. Gunkel worked from the imperfect textual access of his period; what he knew of the Ninurta tradition came largely through its later bilingual receptions and through the fragmentary cuneiform publications available in the 1890s. The basic intuition, however, was correct. _Lugal-e_ belongs in the deep background of the scholarly argument he was making. The intervening century of philological work has filled in the tradition Gunkel could only glimpse, and the tradition has turned out to be richer and stranger than he supposed.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, on Sumerian antecedents; the _Lugal-e_ tradition reached Gunkel primarily through its later bilingual receptions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The reassessment of Gunkel's thesis that Scurlock and Beal edited in 2013 revisits this material with the benefit of the intervening century. _Creation and Chaos_ contains chapters on the Ninurta corpus that relocate the combat-myth framework within the specific politics and cultic logic of Sumerian and Babylonian religion, rather than treating it as a mirror of some generic primordial-imagination-of-chaos. The Ballentine-style critique — that these texts are about rival sovereignty, not formlessness — applies to _Lugal-e_ as cleanly as it does to _Enūma Eliš_. Asag is not an unstructured adversary. He is a rival sovereign, the son of An and Ki, who commands an army, controls a territory, and sets up a competing order in the mountains. Ninurta defeats that order and replaces it with his own.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, chapters on Mesopotamian combat-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=chapters on Mesopotamian combat-myth

One more transmission-history point belongs here before the chapter closes. _Lugal-e_ was excerpted into bilingual Sumerian-Akkadian school tablets through the first millennium BCE. The Akkadian-speaking scribes of the later Babylonian and Assyrian periods could no longer read Sumerian fluently, but they continued to copy and gloss the poem as part of the scholarly curriculum, producing interlinear editions in which each line of Sumerian is followed by an Akkadian rendering. This long afterlife is the mechanism by which the Ninurta-Asag narrative reached the later Mesopotamian combat-myth tradition at all. It is a reception-history matter rather than a part of the original story — but the curriculum-copying is why we have the tablets we have, and it is why later compositions that draw on _Lugal-e_ could do so.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction on first-millennium Akkadian bilingualisation); per the chapter brief, Akkadian bilingual reception is transmission-history, not main narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, introduction on bilingualisation

The net result, from the point of view of a modern reader, is that _Lugal-e_ sits at the beginning of the chain of combat compositions that this book traces. It is not a source in the simple sense — there is no textual inheritance that runs in a straight line from this poem to every later combat myth in the Near East — but it is the earliest full-length example we possess of a narrative in which a warrior-god defeats a monstrous adversary and then, in the same composition, uses the victory to establish the irrigated landscape, the cultic profile of a mother-goddess, the hierarchy of craft materials, and the template for royal legitimation. Each of those functions, separately or in combination, will reappear across the chapters that follow. The comparative work belongs to Part II of the book. The particular shape that _Lugal-e_ gives to combat-plus-order-making is the contribution the Sumerian tradition makes to the larger argument, and it is the reason this chapter comes first.
