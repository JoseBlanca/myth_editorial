=== Source overview

This chapter is a comparative-philological synthesis chapter rather than a primary-source narrative chapter; it draws on two layers of evidence — (1) the IE-comparativist anchor that supplies the chapter's central reconstruction and the bulk of its philological exhibits (Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, Parts IV–V), and (2) the per-tradition area-specialist anchors that ground each daughter-language reflex in its own primary-text witness (Jamison & Brereton 2014 for Vedic; Benveniste & Renou 1934 for Avestan; Hoffner 1998 and Beckman 1982 for Hittite; Larrington 2014 and Faulkes 1987 for Old Norse; West 1966 and Ogden 2013 for Greek; Russell 1987 for Armenian; Bailey & Ivanova 1998 for Slavic *bylina*; Tolkien 1936 for the load-bearing critical move on the *Beowulf* dragon).footnote:[Per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml sources.anchor on the four methodological-comparative anchors (Watkins 1995; West 2007; Benveniste & Renou 1934; Jamison & Brereton 2014) and chapters_drawn_from on the eight Part-I chapters (Chs 6, 11, 13, 14, 15, 18, 19, 20) whose own area-specialist anchors carry the per-tradition narrative-claims layer; Watkins, C., *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1995), entire volume — especially Parts IV–V — as the chapter's principal philological anchor.]

The chapter is the fifth of the eight Part-II comparative chapters (C1 through C8) and is positioned as the load-bearing philological chapter for the Indo-European distribution of the combat-myth, distinguished within the Part-II sequence from the cosmogonic (C1), sovereignty (C2), cyclical-maintenance (C3), and eschatological (C4) subtype-chapters by treating the combat across IE daughter languages as the *linguistic*-and-*poetic* fact whose explanation is genealogical inheritance from a shared ancestor rather than as a typological category whose explanation is structural-functional or independent invention.footnote:[Per scope.md §"Book structure" Part II on the Part-II comparative chapters' division of labour and on C5's status as the book's IE-philological chapter; per glossary.yaml on "HERO SLAY SERPENT (formula)" first-mention gloss "Calvert Watkins's reconstructed Indo-European poetic formula (subject + verb-of-killing + serpent-object), attested in Vedic áhann áhim, Hittite kuenta... illuyanka, and elsewhere".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (formula as IE poetic inheritance)

The chapter's central argument follows Watkins's 1995 reconstruction: the verbal formula HERO SLAY SERPENT (a subject-noun naming the warrior, a verb of killing, and a serpent-object naming the adversary) is preserved across the IE daughter languages at the level of *lexical-and-syntactic* correspondences (Vedic *áhann áhim*; Avestan cognates; Hittite *kuenta… illuyanka*) rather than at the level of structural-typological resemblance alone, and the philological correspondences are best explained as inheritance from a Proto-Indo-European poetic tradition rather than as contact-borrowing or independent convergence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon*, Parts IV–V on the lexical-and-syntactic basis of the reconstruction and on the Indo-European inheritance reading; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml comparative_argument on the chapter's framing of the reconstruction as the strongest linguistic argument for a shared IE combat-tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (lexical-syntactic inheritance reading)

The chapter holds Watkins's argument open to two distinct kinds of pressure. First, the chapter is explicit about the gradient between direct philological evidence (the Vedic / Avestan / Hittite triple of §II, where Watkins's formula stands on regular lexical correspondences) and typological-or-fragmentary evidence (the §III extended cases, where the philological signal weakens and the typological signal carries more of the comparative weight). === I. The formula

Calvert Watkins, in *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* (Oxford University Press, 1995), reconstructs an Indo-European poetic formula of the form HERO SLAY SERPENT, in which a subject-noun naming the warrior, a verb of killing, and a serpent-object naming the adversary together name the combat in a fixed verbal-syntactic structure that the volume's framing chapters lay out as the central philological exhibit of the work.footnote:[Watkins, C., *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1995), Parts IV–V framing chapters on the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula's reconstruction as subject + verb-of-killing + serpent-object, and on the volume's central exhibit-status of the formula; per glossary.yaml on "HERO SLAY SERPENT (formula)" first-mention gloss.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V framing chapters

The formula is not a fixed verbal sequence at the level of word-order — Watkins is explicit that the daughter-language reflexes vary in word-order according to the metrical and syntactic norms of each daughter language — but a fixed *lexical-and-semantic* structure in which three slots (warrior-subject, killing-verb, serpent-object) are filled by inherited words whose etymologies recur across the IE family.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the formula as a lexical-and-semantic structure rather than a fixed surface-word-order sequence, and on the daughter-language word-order variation; per the brief's structure section I on the formula as subject + verb-of-killing + serpent-object.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (lexical-semantic structure, not surface order)

The Vedic reflex anchoring Watkins's reconstruction is *áhann áhim* — "he slew the serpent" — from Rigveda 1.32.2, the second verse of the principal Indra-Vṛtra hymn whose opening verse announces the theme of Indra's manly deeds: the third-person preterite of the verb *han-* ("to strike, to kill") combined with the accusative-singular of *ahi-* ("serpent") supplies a verb-and-object pairing whose etymological components recur across the IE family in cognate forms.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, RV 1.32.1–2, on the opening verse's announcement of Indra's manly deeds and on the *áhann áhim* phrasing in the second verse; Watkins 1995, ch. 46 (and the broader Part V), on *áhann áhim* as the Vedic reflex closest to the reconstructed Indo-European form; per glossary.yaml on the locked rendering "áhann áhim" with diacritics.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32.1-2
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (áhann áhim as reflex anchor)

The Vedic verbal root *han-* ("to strike, to kill") is the principal verb-of-killing in the formula and is etymologically connected, on Watkins's analysis and on the wider Indo-Iranian-comparative literature, with the Avestan verbal root *jan-* and with the Hittite verbal root *kuen-* / *kwen-* (third-singular preterite *kuenta*), all from a reconstructed Proto-Indo-European root *gʷhen-* "to strike".footnote:[Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the *gʷhen-* root and its Vedic *han-* / Avestan *jan-* / Hittite *kuen-* daughter-language reflexes as the verbal-root anchor of the formula's killing-verb slot; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Indo-Iranian *han-* / *jan-* cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48 (PIE *gʷhen- and its daughter-language reflexes)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian *han- / *jan- cognancy

The Vedic noun *ahi-* ("serpent") is the principal serpent-object of the formula and is philologically cognate, by regular Indo-Iranian sound-laws, with the Avestan noun *aži-*, the same word for "serpent" appearing as the first element in the name of the Avestan dragon-tyrant Aži Dahāka; the Vedic-Avestan *ahi-* / *aži-* pair is one of the cleanest Indo-Iranian noun-cognancies in the dragon-combat lexicon.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vedic *ahi-* / Avestan *aži-* cognancy as one of the cleanest Indo-Iranian noun-cognancies; Watkins 1995, ch. 46, on the same; per glossary.yaml on "ahi" first-mention gloss "'serpent' (Sanskrit); the word for Vṛtra in the áhann áhim formula; philologically cognate with Avestan aži".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Vedic ahi / Avestan aži cognancy
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (ahi / aži cognancy)

The Avestan reflex of the formula is most commonly cited in the form *janat aiim* ("he slew the serpent") and similar verb-noun pairings in the Avestan yashts, in which the Avestan verbal root *jan-* (cognate with Vedic *han-*) and the Avestan noun *aži-* (cognate with Vedic *ahi-*) instantiate the same lexical-and-semantic structure that Vedic *áhann áhim* instantiates in Vedic Sanskrit.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan verb-and-noun pairing in the yashts and on the formula's instantiation in Avestan; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the Avestan reflex of the formula and on the *jan-* / *han-* and *aži-* / *ahi-* cognancy basis; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml comparative_argument naming "*janat aiim*" as the Avestan reflex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Avestan yashts (verb-noun pairing)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48 (Avestan reflex)

The Hittite reflex of the formula is *kuenta… illuyanka-*, "he slew… the serpent", from CTH 321 (the Illuyanka tablets), in which the Hittite verbal root *kuen-* (third-singular preterite *kuenta*) is cognate with Vedic *han-* and Avestan *jan-* under the regular Anatolian / non-Anatolian sound-law correspondence, and the noun *illuyanka-* is the common Hittite word for "serpent" used as the proper name of the storm-god's adversary in the same tablets.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46, on the Hittite verb-and-object pairing *kuenta… illuyanka-* in CTH 321 and on the Anatolian / non-Anatolian sound-law correspondence underlying the verbal-root cognancy with Vedic *han-* and Avestan *jan-*; Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2, on the Hittite text containing the pairing; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *editio princeps*.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (Hittite cognate formulation)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

The cross-language identity of the formula is therefore *philological* in a strict sense: the verb-slot is filled by a reflex of PIE *gʷhen-* in each of the three daughter languages (Vedic *han-* / Avestan *jan-* / Hittite *kuen-*), and the noun-slot is filled by reflexes of two distinct PIE etyma (Vedic *ahi-* / Avestan *aži-* on the one hand, Hittite *illuyanka-* on the other), with the lexical-substitutability of the noun-slot being one of Watkins's points about the formula's structure rather than its words.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the philological structure of the formula as verb-slot + object-slot with the verb-slot showing tighter cross-language lexical correspondence than the object-slot, and on the formula as a structure-of-slots rather than a fixed phrase; per the brief's structure section I on the subject + verb-of-killing + serpent-object framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (formula as structure-of-slots)

The formula's distinctive feature, on Watkins's reading, is that it is a *poetic* inheritance — preserved in the verbal diction of poets composing in successive IE languages — rather than a *narrative* inheritance, a *theological* inheritance, or a *ritual* inheritance: what the formula transmits across the daughter languages is the verbal way of saying the combat, not necessarily the same theology of the combat or the same ritual occasion at which the combat is recited.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters on the formula as poetic-verbal inheritance distinct from narrative, theological, or ritual inheritance; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml comparative_argument on the formula as Indo-European poetic tradition rather than as a structural-typological category.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (poetic-verbal inheritance)

This poetic-verbal framing is methodologically distinct from a Frazerian structural-typological comparativism (which would group dragon-killings by structural-functional similarity without making language-specific claims) and from a Dumézilian functional-tripartite comparativism (which would group dragon-slayer figures by their position in the IE warrior-function), and Watkins's argument is that the lexical-syntactic correspondences carry an evidential weight that purely structural-or-functional comparisons cannot.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters on the philological-comparative method's distinctness from Frazerian and Dumézilian comparativisms and on the lexical-syntactic correspondences as evidentially distinct from structural-or-functional resemblances; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml on the methodological-distinctness framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (philological vs. structural method)

The chronological depth of the reconstruction depends on the early-attestation status of Hittite within the IE family. Hittite is the earliest-attested IE language (cuneiform tablets from the Hattusa archives c. 1700–1180 BCE) and is conventionally regarded as an early-branching member of the family; the presence of the formula in Hittite — alongside its Vedic and Avestan reflexes attested c. 1500–500 BCE — implies that the formula was already in place at a stage of the family that predates the divergence of the Anatolian and non-Anatolian branches.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46 and methodological framing chapters on the Hittite reflex's chronological significance for dating the reconstructed formula; on Hittite's status as the earliest-attested IE language and as an early-branching member of the family; per glossary.yaml on "Proto-Indo-European" first-mention gloss "the reconstructed common ancestor of the Indo-European language family, spoken c. 4500–2500 BCE; Watkins's HERO SLAY SERPENT formula is a reconstructed PIE poetic element".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (chronological depth and Hittite early-branching)

The Norse-skaldic kennings for the serpent (the compressed metaphorical phrases in Old Norse / Old English skaldic poetic diction — for example, *umbgjǫrð allra landa* "band of all lands" for Jörmungandr) preserve, on Watkins's reading in *How to Kill a Dragon* Part V, the IE serpent-combat vocabulary in a registered-poetic-diction form that supplies the chapter's principal post-Vedic, post-Anatolian extra-philological exhibit for the formula's continued life in the daughter-language poetic traditions.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on the Norse skaldic kennings for Jörmungandr and on the kennings as preserving the IE serpent-combat vocabulary in registered-poetic-diction form; per glossary.yaml on "kenning" first-mention gloss "an Old Norse / Old English compressed metaphorical phrase; kennings for Jörmungandr (e.g., 'band of all lands') preserve the IE serpent-combat vocabulary that Watkins analyses".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V (Norse skaldic kennings)

=== II. The direct philological cases

The §II direct philological cases are the three IE daughter-language reflexes — Vedic Indra-Vṛtra, Avestan Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka, and Hittite Tarḫunna-Illuyanka — at which Watkins's reconstructed formula is anchored on regular lexical-and-syntactic correspondences across attested-text witnesses, and at which the chapter's case for Indo-European inheritance does not depend on typological reading or on reconstruction from indirect evidence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the Vedic-Avestan-Hittite triple as the philologically-direct anchor of the reconstructed formula; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section II on the three-direct-case framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (Vedic / Avestan / Hittite as direct anchor)

The Vedic case is anchored on Rigveda 1.32, the principal Indra-Vṛtra hymn (fifteen verses), whose second verse contains *áhann áhim*, "he slew the serpent" — the formula's most-cited lexical exhibit; the chapter cites RV 1.32 as the working text via Jamison and Brereton 2014, the scholarly-standard English Rigveda, and reads the *áhann áhim* phrase at 1.32.2 as the Vedic instantiation of the verb-and-object pairing that Watkins reconstructs as the formula.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 1.32 as the principal Indra-Vṛtra hymn and on the *áhann áhim* phrasing at 1.32.2 (the second verse, with the opening verse announcing the theme of Indra's manly deeds); Watkins 1995, ch. 46 (and broader Part V), on the same line as the Vedic reflex anchor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32 (and 1.32.1-2)

The Vedic Vṛtra-corpus extends well beyond RV 1.32: at least eight Rigvedic hymns (RV 1.32; 1.80; 2.11; 2.12; 4.18; 5.32; 6.17; 8.96; 10.113) constitute the working Vṛtra-combat corpus on which Watkins's reading of the Vedic reflex draws, with RV 1.32 supplying the most concentrated narrative spine and the other seven hymns supplying co-witness motifs that the spine-text does not develop, and the formula recurs across the corpus rather than being a one-line feature of a single hymn.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the eight Vṛtra-hymns as the working Vṛtra-combat corpus; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml sources.primary on the same eight hymns; Watkins 1995, chs. 41–48, on the recurrence of the formula across the Vṛtra-corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.32; 1.80; 2.11-12; 4.18; 5.32; 6.17; 8.96; 10.113
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=chs. 41-48 (Vedic Vṛtra-corpus)

The Vedic combat is liturgically embedded: the Rigveda is a corpus of ritual hymns recited at the *soma* sacrifice, and the *áhann áhim* phrasing appears in hymns whose ritual function is to invoke the *soma*-strengthened Indra in the present petition, so that every recitation of RV 1.32 in its ritual context re-actualises the formula in performance — a feature that distinguishes the Vedic reflex from the Hittite reflex (where the formula appears in a one-time CTH 321 narrative rather than in a daily-or-frequent ritual hymn).footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Rigveda's liturgical character and on the ritual-recitation context of the Vṛtra-hymns; per brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the *soma*-sacrifice context of the Vṛtra-hymns; Watkins 1995, on the Vedic reflex's poetic-liturgical character.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Vṛtra-hymns (liturgical-ritual context)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V (Vedic poetic-liturgical context)

The Vedic reflex is reinforced by Trita Āptya, the older Vedic dragon-slayer whose name (Vedic *Trita*, "third") is philologically cognate with Avestan Thraētaona by regular Indo-Iranian sound-laws (Vedic *tr-* / Avestan *thr-*; Vedic *-i-* / Avestan *-aē-*; Vedic *-a* / Avestan *-aona*), and whose attestation in the Rigvedic corpus (RV 10.99.6; 1.105; 2.11.19–20) preserves a pre-Indra Indo-Iranian stratum of the dragon-slayer pattern.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Trita / Thraētaona phonological cognancy and on the pre-Indra Indo-Iranian stratum; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 10.99.6 and 1.105 and 2.11.19–20 as the Trita Āptya hymn-cluster; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the Trita / Thraētaona pair as a load-bearing Indo-Iranian exhibit.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.99.6; 1.105; 2.11.19-20

The Trita / Thraētaona cognancy is documented at two levels — at the level of the names themselves (the regular sound-law correspondences across the Indo-Iranian branches) and at the level of their adversaries (both figures slay a three-headed serpent, the Vedic adversary unnamed in the surviving hymn-allusions and the Avestan adversary named as Aži Dahāka, "the dragon-Dahāka") — and the pair is one of the load-bearing exhibits for an Indo-Iranian phase of the dragon-combat material before the divergence of the Indic and Iranian branches.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the lexical-and-structural Trita / Thraētaona cognancy as evidence for an Indo-Iranian stratum; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the same evidential function; per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the philologically-load-bearing status of the pair.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion (Indo-Iranian phase)

The Avestan case is anchored on the Avestan yashts (Yt. 5.33–35, in which Thraētaona prays to Anāhitā for victory over Aži Dahāka; Yt. 14, in which Vərəθraγna's smashing of resistance is named; Yt. 19, the Frashokereti material on the eschatological combat) and on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn*, in which the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat is preserved as a continuous narrative; Benveniste and Renou 1934 supply the standard philological apparatus integrating both layers.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Avestan yasht material (Yt. 5.33–35; Yt. 14; Yt. 19) and on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* narrative; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the Avestan reflexes within the consolidated IE formula reconstruction; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml on the Avestan-and-Pahlavi witness layers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35; Yt. 14; Yt. 19; Bundahišn material
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V chs. 44-48 (Avestan reflexes)

The Avestan reflex is hymn-allusive rather than narrative: the surviving Avestan corpus does not preserve a continuous narrative of the Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka combat, and the yasht-allusions — Yt. 5.33–35 in particular, where Thraētaona prays for the strength to slay the three-mouthed, three-headed, six-eyed Aži Dahāka — supply only the verbal-and-prayer-context attestations of the combat at the level of formulaic invocation rather than at the level of full storytelling.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the hymn-allusive character of the Avestan attestations and on Yt. 5.33–35 specifically; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml lacunae on the absence of a continuous Avestan narrative and on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* as the fullest narrative witness.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Yt. 5.33-35 (hymn-allusive prayer-context)

The Pahlavi *Bundahišn* — a Middle Persian compilation conventionally dated to the 9th century CE in its surviving redaction, drawing on earlier Sasanian-period materials — supplies the fullest narrative witness to the Thraētaona / Aži Dahāka combat (the binding of the dragon under Mount Damāvand) and to the eschatological pendant in which Aži Dahāka escapes and is finally killed by the revived Kərəsāspa at Frashokereti; the Pahlavi witness is post-Achaemenid and must be marked as such in any Indo-European-comparative argument that draws on it.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Pahlavi *Bundahišn* as the fullest narrative witness and on the binding-and-final-killing structure; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–48, on the Pahlavi material's use within the consolidated IE reconstruction; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml lacunae on the post-Achaemenid status of the Pahlavi witness.]

The Avestan adversary Aži Dahāka is morphologically three-headed (Avestan: three-mouthed, six-eyed, three-jawed), and the three-headed serpent / dragon morphology is precisely the morphology of the Vedic adversary slain by Trita Āptya in RV 10.99.6 — read by Benveniste and Renou and by Watkins as the Indo-Iranian common adversary of the Trita / Thraētaona cognate pair, with the cognancy of the names confirming the cognancy of the combat-narratives.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the three-headed-adversary cognancy across Trita / Aži Dahāka and Thraētaona / Aži Dahāka; Watkins 1995, Part V chs. 44–45, on the structural-and-philological cognancy of the Indo-Iranian dragon-slayer pair; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the three-headed-adversary attestation at RV 10.99.6.]

The Hittite case — the chapter renders the storm-god as Tarḫunna in keeping with the Ch36 §III correction, since Tarḫunna is the Hittite-language name of the storm-god in CTH 321 and Teshub is the Hurrian-language name attested in the Kumarbi Cycle and Hurrian-language ritual material — is anchored on CTH 321 (the Illuyanka tablets from the Hattusa archives), preserved in two co-equal scribal versions on the same tablet group, with both versions framed by their colophons as the recitation belonging to the *purulli* festival of the storm-god of Nerik.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, "Illuyanka" §§1–2 with framing colophons; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *editio princeps* and the two-version structure; per glossary.yaml on "Tarḫunna / Teshub" with Tarḫunna in Hittite-language contexts and Teshub in Hurrian-language contexts.]

The Hittite verb-and-object pairing *kuenta… illuyanka-* — "he slew… the serpent" — is the chapter's principal Hittite philological exhibit: the verb *kuen-* (third-singular preterite *kuenta*) is the Hittite reflex of the PIE killing-verb *gʷhen-* whose Vedic and Avestan reflexes (*han-* / *jan-*) anchor the formula in Indo-Iranian, and *illuyanka-* is the Hittite common-noun "serpent" used as the proper name of the storm-god's adversary.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46 ("Typhoeus and the Illuyankas"), on the *kuenta… illuyanka-* verb-and-object pairing as the Hittite reflex of the IE formula; Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2, on the Hittite text containing the pairing; Beckman 1982, *JANES* 14: 11–25, on the *editio princeps*.]

The Hittite reflex's chronological significance for the reconstruction is decisive: Hittite is conventionally regarded as an early-branching member of the IE family (the "Anatolian hypothesis" reading Anatolian as the first branch to separate; the "Indo-Hittite hypothesis" reading Anatolian as a sister-language to the rest of IE rather than a daughter), and the presence of the formula in CTH 321 alongside its Vedic and Avestan reflexes implies that the formula was already established at a stage of the family that predates the Anatolian / non-Anatolian divergence.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46 and methodological framing chapters, on the Hittite reflex's chronological significance; on the Anatolian-early-branching status of Hittite within the IE family; per glossary.yaml on "Proto-Indo-European" as the reconstructed common ancestor c. 4500–2500 BCE.]

The three direct cases together — Vedic, Avestan, Hittite — supply philological evidence at three independent points in the IE family: Indo-Iranian (Vedic and Avestan as cognate witnesses to a shared Indo-Iranian phase of the formula) and Anatolian (Hittite as an independent early-branching witness), with the three-witness pattern crossing the Anatolian / non-Anatolian boundary and the Indo-Iranian internal boundary so that the formula's Indo-European depth is attested rather than reconstructed across at least two principal branches of the family.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the three-witness Vedic-Avestan-Hittite cross-branch attestation as the chapter's philological-evidential floor; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Indo-Iranian internal-branch witnessing; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section II on the Vedic / Avestan / Hittite triple as the direct philological case.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian internal-branch witnessing

=== III. The extended typological cases

The §III extended typological cases are the IE daughter-language reflexes — Greek Zeus-Typhon; Norse Thor-Jörmungandr and Sigurd-Fáfnir; Old English *Beowulf*-dragon; Celtic Lugh-Balor; Welsh Red-and-White Dragon; Ossetic Batraz; Armenian Vahagn-vishaps; Slavic Perun-Veles and Dobrynya-Zmey-Gorynych — at which Watkins's reconstructed formula is anchored in some cases on preserved poetic-formulaic diction (Norse Sigurd; Old English *Beowulf*) and in other cases on structural-typological resemblance, fragmentary attestation, or contested reconstruction; the §III cases are arranged here on a philological-evidence spectrum from strongest (Norse skaldic and Eddic; *Beowulf*) to weakest (Slavic Perun-Veles, Armenian Vahagn).footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the §III extended-cases gradient; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section III on the Greek / Norse / Germanic / Celtic / Welsh / Ossetic / Armenian / Slavic case-list; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the philological-evidence-spectrum ordering of the family-chapter sub-cases.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (extended-cases gradient)

The Greek Zeus-Typhon combat is the Hellenic instance of the IE warrior-and-serpent pattern: at Hesiod's *Theogony* 820–880 (the chapter's working text via West 1966), Zeus defeats the hundred-headed, serpent-limbed Typhon, the earth-born monster generated by Gaia and Tartarus, in the closing combat of the Olympian succession; the Typhonomachy is structurally the Greek sovereignty-establishing combat that secures Zeus's kingship over gods and men and is the principal Greek exhibit for the IE warrior / dragon-slayer pattern in Hesiodic poetic-narrative form.footnote:[West 1966, *Hesiod: Theogony*, on Theog. 820–880 as the closing combat of the Olympian succession and on the structural function of the Typhonomachy; Ogden 2013, *Drakōn*, chs. 2–3, on the Greek combat-tradition and on Typhon as the principal Greek instance; per brief 11-zeus-typhon.yaml on the Hesiod-and-later-tradition framing.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=chs. 2-3

The Greek-Hittite philological connection in the Zeus-Typhon material is contested rather than consolidated: West 1997 and Burkert 1992 read the Hesiodic succession (Ouranos-Kronos-Zeus) and the Typhonomachy as derived from the Kumarbi Cycle and (less directly) from the Illuyanka tradition via Near Eastern mediation, but this transmission-reading is the C6 chapter's territory rather than the present chapter's, and the present chapter treats Zeus-Typhon as a typological IE instance whose relation to the Hittite material is inheritance-or-contact rather than a direct philological cognate of the *kuenta… illuyanka-* pairing.footnote:[West 1997, *East Face of Helicon*, on the Kumarbi-Hesiod transmission reading; Burkert 1992, *Orientalizing Revolution*, on the Levantine transmission of combat-myth into Greek; per brief 11-zeus-typhon.yaml on the Near-Eastern-transmission framing; per the C6 (Ch39) chapter on the transmission question that the present chapter does not adjudicate.]

=== IV. Where the argument is strong, where it is weak

The §IV strong-and-weak section sorts the eleven IE reflexes treated in §§II–III along the philological-evidence gradient that the chapter has been building. The §II direct cases — Vedic Indra-Vṛtra, Avestan Thraētaona-Aži Dahāka, Hittite Tarḫunna-Illuyanka — sit at the strong end on the strength of regular phonological correspondences across attested-text witnesses; the §III extended cases vary across a wider range, from strong (Norse Sigurd) to weak (Slavic Perun-Veles, Armenian Vahagn).footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the strong-and-weak gradient as a methodological feature of the IE-comparative argument; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section IV on the strong-and-weak framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (strong-and-weak gradient)

The argument is strongest where three conditions are jointly satisfied: (1) attested-text witness in a daughter language with a high-prestige textual record, (2) preserved verb-and-object pairing whose cognates with PIE *gʷhen-* and the noun-slot reflexes are documented by regular sound-law correspondences, and (3) corroborating cognancy at the level of the named figures (Trita Āptya / Thraētaona; *ahi* / *aži*) or at the level of registered poetic diction (Norse skaldic kennings) — the Vedic, Avestan, Hittite, and Norse-Sigurd cases jointly satisfy all three conditions.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Parts IV–V, on the three-condition strength-criterion; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section IV on the Vedic-Avestan-Hittite direct linguistic correspondence and on the Norse skaldic kennings as the strongest cases.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Parts IV-V (three-condition strength-criterion)

The Vedic case is strongest because the attestation is continuous (the Rigveda is orally preserved with extreme precision; the Vṛtra-corpus comprises at least eight hymns; the *áhann áhim* phrasing recurs across the corpus rather than being a one-line feature of a single hymn) and because the formula's verbal slots are filled by inherited words whose Proto-Indo-Iranian etymologies are independently established outside the combat-context, so that the formula's recurrence is not the only evidence for its lexical components.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the continuous Vedic attestation of the Vṛtra-corpus and on the *áhann áhim* recurrence; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Proto-Indo-Iranian etymologies of *han-* and *ahi-*; Watkins 1995, ch. 46, on the Vedic case's strongest-case status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Vṛtra-corpus continuous attestation
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (Vedic strongest-case status)

The Hittite case is the second strongest on the strength of its chronological depth (Hittite as the earliest-attested IE language; the formula's presence in CTH 321 implying a pre-Anatolian / non-Anatolian-divergence stratum) and on the strength of the verb-and-object pairing being preserved in actual primary text (CTH 321) whose philological *editio princeps* is Beckman 1982 *JANES* 14: 11–25.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46, on the Hittite chronological depth and on the *kuenta… illuyanka-* pairing in CTH 321; Hoffner 1998 and Beckman 1982, on the Hittite primary text; per brief 06-illuyanka.yaml on the Hittite material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (Hittite second-strongest-case status)

The Avestan case is strong on philological grounds (the Vedic-Avestan cognancy at *ahi-* / *aži-* and *han-* / *jan-* is a textbook Indo-Iranian pair) but evidentially limited on narrative grounds (the Avestan corpus preserves only hymn-allusive attestations; the continuous narrative is in the post-Achaemenid Pahlavi *Bundahišn*); the philological signal is therefore stronger than the narrative-attestation signal in the Avestan case, and the chapter notes both.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vedic-Avestan philological cognancy and on the hymn-allusive character of the Avestan attestations; per brief 13-thraetaona-azidahaka.yaml lacunae on the absence of a continuous Avestan narrative.]

The Norse Sigurd-Fáfnir case is the strongest of the §III extended cases on Watkins's chapter 42 reading: the Old Norse verb-and-object pairing in *Fáfnismál* is read by Watkins as a direct philological reflex of the IE formula at the level of the verbal diction of the killing-stroke itself, and the Codex Regius preservation supplies a continuous primary-text witness whose philological apparatus is well-developed in twentieth-century Old Norse scholarship.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 42, on Sigurd as the strongest philological evidence in the post-Anatolian, post-Indo-Iranian corpus and on the *Fáfnismál* diction as direct IE-formula reflex; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the Sigurd-as-strongest evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42 (Sigurd strongest §III case)

The Norse Thor-Jörmungandr case is strong principally at the level of the registered-poetic-diction kennings — *umbgjǫrð allra landa* "band of all lands" and similar phrases for the Midgard Serpent — that Watkins reads as preserving the IE serpent-combat vocabulary in the registered poetic diction of skaldic verse; the iconographic dossier (Altuna, Hørdum, Gosforth, Ardre VIII runestones) supplies a third register of attestation alongside the Eddic-and-prose textual witnesses.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on the Norse skaldic kennings; Larrington 2014 and Faulkes 1987 on the Eddic-and-prose Thor-and-Jörmungandr material; per brief 18-thor-jormungandr.yaml on the iconographic dossier.]

The Old English *Beowulf* case is strong philologically (Watkins reads the dragon-vocabulary and killing-vocabulary as IE-formula reflex) and structurally (Tolkien 1936's load-bearing critical move re-established the mythic register of the monsters), but it is also distinctive in narrative-thematic register: Beowulf's dragon-combat is end-of-life and death-bringing rather than heroic-initiatory, structurally paralleling Thor's Ragnarök mutual-killing rather than Sigurd's youthful ambush.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on the *Beowulf* IE-formula reflex; Tolkien 1936 on the mythic-register critical move; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the aged-king-dies-killing-the-dragon thematic distinction.]

The argument is weaker — but not absent — at the §III cases where the philological signal is replaced or supplemented by typological-structural signal, by fragmentary attestation, or by reconstruction from indirect evidence: the Greek Zeus-Typhon case (where the Hellenic reflex is structurally clear but the philological cognancy with the Indo-Iranian or Anatolian witnesses is contested by the Near-Eastern-transmission reading), the Celtic Lugh-Balor case (one-eyed-giant adversary rather than serpent; sling-stone rather than thunderbolt; the IE reflex reading depends on Dumézilian comparative scholarship rather than on direct philological cognancy), and the Welsh Red-and-White Dragon case (dragon-on-dragon combat structure rather than hero-and-dragon combat structure; the Welsh case stands at the edge of the IE HERO SLAY SERPENT pattern rather than as a central instance).footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the Celtic and Welsh cases as morphologically-distinctive IE reflexes; Ogden 2013, ch. on Celtic material, on the structural distinctiveness of the Welsh dragon-on-dragon combat; per brief 19-ie-reflexes.yaml on the morphological-distinctiveness preservation in the IE-comparative apparatus.]

=== V. Transmission vs. inheritance

The §V section makes explicit the distinction on which the chapter's whole argument rests: Watkins's claim is *inheritance* from a Proto-Indo-European poetic tradition, not *contact-borrowing* from one tradition into another, and this distinction is methodologically distinct from — and complementary to — the transmission question that C6 (Ch39) addresses for the Near-East-to-Greek route in the Kumarbi-and-Typhonomachy material.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters and Parts IV–V, on the inheritance-from-PIE-poetic-tradition reading; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section V on the inheritance-vs-contact-borrowing distinction; per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the transmission question that the present chapter does not develop.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (inheritance-vs-contact-borrowing distinction)

Inheritance and transmission are two distinct mechanisms for explaining cross-tradition similarity. Inheritance accounts for the similarity by tracing both daughter-traditions to a shared ancestor whose features are preserved by descent (the IE family-tree model in which Vedic, Avestan, Hittite, Greek, Norse, and other daughter-language reflexes are descendants of a common Proto-Indo-European ancestor); transmission accounts for the similarity by tracing one tradition's features to contact with another, with the receiving tradition acquiring the feature from the donor tradition by cultural-or-textual borrowing (the West 1997 / Burkert 1992 reading of the Hesiodic Typhonomachy as derived from the Kumarbi Cycle by Near-Eastern mediation).footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters, on the inheritance / transmission methodological distinction in IE-comparative work; West 1997, *East Face of Helicon*, on the Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission reading as the contrasting mechanism; Burkert 1992, *Orientalizing Revolution*, on the same transmission reading; per brief 11-zeus-typhon.yaml on the West-Burkert transmission-reading for the Greek case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (inheritance / transmission distinction)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=East Face of Helicon (transmission as the contrasting mechanism)

The two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive: a daughter-tradition can inherit a pattern from its IE ancestor and *also* be reinforced or modified by contact with a non-IE tradition (the Hesiodic Typhonomachy is plausibly both an IE inheritance — Greek as an IE daughter language with the formula's traces visible in the Hellenic warrior-and-serpent pattern — and a Near-Eastern-influenced reception, with the Kumarbi-Cycle structural correspondences supplementing rather than replacing the inherited pattern); the chapter registers the compatibility of the two mechanisms while presenting Watkins's IE-inheritance argument as the chapter's central focus.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters, on the compatibility of inheritance and transmission; West 1997, *East Face of Helicon*, on the Near-Eastern reinforcement of the Hesiodic pattern alongside Greek's IE-daughter-language status; per brief 11-zeus-typhon.yaml on the dual reading of the Greek case; per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the transmission-half of the dual reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (inheritance-and-transmission compatibility)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=East Face of Helicon (Near-Eastern reinforcement)

Watkins's argument is methodologically distinct from a transmission-based argument in three respects. First, the evidential basis is regular phonological correspondences across daughter languages (a feature of inheritance) rather than structural-or-narrative correspondences across geographically-and-temporally adjacent traditions (a feature of transmission). Second, the chronological depth of the argument extends back to a reconstructed Proto-Indo-European stage (c. 4500–2500 BCE on standard estimates) rather than to a documented contact horizon. Third, the daughter-language reflexes are required to instantiate the same lexical-syntactic structure rather than the same narrative-or-theological content.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters, on the three-feature distinction between inheritance and transmission methods; per glossary.yaml on "Proto-Indo-European" first-mention gloss "the reconstructed common ancestor of the Indo-European language family, spoken c. 4500–2500 BCE; Watkins's HERO SLAY SERPENT formula is a reconstructed PIE poetic element"; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml on the inheritance-from-PIE-poetic-tradition reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (three-feature distinction)

The Hittite case displays the inheritance-and-contact-area complication most clearly. Hittite is a member of the IE family (the Anatolian branch) so the Tarḫunna-Illuyanka material is an IE inheritance candidate at the level of the *kuenta… illuyanka-* pairing; Hittite is also a Near-Eastern culture in geographical-and-cultural contact with Hurrian-language traditions (the Kumarbi Cycle is Hurrian-language material translated into Hittite at Hattusa) and with the wider Mesopotamian-Levantine cultural sphere, so the Tarḫunna-Illuyanka material is also a transmission candidate at the level of the festival-aetiology and the storm-god-vs-serpent narrative structure.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46, on the Hittite material's IE-inheritance status at the level of the *kuenta… illuyanka-* pairing; Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, on the Anatolian / Hurrian / Mesopotamian-Levantine cultural-contact context of the Hittite corpus; per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the transmission-half of the Hittite dual reading; per brief 06-illuyanka.yaml on the Hittite-and-Near-Eastern dual context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (Hittite IE-inheritance status)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Hittite Myths (Anatolian / Hurrian / Mesopotamian-Levantine context)

The chapter's resolution of the Hittite dual context is the same as Watkins's: the *kuenta… illuyanka-* pairing's lexical-syntactic structure is best read as IE inheritance because the verb *kuen-* is a regular reflex of PIE *gʷhen-* and the noun *illuyanka-* is a Hittite common noun rather than a Hurrian or Akkadian loanword; the festival-aetiology of *purulli* and the storm-god-vs-serpent narrative structure may have additional Near-Eastern dimensions that C3 (Ch36) and C6 (Ch39) develop, but the philological core of the formula's Hittite reflex is IE-inherited rather than Near-Eastern-transmitted.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 46, on the philological-core IE-inheritance reading of the Hittite reflex; per the C3 (Ch36 §III) framing on the *purulli*-festival cyclical-maintenance dimension and per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the Near-Eastern transmission dimension; Hoffner 1998 on the Hittite primary text.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 46 (philological-core IE-inheritance reading)

The Greek Zeus-Typhon case displays the dual context in the opposite direction: the philological-cognancy evidence is comparatively weaker than in the Indo-Iranian or Anatolian cases (Greek does not preserve a verb-and-object pairing as crisply cognate with the *gʷhen-* / *ahi-* slots as Vedic, Avestan, and Hittite do), while the structural-and-narrative evidence for Near-Eastern transmission (Kumarbi-Cycle parallels; Cilician geographical anchoring; Levantine-coast cultic locations) is comparatively stronger; the C6 chapter (Ch39) develops the transmission-side reading at length, and the present chapter notes the dual context without resolving it.footnote:[Watkins 1995, on Greek's reduced philological cognancy with the Indo-Iranian and Anatolian cores; West 1997, *East Face of Helicon*, on the Near-Eastern transmission reading for the Hesiodic Typhonomachy; Burkert 1992 on the same; per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the transmission-side reading; per brief 11-zeus-typhon.yaml on the Cilician geographical anchoring.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Greek philological cognancy reduced
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=East Face of Helicon (Near-Eastern transmission for Greek)

The inheritance / transmission distinction has implications for the chapter's wider book-internal argument. Where inheritance is the explanation, the IE distribution of the combat-myth is best read as a *family* phenomenon whose explanation is genealogical and whose comparative apparatus is the IE-language-family-tree; where transmission is the explanation, the cross-tradition similarity is a *contact-cultural* phenomenon whose explanation is historical-cultural and whose comparative apparatus is the documentation of borrowing-events.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters, on the family-tree-vs-contact-cultural framing; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section V on the inheritance-vs-transmission distinction's bearing on the chapter's wider argument; per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the book-internal typological-and-comparative framework.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (family-tree vs. contact-cultural)

The chapter's overall position on the inheritance / transmission distinction is therefore that they are complementary rather than competing methods, that Watkins's IE-inheritance argument is the chapter's central focus and is best supported at the §II direct cases (Vedic / Avestan / Hittite philological core), that transmission is the C6 chapter's territory and is best supported at the Greek-Hittite-Levantine intersection (where Hesiod and the Kumarbi Cycle and the Baal-Yamm material plausibly meet), and that the two methods together display the variety of mechanisms by which combat-myth material crosses tradition-boundaries.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters, on the complementarity of inheritance and transmission; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml on the chapter's IE-inheritance focus; per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the transmission-half of the methodological pair; West 1997 *East Face of Helicon* on the Greek-Hittite-Levantine transmission intersection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (inheritance-and-transmission complementarity)

A further methodological feature of the inheritance / transmission distinction is its bearing on what counts as a successful demonstration. An inheritance argument is successful when regular phonological correspondences across daughter languages support the reconstruction of a Proto-Indo-European feature whose preserved descendants are the daughter-language reflexes; a transmission argument is successful when documented contact between traditions, attested borrowing-events, or shared specific-narrative-detail patterns support the diffusion of a feature from a donor-tradition to a receiving-tradition; the two argument-types have different evidential standards and the chapter is explicit that Watkins's argument is to be evaluated on the inheritance standard rather than on the transmission standard.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters, on the different evidential standards for inheritance and transmission arguments; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section V on the chapter's responsibility to evaluate Watkins's argument on the inheritance standard; per the C6 (Ch39) framing on the transmission-standard evaluation that the C6 chapter performs for the Near-East-to-Greek case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters (different evidential standards)

The chapter's IE-inheritance argument is therefore a *bounded* finding: it explains the IE distribution of the combat-myth at the level of philological inheritance, and it does not — and is not designed to — explain the existence of the combat-myth pattern in non-IE traditions; the §VI Mwindo control develops this boundedness explicitly, since the existence of non-IE serpent-combat traditions with their own combat-vocabularies shows that the IE-inheritance argument is one finding among the family of comparative-mythological findings that the book's typology requires.footnote:[Watkins 1995, on the IE-inheritance argument as a finding about the IE distribution rather than about the global existence of combat-myth; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml special_instructions on the bounded character of Watkins's argument and on the §VI Mwindo control as the chapter's closing typological corrective.]

=== VI. The Mwindo problem as a control

The §VI Mwindo control is the chapter's closing typological corrective to a possible over-extension of Watkins's argument: if the IE-inheritance reading were sufficient to explain the existence of combat-myth as such (rather than only its IE distribution), then non-IE serpent-combat traditions would either be IE-borrowings or would not exhibit serpent-combat vocabulary; the Mwindo material — together with other non-IE serpent-combat traditions noted in the book's wider catalogue — shows that this implication does not hold, and the chapter therefore registers the Mwindo case as the methodological control that limits Watkins's argument to its proper IE scope.footnote:[Biebuyck and Mateene 1969, *The Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (Zaire)*, on the Nyanga Mwindo cycle and its serpent-combat material; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml structure section VI on the Mwindo problem as a control; per scope.md §"Inclusion / exclusion criteria" on the in-scope / contrastive-tier status of the Mwindo case in the book's catalogue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (control case)

The Mwindo Epic is a Nyanga oral-epic tradition recorded from the Banyanga of the eastern Democratic Republic of the Congo by Daniel Biebuyck and Kahombo C. Mateene in the 1950s and published in critical edition with English translation in 1969 (University of California Press); the recorded version (the Karisi-version of Shé-Kárisi Rureke) presents the hero Mwindo's birth, exile, descent into the underworld, return, and his contests against various adversaries — including the dragon Kirimu — across an extended epic-narrative.footnote:[Biebuyck and Mateene 1969, *The Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (Zaire)*, introduction and translation, on the Karisi-version of Shé-Kárisi Rureke and on Mwindo's epic-narrative structure; per glossary.yaml on "Mwindo" first-mention gloss "Nyanga epic hero; kills the dragon Kirimu and is punished by the sky-god Nkuba — the contrastive case".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=introduction and translation

Mwindo's dragon-killing of Kirimu is one of the cycle's named adversary-killings, and it occurs alongside other Mwindo combats that include adversaries who are not serpent-or-dragon morphologies; the dragon-combat material in the Mwindo cycle is not a structural-typological focal point of the cycle in the way the Vedic Vṛtra-combat is the structural-typological focal point of the Indra-cycle in the Rigveda, and the Mwindo cycle's narrative interest is broader than dragon-combat.footnote:[Biebuyck and Mateene 1969, on Mwindo's combat with Kirimu and on the wider adversary-cycle of the Mwindo epic; per glossary.yaml on Kirimu first-mention gloss "Nyanga dragon slain by Mwindo".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=Mwindo cycle (Kirimu combat in wider epic-narrative)

The Mwindo case's distinctive feature for the chapter's typological argument is that Mwindo's killing of Kirimu is followed by the hero's *punishment* by the sky-god Nkuba — the killing is treated within the Nyanga cosmology not as a heroic-deed-warranting-celebration but as a transgression that requires expiation, and the cycle's resolution is not the hero's elevation to sovereignty (as in the Indra-Vṛtra and Zeus-Typhon cases) but the hero's chastening and reintegration into a moral-cosmic order whose terms the dragon-combat had threatened.footnote:[Biebuyck and Mateene 1969, on Mwindo's punishment by Nkuba after the killing of Kirimu and on the moral-cosmic-order resolution of the cycle; per glossary.yaml on Nkuba first-mention gloss "Nyanga sky-god; punishes Mwindo for the killing of Kirimu" and on Mwindo as "the contrastive case"; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml special_instructions on the Mwindo control's contrastive function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=Mwindo cycle (punishment by Nkuba)

The Mwindo case shows that the global pattern of "hero-kills-serpent" is not exclusively an IE-inheritance pattern: a fully-formed dragon-killing narrative is preserved in a non-IE oral-epic tradition (Nyanga is a Bantu language family member, not an IE language) with its own combat-vocabulary, its own moral-cosmic frame, and its own resolution-pattern; the Mwindo control thus establishes that Watkins's IE-inheritance argument is a finding *about the IE distribution* of the combat-myth, not a finding *about the existence* of dragon-combat narrative as such.footnote:[Biebuyck and Mateene 1969, on the Mwindo cycle as a fully-formed non-IE dragon-killing narrative with its own combat-vocabulary; per brief 38-C5-ie-hero-slay-serpent-formula.yaml special_instructions on the bounded character of Watkins's argument; Watkins 1995 on the IE scope of the argument.]
