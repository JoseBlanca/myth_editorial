[[ch-05-kumarbi-ullikummi]]
== The Kumarbi Cycle: Ullikummi, Hedammu, and the Song of the Sea

=== Source overview

The text at the centre of this chapter is not a single tablet but a song-cycle. The Kumarbi Cycle is a Hurro-Hittite sequence of compositions on the succession of kingship in heaven, surviving on cuneiform tablets recovered from the Hittite royal archives at Boğazköy, the site of ancient Hattusa.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Boğazköy provenance and the cycle's general framework.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The standard English anthology of the cycle, used as the anchor edition throughout this chapter, is Harry A. Hoffner Jr.'s *Hittite Myths*, second edition, in the Society of Biblical Literature's Writings from the Ancient World series.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, *Hittite Myths*, 2nd ed., SBL Writings from the Ancient World 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=whole volume

Hoffner gathers the cycle's individual songs as scholarly-edited translations and presents each "song" as a discrete sub-section of the larger cycle, so that the reader encounters the cycle not as a continuous epic but as a sequence of named compositions held together by a shared cast and a shared narrative premise.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the individual songs of the Kumarbi Cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The cycle as preserved comprises a sequence of named songs: Kingship in Heaven, which functions as the cycle frame, then the Song of Ullikummi, the Song of Hedammu, the Song of the Sea, the Song of LAMMA, and the Song of Silver.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the song-titles preserved in the Boğazköy archives.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The tablets that preserve the cycle are catalogued under the Boğazköy publication series KUB, the Keilschrifturkunden aus Boghazköi, and KBo, the Keilschrifttexte aus Boghazköi. These two sigla are the standard scholarly references for the Hittite cuneiform corpus, and Hoffner's edition routes its citations through them.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on KUB and KBo as the publication series for the Boğazköy tablets.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The opening song, Kingship in Heaven, is preserved primarily on the tablet KUB 33.120 and joins, which is Hoffner's principal manuscript for §14. The tablet provides the narrative skeleton on which the rest of the cycle depends: a reader who wants to know how the conflict between Kumarbi and Teshub begins reads KUB 33.120 first.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the manuscript basis for Kingship in Heaven.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The cycle's manuscript witnesses are distributed across the Middle Hittite and New Hittite copying periods. The distribution indicates that the songs were transmitted within the Hittite scribal tradition over an extended period rather than as a single one-off composition; the scribes who copied these texts in the New Hittite period had inherited them, in working manuscript form, from the Middle Hittite scribes who had preceded them.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Middle and New Hittite manuscript distribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle is Hurrian in cultural substrate but preserved in the Hittite-language scribal record. The surviving texts move between Hittite-language and Hurrian-language passages, with Hurrian theonyms adopted into the Hittite-language redaction. The scribes are working with a body of material that is not native to the language they are writing in, and the bilingual surface of the manuscripts is itself testimony to that situation.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Hurrian substrate of the cycle and its Hittite-language transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The principal divine name in the cycle is Teshub, the Hurrian storm-god. The name is used in the Hurrian-language passages and in the Hittite-language adaptations of those passages where the Hurrian name has been retained as a proper name.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the use of the Hurrian theonym Teshub throughout the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

In Hittite-language religious contexts outside the Kumarbi Cycle, the storm-god is named Tarḫunna, the Hittite-language equivalent of the Hurrian Teshub. The two names denote functionally the same storm-god across the Anatolian linguistic layers. Within this chapter the name Teshub is used throughout, because the cycle is the Hurrian composition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on Tarḫunna and Teshub as the Hittite and Hurrian forms of the Anatolian storm-god.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle is preserved in fragmentary form across all of its songs, but the fragmentary state is unevenly distributed. The Song of Ullikummi is the best-attested combat narrative, and its central episodes can be read continuously in Hoffner's edition. The Song of Hedammu and the Song of the Sea survive only in disconnected passages, and any reading of them has to work with much greater uncertainty.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§15–17, on the differential preservation of the cycle's combat songs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§15–17

The foundational philological treatment of the cycle is Hans Gustav Güterbock's mid-twentieth-century edition of Kingship in Heaven and the Song of Ullikummi. Güterbock's edition established the scholarly framework that Hoffner inherits and updates; modern citations of the cycle still travel through Güterbock's groundwork even where they refer to Hoffner's English.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on Güterbock's foundational edition of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Beyond the editorial scholarship, the cycle figures prominently in two arguments about the wider Mediterranean. M. L. West's *The East Face of Helicon*, in chapter 6, treats the Kumarbi Cycle as the central documentary case for Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission of theogonic and combat material into Hesiod's *Theogony*.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Kumarbi Cycle as the central transmission case for Hesiod.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

Walter Burkert's *The Orientalizing Revolution* argues that the Kumarbi material reached Greek poets through Phoenician and broader Levantine intermediaries during the early Archaic age. Burkert's case is methodological as well as documentary: he is asking how the transmission worked, and his answer specifies a route.footnote:[Burkert 1992, on Phoenician-mediated transmission of Near Eastern myth into Greek culture.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume

Neil Forsyth's *The Old Enemy*, chapter 3, places the Hurro-Hittite combat material as a key middle stage in the long sequence of the combat-myth tradition that runs from Sumer through the Hebrew Bible into Christian apocalyptic. Forsyth's frame is the long arc; the Anatolian station occupies its middle.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on Hurro-Hittite combat in the combat-myth sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

Scurlock and Beal's 2013 collected volume on the Chaoskampf hypothesis includes essays on the Anatolian material that engage the Kumarbi Cycle as test material for the comparative-combat category. The volume's contributors do not all agree on the verdict, and the cycle is one of the cases on which the disagreements are productive.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, chapters on Anatolian material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=chapters on Anatolian material

The cycle is preserved largely without an explicit liturgical or festival rubric. The partial exception is KUB 44.7, which associates the Song of the Sea with a celebration at Mount Ḫazzi. On the whole the corpus is best classified as scribal-literary narrative rather than ritual recitation, in contrast to the Babylonian *Enūma Eliš*, which has a documented akītu performance context. The contrast matters: the Kumarbi material was a body of texts to be read and copied, not, on the surviving evidence, a body of texts to be performed in a fixed liturgical setting.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the predominantly literary character of the Kumarbi Cycle and on KUB 44.7's Mount Ḫazzi association.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

What the cycle does carry, even without a clear liturgical anchor, is a political-theological assumption. Its narrative subject — the succession of kingship in heaven through deposition and counter-strike — embeds the Hurro-Hittite assumption that the storm-god is the legitimate sovereign of the divine assembly, and, by extension, that the Hittite king is the storm-god's representative on earth. The cycle is, on this reading, the divine-court analogue of a royal-political claim.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the royal-ideological dimension of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

That, in summary, is the documentary situation. The cycle is a Hurro-Hittite scribal-literary corpus, preserved fragmentarily on Boğazköy tablets across two scribal periods, edited canonically by Güterbock and presented in standard English by Hoffner, and read by West, Burkert, and Forsyth as the principal Anatolian station in a wider Near-Eastern-to-Mediterranean combat tradition. The chapter that follows works from Hoffner's edition for body prose and turns to the comparative scholarship for the transmission argument at the end.

=== Setting and context

The cycle's pantheon is Hurrian in primary derivation. Its principal gods carry Hurrian theonyms — Teshub, Kumarbi, Šauška — but the Hurrian poets did not work in isolation. Alongside the Hurrian names there appear Akkadian-language theonyms, Anu and Ea, that the poets adopted from Mesopotamian tradition. The pantheon as it stands in the cycle is therefore a layered object: a Hurrian core with Mesopotamian imports, treated as if both layers had always belonged.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Hurrian pantheon and its Mesopotamian borrowings.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Teshub is the Hurrian storm-god, the reigning sovereign of the divine assembly during the present age of the cycle, and the champion who must defeat each of Kumarbi's monstrous offspring in turn. He is the figure whose kingship the cycle defends; nearly every combat in the surviving record is mounted against a creature designed, by Kumarbi, to depose him.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction and §§15–17, on Teshub as the reigning storm-god of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Kumarbi is the Hurrian father-god deposed by Teshub in the cycle's succession-frame. After his deposition he becomes the cycle's principal antagonist, generating successive monstrous adversaries to challenge Teshub's kingship. He is, in narrative terms, the engine of the cycle's plot: where the cycle has events to narrate, those events are usually the consequence of one of his schemes.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 and §§15–17, on Kumarbi's role as deposed predecessor and antagonist.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu, the Akkadian sky-god, appears in the cycle as Kumarbi's predecessor on the throne of heaven. His Akkadian name is preserved without translation into Hurrian, marking him as a Mesopotamian import into the Hurrian theogonic framework. The choice not to translate the name is significant; it leaves the Mesopotamian borrowing visible on the surface of the text.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's role as Kumarbi's predecessor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Alalu is the cycle's primordial king, the figure deposed by Anu and so the first sovereign in the documented succession. His name is otherwise rare in the Hittite record and is largely confined to this cycle, which gives him the air of a figure whose role is structural rather than substantive: the first sovereign in the chain whose deposition begins the sequence.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Alalu as the primordial king of the succession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Ea, the Akkadian wisdom-god, plays the role of counsellor in the cycle. He is the agent of the decisive intervention that breaks Ullikummi, and he is consulted in the moments when the storm-god's combat capacity is, by itself, insufficient. The Hurro-Hittite tradition adopts the Akkadian form Ea rather than the Sumerian Enki, consistent with the cycle's broader Akkadian-mediated theonymy.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), on Ea's counselor and agent role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Šauška is the Hurrian counterpart of the Mesopotamian Ishtar, the goddess of love and war who appears in the cycle as Teshub's sister and as the seductress in the Song of Hedammu. The brief and the surviving texts pair the two names as Ishtar and Šauška, and the chapter follows that paired usage at first mention.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16 (Song of Hedammu), on Šauška/Ishtar as Teshub's sister and the seductress of Hedammu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Upelluri is the Hurrian giant on whose shoulder the world rests and on whom Ullikummi is implanted to grow. He is a primordial figure embedded in the cosmology of the cycle's earliest layer, and his role in the Song of Ullikummi turns on his position as cosmographic foundation rather than on any narrative agency of his own.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), on Upelluri as the primordial earth-bearing giant.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The cycle's theogonic premise — kingship in heaven passing through a sequence of deposed and deposing sovereigns — is a structural inheritance from Mesopotamian tradition, where successions and divine-court politics are foundational to Sumerian and Akkadian theogony. The specific sequence Alalu, Anu, Kumarbi, Teshub, however, is distinctively Hurrian. The poets took the structural pattern from a Mesopotamian background and populated it with their own divine figures and their own arrangement of names.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction and §14, on the Hurrian succession-sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The Mesopotamian background that supplied the structural pattern includes the Akkadian Anzû Epic. In the Anzû Epic, Anzû seizes the Tablet of Destinies from Enlil and is defeated by Ninurta. The Kumarbi Cycle adapts the storm-god-versus-monstrous-adversary structure that the Anzû Epic exemplifies, with Hurrian innovations in the genealogical and corporeal logic of the adversary. Where the Anzû Epic gives the storm-god a winged thief, the Kumarbi material gives him a stone monster grown from a rock.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on Mesopotamian background for the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The Mesopotamian background also includes the broader Enlil-and-Ninurta tradition that lies behind the Anzû Epic, and the cycle's wisdom-god Ea is the same figure who plays the counsellor role in the Anzû and *Enūma Eliš* narratives (cross-ref: Ch. 2, Ch. 3). The continuity in Ea's role across these compositions is one of the cleanest indications of the depth of the inheritance.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; Annus 2001, introduction, on Ea's counselor role across the Mesopotamian corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle is set in a divine geography that includes the heavens, the netherworld, and the sea. The heavens are Anu's domain and the residence of Teshub. The netherworld, called the Dark Earth in the surviving text, is the place to which deposed sovereigns can be banished and from which monstrous progeny can emerge; Kumarbi sometimes operates from there. The sea is the realm of the marine genitor of Hedammu and the antagonist of the Song of the Sea. These three zones structure the cycle's combats: each adversary is associated with a particular cosmographic domain.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–17, on the divine geography of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–17

The Dark Earth is itself a recurrent topographical category in the cycle, identified explicitly in the surviving text as a place of banishment and as a reservoir of monstrous progeny. It is the cycle's underworld, and its main narrative function is to serve as the destination for sovereigns who have lost their thrones.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the Dark Earth in the cycle's geography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Around these principal figures and zones the cycle's wider divine court includes lesser deities — messengers, attendants, and craftsman-gods — who appear in subsidiary roles across the songs. Together they form the Hurrian-Hittite divine assembly that the cycle's drama presupposes. Most of these figures appear and pass; the spotlight stays on the four principal pairs: Kumarbi against Teshub, Šauška in the seductress role, Ea in the counsellor role, Upelluri as the cosmic platform.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–17, on the divine assembly of the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–17

The Hurrian poets' reworking of Mesopotamian materials is best understood as cultural reception rather than direct translation. Akkadian-language theonyms, Anu and Ea, are imported alongside Hurrian-language theonyms, Kumarbi, Teshub, and Šauška, with the Hurrian poets innovating in the body of the narrative and in the morphology of the monstrous adversaries. The cycle is, in this sense, a record of Hurrian creativity working with borrowed Mesopotamian material; the borrowed material is given a new shape rather than rendered into the new language.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on cultural reception in the cycle.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

=== Kingship in Heaven — the cycle frame

The opening song of the cycle, Kingship in Heaven, narrates the succession of divine sovereigns. Alalu reigns first, then is deposed by Anu, who is in turn deposed by Kumarbi, who is in turn challenged by Teshub. The four-stage succession is the spine of the cycle, and the rest of the surviving songs presuppose it.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the succession-sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The succession-sequence is structured by reigns of nine years. Each sovereign holds the throne of heaven for that period before being unseated by the next. The nine-year unit is the rhythm of the song's first half; what changes from one reign to the next is the identity of the sovereign and the manner of the deposition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the nine-year reigns of Alalu and Anu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Alalu, the first sovereign, reigns for nine years and is then deposed by Anu, who is described as Alalu's cupbearer prior to the deposition. The role-relationship — sovereign and cupbearer — is one of the things the song specifies precisely; the figure who deposes the sovereign rises from immediate subordination, and the violence of the deposition is internal to the throne's own household.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu as Alalu's cupbearer prior to the deposition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

After his deposition, Alalu flees down into the Dark Earth, the netherworld, where he remains across the rest of the cycle's events. His fate is one of permanent banishment rather than destruction: he is removed from heaven, but he is not killed, and the surviving record never has him return.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Alalu's flight to the Dark Earth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu, after deposing Alalu, occupies the throne of heaven for his own nine-year reign. Kumarbi serves as his cupbearer in the inverted parallel of the prior succession. The pattern is being set up for the reader: the cupbearer is the figure who attacks the sovereign, and Kumarbi's place at Anu's elbow is therefore a position of latent threat from the opening.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's reign and Kumarbi as cupbearer.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

==== Kumarbi's deposition of Anu

The deposition of Anu by Kumarbi is the cycle's most striking and best-attested narrative event. Kumarbi attacks Anu, knocks him from the sky, bites off Anu's genitals, and swallows them. The verbs are physical and specific; the song does not abstract the assault. The new sovereign physically consumes the previous sovereign's reproductive organs, and the act is presented in the surviving text as the means of the deposition rather than as a sequel to it.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on Kumarbi's deposition of Anu by genital castration and swallowing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu, after being castrated, declares from the sky that Kumarbi should not rejoice over his swallowing of the genitals. The reason he gives is that, by the act of swallowing, Kumarbi has been impregnated with Anu's seed. The declaration converts the violent triumph into a setback: what Kumarbi has done to Anu has, through the same act, been done to Kumarbi himself.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's prophetic declaration after the castration.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Anu's declaration enumerates the divine offspring that will be born from his seed inside Kumarbi's body. The list includes the storm-god Teshub, who will become Kumarbi's nemesis and the next sovereign of heaven. The enumeration is therefore not just a description of pregnancy but a forecast of the cycle's future: it tells the reader, before any combat has begun, who will eventually depose Kumarbi.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on Anu's enumeration of the offspring to be born from Kumarbi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The narrative thereby establishes an internal-conception logic for the storm-god's birth. Teshub is conceived from Anu's seed inside Kumarbi's body, making Kumarbi simultaneously his birth-vessel and his designated antagonist. The cycle's central conflict, in other words, is biological: father-figure and son-figure are bound together by an internal procreative process that neither can undo.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the conception of Teshub inside Kumarbi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The text describes additional beings conceived from Anu's seed alongside Teshub. The river Aranzaḫ, the Hurrian name for the Tigris, is among them, and other secondary deities are listed as well. The precise enumeration is partially preserved in the surviving manuscript; what reads continuously is the principle that Anu's seed produces multiple beings, of which Teshub is the most consequential.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the additional beings conceived from Anu's seed.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

==== The birth of Teshub

The birth of Teshub from Kumarbi's body is described in surviving fragments of Kingship in Heaven and is presented as a difficult parturition. The route of emergence is specified in the text, but the relevant passage is partially broken; the fragmentary state falls precisely at the point where the manner of the birth would be most fully described.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14, on the birth of Teshub from Kumarbi's body.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

[LACUNA: the precise mechanism of Teshub's emergence from Kumarbi is partially broken in the surviving manuscript | source: Hoffner 1998 §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the broken passage at Teshub's birth | scholarly_reconstruction: Hoffner notes alternative readings; the chapter does not adjudicate among them]

After Teshub's birth, the cycle's premise is set. Teshub assumes the kingship of heaven, displacing Kumarbi from sovereignty and establishing the scenario in which Kumarbi will retaliate by generating monstrous offspring to depose Teshub in turn. The remainder of the cycle is, in narrative terms, the working-out of this premise across a sequence of combat-songs.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 and §15 prologue, on Teshub's accession and the cycle's premise.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

The structural pattern of Kingship in Heaven — castration of the previous sovereign, swallowing, and an internal-conception sequence — is the principal feature that West and Burkert identify as continuous with the Hesiodic Ouranos-Kronos-Zeus succession. The transmission argument that the Greek material took shape in dependence on the Hurro-Hittite material rests in large part on this opening song, and the chapter returns to that argument in its later section.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Hurro-Hittite-Hesiodic correspondence in the succession-frame; Burkert 1992 on transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

==== Cycle frame and the Song of Going Forth

A passage sometimes treated as a separate "Song of Going Forth" describes Kumarbi's plotting against Teshub after his deposition, setting up the combat-songs that follow. Hoffner treats this as transitional material between the cycle frame and the combat songs proper rather than as an autonomous song; the function of the passage is to bridge the opening succession to the subsequent combats.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 conclusion, on the transitional plotting passage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

Kumarbi's plotting passage motivates the generation of each successive monstrous adversary — the stone-monster Ullikummi, the sea-monster Hedammu — as instruments of Kumarbi's revenge against Teshub. The cycle's narrative engine, at the structural level, is therefore Kumarbi's standing motive: he has lost the throne, he intends to regain it, and the monsters are the means.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 conclusion and §§15–16 prologues, on Kumarbi's motive of revenge.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14

=== The Song of Ullikummi — the flagship combat

The Song of Ullikummi is the best-preserved combat narrative in the cycle and the most extensively translated and analysed song in the Hoffner anthology. The reader who comes to the cycle for its combat material spends most of that reading time in this song, because it is here that the cycle's narrative material is sufficiently continuous to support a sustained reading.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), introductory headnote, on the song's preservation status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The song opens with Kumarbi's deliberate generation of a champion to depose Teshub, after his own failure to retain the kingship of heaven. The narrative thus continues directly from the cycle frame: the reader has been told, in Kingship in Heaven, that Kumarbi has been deposed and intends revenge, and the Song of Ullikummi shows him acting on that intention.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, opening, on Kumarbi's generative plan.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Conception on the rock

Kumarbi conceives Ullikummi by a great rock — described as a giant cliff or stone formation — into which he ejaculates. The rock conceives, and the offspring that grows from this conception is itself made of stone. The mechanism of the conception is described in the song without euphemism; the text gives the act as a physical event between deity and stone, and the offspring is the consequence.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Kumarbi's conception of Ullikummi on the rock.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The conception of a stone monster from a stone parent is the central physiological premise of the song. Ullikummi is a stone child of stone, and his stone nature is what makes him invulnerable to ordinary divine combat. The combat that follows will fail, in its first attempt, precisely because the standard methods of divine combat are designed for organic adversaries and Ullikummi has no organic body to be torn or burned.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ullikummi's stone nature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The rock that bears Ullikummi is described in the surviving text in topographical terms that locate it in a specific region of the cosmic landscape. Hoffner's commentary discusses the geographical setting without committing to a single identification. The chapter follows that reticence; the rock has a place in the cosmic topography, but the place is not pinned to a single modern landmark.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the topographical setting of the conception.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Hiding from Teshub

After Ullikummi is conceived, Kumarbi makes arrangements to conceal the infant from Teshub. The reason is straightforward in the narrative logic: Teshub, if he knew, would attempt to destroy the threat in its infancy, before it had grown into the cosmographic monster the cycle requires. The plot depends on the storm-god remaining unaware until the threat has matured.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the concealment of the infant Ullikummi from Teshub.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Kumarbi entrusts the infant to the Irširra deities and other intermediaries who carry him away to a place of safety beyond Teshub's surveillance. The Irširra are subsidiary figures within the cycle's wider divine court; their role in this passage is purely instrumental, the carriers of a hidden cargo who deliver it to its destined platform.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the Irširra deities and the transport of the infant.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Planting on Upelluri's shoulder

The intermediaries place the infant Ullikummi on the right shoulder of Upelluri, the primordial giant who bears the world. Upelluri is so massive and so deeply rooted that he does not at first perceive the burden of the growing monster. The decision to plant Ullikummi on a giant who will not notice him is a tactical one inside the narrative: it gives Kumarbi's monster a hiding place and an anchor at once.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the planting of Ullikummi on Upelluri's right shoulder.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Upelluri is presented as a primordial cosmographic figure: the giant on whose shoulders the heavens and earth were originally separated and rest. His own awareness is dulled by his immense size and his cosmogonic age. He is not so much a character with a perspective as a feature of the cosmos, and the song treats him accordingly.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Upelluri as cosmographic foundation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The choice of Upelluri's shoulder as the planting-place ensures that Ullikummi grows out of the world's foundation itself, so that any combat against him is inseparable from the structure of the cosmos. To attack Ullikummi is, in the song's terms, to attack the world's bearing-point, and the question of how to defeat him without dismantling the cosmos is one that the song will eventually answer through Ea's intervention.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the cosmographic implications of the planting site.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== The growth of Ullikummi

Ullikummi grows at a prodigious rate. The text describes the growth in terms of cubits and acres of growth per day, until he stands as a stone column rising from the sea up toward the heavens. The numerical specifics matter for the song's effect: the reader is given a measurable rate, and the rate is high enough that the threat is palpable from the early passages.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the prodigious growth of Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The growing Ullikummi rises from the sea, his stone body surfacing through the water as he extends upward. The visual image is of a stone shaft thrusting upward from the sea-surface toward the sky. The image is the song's signature, and it is the image that has carried the narrative across the comparative literature: a stone column rising from sea to heaven is the kind of cosmographic object that, once introduced, fixes the reader's eye on the song.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the rising of Ullikummi from the sea.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

When Ullikummi has grown to his full extent, his head reaches up to the gates of heaven where Teshub resides. He threatens, by reaching that height, to displace the storm-god from the sky itself. The cosmographic threat is now explicit: the monster is no longer a hidden child but a structural rival to Teshub's heavenly seat.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ullikummi reaching the gates of heaven.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== The sun-god's report

The sun-god is the first deity to perceive the growing menace. He is the first to report Ullikummi's existence to Teshub, and his role at this point in the song is that of an observer at the right altitude: travelling daily across the sky, the sun-god is positioned to notice a stone column whose growth has begun to obstruct his line of sight.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the sun-god as first to perceive Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The sun-god's report to Teshub is the narrative trigger for Teshub's combat-mobilisation. The storm-god learns of the threat from a divine messenger before he himself observes it; the song is careful to give Teshub a delegated knowledge of the danger first, with his own direct observation following later when he climbs to a vantage point.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the sun-god's report to Teshub.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Teshub views the monster

After receiving the sun-god's report, Teshub goes with his sister Šauška, paired here at first mention with the Mesopotamian Ishtar, to a mountain vantage point from which they can observe Ullikummi. Teshub's first reaction is one of dismay at the size and the apparent invulnerability of the stone column. The dismay is part of the song's set-up: the storm-god is brought to the edge of his confidence before any combat begins, and the failure of his first attack will be prepared for the reader by his initial reaction to what he sees.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Teshub's reconnaissance with Šauška.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Šauška's failed seduction-attack

Šauška attempts a seduction approach to Ullikummi — the same technique she will be assigned in the Song of Hedammu — but Ullikummi, being deaf and blind by virtue of his stone constitution, cannot be reached by her. The technique that will work in Hedammu fails here, and the failure is anchored in Ullikummi's specific physiology: a stone monster has no senses to seduce.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Šauška's attempt and Ullikummi's deafness/blindness.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

A subordinate marine deity informs Šauška that her seduction-method is futile against Ullikummi because of his sensory disabilities, and she abandons the attempt. The marine deity's intervention is brief but functional: it ends one possible line of attack and clears the way for the storm-combat that follows.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the marine deity's warning to Šauška.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Teshub's first attack

Teshub mobilises his combat resources — his bull-team, his weapons, and the seventy gods of his retinue — and engages Ullikummi in storm-combat. The mobilisation is described in the language of a divine campaign: chariot, animals, weaponry, and a numbered retinue, all assembled for the engagement.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Teshub's first combat-mobilisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Teshub's bull-team is named in the text. The two bulls are Šeri and Ḫurri, with Ḫurri sometimes appearing as Tilla in the regional Nuzi-attested variant of the Song of Ullikummi tradition. The bulls draw his combat-chariot in the engagement; they are the storm-god's standard mounts, and their names belong to the iconography of Teshub as much as the chariot itself.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Šeri and Ḫurri / Tilla as Teshub's bulls.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Teshub's first combat-attempt against Ullikummi fails. The storm-weapons are ineffective against the stone body, and Teshub is forced to retreat. The failure is stated in the surviving text without apology: the storm-god's full apparatus, deployed at scale, does not suffice against this adversary.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the failure of Teshub's first attack.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The failure of the first attack is the song's principal narrative crux. The storm-god's standard combat-method — lightning and storm against organic adversary — does not work against a mineral antagonist whose body cannot be torn or burned. The song has carefully built Ullikummi's invulnerability into his physiology, and the failed first attack is the consequence; the narrative now requires a different kind of solution.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the inadequacy of storm-weapons against stone.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

[LACUNA: Song of Ullikummi middle tablets — the gap between Ullikummi's growth on Upelluri's shoulder and the consultation with Ea contains broken passages | source: Hoffner 1998 §15 (Song of Ullikummi), middle tablets | scholarly_reconstruction: Hoffner reconstructs partially from parallel passages but flags the section as incompletely preserved]

==== Consultation with Ea

After the failure of his first attack, Teshub takes counsel. The cycle's pattern of consulting Ea as wisdom-god in moments of impasse is here reactivated: the song, by reaching for Ea at this point, signals that the problem has shifted from one of force to one of intelligence.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Teshub's consultation with Ea after the failed attack.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Ea, the wisdom-god, is consulted because the combat-impasse is intellectual as well as martial. A technical solution is needed that the storm-god alone has not been able to identify, and Ea's role in the cycle is precisely to supply such solutions. He is, in the song's economy, the figure who knows what to do when straightforward combat has failed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ea's role as wisdom-counsellor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Ea travels to Upelluri to investigate the structural origin of the threat. He speaks directly with the giant who bears the world, asking what is on his shoulder. The interview is the song's diagnostic step: the wisdom-god goes to the foundation of the problem and asks the foundation itself what it knows.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ea's interview with Upelluri.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Upelluri's reply expresses his cosmographic obliviousness. He has not noticed the small thing that is growing on his shoulder, because he is so massive and so primordial that minor events on his body register only faintly. The giant, in other words, is too large to feel the weight of a stone column the size of Ullikummi; the same scale that makes him a foundation for the world makes him a poor sentinel against monsters his own size.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Upelluri's failure to notice Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== The primordial copper cutter

Ea identifies the solution. The primordial copper cutter, the implement by which heaven and earth were originally separated, will sever Ullikummi at the base from his anchorage on Upelluri's shoulder. The solution is technical rather than martial: not a stronger weapon, but a different one, designed for a different kind of cut.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on Ea's identification of the primordial cutter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The copper cutter is described as a primordial cosmogonic implement. The same tool that performed the original cosmogonic work of separating sky from earth is repurposed for the separation of the stone monster from his foundation. The choice of instrument is a piece of cosmological symmetry: the implement that defined the cosmos is reused to defend it.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the cosmogonic genealogy of the cutter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Ea retrieves or directs the use of the copper cutter, which is wielded to sever Ullikummi at the point where his stone body joins Upelluri's shoulder. The severing severs the monster's connection to his cosmogonic anchorage; the cut is at the base, not at the head, and the target is not the monster's body but his attachment to the world's foundation.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the severing of Ullikummi from Upelluri.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The severing is a structural rather than physical defeat in the first instance. Ullikummi is not destroyed by the cut, but his power-source — his rooting in the world's foundation — is removed, making him vulnerable to the storm-god's renewed assault. The cutter does not slay; it disables, and the slaying that follows is now a possibility because the disabling has been done.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the structural rather than mortal nature of the severing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

==== Renewed combat

After the severing, Teshub re-engages Ullikummi in combat. The surviving text indicates that the storm-god prevails in this second engagement, but the precise sequence of the renewed combat is partially broken. What survives is the fact of Teshub's return to the field and the implied outcome of his return.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the renewed combat after the severing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The Song of Ullikummi as preserved breaks off in the closing passages. Teshub's victory is implied rather than fully narrated, and the song's ending is not extant in the surviving manuscript witnesses. The reader must rely on the implication; the actual sequence of the storm-god's final blows against the severed monster is not on the surviving tablets.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the broken ending of the Song of Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

[LACUNA: the song's closing passages and the explicit narration of Teshub's final defeat of Ullikummi | source: Hoffner 1998 §15 (Song of Ullikummi), closing tablets | scholarly_reconstruction: Hoffner indicates that Teshub's victory is the implied conclusion, but the verbatim ending is not preserved]

==== Structural features of the Ullikummi narrative

The Song of Ullikummi has a tripartite combat structure. There is a failed first attack, a wisdom-counsellor's intervention, and a successful second attack. The pattern is recognisable across the Mesopotamian combat corpus and is discussed in Annus's introduction to the Anzû Epic in similar terms; it is the structural shape that the cycle inherits and adapts.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15; Annus 2001, introduction, on the tripartite combat structure as a Mesopotamian pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The wisdom-counsellor's intervention specifies a non-obvious technical solution — the primordial cutter applied to the structural anchor — rather than a more powerful weapon. The song thereby foregrounds intelligence over force as the decisive factor in the combat. The combat is not won by a second, larger storm; it is won by a different kind of action altogether, identified by a god whose function is precisely to identify such actions.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the wisdom-and-technique solution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The body-as-cosmography logic of Ullikummi — a stone monster planted in the foundation of the world, growing through sea to sky — is unique within the surviving Hurro-Hittite corpus. It represents one of the most elaborate cosmographic monsters in any ancient Near Eastern combat narrative. No other surviving combat-myth makes the adversary's body so directly continuous with the structure of the cosmos itself.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15, on the cosmographic distinctiveness of Ullikummi.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The Ullikummi narrative is the principal evidence for the comparative argument that the Greek Typhon, a similarly enormous monster who threatens Zeus's kingship by reaching toward heaven, is descended through a Hurro-Hittite intermediary from this Hurrian template (cross-ref: Ch. 11). The argument is the central case of Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission for the combat material, and the Ullikummi-Typhon parallel is the part of it that is most strongly anchored in concrete narrative correspondences.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Ullikummi-Typhon correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The narrative's dual deployment of Šauška and Teshub — the goddess of love-and-war attempting seduction, the storm-god deploying combat — sets a structural template that recurs in the Song of Hedammu, where Šauška's seduction is the successful method. The two songs together establish a division of labour in the cycle: where seduction can work, Šauška is mobilised; where seduction fails, Teshub takes the field with technical help from Ea.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 and §16, on the Šauška-Teshub division of labour.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

Teshub's combat reliance on Šeri and Ḫurri, the bull-team, and on the seventy gods of his retinue, places the storm-god firmly within the Hurrian-Hittite religious imagery of the storm-god as a warrior-king with chariot, retinue, and combat-mounts. The combat is not a solitary duel; it is a divine military operation conducted by a sovereign with a household.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 and introduction, on the iconography of Teshub.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

The interplay between Teshub and Ea in the Ullikummi combat — warrior-god and wisdom-god as complementary functions — replicates the Mesopotamian Ninurta-Ea collaboration in the Anzû Epic and, in altered form, the Marduk-Ea relationship in *Enūma Eliš* (cross-ref: Ch. 2, Ch. 3). Across the three compositions, the same pair of divine functions reappears: a champion who fights and a counsellor who diagnoses. The continuity is one of the cycle's clearest marks of inheritance.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15; Annus 2001, introduction; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15

=== The Song of Hedammu — the fragmentary case

The Song of Hedammu is a fragmentary song of the Kumarbi Cycle preserved only in disconnected passages. The narrative survives in a state that makes continuous reading impossible without significant interpretive supplement; what the reader has are episodes, not a continuous story.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16 (Song of Hedammu), headnote, on the fragmentary state of the song.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

[LACUNA: Song of Hedammu throughout — most of the song's continuous narrative is lost; only disconnected episodes survive | source: Hoffner 1998 §16 (Song of Hedammu) | scholarly_reconstruction: none — the chapter presents the song in its fragmentary state and does not backfill from Ugaritic, Mesopotamian, or Greek parallels]

The fragments preserve the basic premise of the song. After the failure of Ullikummi, Kumarbi generates a second monstrous adversary — the sea-monster Hedammu — by mating with the daughter of the sea-god, named in the surviving fragments as Šertapšuruḫi. The conception is a structural repeat of the Ullikummi conception, with the cosmographic domain shifted: where Ullikummi was a stone monster grown from a rock, Hedammu is a sea-monster generated through a marine female.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the basic premise of the song.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Šertapšuruḫi is the daughter of the sea-god, characterised in the fragments as a marine female of enormous proportions. The precise relationships within her divine family are partially preserved; she is the daughter of the sea-god, and she is the mother of Hedammu by Kumarbi, and beyond that the surviving record is patchy.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on Šertapšuruḫi as the daughter of the sea-god.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Hedammu, the offspring of Kumarbi and Šertapšuruḫi, is described in the surviving fragments as a sea-monster of voracious appetite. He threatens to depopulate the seas and the lands by his consumption; the threat is one of feeding rather than of cosmographic ambition, and the song's central problem is how to stop a creature whose mode of attack is sustained, devouring hunger.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on Hedammu's voracity.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The fragments preserve a passage in which Šauška is mobilised to address the threat by means of seduction rather than direct combat. The deployment is the cycle's standard division-of-labour use of the war-and-love goddess: where the threat can be approached through desire, Šauška is sent rather than the storm-god.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on Šauška's mobilisation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

Šauška presents herself to Hedammu in a seduction-tableau, displaying her beauty in a manner that overcomes the sea-monster's appetite for destruction. The surviving fragments preserve passages of erotic suggestion within the tableau; what survives in continuous form, in other words, is mostly the seduction itself, less the events surrounding it.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the Šauška-Hedammu seduction tableau.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The seduction is implied to incapacitate Hedammu — by intoxication, by sexual stupor, or by both, in the surviving readings. The incapacitation reduces him to a state in which he is no longer capable of his earlier voracious destruction. The mechanism is not full combat; it is a different kind of disabling, in which the threat is neutralised through the diversion of its appetite.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the incapacitation of Hedammu through seduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The sequel to the seduction is not preserved in the surviving fragments. Whether Hedammu is killed, banished, or only neutralised, and by whose hand, cannot be reconstructed from the extant text. The fragments stop before the question of final disposition is answered, and any answer would have to be supplied from outside the surviving record.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the lost sequel to the seduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The Song of Hedammu is thus testimonially fragmentary in a way that the Ullikummi narrative is not. Its principal events — generation, seduction — are preserved, but the combat-resolution that the Ullikummi parallel would predict is missing from the record. The reader who comes to Hedammu with the structure of Ullikummi in mind has to accept that the structure cannot here be filled.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the differential preservation of the cycle's combat songs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The seduction-method as a counter-monster strategy is a distinctive feature of the Hurro-Hittite tradition. It is the principal structural innovation that distinguishes the Hedammu narrative from the Ullikummi narrative within the cycle. Where Ullikummi is met with storm and copper cutter, Hedammu is met with desire; the cycle thus maintains a repertoire of methods rather than a single one.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the seduction-method's distinctiveness.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

The fragmentary state of the Song of Hedammu is itself part of what we know about the cycle. The Hurrian-Hittite scribal tradition transmitted multiple combat-songs, but the differential survival of the manuscript record means that any reconstruction of the cycle's overall shape must work with this asymmetry. Hedammu was a song the scribes copied; what reaches the modern reader is a smaller fraction of it than what reaches them of Ullikummi.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16, on the implications of fragmentary preservation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16

[INFERENCE: the Song of Hedammu's narrative pattern (Kumarbi generates a sea-monster-adversary; Šauška seduces him into incapacitation) suggests a tripartite cycle in which Kumarbi's successive offspring threaten Teshub through different cosmographic domains (stone, sea, and the third song's sea/storm-god combat) | basis: the parallel structure of Ullikummi (stone, mountain, sky) and Hedammu (sea, marine, water) within the cycle's single master-narrative of Kumarbi-vs-Teshub | risk: the inference is structural-typological, not textual, and presupposes that the cycle has a planned overall shape rather than being an aggregation of independent songs.]

=== The Song of the Sea — the most fragmentary case

The Song of the Sea is a fragmentary Hurro-Hittite combat song in which the storm-god confronts the Sea — the marine domain personified as adversary — and survives in a state more fragmentary than even the Song of Hedammu. The reader of this song has the least continuous text to work from of any composition in the cycle.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17 (Song of the Sea), headnote, on the song's fragmentary state.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

[LACUNA: Song of the Sea throughout — most of the storm-god-versus-sea narrative is lost | source: Hoffner 1998 §17 (Song of the Sea) | scholarly_reconstruction: none — the chapter presents the song as fragmentary and explicitly does NOT silently conflate it with Ugaritic Baal-Yamm material]

The surviving fragments name the Sea as the antagonist. The Hurrian river-and-sea name Aranzaḫ appears in some fragments as well, though its identification with the Sea-as-antagonist of this song is not certain in the partial record. What the fragments establish, in other words, is that the song had a personified marine adversary; what they do not securely establish is which specific name was attached to that adversary in this composition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the Sea as antagonist and the appearance of Aranzaḫ.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The fragments suggest a confrontation pattern. The Sea makes demands, the divine assembly is dismayed, and the storm-god is mobilised in response. The precise sequence and outcome are not preserved; the fragmentary state cuts off before the resolution can be read.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the partial confrontation pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The structural overlap with the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm narrative — storm-god against personified Sea, with the Sea making sovereignty-demands of the divine assembly — is striking and has been treated as evidence for shared West-Semitic and Anatolian transmission of a sea-combat narrative. The Hurro-Hittite tradition, on this reading, was working with material that also reached Ugarit, and the parallel between the two surviving witnesses is a comparative-method datum.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the structural parallel; see comparative-hook for the reservation against silent reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The chapter does not, however, silently reconstruct the Song of the Sea from the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm material. The structural parallel is real but the textual reconstruction would be illegitimate, since the surviving Hurro-Hittite fragments are insufficient to license the substitution of Ugaritic content for Hurrian content (cross-ref: Ch. 7 Baal-Yamm; scope.md §"Reconstruction policy" rule 1). The question of what the Song of the Sea narrated cannot be answered from a parallel text in another tradition; it has to be answered from the surviving fragments themselves, and those fragments do not answer it.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17 introduction; scope.md on reconstruction policy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The Song of the Sea's fragmentary state is, like the Song of Hedammu's, itself a part of the chapter's testimony. The Hurro-Hittite combat-myth tradition included a storm-god-versus-sea narrative, but the precise content of that narrative is largely lost. What the cycle records, on this point, is the existence of the narrative and a pattern-shape consistent with the wider regional storm-god-versus-sea tradition; what it does not record, in surviving form, is the narrative itself.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the implications of the fragmentary state.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

The fragments preserve enough to confirm that the cycle's combat-repertoire extended beyond the stone-monster (Ullikummi) and sea-monster (Hedammu) narratives to include a sea-as-adversary narrative in the more direct West-Semitic mode. The cycle thus engaged a wide range of monstrous-cosmographic adversaries: a stone column, a marine voracious offspring, and a personified Sea. The breadth of the repertoire is one of the chapter's findings, even where the individual songs are not fully readable.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §17, on the breadth of the combat repertoire.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§17

=== The transmission argument

The Kumarbi Cycle is the principal documentary case for Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission of theogonic and combat material into Hesiod's *Theogony*. The cycle has been treated as the central transmission case in the modern scholarship since Güterbock's mid-twentieth-century editions; West and Burkert, working in the late twentieth century, build their respective arguments on the foundation Güterbock laid down.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Kumarbi Cycle as the central transmission case; Hoffner 1998 introduction on Güterbock's foundational role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The succession-sequence of Kingship in Heaven — Alalu, Anu, Kumarbi, Teshub — corresponds in structure and key episodes to Hesiod's succession-sequence of Ouranos, Kronos, and Zeus. Hesiod's Kronos-castration-of-Ouranos episode functions as a Greek transformation of Kumarbi's castration of Anu. The parallel is at the level of named events, not just of generic patterns; the same striking action, with the sky-figure as victim and the next sovereign as actor, appears in both compositions.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Hurro-Hittite-Hesiodic correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The detailed correspondence in motifs — castration of the previous sovereign, the swallowing event, the internal-conception-and-birth-of-the-storm-god — places the Hesiodic theogony within a transmission chain that runs through the Hurro-Hittite material. The chain is not direct; what the comparative argument establishes is a relationship of dependence, with the Greek text on the receiving end of a tradition that had taken its decisive shape in the Hurrian and Hittite material.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the motif-level correspondence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

West's argument distinguishes between motifs that could be independent and motifs that are sufficiently specific to require transmission rather than convergence. The castration-and-swallowing combination, the prophetic declaration after castration, and the named succession of sovereigns are the specific motifs on which his case turns. A theogony that has all three is, on West's reading, harder to explain by independent invention than by inheritance.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on motif-specificity as a transmission test.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

Burkert's *Orientalizing Revolution* argues for Phoenician intermediaries as the transmitting culture. The Kumarbi material reaches Greek poets, on Burkert's account, not directly from Hurrian or Hittite sources but through the Phoenician-mediated Levantine network of the early Archaic period. The route is therefore specified: not a leap from Anatolia to Greece but a relay across the Levantine coast and its trading partnerships.footnote:[Burkert 1992, on Phoenician intermediaries in the orientalizing transmission.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume

The Phoenician-intermediary hypothesis is supported by the broader pattern of orientalizing Greek artistic and religious motifs in the eighth and seventh centuries BCE. The route of transmission is best documented for Levantine-Phoenician contact zones, and the textual transmission Burkert proposes runs along the same channel as the artistic and religious imports that the orientalizing horizon attests.footnote:[Burkert 1992, on the orientalizing horizon and Phoenician contact.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume

The Sanchuniathon theogony preserved by Philo of Byblos and quoted by Eusebius is treated by West as an attestation of the Kumarbi succession in Phoenician-Greek mediation. The Sanchuniathon material provides an intermediate textual link between the Hurro-Hittite and Hesiodic versions: a theogony in Greek, attributed to a Phoenician source, that preserves elements of the Kumarbi succession.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on Philo of Byblos's Sanchuniathon as transmission evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The transmission argument's strength is the convergence of independent lines of evidence. There are motif-level correspondences, structural parallels in the succession, and the orientalizing-period archaeological and art-historical context for sustained Levantine-Greek contact. No single line of evidence is decisive on its own; together they make the transmission hypothesis the best available explanation for the pattern of correspondences.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992 on the convergence of evidence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The combat dimension of the transmission — the Ullikummi-Typhon parallel — runs alongside the theogonic dimension. Just as Kumarbi's stone-monster threatens to displace Teshub by reaching toward heaven, Hesiod's Typhon threatens to displace Zeus by similar cosmographic ambition (cross-ref: Ch. 11). The two transmissions, theogonic and combat, are aspects of the same inheritance; the cycle's role as transmission bridge holds for both.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on Ullikummi-Typhon.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The Hurro-Hittite combat material is thus the principal bridge between the Mesopotamian combat-corpus — Anzû, *Enūma Eliš*; cross-ref: Ch. 2, Ch. 3 — and the Greek Hesiodic combat-corpus — Zeus and Typhon; cross-ref: Ch. 11. The case for transmission is strongest in this segment of the wider chain, both because the documentary records on either side are reasonably well preserved and because the correspondences are concrete enough to support the comparative argument.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

The transmission case is taken up in detail in this book's Part II C6, on Near-Eastern-to-Mediterranean transmission. The present chapter provides the primary documentary evidence on which the C6 argument rests; the analytical work on the case structure is reserved for the later treatment.footnote:[Forward-pointer to Ch. 11 and Part II C6; West 1997 and Burkert 1992 as the evidentiary basis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

Forsyth places the Hurro-Hittite combat material as a documented link in the long sequence of the combat-myth tradition that runs from Sumer through the Hebrew Bible to Christian apocalyptic. The Kumarbi Cycle is the Anatolian station in that chain; for Forsyth's framework, it occupies a position that is neither origin nor terminus but a middle-link whose preservation makes the whole chain readable.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the Hurro-Hittite station in the combat-myth chain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

Scurlock and Beal's volume includes Anatolian-focused chapters that engage the Kumarbi material as evidence for or against the comparative-combat category as Gunkel framed it. The volume treats the Hurro-Hittite case as a non-trivial test for the Chaoskampf hypothesis: contributors disagree about how far the cycle supports the category, and the disagreements themselves are part of why the cycle remains a productive case in the literature.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, chapters on Anatolian material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=chapters on Anatolian material

The Hurro-Hittite case, as preserved, supports the argument that combat-myth narratives travel with cultural contact. The cycle is itself a Hurrian reception of Mesopotamian materials; it is then a Hittite reception of the Hurrian cycle; and, via Phoenician mediation, it becomes a Greek reception of the Hurro-Hittite material. Each station modifies the inherited template, and the modifications are themselves the evidence of transmission: a tradition that travels carries a chain of small alterations that mark its passage.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992, on the chain-reception model.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's role as transmission bridge is what makes it the structural fulcrum of this book's geographical argument. It is the chapter through which the Mesopotamian cluster of chapters one through four connects to the Greek cluster at chapter eleven, and it is the chapter through which the Anatolian-Levantine zone is established as the principal transmission corridor. The book's geography is, in effect, organised around this cycle's documentary position.footnote:[Forward-pointer to Ch. 11 and Part II C6; West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6

=== Variants and adjacent songs

The Kumarbi Cycle includes several songs that are narratively adjacent to the combat-songs but are not themselves combat-focused. The brief and Hoffner's edition treat these as cycle-context rather than as body narrative for this chapter, and the chapter follows that treatment.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the cycle's full song-roster.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The Song of LAMMA is one such adjacent song. It narrates an episode in which the deity LAMMA temporarily assumes kingship in heaven during the cycle's broader arc; Hoffner treats it as a cycle-frame song rather than a combat-song. The episode's interest is the further illustration it offers of the cycle's interest in successions and temporary occupations of the throne.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the Song of LAMMA.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The Song of Silver narrates an episode involving the deity Silver, possibly Kumarbi's offspring, and possibly involving a sovereignty-claim against Teshub. The song is fragmentary and is treated as cycle-context. Its evidentiary value for the combat material is therefore secondary, but it adds to the picture of Kumarbi as a generator of successive challengers to the storm-god.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the Song of Silver.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

A further song, sometimes titled Sun-God-and-Cow, sits adjacent to the cycle's main material and engages the wider Hurrian-Hittite mythological corpus. Hoffner includes it in the broader collection but does not assign it to the combat-cycle proper. It is, in the cycle's geography, a piece of adjacent material rather than a constituent piece.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on Sun-God-and-Cow as adjacent material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14–18

The classification of "the Kumarbi Cycle" as a unified cycle versus a loose grouping of related songs is itself a scholarly judgment. Hoffner adopts the cyclic classification because of the recurring cast — Kumarbi, Teshub, Šauška, Ea — and the recurring premise of Kumarbi's revenge against Teshub through monstrous offspring. Where the same actors and the same plot-engine recur across songs, the cyclic classification has prima facie warrant.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the cycle's classification.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

Some scholars treat the cycle's songs as independent compositions linked only by shared cast and shared scribal tradition. The present chapter follows Hoffner's cyclic framing, which is the standard scholarly convention, while flagging the alternative; the evidence does not unambiguously settle the question, and a reader who comes to the manuscript record without Hoffner's editorial framing might reasonably read the songs as a series rather than a cycle.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the cyclic versus aggregational debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's preserved songs are distributed across the Hittite scribal tradition's compositional layers. Some songs are first attested in Middle Hittite copies, others first in New Hittite copies, and the manuscript distribution suggests sustained scribal interest across multiple periods. The cycle was not copied at a single moment and then transmitted as a fossil; it was a live part of the scribal repertoire over time.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the manuscript distribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's distinctive features within the wider Hurro-Hittite corpus — the corporeal generative logic of Kumarbi's offspring, the wisdom-god-as-technical-solver pattern, the Šauška-as-seductress strategy — make it identifiable as a coherent compositional project even as the songs themselves vary in tone and length. The cycle has a recognisable house style; the songs that belong to it can be told from the songs that do not, even where the adjacent material is part of the same scribal tradition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the cycle's distinctive features.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The cycle's relationship to the separate Hittite Illuyanka myth is one of parallel rather than direct continuity. Illuyanka is a discrete combat narrative in the Hittite tradition, attested to ritual context (the purulli-festival), while the Kumarbi Cycle is a Hurrian-Hittite scribal-literary project without an attested ritual rubric (cross-ref: Ch. 4-supplement and Ch. 11 transmission discussion; the Illuyanka material is treated separately in Ch. 6 of this volume's working sequence). The two combat traditions sit side by side in the Hittite record without merging; their relationship is one of coexistence within the same archive rather than of direct mutual influence.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction and §12 (Illuyanka); Beckman 1982, on Illuyanka as discrete myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction

The closing summary on the cycle takes the form the chapter's argument now permits. The Kumarbi material as preserved is the Hurro-Hittite scribal tradition's principal contribution to the Near-Eastern-Mediterranean combat-myth corpus. It is the documented bridge between Mesopotamian combat-narrative and Greek theogonic-combat narrative. And it is the most cosmographically elaborate combat-cycle in the surviving Anatolian record. Each of those three claims is independently supported by the surviving evidence, and together they justify the cycle's position as the structural hinge of this book's wider geography.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction; West 1997, ch. 6; Burkert 1992; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
