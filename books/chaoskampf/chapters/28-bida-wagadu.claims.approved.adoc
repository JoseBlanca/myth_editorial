=== Source overview

The chapter treats the Soninke / Mande oral tradition in which the seven-headed serpent Bida is the protector-spirit of the city of Wagadu and is killed by the suitor Mamadi Sefe Dekote when the suitor's beloved is taken in the city's annual maiden-tribute, with the killing of Bida triggering the abandonment of Wagadu, the cessation of the rains, and the dispersal of the Soninke population — a narrative in which the slaying of the serpent ends rather than establishes the city's prosperity, inverting the order-establishing outcome of the Eurasian chaoskampf material treated in the preceding chapters of the present book.footnote:[Per brief 28-bida-wagadu.yaml summary on the seven-headed serpent Bida as protector of the Soninke city of Wagadu, on the annual tribute of a young woman, on Mamadi Sefe Dekote's killing of Bida to save his beloved, and on the abandonment of Wagadu / cessation of the rains / dispersal of the people that follows; Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa: Ghana, Mali, and Songhay* (New York: Facts on File; ISBN 9780816052622), ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the narrative core common to the recorded Soninke oral-tradition versions; Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa* (Bloomington: Indiana University Press; ISBN 9780253212818), ch. on Soninke traditions, on the Bida / Wagadu narrative as a foundational oral tradition of the Soninke and on the slaying-ends-prosperity narrative pattern that distinguishes the tradition from the Eurasian combat-myth material.]

=== The Soninke, Wagadu, and the historical Ghana polity

The Soninke (also recorded as Soninké, Sarakolé, or Marka in different ethnographic and historical-administrative traditions) are a Mande-speaking population of the western Sahel whose historical heartland is the upper Senegal / upper Niger river basin region of present-day Mauritania, western Mali, and adjacent parts of Senegal, and whose oral tradition designates the medieval western-Sahelian polity that flourished there as Wagadu — the same polity known in the Arabic-language chronicle tradition as Ghana.footnote:[Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa: Ghana, Mali, and Songhay*, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the Soninke (Soninké, Sarakolé, Marka in different ethnographic and historical-administrative traditions) as a Mande-speaking population of the western Sahel, on their historical heartland in the upper Senegal / upper Niger river basin region of present-day Mauritania, western Mali, and adjacent parts of Senegal, and on the Soninke oral-tradition designation of the medieval polity as Wagadu / Arabic-language Ghana.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=conrad-west-africa ; loc=Empires of Medieval West Africa, ch. on Ghana/Wagadu legend

The historical Ghana / Wagadu polity flourished from approximately the eighth century CE through the eleventh-to-twelfth century CE — the conventional periodisation in current Mande-studies scholarship — with its political and economic significance grounded in its position athwart the trans-Saharan trade routes connecting the western Sahel with the Maghreb, and particularly in its control of the gold-producing regions south and southwest of the polity's heartland; the polity's decline in the eleventh-to-twelfth century is conventionally connected with the Almoravid pressures of the late eleventh century and with the subsequent reorganisation of the western-Sahelian political landscape under the rising Mali polity of the thirteenth century onward.footnote:[Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa*, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the historical periodisation of the Ghana / Wagadu polity from approximately the eighth century CE through the eleventh-to-twelfth century CE, on the polity's position athwart the trans-Saharan trade routes connecting the western Sahel with the Maghreb, on its control of the gold-producing regions south and southwest of the heartland, and on the conventional connection of the polity's decline with the Almoravid pressures of the late eleventh century and with the subsequent rise of the Mali polity from the thirteenth century onward.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=conrad-west-africa ; loc=Empires of Medieval West Africa, ch. on Ghana/Wagadu legend

The Soninke oral tradition's own framing of the Wagadu narrative is foundational and aetiological: the narrative is told as the founding charter of the Soninke as a people, as the explanation of their dispersal across the western Sahel and beyond from a once-prosperous heartland, and as the aetiology of the encroachment of the Sahara on the formerly-watered region of the polity's territory; the narrative is therefore a connected foundation-story rather than a primarily cosmogonic narrative, and the cosmos within which it unfolds is the already-existing world of the western Sahel rather than a cosmogonically-emerging cosmos.footnote:[Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the Soninke oral tradition's framing of the Wagadu narrative as the founding charter of the Soninke as a people, as the explanation of their dispersal across the western Sahel and beyond, and as the aetiology of the encroachment of the Sahara on the formerly-watered region; on the narrative's character as a connected foundation-story rather than a primarily cosmogonic narrative, and on the already-existing-world cosmographic framing within which the narrative unfolds; per brief 28-bida-wagadu.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the foundation-narrative, aetiological, and moral-didactic functions of the narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions

The narrative's principal protagonists are: Bida, the seven-headed serpent who is the protector-spirit of Wagadu and to whom the city's prosperity is bound by covenant; the rulers of Wagadu, who maintain the covenant on the city's behalf; the Soninke population of the city, on whose ritual maintenance of the annual tribute the city's prosperity depends; the maiden delivered each year as the tribute (her name and lineage varying by recorded version); the suitor Mamadi Sefe Dekote, who breaks the covenant by killing Bida to save his beloved; and the displaced Soninke population, whose dispersal across the western Sahel after the killing supplies the foundation-narrative for the Soninke diaspora.footnote:[Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa*, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the principal protagonists of the narrative — Bida the seven-headed serpent-protector of Wagadu, the rulers of Wagadu maintaining the covenant, the Soninke population on whose ritual maintenance of the annual tribute the city's prosperity depends, the maiden delivered each year as the tribute (with her name and lineage varying by recorded version), the suitor Mamadi Sefe Dekote breaking the covenant by killing Bida, and the displaced Soninke population whose dispersal supplies the foundation-narrative for the Soninke diaspora; per glossary 'Bida', 'Wagadu', 'Mamadi Sefe Dekote' first_mention_glosses on the principal protagonists.]

=== The covenant: Bida as protector and the annual tribute

The covenant between Bida and the Soninke of Wagadu is the narrative's load-bearing structural premise: in the recorded versions, the seven-headed serpent Bida agrees to protect the city, to grant prosperity in the form of regular rains and (in many versions) an annual rain of gold, in exchange for a yearly tribute of the most beautiful maiden of the community, whose delivery to Bida's well or pool is the ritual act through which the covenant is maintained year by year.footnote:[Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the covenant between Bida and the Soninke of Wagadu as the narrative's load-bearing structural premise; on the seven-headed serpent's protection of the city, on the prosperity granted in the form of regular rains and (in many versions) an annual rain of gold, on the yearly tribute of the most beautiful maiden of the community, and on the delivery to Bida's well or pool as the ritual act through which the covenant is maintained year by year; Conrad 2005, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the same covenant-and-annual-tribute structural premise.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions

The covenant's structural function in the narrative is therefore order-maintenance rather than order-establishment: the cosmos and the city are already in place when the narrative begins, the covenant maintains the prosperity of the city against the always-present threat of disorder (drought, scattering, the encroachment of the desert), and the annual maiden-tribute is the ritual practice through which the maintaining-of-order is performed by the human community in concert with the protector-serpent — a structural arrangement that places the covenant within the *cyclical-maintenance* register of order-making rather than the *cosmogonic* or *sovereignty-establishing* registers per scope.md §"Subject definition".footnote:[Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the covenant's structural function as order-maintenance rather than order-establishment, on the already-in-place cosmos-and-city framing of the narrative, on the maintaining-of-prosperity-against-always-present-threat-of-disorder pattern, and on the annual maiden-tribute as the ritual practice through which the maintaining-of-order is performed; per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the cyclical-maintenance register of order-making (cf. Ra/Apep, Illuyanka in the *purulli* festival) as distinct from the cosmogonic and sovereignty-establishing registers.]

=== Mamadi Sefe Dekote and the killing of Bida

In the year of the killing, the maiden whose turn has come for the annual tribute is the betrothed (or, in some versions, the most-favoured beloved) of the suitor Mamadi Sefe Dekote, whose name is variously translated in the recorded versions as "Mamadi the Taciturn" or "Mamadi of Few Words" in conventional gloss; Mamadi's lineage is given in some recorded versions as descending from the rulers of the city, and in other versions as the head of one of the city's principal warrior-lineages, with the specifics of the lineage being among the principal points of variation across the recorded-version corpus.footnote:[Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa*, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the year of the killing as the year in which the maiden whose turn has come is the betrothed or most-favoured beloved of Mamadi Sefe Dekote, on the conventional gloss of Mamadi's name as "Mamadi the Taciturn" or "Mamadi of Few Words," on the variation in his lineage across the recorded versions (descending from the rulers in some, head of a principal warrior-lineage in others), and on lineage-variation as among the principal points of variation across the recorded-version corpus; per glossary 'Mamadi Sefe Dekote' first_mention_gloss on the suitor who kills Bida.]

Mamadi's response to the impending tribute is to refuse the loss of his beloved and to resolve to kill Bida rather than allow the maiden to be delivered to the protector-serpent; in the recorded versions, this resolution is undertaken in defiance of the rulers of the city, of the elders of the maiden's family, and (in some versions) of the explicit advice of his beloved herself, who urges him to accept the tribute as the price of the city's continued prosperity.footnote:[Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on Mamadi's response to the impending tribute as refusal of the loss of his beloved and resolution to kill Bida, on the resolution being undertaken in defiance of the rulers of the city and of the elders of the maiden's family, and (in some recorded versions) on the explicit advice of his beloved herself urging him to accept the tribute as the price of the city's continued prosperity.]

The killing itself, in the most widely attested form of the narrative, takes place at Bida's watering-place when the protector-serpent emerges to receive the annual tribute: Mamadi conceals himself near the watering-place with his sword, waits until the serpent emerges and lowers its heads to take the maiden, and strikes the heads from the serpent's body in succession — with the head-by-head pattern of the killing being a load-bearing narrative feature in the seven-headed renderings, and with the recorded versions varying in the number of strokes and in the response of the serpent's regenerating heads to each stroke.footnote:[Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa*, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the killing's setting at Bida's watering-place at the moment of the annual tribute, on Mamadi's concealment near the watering-place with his sword, on the waiting for the serpent to emerge and lower its heads, on the head-by-head pattern of the killing as a load-bearing narrative feature in the seven-headed renderings, and on the variation across recorded versions in the number of strokes and in the response of the serpent's regenerating heads.]

=== The covenant-and-consequence reading: the tradition's own framing

The Soninke oral-tradition's own framing of the narrative is, per brief 28-bida-wagadu.yaml cross_cultural_parallels, *covenant-and-consequence* rather than *cosmogonic-reversal*: the narrative is told as the story of a covenant well-kept across many generations, a covenant broken in a single generation by a single act of unsanctioned individual heroism, and the consequence of the breaking — the abandonment of the city, the dispersal of the people, the cessation of the rains — as the moral-didactic kernel that the narrative communicates to its hearers; the inversion-of-chaoskampf reading is a 20th-century-comparativist overlay that the tradition's own framing does not anticipate.footnote:[Per brief 28-bida-wagadu.yaml cross_cultural_parallels on the Soninke tradition's framing of the narrative as covenant-and-consequence rather than cosmogonic-reversal, on the inversion-of-chaoskampf reading as a 20th-century comparativist overlay rather than an internal feature of the tradition, and on the chapter's task of opening with the tradition's own framing before invoking the comparative chaoskampf category; Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the narrative's moral-didactic kernel as the consequence-of-breaking-the-covenant and on the well-kept-then-broken covenant pattern as the tradition's own narrative-structural framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions

The narrative's moral-didactic register within the Soninke tradition is therefore directed at the audience of the performance rather than at any cosmogonic question: the griot's audience is invited to consider the cost of breaking a covenant for the sake of an immediate good, to feel the gravity of Mamadi's choice and the tragic-didactic register of the consequence, and to recognise in the narrative the founding of the Soninke people through the very loss of Wagadu — with the foundation-narrative function and the moral-didactic function being intertwined in the narrative's tradition-internal performance context.footnote:[Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the narrative's moral-didactic register within the Soninke tradition as directed at the audience of the performance rather than at any cosmogonic question, on the griot's audience being invited to consider the cost of breaking a covenant for the sake of an immediate good and to feel the gravity of Mamadi's choice and the tragic-didactic register of the consequence, and on the foundation-narrative function and moral-didactic function as intertwined in the narrative's tradition-internal performance context; per brief 28-bida-wagadu.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the moral-didactic function (the cost of breaking a covenant) alongside the foundation-narrative function (origin of the Soninke diaspora and the end of Wagadu / ancient Ghana).]

=== Typology, area-specialist framing, and forward-links
