== Nanabozho and Mishipeshu (The Refusal)

=== Source overview

This chapter treats the Anishinaabe trickster-culture-hero Nanabozho and his encounters with the underwater manitou Mishipeshu and the Thunderers (Animikiig), presented within the Anishinaabe ontological framework of "other-than-human persons" (A. Irving Hallowell's 1960 phrase) in which Mishipeshu and the Thunderers are not chaos-and-order adversaries but reciprocal counterparts in a relational moral universe — a framing that the area-specialist scholarship of Hallowell, Basil Johnston, Theresa S. Smith, and Gerald Vizenor explicitly holds against the chaoskampf reading the present book elsewhere develops, and that the chapter's contrastive-case (full) treatment registers as the most explicit possible refusal of the chaos-vs-order mapping.footnote:[Per brief 32-nanabozho-mishipeshu.yaml summary on Nanabozho's encounters with Mishipeshu and the Thunderers as necessary counterparts in a reciprocal moral universe rather than as chaos to be defeated; on the area-specialist tradition (Hallowell, Johnston, T. Smith, Vizenor) explicitly declining the chaos-vs-order mapping; and on the Anishinaabe ontological framework of "other-than-human persons" placing Mishipeshu and the Thunderers in a relational web of respect and negotiation rather than in adversarial chaoskampf combat; Hallowell, A.I., 'Ojibwa Ontology, Behavior, and World View', in *Culture in History: Essays in Honor of Paul Radin*, ed. Stanley Diamond (New York: Columbia University Press, 1960), 19–52, on the "other-than-human persons" framework.]

The first thing to register about that material is the shape of the evidence behind it. The Anishinaabe material survives in oral-tradition form among the Anishinaabe (Ojibwe / Ojibway / Chippewa) peoples of the Great Lakes region of North America — extending across what is now Ontario, Manitoba, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, and adjacent provincial-and-state territories — with no pre-contact written attestation in any Indigenous-script tradition. The Anishinaabe languages had no pre-contact alphabetic or syllabic writing system, though syllabic writing was developed in the 19th century and adapted from Cree syllabics for some Anishinaabe communities. === The Anishinaabe world and the ontological framing

The Anishinaabe (Ojibwe / Ojibway / Chippewa) are an Indigenous people of the Great Lakes region of North America, with historical and contemporary territories extending across what is now southern Ontario, Manitoba, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Michigan, and adjacent provincial-and-state areas. They are part of the wider Anishinaabe-Algonquin / Algonquian-language family that also includes the Odawa (Ottawa), the Potawatomi, and other related peoples — collectively the Council of Three Fires across Anishinaabe / Odawa / Potawatomi territories — and the Anishinaabe-language tradition is one of the principal cultural-and-linguistic continuities of the Great Lakes region from the pre-contact period to the present.footnote:[Hallowell 1960, on the Anishinaabe / Ojibwa as an Indigenous people of the Great Lakes region with historical and contemporary territories extending across the present-day Ontario / Manitoba / Minnesota / Wisconsin / Michigan area; Johnston 1990 [1976], on the Anishinaabe and the wider Anishinaabe-Algonquin / Algonquian-language family including the Odawa and Potawatomi as the Council of Three Fires, and on the Anishinaabe-language tradition as one of the principal cultural-and-linguistic continuities of the Great Lakes region from the pre-contact period to the present; Smith 1995, on the Anishinaabe / Ojibwe / Ojibway / Chippewa naming-convention and on the regional and dialectal range of contemporary Anishinaabe communities.]

A. Irving Hallowell's 1960 essay "Ojibwa Ontology, Behavior, and World View", published in *Culture in History: Essays in Honor of Paul Radin* (ed. Stanley Diamond, Columbia University Press), articulates the framework on which the present chapter's ontological framing depends. The Anishinaabe / Ojibwa world is populated not only by human persons but by an extended category of "other-than-human persons" — Hallowell's exact phrase — which includes animals, plants, certain stones and other features of the landscape, the thunder-beings, and the manitouk. These beings are treated, in Anishinaabe ontology, as persons on equal standing with human persons rather than as objects, resources, or natural-kind categories.footnote:[Hallowell 1960, "Ojibwa Ontology, Behavior, and World View", articulating the framework on which the present chapter's ontological framing depends — the Anishinaabe / Ojibwa world populated not only by human persons but by an extended category of "other-than-human persons" including animals, plants, certain stones and other landscape features, the thunder-beings, and the manitouk; Hallowell's "other-than-human persons" exact phrase as the foundational term; the framework's load-bearing claim that other-than-human persons are treated as persons on equal standing with human persons rather than as objects, resources, or natural-kind categories; per glossary.yaml entry on "other-than-human persons" with first_mention_gloss "A. The framework carries direct moral implications for human conduct that the chapter's framing depends on. Because the manitouk are persons rather than objects, the appropriate human comportment toward them is the comportment appropriate to persons — relationships of respect, gift-and-counter-gift reciprocity, ceremonial protocol, formal address — rather than the comportment appropriate to objects (use, exploitation) or to enemies (combat, defeat). The moral framework is relational and reciprocity-grounded throughout, and the moral question in any given encounter is not whether the other-than-human person is friend or foe but whether the human comportment toward them has been appropriate.footnote:[Hallowell 1960, on the moral implications for human conduct of the "other-than-human persons" ontological framework — the appropriate comportment toward the manitouk being the comportment appropriate to persons (respect, gift-and-counter-gift reciprocity, ceremonial protocol, formal address) rather than the comportment appropriate to objects or to enemies; the moral framework as relational and reciprocity-grounded throughout; the moral question in any given encounter as whether the human comportment has been appropriate rather than whether the other-than-human person is friend or foe; Johnston 1990 [1976], throughout, on the Anishinaabe-authored insider register of the relational and reciprocity-respecting moral framework; Smith 1995, on the relational moral framework as the load-bearing register for reading the encounters with Mishipeshu and the Thunderers.]

The cosmographic framing within which Nanabozho's encounters unfold is, per Theresa S. Smith's *The Island of the Anishnaabeg* (University of Idaho Press, 1995) and the wider area-specialist tradition, a layered world. The human-and-island world — the lived-in landscape of forest, lake, and shore — is set between the upper-realm of the Thunderers (the sky-and-storm region) and the underwater-realm of Mishipeshu and the underwater-manitouk (the sub-aquatic-and-subterranean region). The human-and-island world is the meeting-place at which the upper-realm and the underwater-realm encounter one another, and at which Nanabozho's actions and human conduct mediate the relationship between the two realms.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg: Thunderers and Water Monsters in the Traditional Ojibwe Life-World*, throughout, on the Anishinaabe cosmographic framing as a layered world with the human-and-island world set between the upper-realm of the Thunderers (the sky-and-storm region) and the underwater-realm of Mishipeshu and the underwater-manitouk (the sub-aquatic-and-subterranean region); the human-and-island world as the meeting-place at which the upper-realm and the underwater-realm encounter one another; the load-bearing role of human conduct in mediating the relationship between the two realms; the title's "Island of the Anishnaabeg" being itself a load-bearing image of the human-and-island world's mediating position in the layered cosmography.]

The category of *aadizookaanag* — Basil Johnston's term for the class of sacred stories whose telling is restricted by ritual-and-seasonal protocol and whose subjects are the manitouk and culture-heroes including Nanabozho — is distinct, in the Anishinaabe tradition, from the broader category of stories that may be told without restriction. The *aadizookaanag* are conventionally told in winter, when the ground is frozen and the snakes and bears are dormant; the seasonal restriction itself reflects the relational-and-ceremonial protocol that the manitouk's status as other-than-human persons calls for. The sacred-story register is one of the load-bearing institutional features of the Anishinaabe oral-tradition's transmission of the Nanabozho material.footnote:[Johnston 1990 [1976], on the *aadizookaanag* category of sacred stories whose telling is restricted by ritual-and-seasonal protocol and whose subjects are the manitouk and culture-heroes including Nanabozho; on the *aadizookaanag*'s distinction from the broader category of stories that may be told without restriction; on the conventional winter-telling of the *aadizookaanag* with the seasonal restriction reflecting the relational-and-ceremonial protocol; Smith 1995, on the institutional features of the Anishinaabe oral-tradition's transmission of the Nanabozho material; Hallowell 1960, on the Anishinaabe distinction between sacred-story and other narrative registers; per glossary.yaml entry on "manitou / manitouk / aadizookaanag" with chosen=manitou (sing.) / manitouk (pl.); 'aadizookaanag' glossed separately for the sacred-story class of beings.]

The Mide lodge — the Midewiwin or Grand Medicine Society of the Anishinaabe — is the ceremonial institution within which the most extensive transmission of Anishinaabe sacred knowledge has historically occurred, including knowledge of the manitouk, the Nanabozho cycle, healing-and-medicinal practice, and the protocols of relationship with the other-than-human persons. The Mide tradition is itself a continuing institution within named contemporary Anishinaabe communities, and its standing as the load-bearing institutional context for the transmission of the material is one of the methodological features that distinguishes the Anishinaabe situation from the more strictly archival situations of the Eurasian chapters in the present book.footnote:[Johnston 1990 [1976], on the Mide lodge / Midewiwin / Grand Medicine Society of the Anishinaabe as the ceremonial institution within which the most extensive transmission of Anishinaabe sacred knowledge has historically occurred — including knowledge of the manitouk, the Nanabozho cycle, healing-and-medicinal practice, and the protocols of relationship with the other-than-human persons; on the Mide tradition's continuing-institutional standing within named contemporary Anishinaabe communities; Smith 1995, on the Mide lodge as the load-bearing institutional context for the transmission of the material; per brief 32-nanabozho-mishipeshu.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the ritual register of the Mide lodge and ceremonial relations with manitouk.]

=== Nanabozho the trickster-culture-hero

Nanabozho — with regional variants Nanabush, Wenabozho, and others — is, in the Anishinaabe tradition, the principal trickster-culture-hero of the cycle of sacred stories that bears his name. He is a figure whose actions in the time-of-the-aadizookaanag establish features of the present world, instruct the human community in the proper relations with the other-than-human persons, and exemplify both the powers and the limits of trickster-figure action across a wide range of episodes. He is not, in the Anishinaabe tradition, a sovereign-warrior-hero on the chaoskampf model, and his cycle is not structured by combat-and-defeat-of-adversaries as its load-bearing narrative shape.footnote:[Johnston 1990 [1976], throughout, on Nanabozho as the principal trickster-culture-hero of the Anishinaabe cycle of sacred stories — a figure whose actions in the time-of-the-aadizookaanag establish features of the present-day world, instruct the human community in the proper relations with the other-than-human persons, and exemplify both the powers and the limits of trickster-figure action across a wide range of episodes; on Nanabozho's not being a sovereign-warrior-hero on the chaoskampf model and on his cycle's not being structured by combat-and-defeat-of-adversaries as its load-bearing narrative shape; Johnston 1995, *The Manitous*, on Nanabozho as one of the principal manitouk and as the trickster-culture-hero figure; Hallowell 1960, on Nanabozho's place within the Anishinaabe ontological-and-cosmographic framework; per glossary.yaml entry on "Nanabozho" with chosen=Nanabozho per Hallowell 1960 and the Great Lakes scholarship and with first_mention_gloss "Anishinaabe trickster-culture-hero; his relations with the underwater manitou Mishipeshu and the Thunderers are the book's clearest case of a tradition whose own framework declines the chaoskampf reading".]

=== Mishipeshu and the underwater-manitouk

Mishipeshu — with regional spelling variants Mishebeshu, Mishipizheu, and others, the spelling Mishipeshu being the form most common in English-language Anishinaabe studies and locked in the present book's glossary — is, in the Anishinaabe tradition, the principal underwater-manitou. He is a powerful spirit-being of the lakes-and-rivers-and-deep-water, associated with copper, with serpentine and feline morphological features, with the great lakes of the Anishinaabe homeland (especially Lake Superior and the Boundary Waters region), and with the protective-and-dangerous powers that the deep-water realm carries within the layered Anishinaabe cosmography.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg*, throughout, on Mishipeshu as the principal underwater-manitou — a powerful spirit-being of the lakes-and-rivers-and-deep-water associated with copper, with serpentine and feline morphological features, with the great lakes of the Anishinaabe homeland (especially Lake Superior and the Boundary Waters region), and with the protective-and-dangerous powers of the deep-water realm; on the regional spelling variants (Mishebeshu, Mishipizheu, and others); Johnston 1995, *The Manitous*, on Mishipeshu as one of the principal manitouk and as the underwater-realm counterpart to the Thunderers in the layered Anishinaabe cosmography; per glossary.yaml entry on "Mishipeshu" with chosen=Mishipeshu and with first_mention_gloss "Anishinaabe underwater manitou; in the tradition's own framework a necessary counterpart to the Thunderers, not a chaos-adversary to be defeated".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-island-anishnaabeg ; loc=The Island of the Anishnaabeg, ch. on Thunderers and Water Monsters

The morphological description of Mishipeshu across the tradition combines feline-and-serpentine features in a register that the area-specialist scholarship reads as deliberately polymorphic rather than as a stable iconographic type. The figure is variously characterised as having a great-cat or panther body with a long horned-or-spiked tail, with serpentine extensions, with copper colouration or copper appurtenances, and with a fierce-and-protective rather than evil-and-adversarial moral character. These features are registered both in the oral-tradition material recorded in the 19th and 20th centuries and in the figural representations preserved in the rock-art / pictograph record — most famously the Agawa Rock pictographs at what is now Lake Superior Provincial Park in Ontario.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg*, on the morphological description of Mishipeshu across the Anishinaabe tradition combining feline-and-serpentine features in a register the area-specialist scholarship reads as deliberately polymorphic rather than as a stable iconographic type; on the figure's variously characterising as having a great-cat or panther body with a long horned-or-spiked tail, with serpentine extensions, with copper colouration or copper appurtenances, and with a fierce-and-protective rather than evil-and-adversarial moral character; on the features registered in both the oral-tradition material recorded in the 19th and 20th centuries and in the figural representations preserved in the rock-art / pictograph record; on the Agawa Rock pictographs at Lake Superior Provincial Park in Ontario as the most famous such representation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-island-anishnaabeg ; loc=The Island of the Anishnaabeg, ch. on Thunderers and Water Monsters

The moral character of Mishipeshu is, per Smith 1995 and the wider area-specialist scholarship, ambivalent in a register that the chapter's framing must hold against the chaoskampf-typology assimilation. Mishipeshu is dangerous to humans who fail to observe the proper relational protocols of approach, appropriate ceremonial address, and gift-and-reciprocity exchange — such humans may be drowned, capsized, or otherwise harmed by the underwater realm. But Mishipeshu is also a source of medicine, of knowledge of the underwater realm, of access to copper and other deep-water resources, and of protective-and-helpful intervention for humans who do observe the protocols. That ambivalence is what the chaoskampf-typology assimilation systematically misreads, by collapsing the relational-protocol structure into the chaos-vs-order moral binary.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg*, on the moral character of Mishipeshu as ambivalent in the relational-protocol register — dangerous to humans who fail to observe the proper protocols (drowning, capsizing, harm by the underwater realm), but also a source of medicine, of knowledge of the underwater realm, of access to copper and other deep-water resources, and of protective-and-helpful intervention for humans who do observe the protocols; on the ambivalence as systematically misread when collapsed into the chaos-vs-order moral binary; Johnston 1995, *The Manitous*, on Mishipeshu's moral ambivalence within the Anishinaabe relational moral framework; Hallowell 1960, on the relational moral framework within which the ambivalence is properly read.]

The underwater-realm itself is, in the Anishinaabe layered cosmography, a populated and structured realm rather than an unformed primordial chaos. It has its own hierarchy of beings — Mishipeshu as principal manitou; other underwater-manitouk in subsidiary positions; underwater-snakes and other beings inhabiting the realm. It has its own characteristic resources (copper most centrally), its own characteristic dangers (storms-from-below, capsizings, drownings), and its own characteristic protocols of approach and address. That structured-realm character is another structural feature systematically misread by the chaoskampf-typology assimilation, which projects the unformed-primordial-chaos register of Tiamat or Yamm onto a structured-and-populated underwater-realm that the Anishinaabe tradition characterises in much more specific relational-and-protocol terms.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg*, on the underwater-realm as a populated and structured realm rather than an unformed primordial chaos — with its own hierarchy of beings (Mishipeshu as principal manitou; other underwater-manitouk in subsidiary positions; underwater-snakes and other beings inhabiting the realm), its own characteristic resources (copper most centrally), its own characteristic dangers (storms-from-below, capsizings, drownings), and its own characteristic protocols of approach and address; on the structured-realm character as systematically misread by the chaoskampf-typology assimilation; Johnston 1995, *The Manitous*, on the populated-and-structured character of the underwater-realm within the Anishinaabe relational cosmography.]

=== The Thunderers (Animikiig) and the upper-realm

The Thunderers — Anishinaabemowin Animikiig — are, in the Anishinaabe tradition, the principal manitouk of the upper-realm. They are powerful sky-and-storm beings associated with thunder, lightning, and the high-altitude air of the storm-and-rain weather. They are characterised both as flying-bird-like beings (one of the principal forms in which they appear in the oral-tradition material) and as powerful manitouk in their own right, and the figural-and-narrative association of the Thunderers with Thunderbird imagery is one of the load-bearing connections between Anishinaabe narrative and the wider Great Lakes / Northeastern / Plains Indigenous Thunderbird-tradition complex.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg*, throughout, on the Thunderers (Animikiig) as the principal manitouk of the upper-realm — powerful sky-and-storm beings associated with thunder, lightning, and the high-altitude air of the storm-and-rain weather, characterised both as flying-bird-like beings (one of the principal forms in which they appear in the oral-tradition material) and as powerful manitouk in their own right; on the figural-and-narrative association of the Thunderers with Thunderbird imagery as one of the load-bearing connections between Anishinaabe narrative and the wider Great Lakes / Northeastern / Plains Indigenous Thunderbird-tradition complex; Johnston 1995, *The Manitous*, on the Thunderers as one of the principal manitouk and as the upper-realm counterpart to Mishipeshu; per glossary.yaml entry on "the Thunderers" with chosen=the Thunderers (English); Animikiig in first-mention gloss; first_mention_gloss "the Anishinaabe thunder-beings (Animikiig); counterparts of Mishipeshu in the reciprocal cosmic-dialectic".]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-island-anishnaabeg ; loc=The Island of the Anishnaabeg, ch. on Thunderers and Water Monsters

The moral character of the Thunderers in the Anishinaabe tradition is, like that of Mishipeshu, ambivalent rather than straightforwardly benevolent. The Thunderers are protective in some narrative registers — warding against the underwater-manitouk's depredations on humans; bringing the rain that supports human-and-plant life — and they are dangerous in others, in lightning-strikes that kill humans and animals and in the destructive force of the storm. The ambivalence is again read by the area-specialist scholarship in the relational-protocol register rather than in the chaos-vs-order register: the moral question is whether human conduct toward the Thunderers has observed the appropriate protocols (offerings, prayer, ceremonial address) rather than whether the Thunderers are cosmic-order figures opposed to a chaos-vs-order adversary class.footnote:[Smith 1995, *The Island of the Anishnaabeg*, on the moral character of the Thunderers as ambivalent rather than straightforwardly benevolent — protective in some narrative registers (warding against the underwater-manitouk's depredations on humans; bringing the rain that supports human-and-plant life) and dangerous in others (lightning-strikes that kill humans and animals; destructive force of the storm); on the ambivalence read in the relational-protocol register rather than in the chaos-vs-order register; on the moral question being whether human conduct toward the Thunderers has observed the appropriate protocols rather than whether the Thunderers are cosmic-order figures opposed to a chaos-vs-order adversary class; Hallowell 1960, on the relational moral framework within which the ambivalence is properly read; Johnston 1995, *The Manitous*, on the Thunderers' moral ambivalence within the Anishinaabe relational moral framework.]

The Thunderers' relationship to Mishipeshu and the underwater-manitouk — and this is the load-bearing structural feature of the chapter's interpretive frame — is one of *necessary counterpart* rather than of cosmic-order-vs-chaos adversaries. The cultural-historical importance of the Nanabozho / Mishipeshu / Thunderers material within the Anishinaabe tradition and within current Anishinaabe-and-Indigenous-studies scholarship is significant. The Nanabozho cycle is one of the principal narrative-and-cultural traditions of the Anishinaabe people.
