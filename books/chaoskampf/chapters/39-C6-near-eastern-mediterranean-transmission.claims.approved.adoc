=== Source overview

This chapter is a comparative-historical synthesis chapter rather than a primary-source narrative chapter; it draws on two layers of evidence — (1) the comparative-mythology anchor that supplies the chapter's principal transmission framework and the bulk of its route-specific exhibits (West 1997, *The East Face of Helicon*, treating Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek as the central documentary case; Burkert 1992, *The Orientalizing Revolution*, treating Phoenician-mediated transmission as the principal Iron-Age channel; Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, treating Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible as the principal Late-Bronze / Iron-Age channel; Yarbro Collins 1976, *The Combat Myth in the Book of Revelation*, treating Rev 12 as a deliberate Jewish-Hellenistic composite), and (2) the per-tradition area-specialist anchors that ground each upstream chapter's textual basis in its own primary-text witness (Hoffner 1998 for the Hurro-Hittite material; Smith 1994 and Smith & Pitard 2009 for the Ugaritic material).footnote:[Per brief 39-C6-near-eastern-mediterranean-transmission.yaml sources.anchor on the seven anchor IDs (west-1997; burkert-1992; day-1985; yarbro-collins-1976; hoffner-1998; smith-1994-baal-i; smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii); West, M.L., *The East Face of Helicon: West Asiatic Elements in Greek Poetry and Myth* (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1997), as the chapter's principal transmission anchor; Burkert, W., *The Orientalizing Revolution: Near Eastern Influence on Greek Culture in the Early Archaic Age* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1992), as the Phoenician-mediation anchor.]

=== I. The Hurro-Hittite → Greek route

The Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek route is the chapter's most documented transmission case and is the central exhibit of West 1997's *The East Face of Helicon*: the Kumarbi Cycle, surviving on cuneiform tablets from the Hittite royal archives at Boğazköy and treating the succession of divine sovereigns Alalu → Anu → Kumarbi → Teshub, supplies a sequence of motif-specific structural correspondences with Hesiod's *Theogony* whose convergent specificity is, in West's reading, almost inescapable as evidence of historical transmission rather than independent invention.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Kumarbi Cycle as the central documentary case for Near-Eastern-to-Greek transmission of theogonic and combat material into Hesiod's *Theogony*; Hoffner 1998, introduction and §§14–18, on the Boğazköy provenance and the cycle's general framework.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (Kumarbi Cycle as central transmission case)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction and §§14-18 (Boğazköy provenance)

The Kumarbi Cycle is a Hurro-Hittite song-cycle on the succession of kingship in heaven, surviving on cuneiform tablets recovered from the Hittite royal archives at Boğazköy, the site of ancient Hattusa, and copied within the Hittite scribal tradition across the Middle and New Hittite periods (broadly the fifteenth through thirteenth centuries BCE).footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Boğazköy provenance, the Middle and New Hittite manuscript distribution, and the cycle's general framework.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction (Boğazköy provenance and manuscript distribution)

The cycle is Hurrian in cultural substrate but preserved in the Hittite-language scribal record, and the surviving texts move between Hittite-language and Hurrian-language passages, with Hurrian theonyms (Teshub, Kumarbi, Šauška) adopted into the Hittite-language redaction alongside Akkadian theonyms (Anu, Ea) imported from Mesopotamian tradition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction, on the Hurrian substrate of the cycle, its Hittite-language transmission, and the mixed Hurrian / Akkadian theonymy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=introduction (Hurrian substrate and Hittite-language transmission)

The principal divine name in the cycle is Teshub, the Hurrian storm-god; this is the upstream-canonical rendering for §I material because the Kumarbi Cycle is a Hurrian-language composition (in Hittite-language adaptation), and the storm-god in this textual material is named Teshub even where the surrounding text is Hittite-language — the Hittite-language equivalent Tarḫunna is the form attested in Hittite-language religious contexts outside the Kumarbi Cycle, including the CTH 321 Illuyanka tablets treated in Ch6 and Ch36.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §§14–18, on the use of the Hurrian theonym Teshub throughout the cycle; Hoffner 1998, introduction, on Tarḫunna and Teshub as the Hittite and Hurrian forms of the storm-god across the Anatolian linguistic layers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§§14-18 (Teshub in Kumarbi Cycle)

The cycle's opening song, Kingship in Heaven, narrates the succession of divine sovereigns: Alalu reigns first (for nine years), is deposed by Anu (the Akkadian sky-god, imported into the Hurrian theogonic frame), Anu reigns for nine years and is in turn deposed by Kumarbi (the Hurrian father-god, Anu's cupbearer), and Kumarbi is in turn challenged and ultimately superseded by Teshub.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the four-stage succession Alalu → Anu → Kumarbi → Teshub and the nine-year reigns of the first two sovereigns.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14 (Kingship in Heaven succession)

The deposition of Anu by Kumarbi is the cycle's most striking and best-attested narrative event: Kumarbi attacks Anu, knocks him from the sky, bites off Anu's genitals, and swallows them — an act that simultaneously deposes Anu and impregnates Kumarbi with Anu's seed, from which Teshub the storm-god and other deities are subsequently born.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on Kumarbi's deposition of Anu by genital castration and swallowing, and on the conception of Teshub from Anu's seed inside Kumarbi's body.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14 (genital castration and Teshub's conception)

The Hesiodic *Theogony*, conventionally dated to c. 700 BCE, presents a structurally cognate three-generation succession of divine sovereigns — Ouranos (Sky), Kronos, and Zeus — in which each son violently displaces his father from the throne of heaven, and in which the most striking individual motif is the castration of Ouranos by Kronos with a sickle of adamant supplied by Gaia.footnote:[West 1966, on Theog. 154–210 on the Ouranos-Kronos-Zeus succession and on the castration of Ouranos by Kronos at Theog. 154–182.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1966-theogony ; loc=Theog. 154-210 (Ouranos-Kronos-Zeus succession)

The Hesiodic castration motif corresponds in motif-specific structural detail to the Hurro-Hittite genital-castration motif in Kingship in Heaven: in both narratives a younger sovereign castrates his elder predecessor, the act is the mechanism of deposition, and the act has reproductive consequences (Kumarbi's impregnation in the Hurro-Hittite version; the birth of Aphrodite from the cast-into-the-sea genitals in the Hesiodic version).footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Hurro-Hittite-Hesiodic correspondence of the genital-castration motif as one of the closest motif-specific parallels in the comparative-mythological literature; Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the Hurro-Hittite genital-castration narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (genital-castration motif correspondence)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14 (Hurro-Hittite genital-castration narrative)

A second motif-specific correspondence is the stone-swallowing trick: in the Hesiodic narrative Kronos swallows his children to forestall the prophesied succession, Rhea substitutes a stone for the infant Zeus, and Kronos vomits the stone (and the previously swallowed children) when Zeus subsequently administers an emetic; in Kingship in Heaven the post-castration sequence includes a passage in which Kumarbi ingests stones, with the surviving Hittite text broken at the precise mechanism but containing the same swallowed-stone motif as a structural element.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the swallowed-stone correspondence between Kronos's child-swallowing-and-stone-substitution and Kumarbi's ingestion of stones in Kingship in Heaven; Hoffner 1998, §14 (Kingship in Heaven), on the broken passages surrounding Kumarbi's ingestion of stones.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (swallowed-stone motif correspondence)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§14 (Kumarbi's stone-ingestion passage)

The Song of Ullikummi, Hoffner's §15 in the Kumarbi-Cycle anthology, supplies the cycle's principal post-cosmogonic combat narrative: Kumarbi, deposed and resentful, sires a stone monster (Ullikummi) on a rock at the edge of the sea, hides the infant on the shoulder of the primordial earth-bearing giant Upelluri, and waits for the stone to grow into a sky-touching threat to Teshub's kingship; Teshub eventually defeats the threat with the help of Ea, who severs Ullikummi from Upelluri's shoulder with the same primordial cutter that originally separated heaven from earth.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §15 (Song of Ullikummi), on the conception of Ullikummi on a rock, his hiding on Upelluri's shoulder, his growth toward heaven, and Ea's severing-cut as the resolution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§15 (Song of Ullikummi)

The Hesiodic Typhonomachy at *Theogony* 820–880 supplies the cycle's principal Greek-end correspondence: Zeus, having defeated the Titans, faces Typhoeus (Typhon), Gaia's last and youngest child, conceived in her later relationship with Tartarus, a hundred-headed serpent-limbed monster whose growth and threat to Zeus's sovereignty constitute the theogony's closing combat.footnote:[West 1966, on Theog. 820–880 (the Typhonomachy) and on Theog. 821–822 (Typhoeus's parentage from Gaia and Tartarus); Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Hesiodic Typhonomachy as the earliest Greek dragon-combat narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1966-theogony ; loc=Theog. 820-880 (Typhonomachy)

West's principal motif-specific correspondence between Ullikummi and Hesiodic Typhoeus is the lithic-and-cosmographic register of the monster: Ullikummi is a stone monster who grows toward heaven from a base on the primordial giant's shoulder, and the post-Hesiodic Typhon (especially in the Apollodoran and Pindaric traditions that locate him under Mount Etna and in Cilicia) is figured as a lithic / volcanic intrusion reaching from the lower realms toward the sky, with the two figures sharing the cosmographic pattern of an earth-rooted intrusion threatening sky-sovereignty.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the lithic / cosmographic register of the Ullikummi-Typhon parallel as one of the closest concrete and specific motif correspondences between the Kumarbi Cycle and the Greek Typhonomachy tradition; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the post-Hesiodic Typhon's lithic / volcanic locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (Ullikummi-Typhon lithic/cosmographic correspondence)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2 (post-Hesiodic Typhon's lithic/volcanic locus)

The Song of Hedammu (Hoffner §16) supplies a second Kumarbi-Cycle combat: Hedammu, a sea-monster of Kumarbi's siring, is seduced by the goddess Šauška / Ishtar in a stratagem that disables him before Teshub's combat; the Hedammu narrative is highly fragmentary and survives only in disconnected passages, and the chapter follows the upstream Ch5 framing in not silently filling its gaps from Ugaritic or Greek analogs.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, §16 (Song of Hedammu), on the Hedammu narrative's fragmentary state and on Šauška's seduction-stratagem; per brief 05-kumarbi-ullikummi.yaml lacunae on the disconnected-passages preservation of the Song of Hedammu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=§16 (Song of Hedammu)

The Hedammu material's principal Greek-end correspondence is the marine register of Apollodoran-and-Nonnian Typhon's morphology and the seduction-and-disabling stratagem in the Greek narrative (variously the Cadmus-pipe-playing in Nonnus and the Hermes-and-Aigipan recovery in Apollodorus), with West reading the seduction-and-stratagem motif as a Hurro-Hittite layer surviving in the Greek tradition's later expansions of the Hesiodic Typhonomachy.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Hedammu seduction-stratagem and the marine-register correspondences with Apollodoran-and-Nonnian Typhon; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on Apollodorus *Bibliotheca* 1.6.3 and Nonnus *Dionysiaca* 1–2 as the post-Hesiodic Typhon expansions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (Hedammu/Typhon seduction-stratagem correspondence)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ogden-2013 ; loc=ch. 2 (Apollodoran and Nonnian Typhon expansions)

A third Kumarbi-Cycle correspondence with the post-Hesiodic Greek tradition is the storm-god organ-theft motif: in the Hittite Elkunirsa-and-Ašertu fragment (a separate Hurro-Hittite text from the Kumarbi Cycle proper, but in the same cultural-and-linguistic milieu), the storm-god is dispossessed of his organs by an antagonist, and the organs are subsequently recovered by subordinate deities; this corresponds in motif-specific structural detail to the Apollodoran narrative of Typhon's theft of Zeus's sinews, the storage of the sinews in a bear-skin guarded by the dragoness Delphyne in the Corycian cave in Cilicia, and their recovery by Hermes and Aigipan.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Elkunirsa-Apollodoran sinew-theft correspondence; Hoffner 1998, on the Elkunirsa-and-Ašertu fragment in the Hittite corpus; Ogden 2013, ch. 2, on the Apollodoran sinew-theft and the Corycian-cave locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (Elkunirsa-Apollodoran sinew-theft correspondence)

=== II. The Levantine → Aegean route

The Levantine-into-Aegean route is the Iron-Age Phoenician-trading-network channel through which Levantine-coast combat-myth material — most relevantly the Ugaritic Baal-Yamm and Baal-Lôtan complex — reached the early Archaic Greek poetic tradition; the route runs in parallel with the Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek route of §I and is the central exhibit of Burkert 1992's *The Orientalizing Revolution*, whose argument is that the Phoenician traders, scribes, and craftsmen of the eighth and seventh centuries BCE supplied the principal channel through which Near Eastern theogonic and combat material reached early Greek poets.footnote:[Burkert 1992, *The Orientalizing Revolution: Near Eastern Influence on Greek Culture in the Early Archaic Age*, on Phoenician intermediaries as the principal channel for orientalizing-period transmission; West 1997, ch. 6, treating the Phoenician-mediation reading as complementary to the Hurro-Hittite route.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume (Phoenician intermediaries as principal channel)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=ch. 6 (Phoenician-mediation reading)

The Ugaritic Baal Cycle, the principal Late-Bronze-Age Levantine combat-myth corpus, survives on six tablets (KTU 1.1–1.6) recovered from the Late-Bronze-Age city of Ugarit (modern Ras Shamra) on the Syrian coast; the cycle's combat-narrative material is concentrated at KTU 1.1–1.2 (Baal vs. Yamm) and at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 (the Lôtan passage embedded as a boast by Mot), and the chapter follows the upstream Ch7 framing in citing this material via Smith 1994 (KTU 1.1–1.2) and Smith & Pitard 2009 (KTU 1.3–1.4 and the Lôtan passage at KTU 1.5).footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I, on the KTU 1.1–1.2 Baal-Yamm combat material; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, on the KTU 1.3–1.4 palace-building material and on the Lôtan passage at KTU 1.5 i 1–3.]

Baal's seat after his defeat of Yamm is Ṣapānu, the mountain identified in modern geography as Jebel al-Aqra on the Syrian-Turkish border, also known in the wider Near Eastern record as Mount Casius and (in Hebrew Bible reception) as Mount Zaphon; Ṣapānu is the principal geographical anchor of the Ugaritic Baal Cycle, the storm-god's locus and the cult-site whose name remains associated with the storm-god's presence in the wider Northwest Semitic religious vocabulary.footnote:[Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Ṣapānu as Baal's mountain; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II introduction on the geography of Ṣapānu (Jebel al-Aqra) and the wider Levantine geographical anchoring of the Cycle; per glossary.yaml on "Ṣapānu / Mount Zaphon" with the locked rendering.]

The Ṣapānu / Mount Aqra geographical anchoring of the Ugaritic Baal material is directly relevant to the Levantine-into-Aegean transmission argument: the mountain is located on the Syrian-Turkish coast, immediately adjacent to the Cilician region in which the Apollodoran Greek tradition locates Typhon's Corycian-cave episode (§I above), and the geographical proximity of the Ugaritic-Baal cult-site and the Greek-Typhon episode-site is one of the most concrete pieces of geographical evidence for Levantine-into-Aegean transmission of combat-myth material in the orientalizing period.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Ṣapānu / Mount Aqra anchorage of Ugaritic Baal material and its proximity to the Cilician anchorage of Greek Typhon; Burkert 1992, on the Levantine-coast contact zones and the orientalizing-period transmission corridor; Smith 1994, Vol. I introduction on Ṣapānu's geographical position.]

=== III. The Ugaritic → Israelite route

The Ugaritic-into-Israelite route is the chapter's second-most documented transmission case and is the central exhibit of Day 1985's *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*: the Yahweh-vs-sea / Yahweh-vs-Leviathan corpus of the Hebrew Bible — distributed across at least seven distinct passages in four canonical books — preserves, in adapted form, imagery directly continuous with the Ugaritic Baal Cycle, particularly the Baal-Yamm combat and the Lôtan / Leviathan combat, and constitutes the Israelite reflex of a Northwest Semitic combat-myth tradition.footnote:[Day 1985, *God's Conflict with the Dragon and the Sea*, chs. 1–5, on the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible continuity argument as the principal twentieth-century reading of the Yahweh-vs-Leviathan corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-5 (Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible continuity argument)

The Hebrew Bible Yahweh-vs-Leviathan corpus, on which Day's argument is built, comprises seven distinct passages: Psalm 74:12–17, Psalm 89:9–13, Psalm 104:6–9 with verse 26, Isaiah 27:1, Isaiah 51:9–10, Job 26:12–13 with chapter 41, and Habakkuk 3 — drawn from Psalms, Isaiah, Job, and Habakkuk in their canonical Hebrew Bible distribution (per the upstream Ch8 line 14 enumeration).footnote:[Day 1985, chs. 1–5, on the seven-passage corpus; per chapters/08-yahweh-leviathan-rahab.claims.approved.adoc line 14 on the same canonical enumeration of the seven-passage corpus including Isa 27:1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-5 (seven-passage corpus enumeration)

The Ugaritic-into-Israelite route's principal philological exhibit is the linguistic cognancy of Ugaritic Lôtan (unvocalised _ltn_ in the Ugaritic alphabetic-cuneiform script) with Hebrew Leviathan (_lwytn_ / לִוְיָתָן in the Masoretic vocalisation): the two names are phonologically cognate by regular Northwest Semitic sound-laws and refer to the same morphological figure (a multi-headed sea-serpent slain by the storm-god), and the cognancy is one of the cleanest pieces of linguistic evidence for direct Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible transmission of a named mythological figure in the Hebrew Bible corpus.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the Ugaritic Lôtan / Hebrew Leviathan linguistic cognancy as one of the strongest pieces of evidence for direct continuity between Ugaritic and Hebrew Bible combat imagery; Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, on the Ugaritic _ltn_ at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 and on the Northwest Semitic phonological correspondence with Hebrew _lwytn_; per glossary.yaml on "Lôtan" with the locked rendering and on "Leviathan" with the locked Hebrew Bible rendering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (Lôtan/Leviathan cognancy)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=smith-pitard-2009-baal-ii ; loc=KTU 1.5 i 1-3 (Ugaritic _ltn_)

The Ugaritic Lôtan passage at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 describes the figure as a seven-headed serpent (_šlyṭ d šbʿt rašm_, "the wriggling serpent with seven heads") whom Baal has slain, in a boast attributed to the death-god Mot; the seven-headed morphology is preserved in the Ugaritic-language attestation and corresponds directly to the multi-headed Leviathan of Psalm 74:14 and to the seven-headed dragon of Revelation 12:3 (treated in §IV).footnote:[Smith & Pitard 2009, Vol. II, on the seven-headed Lôtan at KTU 1.5 i 1–3 as embedded in Mot's boast; Day 1985, ch. 1, on the seven-headed morphology's correspondence with the multi-headed Leviathan of Psalm 74:14.]

=== IV. The Jewish-Hellenistic → Revelation 12 synthesis

The Jewish-Hellenistic-into-Revelation-12 route is structurally distinct from the §I, §II, and §III routes: rather than a single-substrate transmission from one tradition into another, the Revelation 12 combat is a deliberate composite, layered from at least three distinct sources — a Greco-Roman Leto-Apollo-Python pattern as the closest extant Mediterranean exemplar of the pregnant-goddess-pursued-by-dragon plot; an overlay drawn from the Hebrew Bible Yahweh-vs-Leviathan corpus (the seven-headed dragon, the eschatological combat, Isaiah 27:1's "fleeing serpent" diction); and a Second Temple satanological identification of the dragon with Satan and the Genesis 3 serpent.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, *The Combat Myth in the Book of Revelation*, chs. 2–4, on the Leto-Apollo-Python primary substrate, the Jewish-overlay material, and the Second Temple satanological identification; Aune 1997–98, *Revelation* (WBC 52B), commentary on Rev 12, on the eclectic-composite reading of the same chapter.]

The book of Revelation, conventionally dated to the closing years of the reign of the Roman emperor Domitian (81–96 CE) on the strength of patristic testimony from Irenaeus and internal evidence about the Asian-province persecution context, is a Jewish-Christian apocalypse composed in koine Greek for circulation in seven cities of the Roman province of Asia; the Domitianic dating is load-bearing for Yarbro Collins's reading of Rev 12 as encouragement to a persecuted early Christian community, with the dragon coding for Rome.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 1, on the Domitianic dating; Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 4, on the political reading of Rev 12; Aune 1997–98, *Revelation* (WBC 52A), introduction, on the dating evidence and the Asian-province context.]

The seventeen verses of Revelation 12 narrate a sequence of seven distinct episodes: the appearance of the woman clothed with the sun (vv. 1–2); the appearance of the great red dragon with seven heads, ten horns, and seven diadems (vv. 3–4); the birth and rapture of the male child (v. 5); the woman's flight to the wilderness (v. 6); the war in heaven and the dragon's expulsion by Michael and his angels (vv. 7–9); the heavenly victory hymn (vv. 10–12); and the dragon's earthly pursuit and redirection toward "the rest of her children" (vv. 13–17).footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 2, on the seven-episode structure of Rev 12; Aune 1997–98, *Revelation* (WBC 52B), commentary on Rev 12, on the structural articulation.]

Yarbro Collins's central argument in *The Combat Myth in the Book of Revelation* is that the Revelation 12 narrative reworks an older combat-myth pattern of which the closest extant Mediterranean exemplar is the Greek myth of Leto, Apollo, and Python — a pattern attested most fully in Hyginus's *Fabulae* 140 (drawing on earlier Hellenistic material) and analysed comparatively in Joseph Fontenrose's 1959 *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 2, stating the Leto-Apollo-Python primary-substrate thesis and citing Hyginus *Fabulae* 140 as the principal extant exemplar; Fontenrose 1959, *Python: A Study of Delphic Myth and Its Origins*, on the same plot as analysed comparatively.]

The Leto-Apollo-Python plot, as Yarbro Collins reconstructs it from Hyginus and parallel sources, runs as follows: Leto, pregnant by Zeus, is pursued by the serpent-dragon Python; she finds refuge on the floating island of Delos; she gives birth to Apollo (and Artemis); Apollo subsequently slays Python at Delphi — and the four structural correspondences with Revelation 12 are the pregnant goddess, the dragon adversary who pursues her, the divine child whose birth the dragon seeks to forestall, and the eventual defeat of the dragon by or in connection with the divine child.footnote:[Yarbro Collins 1976, ch. 2, on the four-correspondence Leto-Apollo-Python / Rev 12 structural parallel; Fontenrose 1959, on the Leto-Apollo-Python plot as the Greek combat-myth exemplar.]

=== V. Marduk as possible but undemonstrated source

The Marduk-into-Genesis-1 case is the chapter's weakest documented transmission case and is presented as such: the structural-and-linguistic parallels between the Babylonian *Enūma Eliš* (Marduk's defeat of Tiamat and his subsequent organisation of the cosmos from her body) and the Genesis 1 cosmogony are real and have been read since Hermann Gunkel's 1895 *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* as evidence for an Akkadian-into-Hebrew transmission, but the route is less decisively documented than the Hurro-Hittite-into-Greek (§I) or the Ugaritic-into-Israelite (§III) routes — there is no documented mediating channel comparable to the Phoenician-trading-network corridor for §I-§II or to the Northwest Semitic linguistic-and-cultural continuum for §III.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the post-Wellhausen and post-Gunkel debate over the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 transmission case as less decisively documented than the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible case; Gunkel 1895, Teil I, on the original Marduk-Genesis-1 transmission argument; per brief 39-C6-near-eastern-mediterranean-transmission.yaml structure section V on the less-decisively-documented framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (Marduk-Genesis-1 less decisively documented than Ugaritic case)

The Babylonian *Enūma Eliš* is a creation epic of seven tablets, named in the Akkadian convention from its opening words "When on high"; the standard critical edition is Lambert 2013 (Wilfred G. Lambert, *Babylonian Creation Myths*), which lists more than seventy manuscript witnesses drawn from Assyrian and Babylonian libraries, and the principal English translation used for the chapter's primary-text claims is Foster 2005 (*Before the Muses*).footnote:[Lambert 2013, *Babylonian Creation Myths*, introduction and pp. 3–15 manuscript catalogue, on the seventy-plus witnesses and the standard critical edition; Foster 2005, *Before the Muses*, pp. 436–486, on the standard English translation; per chapters/03-marduk-tiamat.claims.approved.adoc on the source-anchoring conventions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction and manuscript catalogue
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-486 (Enūma Eliš translation)

The *Enūma Eliš* combat-and-cosmogony structure is the chapter's principal Mesopotamian primary witness for the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 case: Marduk defeats the primordial salt-sea Tiamat (the cosmogonic mother whose name is etymologically related to the Akkadian noun _tiāmtu_ / _tâmtu_, "sea"), splits her body into upper and lower halves to form sky and earth, and proceeds to organise the cosmos in a sequence of cosmogonic acts that occupies the central tablets of the epic.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablets I and IV, on Marduk's defeat of Tiamat and the body-splitting cosmogony; Foster 2005, pp. 441–462, on the same.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets I and IV (Marduk-Tiamat combat and cosmogony)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-462 (combat and cosmogony)

The Genesis 1 cosmogony, by contrast, is a six-day creation narrative composed in Hebrew prose (with hymnic rhythmic features) in which Elohim creates the cosmos by speech-act rather than by combat, with the _tehôm_ ("the deep") of Genesis 1:2 representing the pre-creation watery state that is divided rather than personified — a structural difference from the *Enūma Eliš* combat-and-cosmogony pattern that is one of the principal pieces of evidence for the post-Wellhausen scepticism about the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 transmission case.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the structural difference between the Genesis 1 speech-act cosmogony and the *Enūma Eliš* combat-and-cosmogony; on the Genesis 1:2 _tehôm_ as pre-creation watery state without personification; per glossary.yaml on _tehôm_ with the locked italicised rendering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (structural difference Genesis 1 / Enūma Eliš)

Gunkel's 1895 argument turned principally on the etymological cognancy of Hebrew _tehôm_ ("the deep") with Akkadian Tiamat as a personal-divine name: both nouns derive from a common Northwest Semitic / East Semitic root denoting the sea, and Gunkel read the cognancy as evidence that Genesis 1:2's _tehôm_ preserves, in demythologised form, a memory of the Babylonian Tiamat-combat that the Genesis 1 cosmogony otherwise effaces.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, on the _tehôm_-Tiamat cognancy as the principal etymological exhibit for the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 transmission argument; per chapters/03-marduk-tiamat.claims.approved.adoc on Gunkel's 1895 argument.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I (tehôm-Tiamat cognancy)

The post-Wellhausen scepticism about Gunkel's reading rests on three principal observations: first, that _tehôm_ in Hebrew is a common noun ("the deep") rather than a personal-divine name, and that the cognancy with Akkadian Tiamat-as-name is at the level of root-etymology rather than at the level of named-figure transmission (the Hebrew _tehôm_ is never personified or treated as a divine adversary in the Hebrew Bible itself); second, that the Genesis 1 narrative is structurally a speech-act cosmogony rather than a combat-cosmogony, with no Yahweh-vs-Tiamat episode preserved or alluded to; and third, that there is no documented mediating route comparable to the Phoenician-trading-network corridor for §I or to the Ugaritic-Hebrew Northwest Semitic continuum for §III.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the three principal post-Wellhausen objections to Gunkel's 1895 _tehôm_-Tiamat reading; on the absence of a documented mediating route comparable to those of the §I and §III cases.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (post-Wellhausen objections)

// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (post-Wellhausen compositional reading)

The structural parallel between *Enūma Eliš* and Genesis 1 is real at the level of cosmogonic sequence: both narratives move from a primordial watery state through a divine creative act to the establishment of an ordered cosmos with sky, earth, and luminaries, and the Genesis 1 sequence (formless deep → divine spirit → light → firmament → land and sea → vegetation → luminaries → animals → humans → divine rest) and the *Enūma Eliš* sequence (Apsû / Tiamat → divine generations → Marduk's combat → sky-and-earth from Tiamat's body → luminaries → human creation from Qingu's blood) share a comparable seven-or-six-stage sequencing of cosmogonic events.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the structural cosmogonic-sequence parallel between *Enūma Eliš* and Genesis 1; Lambert 2013, on the *Enūma Eliš* cosmogonic sequence at Tablets IV–VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (cosmogonic-sequence parallel)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablets IV-VI (Enūma Eliš cosmogonic sequence)

The structural-cosmogonic parallel does not, however, demonstrate transmission: Day 1985 reads the parallel as evidence that Genesis 1 was composed in awareness of (and possibly in polemical response to) the *Enūma Eliš* cosmogonic pattern as a wider ancient-Near-Eastern type, but distinguishes this kind of awareness-and-response from the direct-transmission relationship that he reads in the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible Yahweh-vs-Leviathan case (§III) — the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 case is, in Day's framing, structurally allusive rather than philologically continuous.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the awareness-and-response framing for the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 case as structurally allusive rather than philologically continuous; Day 1985, ch. 1, on the contrast with the §III Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=ch. 1 (awareness-and-response framing; contrast with Ugaritic case)

The post-1985 comparative literature on the Marduk-into-Genesis-1 question is methodologically divided: some Hebrew-Bible scholars (in the Gunkel-tradition lineage) read the _tehôm_-Tiamat cognancy and the cosmogonic-sequence parallel as evidence for a real but indirect transmission, mediated through the Israelite contact with Mesopotamian culture during the Babylonian exile (sixth century BCE) when Israelite scribes had documented exposure to Akkadian literary materials; other scholars (in the Tsumura-and-post-Wellhausen lineage) read the parallels as the result of a shared ancient-Near-Eastern cosmogonic vocabulary rather than as direct borrowing.footnote:[Day 1985, ch. 1, on the post-Wellhausen and post-Gunkel methodological division; the chapter does not adjudicate between the readings but presents the two options side by side, in keeping with the upstream Ch3 framing on the Marduk-Tiamat material's reception history.]

=== VI. Implications for the book's argument

The five transmission routes developed in §I–§V together describe the Mediterranean cluster of combat-myth traditions as a chain of historical-contact relationships rather than as an array of independent attestations: the Hurro-Hittite Kumarbi Cycle, the Greek Theogony, the Ugaritic Baal Cycle, the Hebrew Bible Yahweh-vs-Leviathan corpus, and the Christian Revelation 12 are connected to one another by documented or strongly inferred routes of literary-and-cultural transmission, with the Mesopotamian *Enūma Eliš* an open question in the Genesis-1 reception but a probable upstream contributor to the Marduk-Tiamat structural pattern that pervades the cluster.footnote:[West 1997, ch. 6, on the Mediterranean cluster as a chain of contact-relationships; Burkert 1992, on the orientalizing-period transmission corridor; Day 1985, chs. 1–5, on the Ugaritic-into-Hebrew-Bible continuity; Yarbro Collins 1976, chs. 2–4, on Rev 12 as a synthesising confluence; per brief 39-C6-near-eastern-mediterranean-transmission.yaml structure section VI on the chain-rather-than-independent-attestations framing.]

// EVIDENCE: source_id=burkert-1992 ; loc=whole volume (orientalizing-period corridor)
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=chs. 1-5 (Ugaritic-Hebrew Bible continuity)

This chain-rather-than-independent-attestations finding has a load-bearing methodological consequence for the book's overall comparative argument: the Mediterranean cluster's similarities to one another are partly explained by the documented contact routes of §I–§IV and the contested route of §V, and the Mediterranean cluster's evidential weight for the book's typological claims is therefore reduced — not eliminated, since the structural-functional readings of C1, C2, and C3 remain independently true at the typological level, but reduced at the level of the cross-cultural-distribution argument that the book's overall typology depends on.footnote:[Per brief 39-C6-near-eastern-mediterranean-transmission.yaml structure section VI and special_instructions on the load-bearing methodological framing; per scope.md §"Book structure" Part II on the Part-II comparative chapters' division of labour and the bounded scope of the typological argument.]

The bounded-scope finding does not, however, undermine the book's broader cross-cultural argument: the *independent* attestations of the combat-myth typology — the cases that fall outside the Mediterranean transmission chain and that cannot plausibly be explained by historical contact with the Mediterranean cluster — supply the typology's strongest cross-cultural evidence, and these cases are treated in the other Part-II chapters at the level of the structural-functional typological readings rather than at the level of the historical-transmission question.footnote:[Per brief 39-C6-near-eastern-mediterranean-transmission.yaml structure section VI on the independent-attestations argument; per scope.md §"Book structure" Part II on the Part-II comparative chapters' coverage.]

The independent attestations carry the book's strongest cross-cultural typological weight precisely because they cannot be explained by transmission within the §I–§V chain: the Mexica and Andean cases are Pre-Columbian; the Chinese Gonggong material is documented in Han-period and earlier sources whose provenance is independent of the Mediterranean Iron Age; the Mwindo material is twentieth-century-recorded oral epic from a Bantu-language tradition with no documented pre-modern Mediterranean contact; and the Vedic case, while connected through the deep IE inheritance to the IE-distribution wing of the broader pattern, is independent of the historical-contact chain of the present chapter.footnote:[Per scope.md §"In-scope catalog" on the independent-attestation cases; per chapters/21-gonggong.adoc, chapters/22-hou-yi.adoc, chapters/24-huitzilopochtli-coatepec.adoc, chapters/25-mesoamerican-cluster.adoc, chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.adoc, and chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.adoc on the per-tradition independent provenance.]
