== Väinämöinen and Iku-Turso (Transformed Variant)

=== Source overview

This chapter treats the Finnic encounter in which the sea-monster Iku-Turso emerges from the sea and is subdued by the shaman-sage hero Väinämöinen through song and knowledge rather than through weapon-combat — a binding-rather-than-slaying narrative that the present chapter handles in a two-layer presentation, with the older rune-tradition material (laconic, fragmentary, recorded by 19th-century rune-collectors from Karelian and Finnish singers) presented alongside Elias Lönnrot's 1835 / 1849 *Kalevala* editorial synthesis (composite, expanded, integrated into the modern Finnish national epic) and the two layers kept explicitly distinct.footnote:[Per brief 31-vainamoinen-iku-turso.yaml summary on the sea-monster Iku-Turso emerging from the sea, on Väinämöinen subduing him by shamanic song rather than by sword, on the encounter as a binding rather than a slaying, and on the chapter's two-layer treatment separating the older rune material from Lönnrot's 1835 / 1849 *Kalevala* editorial synthesis; Siikala, A.-L., *Mythic Images and Shamanism: A Perspective on Kalevala Poetry*, Folklore Fellows' Communications 280 (Helsinki: Academia Scientiarum Fennica, 2002; ISBN 9789514109027), chs. on Kalevala combat and on Väinämöinen as shaman-sage hero, on the song-and-knowledge register of the Iku-Turso encounter, and on the older rune material's relation to Lönnrot's editorial synthesis.]

The shape of the evidence is the first thing to register. The Finnic material survives in oral-tradition form among the Finnish-, Karelian-, Ingrian-, and Estonian-speaking populations of the eastern Baltic and the adjacent Karelian-and-Ingrian forest-and-lake zone, with no pre-modern written attestation in any Finnic indigenous-script tradition (the Finnic languages had no pre-modern alphabetic writing system of their own); the textual witness situation for the present chapter is therefore the body of 19th-century rune-collection fieldwork conducted across Karelia and Finland by Elias Lönnrot, Daniel Europaeus, August Ahlqvist, Arvid Genetz, Julius and Kaarle Krohn, and other 19th- and early 20th-century rune-collectors, supplemented by the comparative Finnish-folklore-studies and Kalevala-poetry critical-scholarship of the later 20th and early 21st centuries.footnote:[Siikala 2002, introductory matter on the oral-tradition character of the Finnic combat-and-encounter material, on the absence of pre-modern Finnic-indigenous-script attestation, on the 19th-century rune-collection fieldwork conducted across Karelia and Finland by Lönnrot and his contemporaries (Daniel Europaeus, August Ahlqvist, Arvid Genetz, Julius and Kaarle Krohn, and others), and on the comparative Finnish-folklore-studies and Kalevala-poetry critical-scholarship of the later 20th and early 21st centuries as the chapter's textual-witness situation.]

Behind that anchor lies the 19th-century fieldwork situation that produced the recordings on which the chapter depends. Within that recorded corpus, the Iku-Turso material itself is unevenly distributed and minimally extended. The material specifically appears in the older rune-tradition record in laconic and fragmentary form: the sea-monster figure is named in incantation-and-charm material recorded across Viena Karelia, Aunus (Olonets) Karelia, Ingria, and northern Finland; the figure also appears within longer narrative-rune material recorded by Lönnrot and his successors; and the figure is invoked in incantation against sickness (Iku-Turso as a personification of disease-bearing power from the sea) as well as appearing in the narrative-encounter context that the present chapter takes as its centre — with the rune-tradition record being characteristically distributed across distinct genre-registers (incantation, narrative-rune, lyric) and across distinct regional traditions rather than concentrated in a single canonical text.footnote:[Siikala 2002, chs. on Kalevala combat and on the Iku-Turso material's appearance in the older rune-tradition record, on the laconic and fragmentary character of the older attestation, on the figure's appearance in incantation-and-charm material recorded across Viena Karelia, Aunus (Olonets) Karelia, Ingria, and northern Finland, on the figure's appearance within longer narrative-rune material recorded by Lönnrot and his successors, on the figure's invocation in incantation against sickness as a personification of disease-bearing power from the sea, and on the rune-tradition record's characteristic distribution across distinct genre-registers (incantation, narrative-rune, lyric) and across distinct regional traditions rather than concentrated in a single canonical text.]

The other layer of textual witness is editorial and was produced by Lönnrot himself. Elias Lönnrot's *Kalevala* — published first in the 1835 *Vanha Kalevala* ("Old Kalevala", 32 cantos) and then in the much-expanded 1849 *Uusi Kalevala* ("New Kalevala", 50 cantos) which became the standard text — is a composite literary-editorial synthesis built by Lönnrot from the rune-tradition material he and his contemporaries had collected, organised into a continuous narrative epic on a model partly suggested by the Homeric epics and partly by the project of producing a national epic for an emerging Finnish national culture under Russian-imperial sovereignty (Finland was a Grand Duchy of the Russian Empire from 1809 to 1917); the Iku-Turso material in the *Kalevala* is reshaped by Lönnrot to serve the *Kalevala*'s overall narrative architecture rather than presented as a transcription of any single rune-tradition performance.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on Lönnrot's *Kalevala* as a composite literary-editorial synthesis built from the rune-tradition material Lönnrot and his contemporaries had collected, on the 1835 *Vanha Kalevala* (32 cantos) and the much-expanded 1849 *Uusi Kalevala* (50 cantos) as the principal editions, on the Homeric-and-national-epic model on which Lönnrot organised the synthesis, on the Finnish-national-cultural context within Russian-imperial sovereignty, and on the *Kalevala*'s Iku-Turso material as reshaped by Lönnrot to serve the *Kalevala*'s overall narrative architecture rather than presented as a transcription of any single rune-tradition performance; on Lönnrot's 19th-century compositional method as the subject of subsequent Finnish-folklore-studies scholarship including the work of Lauri Honko and Thomas A. Three further methodological features of the textual situation are worth registering before the narrative begins. === The Finnic context: Karelia, the rune-singing tradition, and the Kalevala-meter

The Finnic-language area within which the rune-tradition material was collected comprises Finland (the present-day Republic of Finland and the historical Finnish-speaking areas), Karelia (a contested region historically split between Finnish and Russian sovereignty, with the eastern Karelian areas — Viena Karelia in the north, Aunus / Olonets Karelia further south — having been the principal preserves of older rune-tradition material at the time of 19th-century collection), Ingria (the Finnic-speaking region around present-day St. Petersburg), and the wider Baltic-Finnic-speaking area extending to Estonia and parts of Russia north of St. Petersburg, with the rune-tradition itself being shared across this Baltic-Finnic linguistic-and-cultural area rather than confined to any single modern political unit.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the Finnic-language area within which the rune-tradition material was collected — Finland, Karelia (with the eastern Karelian areas, Viena Karelia in the north and Aunus / Olonets Karelia further south, as principal preserves of older rune-tradition material), Ingria around St. Petersburg, and the wider Baltic-Finnic-speaking area extending to Estonia and parts of Russia north of St. Petersburg; on the rune-tradition's distribution across the Baltic-Finnic linguistic-and-cultural area rather than confined to any single modern political unit.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

The medium in which all of that material is cast is a single inherited verse form. The rune-tradition's metrical form is the *Kalevala*-meter (Finnish *Kalevalamitta*), a trochaic-tetrameter line of eight syllables organised into four trochaic feet, with the line's structure governed by alliteration within the line and by parallelism (each line typically followed by a parallel line restating the same content with variation in lexical choice and image), with the *Kalevala*-meter being the shared metrical inheritance of the Baltic-Finnic poetic tradition (attested across Finnish, Karelian, Ingrian, and Estonian material) and being the metrical basis on which Lönnrot composed the *Kalevala*'s synthesis lines as well as the metrical form of the older rune-tradition material from which Lönnrot drew.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the rune-tradition's metrical form — the *Kalevala*-meter (Finnish *Kalevalamitta*), a trochaic-tetrameter line of eight syllables organised into four trochaic feet, with the line's structure governed by alliteration within the line and by parallelism (each line typically followed by a parallel line restating the same content with variation in lexical choice and image); on the *Kalevala*-meter as the shared metrical inheritance of the Baltic-Finnic poetic tradition attested across Finnish, Karelian, Ingrian, and Estonian material; and on the *Kalevala*-meter as both the metrical basis on which Lönnrot composed the *Kalevala*'s synthesis lines and the metrical form of the older rune-tradition material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

That metrical form lived in performance, not on the page. The rune-singing performance tradition within which the older Iku-Turso material was performed before its 19th-century recording was a tradition of named singers — *runonlaulajat* in Finnish — who maintained extensive repertoires of narrative-rune, lyric, incantation, and charm material, often singing in pairs (a lead singer and a second who joined on the parallel-line restatement, sometimes seated face to face holding hands and rocking in time to the song), with the performance contexts including domestic and communal gatherings, calendrical occasions, and the working contexts of rune-incantation-as-practical-action (the singing of charms over wounds, sicknesses, and craft-and-hunting tasks).footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the rune-singing performance tradition within which the older Iku-Turso material was performed before its 19th-century recording — the tradition of named singers (*runonlaulajat* in Finnish) who maintained extensive repertoires of narrative-rune, lyric, incantation, and charm material; on the singers' practice of singing in pairs (a lead singer and a second who joined on the parallel-line restatement, sometimes seated face to face holding hands and rocking in time to the song); on the performance contexts including domestic and communal gatherings, calendrical occasions, and the working contexts of rune-incantation-as-practical-action (the singing of charms over wounds, sicknesses, and craft-and-hunting tasks).]

The rune-tradition's principal cast of figures is small and recurring across the recorded material. The principal protagonists of the wider Finnic rune-tradition material — and of Lönnrot's *Kalevala* synthesis — include Väinämöinen, the shaman-sage hero whose song-and-knowledge is the rune-tradition's principal mode of effective action (he sings ships into being, sings opponents into bogs, sings the Sampo into existence in collaboration with Ilmarinen, and in the Iku-Turso material sings the sea-monster into subjugation); Ilmarinen, the smith-hero who forges the Sampo and other marvellous objects; Lemminkäinen, the reckless young hero whose adventures form a distinct narrative arc; and Louhi, the mistress of Pohjola (the northern realm against which much of the *Kalevala*'s action is directed) — with the principal antagonist-figures including the sea-monster Iku-Turso of the present chapter and the bird-form chthonic adversary appearing in further rune material.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the principal protagonists of the wider Finnic rune-tradition material — Väinämöinen as shaman-sage hero whose song-and-knowledge is the rune-tradition's principal mode of effective action (singing ships into being, singing opponents into bogs, singing the Sampo into existence in collaboration with Ilmarinen, singing the sea-monster Iku-Turso into subjugation); Ilmarinen as smith-hero forging the Sampo and other marvellous objects; Lemminkäinen as reckless young hero with a distinct narrative arc; Louhi as mistress of Pohjola (the northern realm against which much of the *Kalevala*'s action is directed); and the principal antagonist-figures including the sea-monster Iku-Turso of the present chapter; per glossary 'Väinämöinen' first_mention_gloss on the Finnish shaman-sage hero who subdues Iku-Turso by song.]

=== Väinämöinen as shaman-sage hero

Of those figures, the one the chapter turns on is Väinämöinen, and his characterisation in the rune-tradition is unlike the warrior-hero of the surrounding Indo-European literatures. Väinämöinen is presented in the rune-tradition material as a shaman-sage hero of immense age and primordial origin: he is born already old (the rune-tradition material develops his birth from the primordial waters in distinct ways, with versions in which he is the son of the air-maiden Ilmatar and is born after a long gestation, and with the *Kalevala* synthesis collecting and elaborating these origin-versions), and he is consistently characterised as wise, experienced, and possessed of the deep song-and-knowledge that distinguishes the shaman-sage from the warrior-hero of Indo-European combat-tradition.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on Väinämöinen as shaman-sage hero of immense age and primordial origin in the rune-tradition material — born already old, with the rune-tradition material developing his birth from the primordial waters in distinct ways (versions in which he is the son of the air-maiden Ilmatar and is born after a long gestation), with the *Kalevala* synthesis collecting and elaborating these origin-versions; on Väinämöinen's consistent characterisation as wise, experienced, and possessed of the deep song-and-knowledge that distinguishes the shaman-sage from the warrior-hero of Indo-European combat-tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

His mode of agency follows from that characterisation. Väinämöinen's principal mode of effective action across the rune-tradition material is song — *runot* in the broad rune-tradition sense — which he uses both to do practical things (singing a ship out of his knee, singing opponents into bogs by overpowering them in singing-contests, singing the trees into the landscape, singing music from instruments and from his own throat) and to bind, subjugate, name, and know the powers of the world; his song is not a magical incantation in any peripheral or decorative sense but the central mode of his agency, with his identity as shaman-sage and his identity as singer being the same identity in two registers.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on Väinämöinen's principal mode of effective action as song — *runot* in the broad rune-tradition sense — both to do practical things (singing a ship out of his knee, singing opponents into bogs by overpowering them in singing-contests, singing the trees into the landscape, singing music from instruments and from his own throat) and to bind, subjugate, name, and know the powers of the world; on the song's status as the central mode of agency rather than a peripheral or decorative magical incantation; on Väinämöinen's identity as shaman-sage and his identity as singer being the same identity in two registers.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

The rune-tradition's broader register places that mode in a wider northern context. The Finnic rune-tradition's shamanic background is one of the load-bearing elements of Siikala's 2002 reading: the rune-tradition material is connected to a wider northern-Eurasian shamanic complex — extending across the Baltic-Finnic, Sami, Karelian, and adjacent Finno-Ugric and Siberian-shamanic traditions — within which song, drumming, ecstatic states, and the manipulation of names and origin-knowledge are the shaman's principal tools, and within which Väinämöinen's song-and-knowledge mode of effective action is the rune-tradition's elaboration of the shamanic-effective-action register that the Sami noaidi tradition and the wider northern-Eurasian shamanic traditions also exhibit.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the Finnic rune-tradition's shamanic background as a load-bearing element of the chapter's reading; on the rune-tradition material's connection to a wider northern-Eurasian shamanic complex extending across the Baltic-Finnic, Sami, Karelian, and adjacent Finno-Ugric and Siberian-shamanic traditions; on song, drumming, ecstatic states, and the manipulation of names and origin-knowledge as the shaman's principal tools; on Väinämöinen's song-and-knowledge mode of effective action as the rune-tradition's elaboration of the shamanic-effective-action register that the Sami *noaidi* tradition and the wider northern-Eurasian shamanic traditions also exhibit.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

Within that shamanic register, one specific technique does the work of the encounter. Väinämöinen's distinctive use of *origin-incantations* — songs that recite the origin of a being, substance, or affliction in order to acquire mastery over it — is one of the rune-tradition's most characteristic features and is the load-bearing technique by which Väinämöinen subjugates Iku-Turso in the older rune material: the principle is that to know the origin of a power is to be able to bind it, with the singer's recitation of the origin being the act of binding rather than a preliminary to a separate combat-act, and with the encounter being therefore a knowledge-encounter in which the song's content (the origin of the sea-monster) does the work that a weapon would do in a weapon-combat tradition.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on Väinämöinen's distinctive use of *origin-incantations* — songs that recite the origin of a being, substance, or affliction in order to acquire mastery over it — as one of the rune-tradition's most characteristic features and as the load-bearing technique by which Väinämöinen subjugates Iku-Turso in the older rune material; on the principle that to know the origin of a power is to be able to bind it; on the singer's recitation of the origin being the act of binding rather than a preliminary to a separate combat-act; on the encounter being therefore a knowledge-encounter in which the song's content (the origin of the sea-monster) does the work that a weapon would do in a weapon-combat tradition.]

=== Iku-Turso in the older rune material

With the hero established, the chapter turns to the adversary, and to the older rune-tradition material in which the adversary appears. Iku-Turso (the name being a compound of *iku-* "eternal, perpetual" and *Turso*, the proper name of the sea-monster figure) is in the older rune-tradition material a sea-monster figure who emerges from the sea and against whom the rune-tradition develops both narrative-encounter material (Iku-Turso as adversary subjugated by Väinämöinen's song) and incantation-against-sickness material (Iku-Turso as a personification of disease-bearing power from the sea, against whom incantation is sung to drive disease back into the depths whence it came).footnote:[Siikala 2002, chs. on Kalevala combat and on Iku-Turso, on the name as a compound of *iku-* "eternal, perpetual" and *Turso* as the proper name of the sea-monster figure, on Iku-Turso in the older rune-tradition material as a sea-monster figure who emerges from the sea, on the rune-tradition's development of both narrative-encounter material (Iku-Turso as adversary subjugated by Väinämöinen's song) and incantation-against-sickness material (Iku-Turso as personification of disease-bearing power from the sea against whom incantation is sung to drive disease back into the depths whence it came); per glossary 'Iku-Turso' first_mention_gloss on the Finnic sea-monster subdued by Väinämöinen's song.]

=== Lönnrot's Kalevala: the 1835 Vanha and the 1849 Uusi editions

The chapter now turns to the second of its two layers — the 19th-century editorial synthesis through which most readers, Finnish or not, encounter the material. Elias Lönnrot (1802-1884) was a Finnish physician, philologist, and folklore-collector who conducted eleven major rune-collection tours across Karelia, Ingria, and northern Finland between 1828 and 1844, recording rune-tradition material from named singers and assembling the recorded material into the *Kalevala* synthesis under the institutional support of the Suomalaisen Kirjallisuuden Seura (Finnish Literature Society, founded in 1831 in Helsinki) and within the wider Finnish-national-romantic project of producing a national epic for an emerging Finnish national culture under Russian-imperial sovereignty.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on Elias Lönnrot (1802-1884) as a Finnish physician, philologist, and folklore-collector; on Lönnrot's eleven major rune-collection tours across Karelia, Ingria, and northern Finland between 1828 and 1844; on Lönnrot's recording of rune-tradition material from named singers; on Lönnrot's assembly of the recorded material into the *Kalevala* synthesis under the institutional support of the Suomalaisen Kirjallisuuden Seura (Finnish Literature Society, founded in 1831 in Helsinki); on the Finnish-national-romantic project of producing a national epic for an emerging Finnish national culture under Russian-imperial sovereignty (Finland was a Grand Duchy of the Russian Empire from 1809 to 1917).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

The synthesis appeared in two distinct editions, and the difference between them matters. The 1835 *Vanha Kalevala* ("Old Kalevala") is the first published edition of Lönnrot's synthesis, comprising 32 cantos (*runot* in the editorial sense — Lönnrot uses the same word for the rune-tradition's individual songs and for the *Kalevala*'s editorial cantos, with consequent terminological complications for subsequent scholarship) and approximately 12,000 lines of *Kalevala*-meter trochaic-tetrameter, published in Helsinki by the Finnish Literature Society and circulated initially within the Finnish-language and Swedish-language educated readership of the Grand Duchy.footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the 1835 *Vanha Kalevala* ("Old Kalevala") as the first published edition of Lönnrot's synthesis — comprising 32 cantos (*runot* in the editorial sense, with Lönnrot using the same word for the rune-tradition's individual songs and for the *Kalevala*'s editorial cantos) and approximately 12,000 lines of *Kalevala*-meter trochaic-tetrameter; on the 1835 publication in Helsinki by the Finnish Literature Society; on the initial circulation within the Finnish-language and Swedish-language educated readership of the Grand Duchy of Finland.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

Fourteen years later Lönnrot replaced that edition with a much larger one, and it is the larger one that has carried the cultural weight ever since. The 1849 *Uusi Kalevala* ("New Kalevala") is the much-expanded second edition, comprising 50 cantos and approximately 22,800 lines of *Kalevala*-meter trochaic-tetrameter; it was assembled by Lönnrot from further rune-collection material gathered after the 1835 edition (including from his later tours of Viena Karelia, where some of the longest-and-richest narrative-rune material was recorded), and it is the *Uusi Kalevala* of 1849 — not the earlier *Vanha Kalevala* of 1835 — that became the standard text of the Finnish national epic, the basis for subsequent editions, translations, and adaptations, and the version that 20th-century-and-later readers encounter when they encounter "the *Kalevala*".footnote:[Siikala 2002, on the 1849 *Uusi Kalevala* ("New Kalevala") as the much-expanded second edition — comprising 50 cantos and approximately 22,800 lines of *Kalevala*-meter trochaic-tetrameter; on the assembly by Lönnrot from further rune-collection material gathered after the 1835 edition (including from his later tours of Viena Karelia, where some of the longest-and-richest narrative-rune material was recorded); on the 1849 *Uusi Kalevala* — not the earlier 1835 *Vanha Kalevala* — as the standard text of the Finnish national epic, the basis for subsequent editions, translations, and adaptations, and the version that 20th-century-and-later readers encounter when they encounter "the *Kalevala*".]

=== The two layers held apart: methodological discipline

That asymmetry between the *Kalevala*'s reception and the older material's footing is exactly what the chapter's two-layer presentation discipline is meant to keep visible. The chapter's two-layer presentation — older rune-tradition material on the one hand, Lönnrot's 1835 / 1849 *Kalevala* editorial synthesis on the other — is the chapter's principal methodological discipline per brief special_instructions, and its purpose is to keep clear at the narrative-presentation level that the *Kalevala* is a 19th-century editorial synthesis rather than a transparent record of pre-Lönnrot Finnic tradition; the chapter accordingly presents the older rune-tradition material first (laconic, fragmentary, distributed across rune-collection records and across genre-registers), and then presents the Lönnrot *Kalevala* material as a 19th-century compositional layer that reshapes the older material for the modern national epic.footnote:[Per brief 31-vainamoinen-iku-turso.yaml special_instructions on the chapter's two-layer presentation as the chapter's principal methodological discipline — explicitly separating the older rune attestations from Lönnrot's 1835 / 1849 editorial synthesis, presenting the older material first (laconic, fragmentary) and then showing how Lönnrot reshaped it into the *Kalevala* Iku-Turso episode, with the two-layer framing identified as the chapter's contribution; Siikala 2002 on the methodological discipline of holding the two layers apart at the narrative-presentation level and on the *Kalevala* as a 19th-century editorial synthesis rather than a transparent record of pre-Lönnrot Finnic tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=siikala-finnish ; loc=Mythic Images and Shamanism, chs. on Kalevala combat

The Lönnrot question is not unique. The methodological parallels with the Macpherson-Ossian question and with the Ivanov-Toporov Perun-Veles "basic myth" reconstruction (chapter 20 of the present volume) are explicit: Macpherson's 1760s "Ossian" material was presented as a translation of an ancient Gaelic epic but was substantially Macpherson's own 18th-century composition incorporating older Gaelic material to varying degrees; the Ivanov-Toporov Perun-Veles reconstruction is a 1965-74 reconstruction of a putative Slavic combat-myth from folklore-and-toponymic material whose status as reconstruction-of-the-pre-existing rather than scholarly-projection-onto-the-fragmentary remains contested; Lönnrot's *Kalevala* sits alongside both as a case in which 19th-century editorial-and-compositional work has substantially shaped the appearance of an "ancient" tradition that subsequent readers and scholars encounter, with the methodological question — how much of "the tradition" is 19th-century editorial composition? — being one that the present chapter raises and that the methods sidebar (ch. 33) and Part II C8 (ch. 41) take up at greater length.footnote:[Per brief 31-vainamoinen-iku-turso.yaml cultural_relevance.cross_cultural_parallels on Lönnrot's editorial synthesis raising methodological parallels with the Macpherson-Ossian question and the Ivanov-Toporov Perun-Veles reconstruction (chapter 20 of the present volume); per glossary 'basic myth (Ivanov-Toporov)' on the Ivanov-Toporov reconstruction's contested status as a 1965-74 reconstruction of a shared Slavic storm-god-vs-serpent narrative from folklore and toponyms now criticised for methodological circularity; per scope.md §"Book structure" on Part II C8 (refusal and appropriation problem) and the methods sidebar (ch. 33) as the loci for the methodological question about 19th-century editorial composition; Siikala 2002 on the *Kalevala* as a 19th-century editorial synthesis distinct from the older rune-tradition material.]

=== Typology, area-specialist framing, and forward-links

With the two layers presented and the methodological discipline registered, the chapter can place the encounter within the present book's wider category — and can be precise about what it is and is not claiming. The Väinämöinen / Iku-Turso encounter's place within the present book's chaoskampf typology is — per brief special_instructions, which explicitly states that the chapter does NOT claim Iku-Turso fits chaoskampf cleanly — a related-tier (rather than core-tier) *transformed variant* case in which structural features of the chaoskampf typology (named hero, named adversary, encounter at the boundary of human and non-human worlds, decisive subjugation of the adversary by the hero) are present but the typology's load-bearing weapon-combat-and-slaying register is replaced with a song-and-knowledge-and-binding register drawn from the wider northern-Eurasian shamanic complex, with the encounter's principal narrative work being shamanic-ritual subjugation rather than weapon-combat slaying.footnote:[Per brief 31-vainamoinen-iku-turso.yaml special_instructions on the chapter's explicit non-claim that Iku-Turso fits chaoskampf cleanly and on the *transformed variant* framing being explicit; per scope.md §"Inclusion / exclusion criteria" on the related-tier criteria where the cosmogonic-combat work is weaker or transformed; per scope.md §"Book structure" cluster 13 (Finnic) on the chapter's transformed-variant placement; Siikala 2002 on the encounter's load-bearing structural feature as the absence of weapon-combat and on the song-and-knowledge mode of subjugation displacing the weapon-combat mode.]
