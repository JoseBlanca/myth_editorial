== Marduk vs. Tiamat: Enūma Eliš — Claims

=== Source overview

Enūma Eliš is a Babylonian creation epic of seven tablets, named in the Akkadian convention from its opening words "When on high."footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction; Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The text is the canonical case of what Hermann Gunkel in 1895 named Chaoskampf, the combat-with-chaos pattern that organises one strand of ancient Near Eastern cosmology.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, with Marduk vs. Tiamat as the central case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The critical edition used throughout this chapter is W.G. Lambert's *Babylonian Creation Myths* (Mesopotamian Civilizations 16, 2013), which provides the cuneiform transliteration, translation, manuscript score, and commentary.footnote:[Lambert 2013, whole volume.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=whole volume

Lambert's edition replaces Philippe Talon's 2005 edition and the older King 1902 edition as the standard critical text of Enūma Eliš.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The working English translation cited in this chapter is Benjamin Foster's "Epic of Creation," in *Before the Muses*, 3rd edition (2005), pp. 436–486.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 436–486, "Epic of Creation."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-486

Foster's translation is more narratively fluid than Lambert's conservative rendering, and is therefore preferred here for body prose; Lambert is preferred for textual and philological matters.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote on translation principles; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 436

Lambert's 2013 manuscript catalogue lists more than seventy witnesses to Enūma Eliš, drawn from Assyrian and Babylonian libraries.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The bulk of the manuscript witnesses come from Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian copies of the first millennium BCE.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on witness distribution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

Significant Neo-Assyrian copies were recovered from the libraries of Ashurbanipal at Nineveh and from the temple libraries at Aššur, Kalhu, and Sultantepe.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the Neo-Assyrian witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

Neo-Babylonian witnesses come from Babylon, Borsippa, and other centres, and continue into the late Achaemenid and Seleucid periods.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the Neo-Babylonian and later witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The composition's date of authorship is debated, with the prevailing view placing it in the late second millennium BCE under Nebuchadnezzar I (r. ca. 1125–1104 BCE), though earlier and later dates have been argued.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the dating debate; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis ties the composition to the recovery of the cult-statue of Marduk from Elam and to a programme of Babylonian theological assertion.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the Nebuchadnezzar I hypothesis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The text was recited liturgically in the Babylonian akītu New Year festival, on the fourth day, by the high priest before the cult-image of Marduk in Esagil.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu recitation; Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu ritual recitation is attested in late ritual texts and is the principal performative context of Enūma Eliš in the surviving record.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the ritual texts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

Bruce Lincoln treats Enūma Eliš as a paradigm case of myth as political-ideological work, with the cosmogony serving the legitimation of Babylonian kingship.footnote:[Lincoln 2012, essays on combat-myth as political ideology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

Debra Scoggins Ballentine's 2015 *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition* argues that "chaos" is the wrong analytical category for Tiamat: the adversary is better read as an alternative-sovereign claimant rather than as the embodiment of disorder.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1 on Tiamat as alternative-sovereign rather than chaos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

Ballentine's reading shifts the centre of gravity from cosmology to politics: the question is who reigns, not order versus disorder.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

Scurlock and Beal's 2013 collected volume reassesses Gunkel's Chaoskampf hypothesis across multiple traditions, with several essays treating Enūma Eliš as the type-specimen.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim

Neil Forsyth places Enūma Eliš within a long combat-myth sequence running from Sumerian Ninurta material through the Hebrew Bible into Christian apocalyptic.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on Enūma Eliš within the combat-myth sequence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Amar Annus argues that Enūma Eliš is a deliberate literary descendant of the Standard Babylonian Anzû Epic, reusing and outbidding the Ninurta-vs-Anzû combat in a Marduk-centric cosmogony.footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction on the Anzû-Marduk genealogy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

=== Setting and context

Marduk is the patron god of Babylon, originally a city-god of relatively limited regional importance who rises in the second millennium to become the chief god of the Babylonian pantheon.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on Marduk's rise; Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

Marduk's elevation in Enūma Eliš is the narrative complement of the historical elevation of Babylon as imperial capital: the text and the political claim are co-constituted.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

In the Mesopotamian theogonic background, Marduk's father is Ea, god of wisdom and the deep fresh waters of the Apsû.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, pp. 439–440.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Ea in Akkadian context corresponds to Sumerian Enki, but Enūma Eliš is an Akkadian composition and uses the Akkadian theonym throughout.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, p. 439.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Marduk's mother is Damkina, Ea's consort, and his birth at the centre of the Apsû is signalled by elaborate praise-formulae.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Tiamat is the primordial salt-sea, the cosmogonic mother who, with Apsû the fresh-water, generates the first gods.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, pp. 436–438.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

The Akkadian noun *tiāmtu* / *tâmtu* (sea) is etymologically related to Tiamat as a personal-divine name; Gunkel's 1895 argument turns on the cognancy with Hebrew *tehôm*, the deep of Genesis 1:2.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I on the *tehôm*-Tiamat cognancy; Day 1985 for the later debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

Apsû is the personified primordial fresh-water, Tiamat's consort and male counterpart in the opening generation.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, pp. 436–437.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

The Akkadian noun *apsû* names the cosmic fresh-water region beneath the earth; the same word in Sumerian context is Abzu, but the Akkadian form with circumflex is the one used in Enūma Eliš.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

The Anzû Epic is the most direct Mesopotamian antecedent of Enūma Eliš, and the scholarship treats Marduk's combat as a deliberate rewriting of Ninurta's combat against Anzû (cross-ref: Ch. 2).footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction on the Anzû-to-Enūma-Eliš genealogy; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

The Tablet of Destinies, the cosmic decree-token familiar from the Anzû Epic, returns in Enūma Eliš as the object Tiamat bestows on Qingu and that Marduk reclaims after the combat.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Foster 2005, p. 459.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet IV

The akītu festival, at which Enūma Eliš was recited, was the Babylonian New Year ritual celebrated in the spring month of Nisan, and centred on the cult of Marduk in his Babylon temple Esagil.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu New Year festival.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

Esagil is Marduk's temple in Babylon, named in the epic as founded after the combat; in Babylonian theology it is the cosmic axis where heaven and earth meet.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet VI; Foster 2005, pp. 472–473.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet VI

=== Movement I — Theogony and the killing of Apsû (Tablets I–II)

==== Tablet I — primordial waters, the first gods, the killing of Apsû

Tablet I opens with the celebrated incipit "When on high" (*enūma eliš*), establishing a primordial setting before any gods or world have been distinguished.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 436, Tablet I opening; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 436

The opening describes the mingling of two primordial waters: Apsû the fresh and Tiamat the salt, undifferentiated into gods, lands, or names.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 436–437, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-437

The first generation of gods is born from this mingling: Lahmu and Lahamu, identified as a paired male-female deity-couple at the dawn of differentiation.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 437, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 437

The second generation is Anšar and Kišar, sky-horizon and earth-horizon, who in turn beget Anu, the sky-god.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 437, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 437

Anu begets Nudimmud, the alternative name for Ea in this composition, characterised by surpassing wisdom.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 437–438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 437-438

The theogonic sequence — Apsû / Tiamat → Lahmu / Lahamu → Anšar / Kišar → Anu → Ea — establishes a four-tier descent from the primordial waters to the wisdom-god who will become Marduk's father.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 436–438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 436-438

The younger gods, born within Apsû and Tiamat, are noisy and disturb the primordial repose of their parents.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 438

The text gives Apsû's perspective as exasperation: the noise of the younger gods makes rest impossible, and Apsû resolves to destroy them so that quiet may return.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 438-439

Apsû takes counsel with his vizier Mummu, and Mummu encourages the plan: the younger gods are to be annihilated.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 438, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 438

Apsû then approaches Tiamat and proposes the destruction of their offspring, but Tiamat refuses: she will not destroy what she has herself brought forth.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 438-439

Tiamat's refusal is the first divergence between Apsû and Tiamat in the text and is later cited as evidence that her motive in the second movement is grief and obligation rather than primordial malice.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The younger gods learn of Apsû's plan and are paralysed with fear: they sit silent.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Ea, the wise one, alone among the younger gods devises a counter-strategy: he composes a powerful incantation, recites it over Apsû, and casts him into a sleep.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

With Apsû asleep, Ea binds him, strips him of his crown and divine radiance, and kills him.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Ea also captures Mummu, Apsû's vizier, binds him, and takes his nose-rope as a leash.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Ea then establishes his dwelling on top of the slain Apsû's corpse, founding what becomes the cosmic Apsû — the abode of the wisdom-god above the primordial fresh-water.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 439–440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 439-440

The killing of Apsû is the first combat in the epic and establishes the pattern: the threatened younger god uses incantation and binding to subdue and dismember a primordial parent.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 439, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 439

Within Ea's new dwelling, in the Apsû, Marduk is born to Ea and Damkina; the text describes his birth in terms of unprecedented brilliance and divine perfection.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Marduk is described as having four eyes and four ears, of surpassing physical proportions, and as radiant from birth.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Anu, Marduk's grandfather, fashions the four winds as a gift, and gives them to Marduk to play with; the winds will later prove decisive in the combat.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 440, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 440

Marduk's playing with the winds disturbs Tiamat in her primordial waters, and the disturbance compounds the grievance of the older gods who have been agitating Tiamat to act.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 440–441, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 440-441

A faction of gods aligned with the slain Apsû reproaches Tiamat for her passivity: she did not stand up for Apsû when he was killed, and now her offspring's noise compounds the insult.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 441, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 441

The reproach faction's demand is explicit: Tiamat must avenge Apsû.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 441, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 441

Tiamat is moved to action by the reproach: she resolves to make war on the younger gods and to set the cosmos in order through the destruction of those she now reframes as rebels.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442

[INFERENCE: Tiamat's reframing of the younger gods as rebels rather than her offspring is read in the scholarship as a transformation of obligation into adversariality | basis: Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, and Ballentine 2015, ch. 1, both note that Tiamat's anger is provoked, not primordial | risk: the surviving text does not explicitly mark this as a reframing; this reading is interpretive.]

Tiamat brings forth a host of monstrous creatures to fight on her side: serpents with venom for blood, Mušhuššu-dragons, lahmu-monsters, lion-demons, scorpion-men, fish-men, bull-men, mighty winds, and other composite beasts.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442

The text enumerates eleven monstrous champions, a catalogue that becomes a recurring formula in the epic, repeated four times across Tablets I–IV.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I, on the eleven-monsters formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442

Anzû, the lion-headed eagle of the earlier Anzû Epic, appears in the catalogue of Tiamat's monsters: he has been demoted from rebel-protagonist to subordinate creature.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I, on Anzû's appearance among Tiamat's brood; Annus 2001, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Tiamat appoints Qingu, identified in the text as her firstborn or as her chosen consort after Apsû, as commander-in-chief of her host.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

Tiamat invests Qingu with the Tablet of Destinies, fastening it to his chest, and thereby confers on him the cosmic decree-power.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

The Tablet's transfer to Qingu is the structural inheritance from the Anzû Epic, where the Tablet had been seized by a usurper; here Tiamat herself bestows it.footnote:[Lambert 2013, Tablet I; Annus 2001, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet I

Tiamat declares Qingu her husband and supreme among the gods of her party, raising him above all others.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I; Lambert 2013, Tablet I.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

The fashioning of Tiamat's host and the elevation of Qingu close Tablet I.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 442, Tablet I closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet I closing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 442

==== Tablet II — Ea's report and the search for a champion

Tablet II opens with Ea learning of Tiamat's preparations and her appointment of Qingu.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 443–444, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 443-444

Ea brings the news to his father Anšar, reporting Tiamat's mobilisation and the catalogue of her monsters in formal repetition.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 443–444, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 443-444

Anšar receives the report with distress, striking his thigh and biting his lip in formulaic gestures of dismay.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 444, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 444

Anšar dispatches Ea against Tiamat: as the slayer of Apsû, Ea is the first candidate for the new combat.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 444–445, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 444-445

Ea sets out, but turns back: faced with Tiamat directly, he cannot prevail and returns to Anšar without engaging.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 445, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 445

Anšar then dispatches Anu against Tiamat, with the same outcome: Anu also turns back unable to confront her.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 445–446, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 445-446

The threefold-refusal pattern of the Anzû Epic — three candidates decline before the eventual champion is named — is structurally inherited here, with two failed embassies before Marduk is proposed.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 444–446, Tablet II; Annus 2001, introduction; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

Anšar despairs: no god of the older generations can confront Tiamat, and the divine assembly is left without a champion.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 446, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 446

Ea then proposes Marduk: his young son, born in the Apsû, surpasses all the other gods in strength and is the only candidate who can face Tiamat.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 446–447, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 446-447

Ea brings Marduk before Anšar, and Anšar receives him with relief and instruction.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 447, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 447

Anšar instructs Marduk to face Tiamat, and Marduk responds by declaring his readiness — but with a condition that closes Tablet II and dominates Tablet III.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 447–448, Tablet II closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 447-448

Marduk's condition is that, if he is to fight Tiamat as the champion of the gods, his decree must be supreme: whatever he commands must come to pass, and his word must take precedence over the assembly's.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 448, Tablet II closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet II.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 448

The condition transforms the combat from a defensive engagement into a kingship-acquisition: Marduk's victory will entail his elevation to supreme rule.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 448, Tablet II; Lambert 2013, Tablet II; Lincoln 2012; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

=== Movement II — Marduk's elevation and the combat with Tiamat (Tablets III–V)

==== Tablet III — the assembly is convened

Tablet III opens with Anšar dispatching his vizier Kaka to Lahmu and Lahamu, the eldest gods, to summon them to the assembly.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 449–450, Tablet III; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 449-450

Kaka delivers the news to the elder gods in formal repetition: Tiamat's mobilisation, Qingu's commission, the failed embassies of Ea and Anu, and Marduk's condition.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 450–453, Tablet III; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 450-453

The repetition is structurally important: the catalogue of monsters and the substance of Marduk's terms are repeated four times across the first four tablets.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 441–442, 443–444, 450–453, 458–459, on the four-fold repetition; Lambert 2013, Tablet III commentary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 441-442, 450-453

Lahmu and Lahamu, hearing the report, gnash their teeth in distress and depart to the assembly.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 453, Tablet III; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 453

The assembly of the great gods, the Igigi and the Anunnaki, gathers and feasts: they drink beer, they sit, they eat bread.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 453–454, Tablet III closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 453-454

The assembly's ceremonial drinking and feasting close Tablet III, with the formal decision on Marduk deferred to Tablet IV.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 453–454, Tablet III closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet III.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 453-454

==== Tablet IV — the assembly's elevation, the test of the cloth, the combat

Tablet IV opens with the assembly's formal decision: Marduk is granted the supremacy he demanded, and his decree is elevated above all others.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 455–456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 455-456

The gods construct a princely throne for Marduk, set him upon it, and acclaim him with the formula "your decree is supreme; your word is Anu."footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The formula equates Marduk's word with the decree of Anu, the sky-father — the highest divine authority in the older Mesopotamian theology.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The assembly tests Marduk's new power by placing a cloth or garment in their midst and asking him to destroy it and then restore it by command alone.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

Marduk speaks; the cloth vanishes. He speaks again; the cloth is restored intact.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The cloth-test confirms that Marduk's word now possesses the cosmic decree-power that the older gods recognise as a property of supreme rule.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The assembly, satisfied, hails Marduk as king: "Marduk is king."footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 456

The acclamation establishes the kingship before the combat: Marduk fights as already-king, and his victory ratifies rather than acquires the office.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 456, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Lincoln 2012; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The gods then arm Marduk for the combat: he takes a bow, an arrow, a mace, lightning, a flaming flame held in his lips, and a net to entrap his foe.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 456–457, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 456-457

Marduk fashions the four winds his grandfather Anu had given him into weapons, and he raises in addition seven evil winds — the Wind, the Tempest, the Whirlwind, the Hurricane, the Cyclone, the Imhullu (literally "evil wind"), and others — that he holds in reserve for the combat.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 456–457, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 456-457

Marduk mounts his storm-chariot, drawn by four named horses — Killer, Pitiless, Trampler, and Fleet — and sets out to confront Tiamat.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 457, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 457

Marduk draws near to Tiamat, and the two engage in a verbal challenge before the physical combat begins.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 457–458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 457-458

Marduk taunts Tiamat with the charge of injustice: she had refused to destroy her offspring with Apsû, yet now she elevates Qingu, who is not her firstborn by right, to a kingship that does not belong to him.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Tiamat answers, and the text describes her as enraged, taking up the combat: she opens her mouth wide.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Marduk drives the Imhullu, the evil wind, into Tiamat's open mouth so that she cannot close her lips.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Other winds rush in after Imhullu, distending Tiamat's belly and rendering her immobile.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

With Tiamat unable to close her mouth, Marduk shoots an arrow into her open belly, splitting her heart.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 458, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 458

Tiamat falls dead, and Marduk stands upon her corpse.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 458–459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 458-459

The host of monstrous creatures Tiamat had fashioned scatters in flight, but Marduk's net catches them; he binds them and treads them under foot.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 459

Qingu, Tiamat's general, is captured among the monsters; Marduk binds him and seizes from his chest the Tablet of Destinies that Tiamat had bestowed.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 459

Marduk seals the Tablet of Destinies and presses it to his own chest, transferring the cosmic decree-power to himself.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 459, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 459

Marduk returns to Tiamat's corpse and rests his foot upon it; he then turns to the work of cosmogony.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 459–460, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 459-460

Marduk splits Tiamat's body lengthwise like a flat fish, into two halves.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 459–460, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 459-460

He raises the upper half of Tiamat's body and sets it as the sky, fixing it in place with bars and a watchman so that her waters cannot escape.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 460, Tablet IV closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 460

The use of Tiamat's body as the material from which the sky is made establishes the cosmogony as combat-cosmogony: the world is the slain adversary's matter, ordered by the victor.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 460, Tablet IV; Lambert 2013, Tablet IV; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

==== Tablet V — the cosmogony, the calendar, the stations of the sky

Tablet V opens with Marduk continuing the cosmogonic work: he establishes the stations of the great gods in the sky, fixing them as constellations.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 461–462, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 461-462

Marduk fixes the year of twelve months, with three stars assigned to each month and the sequence of stations governing the calendar.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 461–462, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 461-462

He establishes the place of Nēberu, the crossing-place — identified with Marduk's own astral station — at the heart of the heavens to fix the boundaries of the year.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 462, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 462

Marduk creates the moon, gives it a crown, and assigns it the office of regulating the night and marking the months.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 462–463, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 462-463

The text gives explicit instructions for the moon's monthly cycle: at the new moon it is to shine as a horn; at the seventh it is to appear as a half-disk; at the fifteenth, the full moon, it is to face the sun directly; and thereafter to wane.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 462–463, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 462-463

Marduk creates the sun and assigns it the office of regulating the day; sun and moon together divide cosmic time.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 463, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 463

[LACUNA: Tablet V contains small gaps in the description of the stations of the moon and the organization of time, around the calendrical instructions for the lunar cycle | source: Lambert 2013, Tablet V apparatus | scholarly_reconstruction: Lambert's critical score integrates Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian witnesses to reconstruct the affected passages.]

Marduk takes the lower half of Tiamat's body and uses it for the earth-cosmogony: from her thighs he makes the great mountains; her eyes he opens for the sources of the Tigris and Euphrates; her nostrils become springs.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 463–464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 463-464

He piles the mountains on Tiamat's head and bores wells through her skull.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 464

He coils her tail to form the cosmic bond, the *durmahu*, that links the heavens and the earth at the horizon.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 464

He arches her crotch to support the heavens, an architectural element of the new cosmos.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 464, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 464

The total cosmogony from Tiamat's body provides sky, mountains, rivers, springs, the cosmic bond, and the architectural support of the heavens — a comprehensive ordering of the visible world from the corpse of the slain adversary.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 459–464, Tablets IV–V; Lambert 2013, Tablets IV–V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 459-464

Marduk presents his completed work to the assembled gods, and the gods respond with celebration.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 464–465, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 464-465

The gods give Marduk the insignia of kingship: a sceptre, a throne, and a royal robe, marking the ratification of his rule over the new cosmos.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 464–465, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 464-465

The gods also pledge their submission to Marduk and make a formal commitment to obey his decree.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 465, Tablet V; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 465

Tablet V closes with Marduk preparing to receive the gods at his side and with the gods' acclamation of his sovereignty over the ordered cosmos.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 465–466, Tablet V closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet V.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 465-466

=== Movement III — Creation of humans, founding of Babylon, the fifty names (Tablets VI–VII)

==== Tablet VI — humans from Qingu's blood, the building of Babylon and Esagil

Tablet VI opens with Marduk announcing his plan to relieve the gods of their labour: he will create humans to perform the work of maintaining the cosmos, freeing the gods for repose.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 467–468, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 467-468

Marduk consults Ea on the means: blood is required, blood from a god who instigated the rebellion of Tiamat.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 468, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 468

The assembly identifies Qingu as the responsible party: he held the Tablet of Destinies, he led Tiamat's host, he is the one whose blood will pay.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 468, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 468

Qingu is bound, brought before Ea, and his blood is opened; from his blood the first humans are fashioned.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 468–469, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 468-469

The aetiology is explicit: humans are made from the blood of a rebel god so that they may bear the labour-burden of the gods.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 468–469, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

The aetiology gives religious sanction to the corvée-and-temple-service economy of Babylonian temple-states: humans are constituted as labour for the gods at their inception.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 468–469, Tablet VI; Lincoln 2012; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

Marduk then divides the gods, allotting three hundred to the heavens above and three hundred to the earth below, fixing the divine assembly's distribution in the new cosmos.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 469, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 469

In gratitude for their relief from labour, the gods undertake to build a sanctuary for Marduk in Babylon: they will construct Esagil, the temple-house, and the city around it.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 470–471, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 470-471

The Anunnaki, the gods of the underworld, undertake the construction; they make bricks, raise walls, and build Esagil over the course of two years.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 471–472, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 471-472

Esagil is constructed as the cosmic counterpart of the Apsû above and the Esarra of Anu — the three-tier structure that mirrors Anšar / Anu / Ea theogonically.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 471–472, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 471-472

Esarra, the earthly counterpart of the heavenly Esagil-above, is established as Anu's temple, paralleling Marduk's seat below.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 471, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 471

When Esagil is complete, the gods enter and feast at Marduk's house: the sanctuary becomes the venue of the divine assembly, with Marduk presiding.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 472–473, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 472-473

The founding of Babylon and Esagil establishes the city as the cosmic centre: the temple is the axis where heaven, earth, and Apsû meet under Marduk's rule.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 471–473, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

The text identifies Babylon as "the house where the great gods reside," establishing its theological status as the seat of the cosmic kingship.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 472, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 472

Within Esagil, Marduk takes his seat upon the throne, and the gods kneel before him in homage.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 472–473, Tablet VI; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 472-473

The gods then begin the ceremonial naming of Marduk: the assembly will pronounce his fifty names, each marking an aspect of his divinity, his combat, or his rule.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–474, Tablet VI closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet VI.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-474

The fifty-names ceremony begins in Tablet VI and continues throughout Tablet VII; structurally it functions as the doxological closure of the epic.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–486, Tablets VI–VII; Lambert 2013, Tablets VI–VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-486

==== Tablet VII — the fifty names

Tablet VII is given over almost entirely to the fifty names of Marduk and their explications, with each name accompanied by interpretive lines that gloss the name's meaning.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–486, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-486

The first names cluster in Tablet VI: Marduk, Marukka, Marutukku, Mershakushu, Lugaldimmerankia (king of the gods of heaven and earth), Nari-Lugal-dimmer-an-ki-a, Asalluhi, and others associated with rule, exorcism, and creation.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 473–476, Tablets VI–VII; Lambert 2013, Tablets VI–VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 473-476

A second cluster of names emphasises Marduk's combat and victory: titles like Asaru, Tutu, and the storm-god names that recall his defeat of Tiamat.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 476–479, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 476-479

A third cluster emphasises Marduk's cosmic ordering work: names that bind him to the calendar, the constellations, and the regulation of cosmic time.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 479–482, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 479-482

A fourth cluster identifies Marduk with the prerogatives of older gods: he absorbs functions and epithets of Enlil, Ea, Anu, Adad, and others, becoming a supreme deity who concentrates the older pantheon's offices in himself.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 482–485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 482-485

The fiftieth name is bestowed by Anšar himself, who declares Marduk's name "Asaru" or, in some witnesses, names him with the formula that closes the catalogue.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 485

Anu then bestows the number fifty on Marduk, equating Marduk numerologically with Enlil — whose sacred number was fifty in the older theology.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 485

The bestowal of Enlil's number on Marduk is theologically decisive: it affirms that Marduk has supplanted Enlil in the supreme office, completing the displacement that the combat had begun.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 485, Tablet VII; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII; Annus 2001, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=Tablet VII

[LACUNA: Tablet VII closing fifty-names passage has some name-explications partially broken | source: Lambert 2013, Tablet VII apparatus | scholarly_reconstruction: Assyrian and Babylonian manuscripts overlap, allowing Lambert's critical score to reconstruct most of the affected passages.]

Tablet VII closes with a doxological hymn that calls on humans to remember and recite Marduk's fifty names, with the explicit liturgical exhortation that the names be passed from father to son and from elder to junior.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 485–486, Tablet VII closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 485-486

The closing hymn frames Enūma Eliš as a recitation-text: it is meant to be spoken aloud, learned by heart, and transmitted through the generations.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 485–486, Tablet VII closing; Lambert 2013, Tablet VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=pp. 485-486

The closing hymn is consistent with the akītu-festival liturgical use of the text: the epic is composed to be performed.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 486, Tablet VII closing; Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu recitation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

=== The Gunkel and Ballentine debate

Hermann Gunkel's 1895 *Schöpfung und Chaos in Urzeit und Endzeit* identified Enūma Eliš as the type-specimen of a religious-historical pattern he named Chaoskampf, in which a sovereign deity defeats the adversary of order and establishes the cosmos.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, with Marduk vs. Tiamat as the central case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

Gunkel's argument was developed in part to illuminate the Hebrew Bible: he proposed that Genesis 1's *tehôm* (the deep) was cognate with Tiamat and that Israelite cosmogony preserved a demythologised version of the Babylonian combat.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I; Day 1985 for the inheriting debate.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The Gunkel reading dominated the twentieth-century literature on Enūma Eliš and on the Hebrew Bible's *tehôm* / Leviathan / Rahab corpus, and it remains the framework against which contemporary alternatives are argued.footnote:[Day 1985 on the dominant Chaoskampf reading; Scurlock & Beal 2013 on the reassessment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=day-1985 ; loc=passim

Debra Scoggins Ballentine's 2015 alternative-sovereign reading argues that "chaos" mistranslates the conceptual work the Mesopotamian text is doing: Tiamat is not undifferentiated disorder but a rival claimant to divine sovereignty.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

On Ballentine's reading, the conflict in Enūma Eliš is between two competing political orders — Tiamat's coalition with Qingu and the assembly's elevation of Marduk — and not between order and disorder.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

Ballentine's reading is anticipated by Bruce Lincoln's argument that combat myths function as political-ideological work, legitimating the victor's regime by recasting the loser's claim as illegitimate.footnote:[Ballentine 2015, ch. 1; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

The Scurlock and Beal 2013 collected volume gathers a range of essays that, in different ways, qualify or reject Gunkel's framing while preserving the recognition that Enūma Eliš is centrally about combat and cosmic order.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim

The book's working position, following Scurlock and Beal and Ballentine, is that "combat myth" is a more accurate descriptive term than "Chaoskampf," but that "Chaoskampf" is retained as the term of art that the scholarly conversation continues to use.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=passim

The Tiamat-as-mother-against-her-children reading is supported by the textual evidence that her initial impulse is refusal of Apsû's destruction-plan and that her later anger is provoked by reproach rather than primordial.footnote:[Foster 2005, pp. 438–442, Tablet I; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=ch. 1

[VARIANT: primary=Gunkel 1895 ("Marduk's combat with Tiamat as the type-specimen of a chaos-versus-order pattern"); alt=Ballentine 2015 ("the conflict is between rival sovereign claimants, and 'chaos' mistranslates the conceptual structure") | chosen: both | reason: the chapter must engage both readings substantively rather than choose one; the brief specifies that both Gunkel and Ballentine be addressed in the body, not in footnotes.]

=== The akītu festival context

The akītu New Year festival is the principal performative context of Enūma Eliš: the text was recited on the fourth day of the festival before the cult-image of Marduk in Esagil.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465, on the akītu recitation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu festival was celebrated in Nisan, the spring month, and structured the Babylonian liturgical year around the renewal of Marduk's kingship and the king's submission to him.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The recitation of Enūma Eliš at the akītu was performed by the high priest before Marduk's image, and the text functioned as the liturgical re-presentation of the cosmogony.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu also included a ritual humiliation and reinstatement of the king: the king was stripped of his insignia, struck on the cheek, and restored — a performance that subordinated the human king to Marduk's cosmic kingship.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The combat-narrative and the ritual frame are mutually constitutive: Enūma Eliš is not a narrative that happens to be recited at the akītu; it is co-extensive with the akītu's annual reactivation of the cosmogony.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Lincoln 2012; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 463-465

The akītu context establishes that the text was understood, in its Babylonian context, as a performative re-creation of the order it narrates — not a one-time historical event but an annually renewed reality.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 463–465; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lincoln-2012 ; loc=essays on combat-myth as political ideology

=== Variants and reception

The Enūma Eliš witnesses preserved across the Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian copies show overall textual stability, with localised variants in spelling, divine epithets, and minor phrasing rather than competing recensions.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the manuscript stability.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

Lambert's critical score for each tablet integrates the variant readings; the standard text presented in Lambert 2013 and in Foster 2005 is a composite drawn from this integration.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15 and per-tablet apparatus; Foster 2005, p. 436.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The Sultantepe tablet group preserves an Assyrian recension in which Aššur, the chief Assyrian deity, replaces Marduk as the cosmic champion in some passages.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the Aššur substitution in the Assyrian recension.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Aššur substitution attests to the political portability of the narrative: the same combat-and-cosmogony structure could be retooled to centre on a different patron deity when the political centre shifted.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction; Lincoln 2012.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

Royal inscriptions from Nebuchadnezzar I onward invoke Marduk's victory over Tiamat as the charter for the king's own legitimacy: the king reigns as Marduk's terrestrial counterpart.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the royal-inscription tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

The Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis ties the composition to the recovery of Marduk's cult-statue from Elam after a period of foreign captivity, framing the epic as theological response to that historical episode.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

[INFERENCE: the Nebuchadnezzar I commissioning hypothesis is the prevailing scholarly view but not universally accepted | basis: Lambert 2013 introduction surveys the dating debate and presents Nebuchadnezzar I as the likely commissioning patron | risk: alternative dates (earlier Kassite, later Neo-Babylonian) have been argued; the commissioning hypothesis is consensus but not unanimous.]

The fifty-names ceremony shows continuity with the older Sumerian and Akkadian tradition of compiling divine names — with the closer antecedent being the honorific names bestowed on Ninurta at the close of the Anzû Epic (cross-ref: Ch. 2).footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction and Tablet III commentary; Lambert 2013, Tablets VI–VII.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

The reuse and outbidding of the Anzû Epic in Enūma Eliš is the clearest documented case of combat-myth rewriting within Mesopotamian literature, with the older Ninurta narrative restructured as a more comprehensive cosmogony with Marduk in the warrior-role.footnote:[Annus 2001, introduction; Lambert 2013, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=annus-2001-anzu ; loc=introduction

In the Mesopotamian reception, Enūma Eliš circulated as a pedagogical text in the scribal curriculum, with copies and excerpts attested from school contexts.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the manuscript distribution including school contexts.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The text continued to be copied and recited into the late Achaemenid and Seleucid periods, attesting its centrality in Babylonian religion across more than a millennium.footnote:[Lambert 2013, pp. 3–15, on the late witnesses.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=pp. 3-15

The post-canonical reuses include the incorporation of Marduk's combat and cosmogony into late Babylonian and Hellenistic-period theological compendia, where the fifty-names tradition continued to develop.footnote:[Lambert 2013, introduction on the post-canonical reception.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=lambert-2013 ; loc=introduction

Foster identifies Enūma Eliš as the most widely studied text of the Akkadian literary corpus and as the central case for any treatment of Mesopotamian cosmology.footnote:[Foster 2005, p. 436, headnote.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=foster-2005 ; loc=p. 436

The text's reception in the modern study of religion runs through Gunkel 1895 and into all subsequent treatments of the combat-myth pattern, making Enūma Eliš the type-specimen of the comparative category that this book examines.footnote:[Gunkel 1895; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2; Scurlock & Beal 2013; Ballentine 2015, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Ch. 2 establishes the antecedent; this chapter establishes the descendant; Part II C2 (combat as sovereignty) uses the pair as template for "how a sovereignty combat gets escalated to a cosmogonic combat" (annus-2001-anzu; lambert-2013).
