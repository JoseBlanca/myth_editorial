== Tarḫunna vs. Illuyanka: The Purulli Festival — Claims

=== Source overview

The myth of Tarḫunna and Illuyanka survives on cuneiform tablets recovered from the Hittite royal archives at the capital Hattusa, modern Boğazköy in central Anatolia.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, "The Anatolian Myth of Illuyanka," JANES 14: 11–25, on the manuscript provenance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The text is catalogued in Emmanuel Laroche's *Catalogue des textes hittites* as CTH 321, the dossier number under which the surviving manuscripts of the Illuyanka myth are grouped.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka," giving the CTH 321 designation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The composition is preserved on multiple manuscripts of varying completeness rather than on a single tablet, and modern editions are composite reconstructions from these witnesses.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka," on the manuscript group; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the witness situation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

The language of CTH 321 is Hittite, the Indo-European language of the Hittite royal court used for the bulk of the cuneiform literary corpus from Hattusa.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, general introduction to *Hittite Myths* on the linguistic situation of the corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=general introduction

The CTH 321 dossier preserves two distinct narrative accounts of the same combat between the storm-god and the serpent Illuyanka, conventionally numbered Version 1 and Version 2 by modern editors.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2, the two versions; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the two-version structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

Both versions are written by the same scribal tradition on the same tablet group and are presented side by side rather than as a single composite narrative.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the side-by-side presentation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

Modern scholarship treats the two versions as co-equal scribal accounts rather than ranking one as primary, since neither the scribes themselves nor the surviving cult contexts privilege one over the other.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The framing material on the tablets identifies the composition as a recitation associated with the _purulli_ festival, the Hittite spring liturgical occasion at which the myth was performed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening colophon naming the purulli festival; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 opening

The named scribal author in the colophon is Kella, identified on the tablet as the priest who set down the recitation of the purulli festival.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, colophon naming Kella; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 colophon

The editio princeps used as the philological anchor for this chapter is Gary Beckman's "The Anatolian Myth of Illuyanka," published in the *Journal of the Ancient Near Eastern Society* 14 (1982): 11–25.footnote:[Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, full article.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

The working English translation cited in this chapter is Harry A. Hoffner Jr.'s rendering in *Hittite Myths*, 2nd edition (Society of Biblical Literature, 1998), where the two versions are printed as §1 and §2 of the "Illuyanka" entry.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka," §§1–2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

The Hoffner edition presents the two versions in continuous English translation with section numbering that follows the tablet-paragraphs of the underlying Hittite text.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka," editorial conventions and §§1–2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka editorial conventions

Calvert Watkins's *How to Kill a Dragon* (Oxford, 1995) provides the principal Indo-European linguistic and poetic-formula analysis of the Hittite text, treating Illuyanka as one of the four main demonstration cases for the reconstructed combat formula.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26, on Illuyanka and the Indo-European combat formula in Hittite.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

M.L. West's *The East Face of Helicon* (Oxford, 1997) treats the Hittite dragon-combat in the broader context of Indo-European and Near Eastern transmission, with cross-references to Greek and Levantine cognates.footnote:[West 1997, references on the Hittite dragon-combat in IE and Near-Eastern context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=references on the Hittite dragon-combat

Neil Forsyth's *The Old Enemy* (Princeton, 1987) places the Illuyanka combat within the Anatolian segment of his long history of the combat-myth from Sumer through Christian apocalyptic.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on Anatolian combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

=== Setting and context — the storm-god of Nerik and the purulli festival

Tarḫunna is the Hittite storm-god, the head of the Hittite state pantheon during the Old and New Kingdom periods of the second millennium BCE.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, general introduction on Hittite religion and the storm-god.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=general introduction

The Hurrian-language counterpart of Tarḫunna is Teshub, and the same deity appears under the Hurrian name in the Kumarbi Cycle and other Hurrian-derived compositions of the Hittite archive.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, general introduction on Hittite-Hurrian theological correspondences.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=general introduction

The Illuyanka myth is a Hittite-language composition and consequently uses Tarḫunna throughout, distinguishing it from the Hurrian-derived material that uses Teshub.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2, throughout.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

Nerik is a northern Hittite cult-centre and the principal locus of the storm-god's cult, and is the site at which the _purulli_ festival was annually celebrated.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on Nerik and the purulli festival.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

Nerik lay in the northern frontier zone of the Hittite kingdom and was for long stretches of the second millennium under the control of the Kaska peoples, with the consequence that the cult of the storm-god of Nerik was at times conducted in exile from the city.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on Nerik's geopolitical situation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The cult of the storm-god of Nerik is tied into Hittite royal religion, and the _purulli_ festival therefore connects the Illuyanka recitation to the legitimation of the Hittite kingship.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on the royal-religious context.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The _purulli_ festival is a spring liturgical occasion, and the surviving framing texts identify its purpose as ensuring the prosperity of the land and the cosmic order in the agricultural year ahead.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the purulli framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 opening

The term _purulli_ is preserved with a determinative for festival in the cuneiform script and is a Hittite ritual term rather than a loanword from Hurrian or Akkadian.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, glossary entry under purulli; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the term's status.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=glossary entry purulli

The opening framing of CTH 321 consists of a scribal colophon that explicitly states that what follows is the recitation associated with the _purulli_ festival of the storm-god.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening colophon; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 opening colophon

The framing colophon links the festival's annual recurrence to the recitation of the combat narrative, embedding the myth in the liturgical year as the verbal component of the festival.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1 framing; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 framing

This explicit ritual embedding is what marks the Illuyanka myth as one of the few ancient Near Eastern combat myths whose liturgical context is directly attested on the tablet itself rather than reconstructed from later cult evidence.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the unusually explicit framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

Inara is the daughter of the storm-god Tarḫunna and is the principal divine actor of Version 1 of the myth.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Inara as Tarḫunna's daughter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Hupasiya is a mortal man, the human protagonist of Version 1, whose home town is named in the text as Ziggaratta.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Hupasiya and his town Ziggaratta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Illuyanka is a serpent or dragon, the cosmic adversary of the storm-god, and the antagonist whose defeat in either version constitutes the combat that the festival commemorates.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2, on Illuyanka as serpent-adversary.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

The Hittite noun *illuyanka-* is itself the common-noun word for "snake" or "serpent," used as a proper name for the cosmic adversary in CTH 321.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26, on the etymology of Illuyanka as the common-noun "serpent"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

Forsyth situates the Illuyanka combat within the family of Anatolian combat-myths that includes the Kumarbi Cycle's Ullikummi and Hedammu, distinguishing the Hittite-language Illuyanka from the Hurrian-derived material in register and ritual function.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on Anatolian combat-myths.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

=== Version 1 — Inara and Hupasiya

Version 1 of the Illuyanka myth, preserved as Hoffner's "Illuyanka" §1, opens with the storm-god and the serpent Illuyanka meeting in combat at a place called Kiskilussa.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, opening narrative at Kiskilussa.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

In this first encounter, Illuyanka defeats Tarḫunna: the serpent overcomes the storm-god, an outcome that establishes the narrative problem the rest of Version 1 addresses.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the storm-god's initial defeat by Illuyanka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Defeated, Tarḫunna calls upon all the gods for help, and Inara, his daughter, comes forward and devises a stratagem to enable a second engagement.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the storm-god's appeal and Inara's response.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Inara prepares a great banquet, with vessels filled with all kinds of drink — wine, *marnuwan*-beer, and *walhi*-beer are named — designed to lure Illuyanka out of its hole.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the banquet preparation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Inara then descends to the town of Ziggaratta and there finds Hupasiya, a mortal man, whose recruitment to her plan is the next step.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Inara's visit to Ziggaratta and Hupasiya.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Inara proposes a bargain to Hupasiya: he is to assist her against the serpent, and in exchange she offers to spend a night with him.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Inara's bargain with Hupasiya.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Hupasiya agrees to the bargain, and the text records the night spent between them as a fulfilled stipulation of the agreement.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the consummated bargain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Inara conducts Hupasiya to the site of the planned banquet, and there conceals him so that he is positioned to act once the serpent has come out of its hole.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the concealment of Hupasiya at the banquet site.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Inara dresses herself in finery and summons Illuyanka and his offspring out of their hole to the banquet.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Inara's summoning of the serpent.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Illuyanka and his offspring come up from the hole and eat and drink at the banquet that Inara has prepared.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the serpent's feast.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

The serpents drink to satiation, and the text states that they are no longer able to descend back into their hole because they are too engorged.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the serpents' inability to return to the hole.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

While the serpents are immobilised by their gorging, Hupasiya emerges from concealment and binds Illuyanka with a rope.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Hupasiya's binding of Illuyanka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

The storm-god Tarḫunna then returns, accompanied by the assembled gods, and slays the bound Illuyanka.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the storm-god's killing of the bound Illuyanka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

The storm-god's killing of the serpent in Version 1 is achieved only because Illuyanka has been immobilised by Hupasiya's binding, not by direct combat — the divine victory depends on the mortal helper.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on the mortal helper's role.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

After the killing, Inara takes Hupasiya to a house she has built for him on a cliff in the land of Tarukka, and there installs him.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on the house in the land of Tarukka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Inara imposes a prohibition on Hupasiya: he is forbidden from looking out of the window of his cliffside house, lest he see his wife and children whom he has left behind.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Inara's prohibition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

After twenty days, Hupasiya looks out of the window in defiance of the prohibition and sees his wife and children.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Hupasiya's transgression.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

Upon Inara's return, Hupasiya begs to be sent home to his family, and Inara reacts in anger to his transgression of her prohibition.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, on Hupasiya's request and Inara's reaction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

The text at this point becomes broken, and the precise fate of Hupasiya in Version 1 is not preserved in continuous form on the surviving manuscripts.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, marked as broken at the close of the Hupasiya episode.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 (broken)

[LACUNA: the closing section of Version 1, including the precise fate of Hupasiya and the framing of the purulli festival as the myth's aetiology, is partially broken on the surviving manuscripts | source: Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1, end of tablet | scholarly_reconstruction: the ritual colophon connecting the narrative to the purulli festival is extant in the framing material, but the narrative continuity between Hupasiya's transgression and the festival aetiology cannot be fully reconstructed from the surviving witnesses.]

The framing material that survives at the close of Version 1 connects the recitation back to the _purulli_ festival, identifying the narrative just told as the ritual aetiology of the spring observance.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1 closing framing; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 closing framing

The closing of Version 1 also includes liturgical material referring to a building or house in which the gods convene, and to a procession of cult objects as part of the festival.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1 closing; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the ritual closing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1 closing

Version 1 is thus structured as a god–goddess–mortal-man triad, with the storm-god as the threatened sovereign, Inara as the divine strategist, and Hupasiya as the indispensable mortal binder.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §1; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the triadic structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §1

=== Version 2 — the storm-god's son and the bride-price

Version 2 of the Illuyanka myth, preserved as Hoffner's "Illuyanka" §2, opens with a different combat scenario: the serpent Illuyanka defeats the storm-god in a manner that involves bodily mutilation rather than mere overpowering.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, opening combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

In Version 2, Illuyanka takes from the storm-god his heart and his eyes, removing these organs as the prize of his victory.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the loss of heart and eyes.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The mutilated storm-god, now without heart and eyes, takes a daughter of a poor man as his wife, with whom he begets a son.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the storm-god's mortal marriage and son.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The storm-god's son grows to maturity within his mortal mother's family without the text recording the kind of supernatural acceleration that characterises some other Near Eastern hero-births.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the son's upbringing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The son is then given in marriage into the household of Illuyanka, taking as his wife a daughter of the serpent.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the marriage into Illuyanka's house.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

Before the son enters Illuyanka's household, the storm-god instructs him: when his bride-price is to be set, he is to ask for the storm-god's heart and eyes — the organs Illuyanka had taken — as the gift demanded from the bride's family.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the storm-god's instruction concerning the bride-price.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The son enters Illuyanka's house and demands as bride-price the storm-god's heart and eyes.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the son's demand.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

Illuyanka's household yields the heart and the eyes to the son, who carries them back to his father the storm-god.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the recovery of the organs.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The storm-god, with his heart and eyes restored, regains his full divine vigour and prepares for a renewed combat with Illuyanka.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the restoration of the storm-god.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

[LACUNA: the mid-narrative transitions of Version 2, between the son's marriage into Illuyanka's house and the recovery of the organs, are broken on the surviving manuscripts | source: Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, mid-narrative | scholarly_reconstruction: the tablet preserves enough text for the basic narrative arc to be followed, but the specific staging of the bride-price negotiation and any intervening episodes are not continuously preserved.]

The storm-god then engages Illuyanka anew, this time in a combat at the sea, and slays the serpent.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the renewed combat at the sea.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

In the renewed combat, the storm-god's son must also be killed, because by virtue of his marriage into Illuyanka's house he has become bound to the serpent's family by the obligations of kinship.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the killing of the son alongside Illuyanka.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The son himself acknowledges the obligation, and according to the surviving text he calls upon his father the storm-god to kill him along with Illuyanka, since he has kept faith with his father-in-law's house and cannot be spared.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the son's request for his own death.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The storm-god kills both Illuyanka and his own son in the same combat, and the text registers no exemption for the son despite his role in the recovery of the organs.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2, on the dual killing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The closing of Version 2, like that of Version 1, returns to the framing of the _purulli_ festival, identifying the recitation as the liturgical companion of the spring rite at Nerik.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2 closing framing; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2 closing

Version 2 is thus structured around a loss-and-recovery pattern: the storm-god's organs are taken, are restored through the agency of his half-mortal son, and the second combat completes what the first could not.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §2; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3, on the loss-and-recovery pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §2

The two versions share a common backbone: an initial defeat of the storm-god by Illuyanka, the recruitment of an indirect agent (a mortal man in Version 1, a half-mortal son in Version 2), and a second engagement in which the serpent is killed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the shared narrative armature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The two versions diverge sharply on the role of the mortal collaborator: in Version 1 the mortal Hupasiya is rewarded and then punished for transgression; in Version 2 the storm-god's son is killed for keeping faith with the wrong household.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

The two versions also diverge on the mode of the storm-god's initial defeat: Version 1 narrates an unspecified overpowering, while Version 2 specifies bodily mutilation through the loss of heart and eyes.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

Beckman in the editio princeps argues that the two versions are best understood as alternative liturgical recitations of the same myth, drawn from different cult centres or different ritual occasions and gathered onto the same tablet group by the scribes of Hattusa.footnote:[Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the relation between the two versions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

Hoffner notes that the question of whether one version is older than the other has been debated, but that the surviving tablet group does not itself establish a relative chronology between them.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka," on the chronological question.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

=== The ritual context — purulli at Nerik

The _purulli_ festival was an annual spring observance of the Hittite cultic year, performed at Nerik when the cult could be conducted there and at substitute locations (notably Hakmis) when Nerik was not under Hittite control.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on the festival's annual cycle and locations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The festival is referenced in Hittite ritual texts beyond CTH 321 itself, and these ritual descriptions establish its character as a multi-day cultic event involving the storm-god of Nerik.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on the festival's broader cultic attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The framing colophons of CTH 321 indicate that the recitation of the Illuyanka myth was a verbal component of the festival, performed alongside the cultic actions described in the ritual texts.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2 framing material; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the recitation's role in the festival.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka framing material

The combat narrative is thus presented as the verbal score of an annual ritual: the killing of the serpent is re-enacted each spring through recitation, and the festival's recurrence is the festival's whole point.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the cyclical character of the rite.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The cyclical-maintenance character of the Illuyanka myth — combat as something annually re-enacted rather than once-and-done — is one of the principal grounds on which modern scholarship pairs the Hittite material with the Egyptian Ra-Apep complex as the type-cases of the cyclical-maintenance subtype.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The cult of the storm-god of Nerik is closely tied to the Hittite kingship: the king himself participated in the _purulli_ festival, and royal documents register the storm-god of Nerik among the principal patron deities of the Hittite state.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on the royal-religious dimension.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The connection between the _purulli_ festival and Hittite kingship gives the Illuyanka myth a double function: ritual-aetiological for the spring festival and royal-legitimating for the dynasty under whose authority the festival was performed.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on the royal dimension.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The historical period over which the _purulli_ festival is attested spans the Old Hittite through the New Hittite kingdom, with the bulk of the surviving tablets dating to the New Hittite period of the late second millennium BCE.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka" on the chronological span of the attestation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

[INFERENCE: the recitation of both Version 1 and Version 2 in connection with the same festival suggests that the Hittite cult tolerated, and perhaps required, narrative redundancy — the same combat told in two manners as part of a single liturgical occasion | basis: Hoffner 1998 introduction notes that both versions sit on the same tablet group framed by purulli colophons, and Beckman 1982 on the side-by-side scribal presentation; the inference draws together those observations | risk: the surviving tablets do not state explicitly that both versions were recited in the same festival, only that both are connected to the festival in their framing.]

The Illuyanka myth's explicit ritual embedding distinguishes it from the cosmogonic combat-myths of Mesopotamia (Enūma Eliš, Lugal-e), where the ritual context must be reconstructed from secondary evidence, and from the Levantine Baal Cycle, where the connection to specific festivals is debated.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on the contrast with Mesopotamian and Levantine combat-myths; Hoffner 1998, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 3

The myth's framing as the verbal counterpart of an annual rite places it in the cyclical-maintenance subtype of combat-myth — combat re-enacted to preserve an order that is always under threat, rather than combat-as-cosmogony or combat-as-once-only-sovereignty-establishment.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The fact that two versions coexist on the same tablet group also distinguishes the Illuyanka dossier from many other ancient Near Eastern combat-myths, where a single canonical version is sought and variant readings are treated as scribal errors rather than as parallel recitations.footnote:[Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25, on the unusual co-equal scribal handling; Hoffner 1998, introduction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=beckman-1982-illuyanka ; loc=JANES 14: 11-25

=== The Watkins formula

Calvert Watkins, in *How to Kill a Dragon* (1995), identifies a reconstructed Indo-European poetic formula of the form HERO SLAY SERPENT, in which a subject-noun, a verb of killing, and a serpent-object together name the combat.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26 and the volume's framing chapters on the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

The formula is anchored in Watkins's analysis on the Vedic phrase _áhann áhim_, "he slew the serpent," from Rigveda 1.32.1, which he treats as the reflex closest to the reconstructed Indo-European form.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26 on _áhann áhim_ as the reflex anchor.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

Watkins argues that the Hittite text of CTH 321 preserves a cognate formulation in the verb-and-object pairing *kuenta… illuyanka-* — "he slew… the serpent" — which uses a verb of killing etymologically related to the Vedic *han-* and an object that is the common-noun "serpent."footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26 on the Hittite cognate formulation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

The Hittite reflex thus places Illuyanka, alongside Vedic Vṛtra and Avestan Aži Dahāka, among the principal demonstration cases for Watkins's reconstructed Indo-European combat-myth formula.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26 on Illuyanka as a demonstration case.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

Watkins's argument turns on lexical and syntactic correspondences across the Indo-European daughter languages, not on narrative-structural parallels alone, and is therefore methodologically distinct from a Frazerian or Dumézilian comparativism.footnote:[Watkins 1995, methodological framing chapters on the lexical-syntactic basis of the reconstruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=methodological framing chapters

The presence of the formula in Hittite is significant for the dating of the reconstructed formula, since Hittite is the earliest-attested Indo-European language and an early-branching member of the family.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26 and methodological framing on the chronological significance of the Hittite reflex.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

West reinforces Watkins's reading by placing the Hittite material within the broader Near Eastern transmission picture, noting that Illuyanka stands at the intersection of Indo-European inheritance and Anatolian local cult.footnote:[West 1997, references on the Hittite dragon-combat as an Indo-European / Near-Eastern intersection.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-1997 ; loc=Hittite dragon-combat references

The full elaboration of the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula, including the Greek (Apollo, Heracles), Germanic (Sigurd, Beowulf), and Iranian (Thraētaona vs. Aži Dahāka) reflexes, is reserved for the comparative chapter on Indo-European combat-myth (Part II C5), and is not developed further in the present chapter.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26 and following chapters; cross-reference to Part II C5 of this volume.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26 and following chapters

=== Closing — the Illuyanka dossier and its cyclical work

The Illuyanka dossier consists of two co-equal scribal versions of a single combat between Tarḫunna and Illuyanka, framed in their tablet form as the recitation belonging to the _purulli_ festival of the storm-god of Nerik.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, "Illuyanka" §§1–2 with framing colophons; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka §§1-2

The dossier's distinctive contribution to the comparative study of the combat-myth is its explicit, on-tablet ritual embedding: the combat is presented as the verbal score of an annual rite, not as a one-off cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing act.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Beckman 1982, JANES 14: 11–25; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

This cyclical-maintenance framing pairs the Illuyanka myth with the Egyptian Ra-Apep complex as the two type-cases of combat-myth-as-annual-recurrence within the ancient Near Eastern corpus.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3 on the cyclical pairing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=hoffner-1998 ; loc=Illuyanka introduction

The Illuyanka myth's place in the Indo-European combat-myth complex, secured by Watkins's formula analysis, gives it a second comparative function: as one of the earliest-attested reflexes of the reconstructed HERO SLAY SERPENT formula across the Indo-European daughter languages.footnote:[Watkins 1995, ch. 26.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 26

The dossier thus stands at the intersection of two comparative axes — the Near Eastern cyclical-maintenance subtype and the Indo-European combat-formula tradition — both of which the Part II thematic chapters develop further.footnote:[Hoffner 1998, introduction to "Illuyanka"; Watkins 1995, ch. 26; Forsyth 1987, ch. 3.]
