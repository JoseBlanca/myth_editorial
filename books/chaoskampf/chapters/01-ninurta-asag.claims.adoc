=== Source overview

The Sumerian composition known by its incipit _Lugal-e_ is catalogued as ETCSL 1.6.2 and bears the scholarly title "Ninurta's Exploits."footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text (Black, Cunningham, et al.); the composition is titled from its opening line _lugal-e ud me-lám-bi nir-ĝál_, "O king, storm of majestic splendour."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text header

_Lugal-e_ is one of the longest Sumerian literary compositions, running to approximately 728 lines in the composite text reconstructed by J.J.A. van Dijk.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction); the edition's full title names the composition by its first line and subtitles it "Le récit épique et didactique des Travaux de Ninurta, du Déluge et de la Nouvelle Création."]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, introduction

The composition is preserved in dozens of Old Babylonian tablet witnesses recovered from scribal-school contexts, with the bulk coming from Nippur and a smaller cluster from Ur.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (manuscript catalogue and introduction to the transmission); ETCSL 1.6.2 editorial notes list the Old Babylonian witnesses that underlie the composite.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, manuscript catalogue

The text scholars read is a composite: no single tablet preserves the whole poem, and the running line-numbered edition is stitched together from overlapping manuscript witnesses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 composite text, editorial note on manuscript base; van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction to the composite method).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, editorial notes on composite

_Lugal-e_ was excerpted, copied, and bilingualised (Sumerian-Akkadian interlinear) in temple-school curricula well into the first millennium BCE, long after Sumerian had ceased to be a spoken language.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction on the transmission and the first-millennium bilingual redactions).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vol. 1, introduction on transmission

The standard critical edition is van Dijk 1983, two volumes, Brill: volume 1 contains introduction, transliteration, and French translation; volume 2 contains the cuneiform copies, manuscript apparatus, and glossary.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, 2 vols. (Leiden: Brill); ISBN 9789004069169.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vandijk-1983-lugale ; loc=vols. 1-2

The English composite translation on which this chapter's narrative is keyed is the ETCSL edition produced by Jeremy Black, Graham Cunningham, Esther Flückiger-Hawker, Eleanor Robson, and Gábor Zólyomi between 1998 and 2006.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text and English translation; Black et al. 2004 (print edition of the ETCSL translations).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2 front-matter

[VARIANT: primary="van Dijk 1983 French critical translation" | alt="ETCSL English composite (Black et al.), which follows van Dijk's Sumerian reconstruction but modernises a handful of lexical choices where van Dijk used tentative French readings" | chosen: ETCSL English for body prose, keyed to van Dijk's line numbers | reason: "Per brief: ETCSL follows van Dijk's composite and provides the accessible English; van Dijk remains the philological anchor. Minor divergences are flagged in footnotes where meaningful."]

=== Setting and context

Ninurta is a Sumerian warrior-god, the son of Enlil, and the divine patron of the city of Nippur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 (Lugal-e), proem lines 1–19, where the opening epithets identify Ninurta as Enlil's son and as the martial champion of the gods.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1-19

Enlil is the chief god of the Sumerian pantheon, lord of air and command, resident at Nippur's temple-complex the E-kur.footnote:[Lugal-e references to Enlil throughout (e.g., ETCSL 1.6.2 lines 186–227); for Enlil's profile in the Sumerian pantheon see Forsyth 1987, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 1

Enki is the Sumerian god of wisdom and fresh water, counsellor of the gods in the Sumerian corpus; his Akkadian counterpart Ea appears in the later receptions of this narrative.footnote:[Glossary of Sumerian deities in Black et al. 2004; for the language-conditional pairing Enki (Sumerian) / Ea (Akkadian) see the book's glossary entry.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL glossary of deities

_Lugal-e_ opens with a hymnic proem addressing Ninurta and celebrating his martial prowess and divine favour.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 (Lugal-e), proem lines 1–19.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1-19

[LACUNA: Proem partially broken across witnesses | source: ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1–15 (Ur and Nippur manuscripts) | scholarly_reconstruction: van Dijk 1983 reconstructs the opening from overlap across Ur and Nippur manuscripts; ETCSL composite follows van Dijk's reconstruction]

Ninurta is seated in celebration at the opening of the narrative's action when his weapon alerts him to a threat rising in the mountains.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17–23: Ninurta's feast interrupted by Šarur's first report.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17-23

The adversary named in the report is Asag, a monstrous being whose parentage is given as Heaven (An) and Earth (Ki) and who rules the mountains at the head of a stone-army.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: Šarur's announcement of Asag's birth and of the stone-warriors in Asag's retinue.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

The Sumerian term _kur_ ("mountain" / "foreign land" / "netherworld") denotes the zone from which Asag and his stone-army come, and carries a cluster of negative associations in the Sumerian literary corpus.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2 passim (lines 24ff., 70–118); for the semantic range of _kur_ see Forsyth 1987, ch. 1.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, passim

Ninurta's weapon is named Šarur and is represented throughout the composition as an animate, speaking agent — a talking mace that both scouts and counsels.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17–23 and passim: Šarur speaks, flies, reports, and argues. The animate-weapon motif is a structural feature of the composition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 17-23 and passim

=== Narrative sequence — the scouting and the challenge

Šarur brings Ninurta a first report: Asag has been born from the union of An and Ki and now reigns among the mountains.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: Šarur's first speech to Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

Asag is accompanied by an army of stone-warriors; the ETCSL composite names several of the stone-types (for example _šu_, _saĝkal_, _esi_, _usium_, and _kagena_) as members of this retinue.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: the stone-warrior catalogue in Šarur's first report.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

Asag's forces are reported to be terrorising the cities of the land.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24–47: Šarur's description of Asag's depredations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 24-47

Šarur then delivers a second speech urging Ninurta to act, framing the threat as a direct challenge to Ninurta's kingship and to the divine powers that support it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 48–56: Šarur's second speech, a call to arms.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 48-56

In a third speech Šarur describes Asag's invulnerability and warns that conventional weapons cannot penetrate the adversary.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 57–69: Šarur on Asag's hide and on the limits of ordinary weaponry.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 57-69

Ninurta, roused, gathers his army and his storm-weapons and marches toward the mountain lands, accompanied by cosmic disturbance — clouds, lightning, and the eight winds.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 70–95: Ninurta's mobilisation and the storm-imagery of his approach.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 70-95

In the first combat phase Ninurta destroys the enemy cities and rebel settlements of the kur; Šarur flies forward as a reconnaissance weapon and returns with reports on Asag's movements.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 96–118: the first combat phase and Šarur's reconnaissance flight.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 96-118

Šarur enumerates Ninurta's earlier victories — including defeats of Kuli-ana, the Dragon, the Gypsum-monster, the Copper-monster, the Six-headed Ram, the Magilum-boat, Saman-ana, the Bison, the Palm-king, Anzud (the lion-headed eagle), and the Seven-headed Snake — as a rhetorical preface to his warning about Asag.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119–134: Šarur's catalogue of Ninurta's prior victories, sometimes known as the "trophy list" of Ninurta's combats.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119-134

Šarur's catalogue functions as an argument from precedent: past victories are listed to measure the gravity of the present challenge, not to flatter the hero.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119–150: Šarur's transition from catalogue to warning.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 119-150

Šarur, despite being Ninurta's weapon, urges caution: Asag surpasses every previous foe, and Šarur pleads that Ninurta not engage directly.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 135–150: Šarur's caution, an unusual moment in which the weapon dissuades its wielder.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 135-150

Ninurta refuses the counsel to withdraw and advances into the mountain; the text marks the scene with images of day turning to darkness as he marches.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 151–167: Ninurta's refusal to withdraw and the advance into the kur.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 151-167

Asag emerges against Ninurta with apocalyptic force; the cosmic order shakes, and the gods of the pantheon are reported as cowering before the confrontation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 168–186: Asag's emergence, cosmic upheaval, and the gods' reaction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 168-186

Enlil laments to his consort Ninlil that his son is no longer visible and that the pillars of his authority are shaken: "My son is no longer here; what supports me?"footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 187–190: Enlil's lament to Ninlil.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 187-190

Šarur flies from the battlefield to Enlil at the E-kur in Nippur to receive further instruction.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 191–214: Šarur's errand to Enlil and the intercession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 191-214

Enlil issues a conditional blessing: Ninurta is authorised to slay Asag, but must preserve the fertility of the land and the species that dwell upon it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 215–224: Enlil's charge to Ninurta through Šarur.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 215-224

Šarur races back to Ninurta carrying Enlil's mandate; the text marks the weapon's speed and joy at bearing the authorising word.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 225–227: Šarur returns with Enlil's instructions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 225-227

Šarur then delivers a battle-directive to Ninurta: pierce Asag's liver and bring that organ to the E-kur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 228–243: Šarur's delivery of Enlil's specific combat instruction.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 228-243

=== Narrative sequence — the combat

As the combat intensifies, Ninurta's weapons devastate the landscape but Asag endures; Šarur again interjects to urge retreat.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 244–264: the exchange of blows and Šarur's renewed caution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 244-264

Šarur's final plea frames Asag as storm-like and uncontrollable, a force of an order unfamiliar to conventional combat.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 265–280: Šarur's last cautionary speech.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 265-280

Ninurta overpowers Asag and reduces the adversary to a heap of rubble and dust, fulfilling the combat charge from Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 281–299: Ninurta's victory over Asag.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 281-299

After the slaying, the gods prostrate themselves before Ninurta; Šarur sings the victor's praise.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 300–309: divine homage and Šarur's victory hymn.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 300-309

[LACUNA: List of stone-soldiers in Asag's army variably preserved across witnesses | source: ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 300–340 | scholarly_reconstruction: ETCSL composite follows the best-preserved Old Babylonian witness for the catalogue; van Dijk 1983 documents the witness-by-witness variation]

Ninurta pronounces a transforming decree over the defeated Asag: henceforth the adversary shall be called "Stone."footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 310–330: the naming of the defeated Asag as Stone, transitioning the narrative toward the lapidary aetiology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 310-330

Ninurta pronounces a blessing over his own club, a formulaic closure of the combat proper.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 331–333: the mace's blessing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 331-333

=== Narrative sequence — the ordering of the waters

After the combat the landscape is disturbed: the waters of the mountain are blocked, the fields lie barren, and famine threatens the land.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 334–346: the post-war ecological crisis.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 334-346

Ninurta addresses the water-crisis by heaping up a great embankment of stones over the mountain, damming the uncontrolled waters and channelling their flow into the Tigris.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 347–359: the construction of the stone-barrage and the engineering of the Tigris flow.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 347-359

The Tigris is the great river of eastern Mesopotamia, and in _Lugal-e_ it is Ninurta's reordering of its flow that establishes the irrigation regime on which the farmland of Sumer depends.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 347–367: the Tigris reordering; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the cosmological reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

After the Tigris is set in order, barley flourishes, kings rejoice, and the trading colonies expand — the text registers a restored economic and agricultural prosperity.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 360–367: the prosperity that follows the water-ordering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 360-367

=== Narrative sequence — the naming of the mountain

Ninurta's mother, the goddess variously named Ninmaḫ, laments the inaccessibility of the mountains where she used to dwell; she travels to Ninurta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 368–386: Ninmaḫ's lament and journey to Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 368-386

Ninurta honours his mother by decreeing that the stone-heap raised over the defeated Asag shall be called "Mountain" — _ḫursaĝ_ — and that she shall be its divine keeper under the name Ninḫursaĝa, "Lady of the Mountain."footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410: the renaming of the mother goddess as Ninḫursaĝa and the endowment of the mountain.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387-410

Ninurta endows the new Mountain with abundance: herbs, honey, metals, and wildlife are conferred as the mountain's produce.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410: the mountain's endowment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387-410

The episode of the renaming ties the combat's aftermath directly to the cultic profile of Ninḫursaĝa, one of the senior goddesses of the Sumerian pantheon.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the cosmogonic-royal logic of the renaming.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

=== Narrative sequence — the lapidary aetiology

After the mountain is named, the goddess Aruru reminds Ninurta that the fates of the slain warriors — now stones — have yet to be fixed.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–434: the opening of the stone-judgements, prompted by Aruru.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411-434

The lapidary aetiology that follows (running approximately lines 416–647) assigns each stone-type a destiny — honour or contempt, cultic use or mundane use, reward or punishment — according to the stone's conduct during the battle.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 416–647: the lapidary aetiology as a whole; for the ritual-aetiological reading see Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 416-647

The sequence is introduced by Ninurta's cursing of the _u_ stone (emery), whose resistance in the battle is punished by an unfavourable destiny.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–434: Ninurta's curse on the emery stone (_u_).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411-434

The _šu_ and _gasura_ stones are assigned to be shaped by goldsmiths into cultic vessels — a destiny of ritual honour.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 435–447: the judgement on _šu_ and _gasura_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 435-447

The _saĝkal_, _gulgul_, and _saĝĝar_ stones are condemned to a destiny of contempt, poverty, and servitude.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 448–462: the punitive judgement on _saĝkal_, _gulgul_, and _saĝĝar_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 448-462

The _esi_ stone (diorite) receives a destiny of high honour: it is to be quarried from Magan (the land across the Persian Gulf) and shaped for temple use, including statuary and libation-vessels.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 463–478: the judgement on _esi_ (diorite), and the Magan sourcing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 463-478

The _na_ stone is cursed: it is to be broken up, its fragments discarded.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 479–486: the punitive judgement on _na_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 479-486

The _elel_ stone is rewarded: it is to be set on a pedestal and praised by all the lands.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 487–496: the honorific judgement on _elel_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 487-496

The _kagena_ stone (haematite) is blessed with a destiny of high status: it is to serve as a judge-stone in foreign lands and to be valued alongside gold.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 497–511: the judgement on _kagena_ (haematite).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 497-511

The _ĝišnugal_ stone (alabaster) is honoured for loyalty and is assigned the role of treasury seal-keeper.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 512–521: the judgement on _ĝišnugal_ (alabaster).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 512-521

The _algameš_ stone is cursed: it is to be consumed by smiths' fires as a daily sacrifice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 522–527: the punitive judgement on _algameš_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 522-527

A cluster of precious stones — including _nir_, _gug_ (carnelian), and _zagin_ (lapis lazuli) — receives a collective honour: to be mounted in precious metal and displayed at libations.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 528–542: the honorific judgement on the precious-stone cluster.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 528-542

The _ĝir-zu-ĝal_ stone (flint) is cursed: its horns are split, and it is reduced to powder under the hands of metalworkers and carpenters.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 543–553: the punitive judgement on _ĝir-zu-ĝal_ (flint).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 543-553

The _iman_ and _alliga_ stones are punished: their cries go unheeded and they are barred from the palace.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 554–565: the punitive judgement on _iman_ and _alliga_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 554-565

The _mašda_, _dubban_, and _urutum_ stones are condemned to be slaughtered, crushed, sharpened for weapons, and battered.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 566–578: the punitive judgement on _mašda_, _dubban_, and _urutum_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 566-578

The _šagara_ stone is punished: it is cast on the dais and fed malt like a sheep, a deliberate lowering of its cultic status.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 579–591: the punitive judgement on _šagara_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 579-591

The _marḫuša_ stone is rewarded: it becomes a filter-bowl for sacred libations and an inlay-work ornament.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 592–599: the honorific judgement on _marḫuša_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 592-599

The _ḫaštum_ stone is renamed for its function: henceforth it is to be known as "Ditch" (_ḫaštum_), its name derived from the trench-like use to which Ninurta assigns it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 600–608: the renaming of _ḫaštum_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 600-608

The _durul_ stone is honoured: Ninurta magnifies its name and decrees that foreign nations shall seek it out.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 609–617: the honorific judgement on _durul_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 609-617

The _šegšeg_, _engen_, and _ezinum_ stones receive a mixed set of judgements in a passage whose imagery is dense and whose outcome varies by stone.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 618–633: the mixed judgements on _šegšeg_, _engen_, and _ezinum_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 618-633

The _kurgaranum_, _bal_, and _šembi_ (kohl) stones are assigned to the cult of Ninḫursaĝa, where they are honoured in her festivals.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 634–644: the judgement on _kurgaranum_, _bal_, and _šembi_.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 634-644

The structural logic of the lapidary aetiology is retributive: stones that took Asag's side are punished, stones that stayed neutral or favoured Ninurta are rewarded, and the destinies decreed map the actual ritual and craft-economic status of each stone-type in historical Mesopotamian practice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–644, as a unit; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the lapidary aetiology's mapping onto Sumerian craft-hierarchies.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

[INFERENCE: The lapidary aetiology is simultaneously a narrative reward-and-punishment sequence and an aetiological explanation of the hardness, colour, and ritual status of individual stone-types as used by Sumerian craftsmen. | basis: The stone-judgements correspond closely to the actual uses of these materials documented in the Mesopotamian craft-record (diorite for statuary, carnelian and lapis for jewelry, flint knapped to powder in metalworking). | risk: The one-to-one correspondence is an interpretive reading; the text itself presents the judgements in mythic register.]

=== Narrative sequence — the triumphant return

After the stone-judgements conclude, Ninurta embarks on the barge Ma-kar-nunta-ea for the return voyage to Nippur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 645–651: the triumphal boarding of the barge and the beginning of the return voyage.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 645-651

The boatmen sing a hymn exalting Ninurta as the unrivalled king of the Anuna (the Sumerian divine assembly).footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 652–661: the boatmen's hymn to Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 652-661

The text then offers a prayer-passage blessing the devotee who maintains Ninurta's temple.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 662–668: the prayer for the devotee.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 662-668

A short invocation calls upon the sky-god An and the healing-goddess Bau to look favourably upon the worshipper.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 669–671: the invocation of An and Bau.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 669-671

The Anuna, the divine assembly, receive Ninurta home with prayers and praise.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 672–680: the Anuna's reception of Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 672-680

Enlil then bestows on Ninurta a final blessing: eternal life, a celestial mace, and an unchanging rule.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 681–697: Enlil's blessing of Ninurta.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 681-697

Ninurta appoints the wise goddess Nisaba as guardian of the granaries and of agricultural order, entrusting her with the stewardship of grain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 698–711: the grain-stewardship entrusted to Nisaba.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 698-711

Extended epithets celebrate Nisaba as judge, keeper of records, and divine administrator — tying the post-combat order into the scribal-administrative life of the Sumerian city.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 712–723: the praise of Nisaba.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 712-723

The composition closes with a brief invocation calling for praise of Ninurta and Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 724–725: the closing doxology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 724-725

The colophon of the composite identifies the poem as a _šir-sud_ (?) to Ninurta — a classification of Sumerian hymnic song whose exact performance context is uncertain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, line 726 (colophon); van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (discussion of the _šir-sud_ classification).]

=== The combat's order-making work

The combat reorders the flow of the Tigris and establishes the irrigation regime that underwrites Sumerian agriculture.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 334–367; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on Ninurta's hydraulic ordering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The combat renames the stone-heap the Mountain (_ḫursaĝ_) and creates the cultic profile of Ninḫursaĝa.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 387–410; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The combat fixes the destinies of the stones, assigning to each stone-type its ritual status, craft-use, and social value within the Sumerian economy.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 411–644; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Ninurta's victory in _Lugal-e_ provides a model of the victorious king that becomes the template for Sumerian and later Akkadian royal ideology of the warrior-ruler.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2 on the royal-legitimation function of the Ninurta narratives.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Gunkel's 1895 thesis already identified the Sumerian Ninurta narratives as important antecedents to the combat-myth pattern he reconstructed for Genesis 1 and Revelation 12, although Gunkel worked from the imperfect textual access of his period.footnote:[Gunkel 1895, Teil I, on Sumerian antecedents; the _Lugal-e_ tradition reached Gunkel primarily through its later bilingual receptions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=gunkel-1895 ; loc=Teil I

The _Creation and Chaos_ volume edited by Scurlock and Beal reassesses Gunkel's thesis for the Mesopotamian material, with essays on the Ninurta corpus that relocate the combat-myth framework within the specific politics and cultic logic of Sumerian and Babylonian religion.footnote:[Scurlock & Beal 2013, chapters on Mesopotamian combat-myth.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=scurlock-beal-2013 ; loc=chapters on Mesopotamian combat-myth

_Lugal-e_ was excerpted into bilingual Sumerian-Akkadian school tablets through the first millennium BCE; this transmission is a reception-history matter and belongs to the footnotes of the narrative chapter rather than to the main account.footnote:[van Dijk 1983, vol. 1 (introduction on first-millennium Akkadian bilingualisation); per the chapter brief, Akkadian bilingual reception is transmission-history, not main narrative.]
