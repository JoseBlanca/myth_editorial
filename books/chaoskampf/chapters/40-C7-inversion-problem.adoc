== C7. The Inversion Problem

=== Source overview

This chapter is the seventh of the eight Part-II comparative chapters and is positioned as the comparative chapter in which the chaoskampf typology's *edges* are tested most rigorously: the cases gathered in §§I–V are the cases at which the standard chaoskampf description either runs in reverse (the slaying of the serpent ends rather than establishes prosperity), is morally inverted (the slaying is hubris and is punished), or is held by area-specialists not to be a chaoskampf reading at all (the _wak'a_-succession reading of the Pariacaca cycle; the metallurgical-allegory reading of the Susanoo / Yamata no Orochi episode).footnote:[Per brief 40-C7-inversion-problem.yaml comparative_argument on the inversion problem as the chapter's organising question; per scope.md §"Subject definition" on the typology's four-subtype operationalisation that Part II tests; per scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" on the area-specialist citation requirement that the present chapter applies more rigorously than any other Part-II comparative chapter.]

=== I. The anchor case — Bida and the covenant-broken framing of the Soninke Wagadu narrative

The Bida narrative is the chapter's anchor case for the inversion problem because the Soninke tradition's covenant-broken framing is best developed in the current English-language scholarship and because the inversion the comparativist sees runs at the level of outcome rather than at the level of the narrative's own categories.

The Soninke / Mande oral-tradition narrative of Bida and Wagadu, recorded across the late-nineteenth- and twentieth-century European-language ethnographic corpus and synthesised in current English-language scholarship by David Conrad's *Empires of Medieval West Africa: Ghana, Mali, and Songhay* (Facts on File, 2005) and Stephen Belcher's *Epic Traditions of Africa* (Indiana University Press, 1999), tells of the seven-headed serpent Bida who is the protector-spirit of the city of Wagadu — the historical western-Sahelian polity known in the Arabic-language chronicle tradition as Ghana, distinct from the modern nation-state of the same name — and to whom the city's prosperity is bound by an annual covenant whose principal stipulation is the delivery of a young woman as tribute, with the city's continued flourishing contingent on the maintenance of the covenant.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the seven-headed serpent Bida as the protector-spirit of Wagadu, on the annual maiden-tribute as the covenant's principal stipulation, and on the covenant's load-bearing function for the city's prosperity; Conrad 2005, *Empires of Medieval West Africa*, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on Wagadu as the medieval western-Sahelian polity (eighth through eleventh-to-twelfth century CE) known in the Arabic-language chronicle tradition as Ghana; Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the narrative's place in the Soninke / Mande oral-epic tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=conrad-west-africa ; loc=Empires of Medieval West Africa, ch. on Ghana/Wagadu legend

The covenant's narrative-structural role within the Soninke tradition's own framing is the load-bearing organising principle of the entire narrative arc, not a peripheral motif: the city of Wagadu exists *because* of the covenant with Bida, the city's prosperity *consists in* the rains that fall and the gold that is dug, and the city's eventual abandonment is presented as the *consequence* of breaking the covenant — the narrative's cosmography is the already-existing Sahelian world of Soninke ethnogeographic memory rather than a cosmogonically-emerging cosmos, and the narrative's own logic is contractual-and-aetiological rather than cosmogonic.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the Soninke oral-tradition's framing of the Wagadu narrative as foundational and aetiological, on the covenant as the narrative's load-bearing organising principle, and on the cosmographic situation of the narrative within the already-existing Sahelian world rather than within a cosmogonic emergence; Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the connected-foundation-story character of the narrative; Conrad 2005, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the contractual-aetiological logic of the narrative's covenant-and-consequence structure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions

The covenant's principal stipulation — the annual delivery of a young Soninke woman to Bida — is not a free-floating dragon-feeding motif but is bound up with the city's specifically-named institutions: the rulers of Wagadu maintain the covenant on the city's behalf, the population of the city contributes to its ritual maintenance, and the maiden delivered each year is identified by name and lineage in the recorded versions, with her selection (and her family's standing) participating in the city's social-political fabric rather than being an anonymous "victim of the dragon" in the European chivalric-romance sense.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the social-political-institutional embedding of the maiden-tribute covenant within Wagadu's named institutions — the rulers of the city as covenant-maintainers, the population as ritual-contributors, and the maiden delivered each year as an individually-named figure with stated lineage in the recorded versions; Conrad 2005, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the institutional embedding of the covenant.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=conrad-west-africa ; loc=Empires of Medieval West Africa, ch. on Ghana/Wagadu legend

The covenant's breaking is precipitated by the suitor Mamadi Sefe Dekote — a figure whose name varies somewhat across the recorded versions (Mamadi Sefe Dekote being the standard form per current Soninke-tradition scholarship in Belcher 1999 and Conrad 2005) — when his beloved is selected as the year's tribute and he, refusing to allow her to be delivered to Bida, ambushes the serpent at the moment of the annual offering and kills him; the narrative's recorded versions specify the manner of the killing (decapitation, with the seven heads severed in succession) and the location (the place of the annual offering, in the city's ritual landscape) with sufficient consistency across the recordings that the killing is securely the narrative's central act, and the narrative arc that follows the killing is the narrative's distinctive feature — not the killing itself, but its consequences.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on Mamadi Sefe Dekote as the suitor whose intervention precipitates the covenant-breaking, on the killing's manner (decapitation of the seven heads in succession) and location (the place of the annual offering in the city's ritual landscape), and on the killing's consistency across the recorded versions; Belcher 1999, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the killing's narrative-structural centrality and on the consequences-of-the-killing as the distinctive feature of the narrative arc; per glossary 'Mamadi Sefe Dekote' first_mention_gloss on the suitor's role in the narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions

The consequences of the killing, in the recorded versions of the Soninke narrative, are catastrophic and explicitly framed as the covenant's broken-state coming due: the rains stop, the springs dry, the gold ceases to be found, and the population of Wagadu disperses across the western Sahel — with the dispersal supplying the narrative's foundation-charter for the Soninke diaspora that current Soninke communities trace to the abandonment of the polity, and with the dryland Sahelian landscape of present-day Mauritania, western Mali, and adjacent regions being read in the tradition as the visible aftermath of the covenant's breaking.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the catastrophic consequences of the covenant-breaking — the cessation of the rains, the drying of the springs, the cessation of the gold, the dispersal of the Soninke population across the western Sahel — and on the consequences' narrative-structural function as the foundation-charter for the Soninke diaspora and as the aetiology of the dryland Sahelian landscape; Conrad 2005, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the same catastrophic-consequences pattern; Belcher 1999, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the foundation-charter and aetiological functions of the consequences-narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=conrad-west-africa ; loc=Empires of Medieval West Africa, ch. on Ghana/Wagadu legend

The narrative's *internal* moral evaluation of the covenant-breaking is, on the evidence of the recorded versions and as Belcher 1999 and Conrad 2005 read them, neither univocally condemnatory of Mamadi Sefe Dekote nor univocally exculpatory: the suitor acts to save his beloved and the narrative grants him the legitimacy of the rescuing-suitor role, but the narrative also registers the destruction of Wagadu as the consequence of his act, with the result that the Soninke tradition's framing presents the dilemma at the heart of the narrative — the conflict between an individual's claim on his beloved and the collective covenant on which the city's prosperity rests — without resolving the dilemma in favour of either claim, and the narrative's didactic register is the registration of the dilemma rather than its adjudication.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the internal moral evaluation of the covenant-breaking in the Soninke tradition's framing — neither univocally condemnatory of Mamadi Sefe Dekote nor univocally exculpatory, with the conflict between the individual's claim and the collective covenant the narrative's load-bearing dilemma; Belcher 1999, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the dilemma-registering rather than dilemma-adjudicating function of the recorded narrative; Conrad 2005, ch. on the Ghana / Wagadu legend, on the same moral-ambivalence pattern in the recorded versions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions

The covenant-broken framing has no need of the chaoskampf category to make sense of itself: the narrative's protagonists, motivations, and consequences are exhausted within the covenant-and-consequence frame, and a hearer who reads the narrative within the Soninke tradition's own moral and aetiological registers does not require any concept of "primordial chaos" or "cosmos-establishing combat" to follow what happens or to take the moral point — the narrative is *complete* within its own categories, and the chaoskampf reading of it is an *additional* analytic move performed from outside the Soninke tradition by a reader whose reading apparatus has been formed elsewhere.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the completeness of the covenant-broken framing within the Soninke tradition's own categories and on the chaoskampf reading as an additional analytic move performed from outside the tradition; Belcher 1999, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the self-sufficiency of the Soninke tradition's framing for the narrative; Ballentine 2015, *The Conflict Myth and the Biblical Tradition*, on the methodological discipline of separating area-specialist framings from comparative-typology overlays.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Soninke traditions
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=on the methodological discipline of separating area-specialist framings from comparative typology

Once the area-specialists' framing is in place, the comparativist's "inverse chaoskampf" reading of the narrative can be registered without subordinating the area-specialists' framing to it: read against the long-arc combat-myth typology that Forsyth 1987 surveys (Sumer through Judeo-Christian apocalyptic), the Bida narrative *does* exhibit a striking inversion of the standard typological outcome — the slaying of the serpent does not establish prosperity, it ends it; the serpent is not the cosmos's adversary, it is the city's protector; and the killing does not deliver order, it delivers the dryland Sahel — and the inversion is structurally legible as soon as the typology is brought to bear, even though the inversion itself is not what the Soninke tradition is *doing* with the narrative on its own terms.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the inversion-of-typology reading as comparativist overlay rather than as Soninke tradition's own framing; Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy*, on the long-arc combat-myth typology against which the inversion is structurally legible; per brief 28-bida-wagadu.yaml special_instructions on the discipline of presenting the Soninke framing first and only then registering the inversion against the comparative typology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=combat-myth long-arc typology (Sumer through Judeo-Christian apocalyptic)

The "inverse chaoskampf" framing is therefore a real comparative observation but also a *modern overlay*: the Soninke griot tradition that transmits the narrative does not need the chaoskampf category to recite or to interpret it, the audiences who hear the narrative within Soninke communities do not encounter it as the inverse of any other narrative they know, and the inversion-reading exists at the level of the typological synthesis that the comparativist performs from the outside — a real reading, but one whose epistemic standing is comparativist-typological rather than tradition-internal.footnote:[Per chapters/28-bida-wagadu.claims.approved.adoc on the inverse-chaoskampf framing as a modern overlay performed at the level of the typological synthesis rather than at the level of the Soninke tradition's own self-understanding; Belcher 1999, ch. on Soninke traditions, on the tradition-internal interpretive resources of the recorded versions; per scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" on the methodological status of comparativist-typological readings as additional moves rather than as primary readings of non-Eurasian materials.]

=== II. The full refusal — Mwindo and the Nyanga maturation-through-hubris framing

The Mwindo / Kirimu episode is the chapter's most pointed area-specialist refusal of the chaoskampf typology's moral-cosmographic apparatus, and the section presents the Nyanga tradition's maturation-through-hubris framing on its own terms before noting that the moral valence of the dragon-killing is the precise contrary of the typology's standard valuation.

The Nyanga oral epic of Mwindo, recorded in 1956 from the named narrator Shé-Kárisi Candi Rureke at Bese on the upper Oso river in the Kivu region of what was then the eastern Belgian Congo and published in Daniel Biebuyck and Kahombo C. Mateene's *The Mwindo Epic from the Banyanga (Zaire)* (University of California Press, 1969) as the standard English-language critical edition and translation, presents the dragon-slaying not as the establishing act of cosmic order or of the hero's sovereignty but as an act of hubristic over-reach for which the sky-god Nkuba punishes the hero — a structural and moral feature of the recorded performance that Biebuyck and Mateene 1969 and Belcher 1999 (*Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Mwindo) both develop as the load-bearing didactic register of the Kirimu episode within the wider epic.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the 1956 Bese recording from Shé-Kárisi Candi Rureke as the chapter's anchor text and on Biebuyck and Mateene 1969 as the standard English-language critical edition and translation; on the dragon-slaying's narrative-structural function as hubristic over-reach that the sky-god Nkuba punishes; Belcher 1999, *Epic Traditions of Africa*, ch. on Mwindo, on the maturation-through-hubris reading as the area-specialist consensus reading of the episode's didactic register; per glossary 'Mwindo', 'Kirimu', 'Nkuba' first_mention_glosses on the principal protagonists.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The Nyanga tradition's own framing of the Mwindo epic — as Biebuyck and Mateene 1969 develop it from the recorded performance and as Belcher 1999 reads it within the comparative African-oral-epic apparatus — is a connected biographical-and-moral narrative of the hero's life, not a cosmogonic narrative: the cosmos is already in place when the epic begins, layered into Nkuba's sky-realm above, Muisa's underworld below, and Iyangura's river-and-water realm laterally; Mwindo's miraculous birth, his disputes with his father Shemwindo (chief of Tubondo, hostile to the son's birth), his journeys across the human and spirit worlds, his testing by the powers above and below, and his eventual chastened return as a properly-formed chief organise the epic from beginning to end, and the hero's biographical-and-moral arc is the compositional centre rather than the establishment of any cosmographic frame.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the Nyanga tradition's framing of the *Mwindo Epic* as a connected biographical-and-moral narrative rather than a cosmogonic narrative, on the layered cosmography (Nkuba's sky-realm above, Muisa's underworld below, Iyangura's river-realm laterally) within which the hero's journeys unfold, and on the hero's biographical-and-moral arc as the compositional centre; Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, translators' introduction on the same biographical-arc framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The middle-epic passages of the recorded performance present Mwindo's accumulating power as *increasingly dangerous*: the hero's aunts and various other figures along the way warn him repeatedly that his power is exceeding what is permitted, the formulaic refrains and direct narrative comments register the powers above and below as watching the hero's career with a view to the eventual moment at which the correction will fall, and the audience is prepared by these passages to read the Kirimu episode when it arrives as the moment for which the foreshadowing pattern has been preparing — not as a triumph but as an act for which the hero will be answerable.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the middle-epic foreshadowing pattern in the recorded performance — formulaic refrains and direct narrative comments registering Mwindo's accumulating power as dangerous, his aunts' warnings, and the announced expectation that the hubris will require correction; on the audience-preparation function of the foreshadowing for the Kirimu episode; Belcher 1999, ch. on Mwindo, on the foreshadowing-of-correction pattern as load-bearing for the epic's middle sections.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

Kirimu — the dragon at the chapter's narrative centre — is presented in the recorded performance as a being of the spirit world whose existence is part of the cosmographic equilibrium between the human and spirit realms, not as a cosmic adversary whose defeat is the cosmos's establishment; the dragon's narrative-structural role is to be the cosmographic counterpart whose presence the human world must respect, and the hero's killing of him is registered in the recorded performance — by formulaic refrains and direct narrative comments — as an *unsanctioned act of human power against the spirit world* rather than as an act of cosmos-ordering.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on Kirimu's narrative-structural role as a being of the spirit world whose existence is part of the cosmographic equilibrium, on the killing's registration in the recorded performance as an unsanctioned act of human power against the spirit world, and on the moral-cosmographic principle (developed in the recorded text's formulaic refrains and direct narrative comments) that such an act demands a counter-act from the powers above to restore the equilibrium; Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, Kirimu episode passages on the same transgression-not-triumph framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The killing itself, in the recorded performance, is completed without any of the order-establishing apparatus that the chaoskampf typology expects at this point: there is no narrative passage in which the killing of the dragon establishes the hero's sovereignty, no passage in which the cosmos is reorganised around the dragon's defeat, no festival celebrating the killing as cosmic-order-establishing — the hero kills the dragon, takes the carcass or pieces of it back toward the human settlements, and the narrative immediately turns to Nkuba's response-passage; the *structural absence* of order-establishing apparatus is itself one of the recorded performance's load-bearing features for the Nyanga tradition's reading of the episode.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the killing's completion with no immediate triumphal conclusion — no sovereignty-establishment passage, no cosmos-reorganisation passage, no order-establishing festival — and on the structural absence of order-establishing apparatus as itself a load-bearing feature of the recorded performance; on the immediate narrative turn from the killing to Nkuba's response.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

Nkuba's response is immediate, deliberate, and morally-motivated: the sky-god descends in lightning, seizes the hero out of the human world, and conveys him by lightning from the forest-and-village landscape upward into the celestial realm, where Mwindo is subjected to a sequence of trials and humiliations — exposure to extreme heat and cold, compulsion to endure the celestial powers' direct presence in a way that strips him of his heroic confidence, and explicit instruction about the limits of his power and the necessity of recognising the cosmographic authority of the powers above — that the recorded performance presents not as arbitrary cruelty but as a structurally-necessary correction of the hero's hubristic over-reach.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on Nkuba's lightning-descent and seizing of the hero, on the celestial-realm trials and humiliations (exposure to extreme heat and cold, compulsion to endure the celestial powers' direct presence, explicit instruction about the limits of the hero's power), and on the moral-didactic register of the punishment as a structurally-necessary correction rather than as arbitrary cruelty; per glossary 'Nkuba' first_mention_gloss on Nkuba as Nyanga sky-god who punishes Mwindo for the killing of Kirimu.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The conditions Nkuba imposes on Mwindo's return to the human world are explicit, named, and binding within the recorded performance's framing: the hero is required to renounce certain practices, to accept the binding force of the cosmographic limits the celestial powers have set, and to commit to a chastened mode of conduct as a properly-formed chief on his return — and the hero's eventual restoration to the forest-and-village landscape is conditional and supervised, registered in the recorded performance as a return on the celestial powers' terms rather than as a triumphant homecoming on the hero's own terms.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the conditions Nkuba imposes on Mwindo's return — explicit, named, and binding renunciations and commitments — and on the hero's restoration to the human world as conditional and supervised rather than as a triumphant homecoming; on the chastened character of the return as structurally load-bearing for the episode's resolution alongside the celestial humiliation itself.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The post-Kirimu narrative continuation in the wider epic presents the chastened hero as at last in a position to assume the chiefly office for which the early-and-middle epic's father-son conflict has been preparing him — the chiefly succession being narratively conditional on the hero's having undergone the celestial humiliation and on his having accepted the cosmographic limits the celestial powers have imposed — and the moral-developmental shape of the epic's overall arc is therefore the *maturation-through-humiliation* pattern that Biebuyck and Mateene 1969 identify as the narrative's didactic kernel and that Belcher 1999 reads as the load-bearing feature distinguishing the Mwindo material from the Eurasian combat-myth corpus.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the post-Kirimu narrative continuation, on the chiefly succession's narrative framing as conditional on the celestial humiliation, and on the maturation-through-humiliation pattern as the narrative's didactic kernel; Biebuyck & Mateene 1969 and Belcher 1999, ch. on Mwindo, on the maturation-through-humiliation reading as the load-bearing feature distinguishing the Mwindo material from the Eurasian combat-myth corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=belcher-epic ; loc=Epic Traditions of Africa, ch. on Mwindo

The maturation-through-hubris framing has, like the covenant-broken framing of §I, no need of the chaoskampf category to make sense of itself: the hero's biographical arc, his accumulation of power, the foreshadowing of correction, the killing as transgressive over-reach, the celestial humiliation, the chastened return, and the chiefly succession are all internal to the Nyanga tradition's own moral-didactic and reciprocal-balance registers, and a hearer who reads the recorded performance within those registers does not require any concept of "primordial chaos" or "cosmos-establishing combat" to follow what happens or to take the moral point — the narrative is *complete* within its own categories, and the chaoskampf reading of the Kirimu episode is an *additional* analytic move performed from outside the Nyanga tradition by a reader whose reading apparatus has been formed elsewhere.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the completeness of the maturation-through-hubris framing within the Nyanga tradition's own categories and on the chaoskampf reading as an additional analytic move performed from outside the tradition; Biebuyck & Mateene 1969, translators' introduction and Kirimu episode passages on the self-sufficiency of the Nyanga tradition's framing for the recorded performance; Ballentine 2015, on the methodological discipline of separating area-specialist framings from comparative-typology overlays.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu
// EVIDENCE: source_id=ballentine-2015 ; loc=on the methodological discipline of separating area-specialist framings from comparative typology

The Mwindo / Kirimu episode is therefore not an *inversion* of the chaoskampf typology in the sense of Bida (where the *outcome* is inverted: slaying ends rather than establishes prosperity), but an *inversion of moral valence*: the killing is presented as hubris rather than as order-making, the dragon's death is registered as a transgression that demands correction rather than as the cosmogonic or sovereignty-establishing act, and the moral evaluation is the precise contrary of the chaoskampf typology's standard valuation of the hero's slaying — and this moral inversion is more pointed than Bida's outcome inversion because it leaves no narrative-structural space for reading the killing as a chaoskampf event whose moral framing has been negotiated downward; the killing is the episode at which a *categorically different* reading of the dragon-killing motif is in play.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the moral-valence inversion of the Mwindo / Kirimu episode relative to the chaoskampf typology — the killing as hubris rather than as order-making, the death as transgression rather than as cosmos-establishment, and the moral evaluation as the precise contrary of the chaoskampf valuation; on the moral inversion as more pointed than Bida's outcome inversion in leaving no narrative-structural space for negotiated chaoskampf reading; per brief 40-C7-inversion-problem.yaml comparative_argument on the Mwindo material as the chapter's case of full refusal of the chaos-order narrative.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=biebuyck-mateene-mwindo ; loc=The Mwindo Epic, episode of Kirimu

The chapter's reading of the Mwindo material as a *refusal* of the chaoskampf typology's moral-cosmographic apparatus, rather than as a member of an "inverse chaoskampf" subtype within that apparatus, follows the area-specialist tradition that Biebuyck and Mateene 1969 anchor and that Belcher 1999 develops within the comparative African-oral-epic apparatus: the Nyanga tradition's framing — maturation-through-hubris, reciprocal balance between human and spirit worlds, the sky-god's authority over the hero — is the tradition's own categorical apparatus, and reading the Kirimu episode as the inverse of a Eurasian combat-myth pattern would be to subordinate the Nyanga categorical apparatus to a comparative typology developed for materials whose categorical apparatus is structurally different.footnote:[Per chapters/29-mwindo-kirimu.claims.approved.adoc on the area-specialist reading of the Mwindo material as refusing the chaoskampf typology's moral-cosmographic apparatus rather than fitting within it as an "inverse chaoskampf" instance; Biebuyck & Mateene 1969 and Belcher 1999 on the Nyanga tradition's categorical apparatus (maturation-through-hubris, reciprocal balance, sky-god authority); per scope.md §"Appropriation protocol" on the methodological status of comparativist-typological readings as additional moves rather than as primary readings of non-Eurasian materials.]

=== III. The partial case — Sigurd, the IE formula, and the curse on the gold

Sigurd is the chapter's partial case, sitting on the edge of the IE-comparative tradition where Watkins's philological reading of the killing-stroke and the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle pull the typology in different directions at different analytic levels.

The Old Norse Eddic Sigurd-cycle, preserved principally in the *Fáfnismál* of the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE) — the second of the three closely-grouped Sigurd-cycle poems in the Codex (with *Reginsmál* preceding and *Sigrdrífumál* following) — and cited in the present chapter through Carolyne Larrington's revised Oxford World's Classics translation of *The Poetic Edda* (Oxford University Press, 2014), presents a partial case for the inversion problem: Watkins 1995 reads the *Fáfnismál*'s diction as the strongest philological reflex of the reconstructed Indo-European poetic formula HERO SLAY SERPENT in the entire Indo-European corpus, but the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle complicates the order-establishing-by-combat reading that the formula's surface structure would suggest.footnote:[Per chapters/19-ie-reflexes.claims.approved.adoc on the *Fáfnismál* (Codex Regius GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE) as the principal primary text for the Sigurd-Fáfnir narrative and on Larrington 2014 (rev. Oxford World's Classics) as the chapter's standard English-language translation; Watkins 1995, *How to Kill a Dragon: Aspects of Indo-European Poetics*, ch. 42 on the *Fáfnismál* diction as the strongest philological reflex of the reconstructed IE poetic formula HERO SLAY SERPENT in the entire IE corpus; per brief 40-C7-inversion-problem.yaml structure on Sigurd as the chapter's partial case at which the IE-formula reading and the long-arc tragedy of the cycle pull in different directions.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=ch. 42
// EVIDENCE: source_id=larrington-poetic-edda ; loc=Fáfnismál

The narrative core of the *Fáfnismál* is the killing of the dragon Fáfnir by the young hero Sigurd, son of Sigmund of the Völsung lineage and foster-son of the smith Regin (Fáfnir's brother): Sigurd ambushes the dragon by digging a pit on the dragon's path to the water, lying in wait below, and thrusting upward into Fáfnir's underbelly with the sword Gram (forged by Regin from the fragments of Sigmund's broken sword) as the dragon passes over the pit; the killing-stroke is delivered as the central act around which the poem's dialogue is organised, with the bulk of the *Fáfnismál* given over to the post-killing wisdom-dialogue between the dying dragon and the hero rather than to extended combat-narrative.footnote:[Per chapters/19-ie-reflexes.claims.approved.adoc on the *Fáfnismál*'s narrative core — Sigurd's lineage and Regin's foster-role, the pit-and-ambush mechanism, the upward thrust with Gram into Fáfnir's underbelly, and the central placement of the killing-stroke around which the poem's dialogue is organised; Larrington 2014, *Fáfnismál* opening and middle stanzas, on the killing-stroke and on the post-killing wisdom-dialogue's bulk.]

But the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle complicates the surface reading: the gold of Andvari that Loki extorts to pay Hreidmar's wergild for the killing of Otr (Hreidmar's son and Fáfnir's and Regin's brother) is *cursed* by Andvari at the moment of the extortion, and the curse drives the entire downstream cycle — the death of Hreidmar at Fáfnir's hands, Fáfnir's transformation into a dragon by his obsessive guardianship of the cursed gold, Regin's treachery toward Sigurd, Sigurd's eventual death at the hands of Gunnar's brother Gutthorm in the Burgundian episode, Brynhild's death, and the wider Burgundian disasters — and the killing of the dragon, far from establishing Sigurd's prosperity, is the *acquisition of the curse* that destroys him and the people around him.footnote:[Per chapters/19-ie-reflexes.claims.approved.adoc on the long-arc tragedy of the Sigurd-cycle from *Reginsmál* through *Fáfnismál*, *Sigrdrífumál*, and the Burgundian poems to Sigurd's death — the curse on Andvari's gold as the load-bearing motif driving the cycle from the killing of the dragon to Sigurd's own death and the Burgundian disasters; Larrington 2014, *Reginsmál* and *Fáfnismál* on the curse's establishment; on the killing as the acquisition of the curse rather than the establishment of prosperity.]

=== IV. The area-specialist refusal — Pariacaca, the _amaru_, and the _wak'a_-succession framing

The Pariacaca cycle is the chapter's case of categorical area-specialist refusal short of inversion: the Andean-studies tradition's _wak'a_-succession framing employs a conceptual apparatus that the chaoskampf typology can only register as an absence rather than as an inversion.

The early-colonial Andean *Huarochirí Manuscript*, a Quechua-language compilation conventionally dated to approximately 1608 CE and held at the Biblioteca Nacional de España in Madrid as MS 3169 within materials connected to the early-seventeenth-century anti-idolatry campaign of the Cuzco-born secular priest Francisco de Ávila in the Lima archdiocese, preserves the principal Andean narrative cycle within the present book's scope: the cycle of the storm-_wak'a_ Pariacaca, born as a clutch of falcon-eggs on Mount Condorcoto, who opposes and defeats the rival _wak'a_ Huallallo Carhuincho and a sequence of further rivals across the Huarochirí region's high-altitude landscape, with the serpentine _amaru_ figuring across the cycle as an ally-monster summoned by defeated rivals rather than as a primordial cosmic adversary.footnote:[Per chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.claims.approved.adoc on the *Huarochirí Manuscript* (c. 1608 CE; Biblioteca Nacional de España, Madrid, MS 3169) as the chapter's principal primary source, on the Pariacaca cycle's distribution across chs. 6, 8, and 21 of the manuscript, on Pariacaca's birth as a clutch of falcon-eggs on Mount Condorcoto, and on the *amaru* as ally-monster summoned by defeated rivals rather than as a primordial cosmic adversary; Salomon and Urioste 1991, *The Huarochirí Manuscript: A Testament of Ancient and Colonial Andean Religion* (Austin: University of Texas Press, 1991; ISBN 9780292730533), translators' introduction and chs. 6, 8, 21, on the same; per glossary 'Pariacaca', 'amaru', 'wak'a' first_mention_glosses on the principal terms.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 6, 8, 21 (Pariacaca and amaru passages)

The area-specialist reading of the cycle, anchored in the present book by Salomon and Urioste 1991 and developed within the wider current Andean-studies scholarly tradition by Frank Salomon and George Urioste themselves (in their 1991 critical edition's translators' introduction), Gérald Taylor in his Lima IFEA critical edition of the *Huarochirí Manuscript* (named in body prose at Ch26 of the present book per the named-but-flagged secondary-anchor pattern), and Carolyn Dean on Andean ritual-political imagination (named in body prose at Ch26 per the same pattern), is _wak'a_-succession: the cycle's narrative work is the displacement of one hegemonic regional _wak'a_ (Huallallo Carhuincho) by another (Pariacaca) within an already-existing _wak'a_-cosmography rather than the establishment of cosmic order ex novo from the primordial cosmos-versus-disorder opposition that the chaoskampf typology presupposes.footnote:[Per chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.claims.approved.adoc on the area-specialist reading of the Pariacaca cycle as wak'a-succession rather than as cosmogonic combat, on the named area-specialists Salomon, Urioste, Taylor, and Dean as supplying the reading, and on the wak'a-succession framing as the area-specialist consensus reading; Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction on the wak'a-succession reading; per glossary 'wak'a' first_mention_gloss on the wak'a-succession-not-chaos-versus-order pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction

The _wak'a_-succession framing differs from both the Bida outcome-inversion (§I) and the Mwindo moral-valence-inversion (§II) in that it is *not an inversion at all* but a different combat-myth pattern: the Pariacaca cycle does narrate combat between named divine figures (Pariacaca's five-day flood-and-storm engagement with Huallallo Carhuincho in chapter 8 of the manuscript; the engagements with the _amaru_-summoning rivals in subsequent chapters) and a _wak'a_-political reorganisation does follow from the combat-outcome (Pariacaca takes possession of the eponymous mountain peak; his five sons or brothers take possession of subsidiary peaks and ritual centres; Huallallo Carhuincho's former sphere is reorganised under Pariacaca's authority), but the combat's narrative work is the reorganisation of an already-existing _wak'a_-cosmos rather than the establishment of a cosmos from a primordial disorder.footnote:[Per chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.claims.approved.adoc on the wak'a-succession framing's structural difference from both outcome-inversion and moral-valence-inversion; on the Pariacaca-Huallallo five-day combat in ch. 8 of the manuscript, on the post-combat geographical settlement (Pariacaca's possession of the eponymous mountain peak, his five sons or brothers taking subsidiary peaks and ritual centres, the reorganisation of Huallallo's former sphere under Pariacaca's authority), and on the combat's narrative work as wak'a-political reorganisation rather than as cosmos-establishment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 8

The _amaru_'s narrative role in the cycle is itself the area-specialists' principal evidence for the _wak'a_-succession reading rather than for any chaoskampf reading: the _amaru_ figures across the cycle as an ally-monster summoned by a defeated rival _wak'a_ in the course of further engagements, with the _amaru_'s narrative function being the discharge of the defeated _wak'a_'s residual combat-capacity rather than the embodiment of a primordial cosmic adversary, and with the _amaru_'s defeat by Pariacaca (or by his sons) typically being a follow-on to the principal _wak'a_-combat rather than the cycle's central engagement.footnote:[Per chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.claims.approved.adoc on the *amaru*'s narrative role as ally-monster summoned by a defeated rival, on the *amaru*'s function as discharge of the defeated *wak'a*'s residual combat-capacity, and on the *amaru*'s defeat as a follow-on to the principal *wak'a*-combat; per glossary 'amaru' first_mention_gloss on the *amaru* as ally-monster of a defeated rival *wak'a* rather than as primordial chaos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 8 and following

The _amaru_'s defeat is itself patterned differently from the kill-and-eradicate pattern of much Eurasian chaoskampf material: the manuscript narrates the _amaru_'s defeat as a cutting, splitting, or driving-into-a-confined-space rather than as comprehensive destruction, with the defeated _amaru_'s body becoming a feature of the landscape (a lake's outline, a watercourse's bend, a stone formation) rather than being reduced to nothing — a pattern that, like the displacement-rather-than-killing of Huallallo Carhuincho, distinguishes the Andean material from the kill-and-eradicate chaoskampf-typology pattern at a load-bearing structural level.footnote:[Per chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.claims.approved.adoc on the *amaru*'s defeat as cutting, splitting, or driving-into-a-confined-space rather than comprehensive destruction; on the defeated *amaru*'s body becoming a feature of the landscape (a lake's outline, a watercourse's bend, a stone formation); on the parallel between this displacement-rather-than-killing pattern and the wider cycle's wak'a-succession-rather-than-eradication framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 8 and following

The Pariacaca case's significance for the inversion-problem chapter is therefore methodologically distinctive: it is *not* an inversion case (the killing does not establish prosperity that is then inverted; the moral valence is not the precise contrary of the chaoskampf reading; the IE-formula reading does not apply) but a case in which the area-specialists' framing does not employ the disorder-versus-order opposition that the chaoskampf typology presupposes, and the comparative observation the chapter takes from §IV is that *not all area-specialist refusals are inversions* — some refusals are categorical, in the sense that the area-specialists' framing employs a different conceptual apparatus (_wak'a_-succession; landscape-reorganisation rather than cosmos-establishment) that the chaoskampf typology can only register as an absence rather than as an inversion.footnote:[Per chapters/26-pariacaca-amaru.claims.approved.adoc on the Pariacaca case's methodological distinctiveness as a categorical area-specialist refusal rather than as an inversion case; per brief 40-C7-inversion-problem.yaml structure on Pariacaca as the chapter's case at which the area-specialist framing does not employ the disorder-versus-order opposition that the chaoskampf typology presupposes; on the comparative observation that not all area-specialist refusals are inversions.]

=== V. The metallurgical case — Susanoo, Yamata no Orochi, and the sword-etiology framing

The Susanoo / Yamata no Orochi episode is the chapter's strongest area-specialist refusal, registering an objection not at the level of outcome or moral valence but at the level of the combat-myth framing itself in favour of metallurgical allegory and sword-etiology.
