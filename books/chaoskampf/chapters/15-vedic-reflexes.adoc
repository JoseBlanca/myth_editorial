== Vedic and Post-Vedic Reflexes (Trita Āptya, Krishna/Kāliya)

=== Source overview

This chapter takes up two clearly-distinct Indian dragon-combat sub-cases that bracket the Indra-Vṛtra flagship of the preceding chapter: the older Indo-Iranian Trita Āptya stratum, attested in a small cluster of Rigvedic hymns, and the bhakti-era Krishna-Kāliya reworking, attested in the *Harivaṃśa*, the *Viṣṇu Purāṇa*, and the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa*.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml; Jamison and Brereton 2014 on the Trita Āptya material in the Rigvedic corpus; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long arc of Indian dragon-combat material from the Rigveda to the bhakti-period reworkings.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Trita Āptya material

The chapter's primary text for the Trita Āptya material is the Rigveda (Sanskrit Ṛgveda), the same Vedic Sanskrit corpus that supplies the Indra-Vṛtra material of the preceding chapter, cited here at hymn-and-verse granularity (RV book.hymn.verse) keyed to Stephanie Jamison and Joel Brereton's three-volume *The Rigveda: The Earliest Religious Poetry of India*, published by Oxford University Press in 2014.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, *The Rigveda: The Earliest Religious Poetry of India*, three-volume Oxford translation, ISBN 9780199370184, used as the working English text for all Rigvedic material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=publisher and edition

The Trita Āptya material is distributed across the Rigvedic corpus in hymn-allusive rather than narrative-continuous form, and the chapter's working textual base for the figure is the small cluster of hymns in which Trita is named as a combat-figure: principally RV 10.99.6 (the standard locus for Trita's slaying of a three-headed adversary) and RV 1.105 and RV 2.11.19–20, in which Trita appears alongside Indra in the combat-context.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the Trita Āptya material in RV 10.99.6, 1.105, and 2.11.19–20; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml, sources.primary, listing the Trita-cluster hymns.]

Jamison and Brereton's translation is printed-only in the present project's working corpus, and the chapter cites it at hymn-and-verse level for specific Trita passages and at section level for general points about the corpus, in line with the citation discipline applied throughout the Iran-and-India cluster of the present book.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, three-volume printed edition; cited at hymn-and-verse level for specific RV passages and at section level for corpus-wide points, per the established discipline of chapters 13 and 14.]

The primary texts for the Krishna-Kāliya material are three post-Vedic Sanskrit narrative compositions: the *Harivaṃśa* (the earliest fully-narrative witness, conventionally dated to roughly the 4th century CE and transmitted as an appendix to the *Mahābhārata*), the *Viṣṇu Purāṇa* (a 5th-century CE Vaishnava Purāṇa whose book 5 contains the Krishna boyhood narrative), and the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* (a 9th- to 10th-century CE Vaishnava Purāṇa whose book 10 chapters 15 to 17 supply the most famous and iconographically pervasive version of the episode).footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the post-Vedic Indian dragon-combat reworkings including the bhakti-era Krishna-Kāliya material; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml, family_members entry on the Krishna-Kāliya sources.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Krishna-Kāliya primary texts are printed-only Sanskrit compositions whose standard English translations include the H. H. Wilson nineteenth-century *Viṣṇu Purāṇa*, the Motilal Banarsidass Tagare translation of the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa*, and various twentieth-century editions of the *Harivaṃśa*; per brief, edition-pinning is deferred to the glossary-lock stage and the chapter cites at canto-and-chapter level rather than at sub-page granularity.footnote:[Brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml shared_variant_handling, on edition-pinning deferred to glossary-lock; cited at canto-and-chapter level per the established discipline of the present book.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

Neil Forsyth's *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth*, published by Princeton University Press in 1987, supplies the chapter's wider scholarly anchor for the long arc of Indian dragon-combat material from the Rigveda to the bhakti-era reworkings, with chapter 2 treating the Indo-Iranian combat-myth corpus and its post-Vedic transformations.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, *The Old Enemy: Satan and the Combat Myth*, ch. 2 on the Indo-Iranian combat-myth corpus and its post-Vedic transformations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The chapter is a family-chapter (related-tier) treatment, organising two distinct Indian dragon-combat sub-cases by their position in the long arc of the tradition — pre-Indra Indo-Iranian (Trita Āptya) and post-Vedic bhakti-era (Krishna-Kāliya) — rather than by giving each a thin full chapter, and the two sub-cases are treated in clearly-bounded sub-sections that are not merged at the level of either narrative content or doctrinal framing.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml, family-chapter framing with two sub-cases organised by position in the long arc of the Indian tradition; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The framing premise is that the Indian tradition preserves alongside the central Indra-Vṛtra combat (chapter 14 of the present volume) two productive variations on the dragon-combat pattern: one older and partially displaced by Indra (Trita Āptya), the other later and devotionally transformed into a non-lethal subjugation of the serpent by the child-hero Krishna (Krishna-Kāliya). The two variations bracket the Indra-Vṛtra flagship in the historical-genealogical depth of the tradition.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the bracketing framing; Jamison and Brereton 2014 on Trita Āptya as the older displaced figure; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the bhakti-era reworking.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Trita Āptya material

What follows takes the two sub-cases in turn: first the Trita Āptya material in its Rigvedic and Indo-Iranian setting, then the Krishna-Kāliya episode in its post-Vedic Vaishnava setting, and finally a closing section on the family pattern that links the two and on the forward links to the comparative chapters of the present book.

=== Trita Āptya and the three-headed serpent

Trita Āptya (Sanskrit त्रित आप्त्य) is a Vedic combat-figure attested in the Rigveda as the slayer of a three-headed adversary, and the figure's name carries two distinct elements: a personal name (Trita, meaning "third") and a patronymic-or-toponymic epithet (Āptya, conventionally read as "of the waters" or "son of Āpta").footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Trita Āptya as a Vedic combat-figure with the personal name "Trita" and the epithet "Āptya"; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the etymology of the two name-elements.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Trita Āptya hymns

By frequency of attestation Trita Āptya is a marginal figure in the Rigvedic corpus: he is named in only a small cluster of hymns, and the hymns that name him generally do so in passing rather than as the principal subject of the hymn, in marked contrast to the more than two hundred Indra-hymns that dominate the Rigveda's combat-material.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the marginal frequency of Trita Āptya in the Rigvedic corpus relative to Indra; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on Trita's marginality.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Trita Āptya hymns

The standard Rigvedic locus for Trita's slaying of a three-headed adversary is RV 10.99.6, in which the hymn refers to the killing of a three-headed and seven-rayed (or seven-rein-bearing) adversary in language that the philological tradition consistently reads as a Trita-stratum combat-allusion.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 10.99.6 as the standard Rigvedic locus for Trita's slaying of a three-headed adversary; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the same locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 10.99.6

The three-headed adversary of RV 10.99.6 is morphologically and structurally cognate with the three-headed and three-mouthed Aži Dahāka of the Avestan corpus treated in chapter 13 of the present volume: both are tricephalous serpent-or-monstrous adversaries, both are slain by an Indo-Iranian dragon-slayer figure, and the cognancy is one of the principal Indo-Iranian comparative observations of the chapter.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the cognancy of the three-headed adversary across the Vedic Trita and Avestan Thraētaona witnesses; Watkins 1995, Part V (chs. 44–45), on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

[INFERENCE: the identification of the three-headed adversary of RV 10.99.6 specifically with the Indo-Iranian three-headed-serpent cognate of Avestan Aži Dahāka, while standard in Benveniste-Renou 1934 and Watkins 1995, is a comparative-philological reading rather than an unmediated feature of the Rigvedic text itself; the Rigvedic hymn does not name its three-headed adversary or supply a continuous narrative of the killing, and the cognancy claim is inferred by reading RV 10.99.6 alongside the Avestan corpus and the wider Indo-Iranian combat-material. | source: Jamison and Brereton 2014 (Rigvedic philology); Benveniste and Renou 1934 (Trita-Thraētaona discussion); Watkins 1995 Part V chs. 44–45 (Indo-European poetic family) | scope: comparative-philological identification of the RV 10.99.6 adversary with the Indo-Iranian three-headed-serpent type, flagged for transparency.]

RV 1.105 is a long Rigvedic hymn in the first *maṇḍala* in which Trita appears as a co-witness alongside Indra in the combat-context, and the hymn is one of the principal Rigvedic loci for the joint Trita-and-Indra combat-pattern that the philological tradition reads as the textual residue of the Trita-pre-Indra stratum.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 1.105 as the principal first-*maṇḍala* locus for the joint Trita-and-Indra combat-pattern; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the Trita-pre-Indra stratum.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.105

RV 2.11.19–20 is a passage in the second *maṇḍala* in which Trita appears alongside Indra in the combat-context, with the two figures named together as participants in the killing of the serpent-adversary; the passage is the principal second-*maṇḍala* locus for the same joint pattern that RV 1.105 attests in the first *maṇḍala*.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on RV 2.11.19–20 as the principal second-*maṇḍala* locus for the joint Trita-and-Indra pattern; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the second-*maṇḍala* attestations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 2.11.19-20

In the joint Trita-and-Indra passages of RV 1.105 and RV 2.11.19–20 Trita is consistently the junior partner: where the verbal subject of the killing is named, it is more often Indra than Trita; where the two are named together, Trita is grammatically and ritually subordinated to the storm-god; and the textual situation has the appearance of a layer of inherited Trita-material in the process of being absorbed into the Indra-centred Rigvedic ritual mythology.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Trita as the junior partner of Indra in RV 1.105 and 2.11.19–20; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the absorption of inherited Trita-material into the Indra-centred mythology.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=RV 1.105; 2.11.19-20

The Vedic textual situation of Trita Āptya is therefore the clearest evidence within the Rigveda itself that the Trita-figure is older than the Indra-centred ritual mythology of the corpus: an older combat-figure whose adversary-killing has been partially redistributed onto Indra and whose own attestations are residual rather than central, in the form that Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph articulates as the principal Indo-Iranian observation about the figure.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on Trita Āptya as the older Indo-Iranian combat-figure partially displaced by Indra; Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the residual character of the Trita-attestations in the Rigvedic corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The Trita Āptya / Thraētaona cognancy is the principal Indo-Iranian observation about the Trita-figure: the Vedic Trita Āptya and the Avestan Thraētaona are philologically the same name in the two languages, and Benveniste and Renou's 1934 monograph treats the two figures as joint witnesses to a shared Indo-Iranian dragon-slayer of the pre-storm-god phase.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the philological cognancy of Vedic Trita Āptya with Avestan Thraētaona; Watkins 1995, Part V (chs. 44–45), on the same cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The cognancy is documented at the level of the two figures' names: both names are formed from the Indo-Iranian root for "third" plus a patronymic-or-toponymic suffix, and the regular phonological correspondences between Vedic Sanskrit and Avestan are visible across the pair (Vedic *tr-* / Avestan *thr-*; Vedic *-i-* / Avestan *-aē-*; Vedic *-a* / Avestan *-aona*).footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the phonological correspondences across the Trita / Thraētaona pair; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the regular sound-laws of the cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The cognancy is also documented at the level of the two figures' adversaries: both slay a three-headed serpent-or-monstrous adversary, the Vedic adversary unnamed in the surviving hymn-allusions and the Avestan adversary named as Aži Dahāka (the "dragon-Dahāka"), and the structural identity of the two killings reinforces the philological cognancy of the names.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the structural identity of the Trita and Thraētaona killings; Watkins 1995, Part V (chs. 44–45), on the three-headed-adversary structural cognancy.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The Trita-Thraētaona cognancy is therefore a documented Indo-Iranian inheritance at both the lexical-philological level (the names) and the narrative-structural level (the three-headed-adversary killing), and the pair is one of the load-bearing exhibits for the existence of an Indo-Iranian phase of the dragon-combat material before the divergence of the Indic and Iranian branches.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the joint lexical-and-structural cognancy of the pair as evidence for the Indo-Iranian phase; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the same evidential function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

Trita Āptya's epithet *Āptya* is conventionally derived from the Sanskrit *ap-* ("water"), and the figure's name is therefore read as "Trita of the Waters" — an etymological reading that aligns the Vedic figure with the storm-god's water-release combat-pattern even though the surviving hymn-allusions do not narrate the water-release explicitly in the Trita-passages.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on the etymological derivation of *Āptya* from *ap-* ("water"); Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the same derivation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Trita Āptya etymology

[INFERENCE: the alignment of the *Āptya* epithet with the storm-god's water-release combat-pattern is a comparative reading drawn from the wider Indo-European storm-god / water-dragon complex (West 2007 ch. 6) and from the Vedic ritual frame of the Indra-hymns (Jamison and Brereton 2014), rather than a continuous-narrative attestation in the Trita-passages themselves; the Rigvedic hymn-allusions to Trita do not say in so many words that Trita's killing releases the waters. | source: Jamison and Brereton 2014 (Trita-passage philology); West 2007 ch. 6 (IE storm-god / water-dragon complex) | scope: alignment of Trita's *Āptya* epithet with the wider water-release pattern, flagged for transparency.]

The Trita-figure is also associated in the Rigvedic corpus with the *soma* sacrifice: in some hymn-allusions Trita is presented as a *soma*-presser, a participant in the *soma*-ritual, and a recipient of *soma*-offerings, and the ritual association reinforces the figure's inheritance from the Indo-Iranian *soma*-ritual complex that the Vedic and Avestan traditions share.footnote:[Jamison and Brereton 2014, on Trita's association with the *soma* sacrifice in the Rigvedic corpus; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the Indo-Iranian *soma*-ritual inheritance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jamison-brereton-2014 ; loc=Trita Āptya hymns

Both the Trita-figure's association with the *soma* sacrifice and his association with the dragon-killing are inherited Indo-Iranian features: both the *soma*-ritual complex and the three-headed-serpent killing have Avestan reflexes (the *haoma* ritual and the Thraētaona / Aži Dahāka combat respectively), and the joint association of *soma* and dragon-killing in the Vedic Trita-figure is consistent with the joint Indo-Iranian inheritance.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the joint Indo-Iranian inheritance of *soma* / *haoma* ritual and dragon-killing; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the same joint inheritance.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Indo-Iranian discussion

In Watkins's reading in *How to Kill a Dragon* Part V, the Trita-figure is one of the principal Vedic exhibits for the reconstructed Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT poetic formula, alongside Indra: where Indra supplies the most thoroughly-attested Vedic instance of the formula, Trita supplies an older instance whose pre-Indra stratum is the chief evidential value of the figure for the Indo-European reconstruction.footnote:[Watkins 1995, Part V, on Trita and Indra as joint Vedic exhibits for the HERO SLAY SERPENT formula, with Trita supplying the older pre-Indra stratum.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The pre-Indra Indo-Iranian stratum thesis holds that the Indo-Iranian phase of the dragon-combat tradition was organised around a Trita / Thraētaona-type figure whose Indic reflexes were partially displaced when the storm-god Indra emerged as the principal warrior-god of the Vedic ritual mythology, and that the Rigvedic Trita-attestations are the textual residue of that displacement.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the pre-Indra Indo-Iranian stratum thesis; Watkins 1995, Part V (chs. 44–45), on the same thesis; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the load-bearing status of the thesis for the chapter.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The thesis is supported on the Vedic side by the residual character of the Trita-attestations and on the Avestan side by the unbroken centrality of the Thraētaona-figure in the Iranian tradition: where Trita is marginal in the Indic record because Indra has taken his place, Thraētaona remains central in the Iranian record because no comparable storm-god displacement occurred in the Iranian branch.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the asymmetric trajectories of the Trita and Thraētaona figures across the two Indo-Iranian branches; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the same asymmetry.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The asymmetric trajectory of the Trita-figure within the Indic tradition is itself a piece of evidence for the structural-typological place of the storm-god in the Vedic ritual mythology: where the storm-god (Indra) takes the centre, the older dragon-slayer (Trita) becomes residual; where the storm-god is differently positioned (the Iranian case), the older dragon-slayer (Thraētaona) remains central.footnote:[West 2007, ch. 6, on the structural-typological place of the storm-god in the Indo-European traditions; Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the Vedic-Avestan asymmetry as evidence for the storm-god's structural function.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=west-2007 ; loc=ch. 6

The Trita-figure's residual character in the Vedic tradition is also visible in the post-Rigvedic Sanskrit literature: Trita largely disappears from the Brāhmaṇa, Upaniṣadic, and Epic literature as a combat-figure, with only occasional references in cosmogonic-and-mythological catalogues, and the figure does not survive into the bhakti-era devotional literature in any prominent form.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the post-Rigvedic disappearance of Trita as a combat-figure; Jamison and Brereton 2014 on the residual character of the Trita-attestations even within the Rigvedic corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The near-disappearance of the Trita-figure from the post-Rigvedic Sanskrit literature is the long-arc end-state of the same displacement that the Rigvedic textual situation already attests in mid-process: by the bhakti-era the combat-pole of the Indian dragon-tradition has migrated entirely off the Trita-figure (onto Indra in the Vedic phase, then onto Krishna in the bhakti phase), and the older Indo-Iranian figure survives only as a philological exhibit rather than as a living religious figure.footnote:[Benveniste and Renou 1934, on the long-arc displacement of Trita; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the post-Rigvedic disappearance of the figure.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Trita-figure's principal value for the present chapter is therefore evidential rather than narrative: the chapter's working narrative-spine of the Vedic dragon-combat is the Indra-Vṛtra combat of the preceding chapter, and the Trita-figure is treated here as the older Indo-Iranian witness whose textual residue confirms the depth and the inheritance-pattern of the combat-tradition behind the Indra-centred Rigvedic mythology.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the evidential value of the Trita-material; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the Trita-Thraētaona cognancy as load-bearing for the comparative argument.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The Trita Āptya material is therefore the chapter's principal exhibit for the Indo-Iranian phase of the combat-myth, and its forward-link is to Part II C5 of the present book (the comparative chapter on the Indo-European HERO SLAY SERPENT formula), in which the Trita-Thraētaona cognancy joins the Vedic Indra-Vṛtra material, the Avestan Aži Dahāka material, and the Hittite, Greek, Norse, and Italic comparanda in the consolidated Indo-European argument.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the forward-link to Part II C5; Watkins 1995, Part V, on the consolidated Indo-European argument.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=watkins-1995 ; loc=Part V

The Trita Āptya material is also the chapter's principal back-link to chapter 13 of the present volume (the Avestan Aži Dahāka chapter), in which Thraētaona's combat is treated in its Avestan and post-Avestan forms, and the Trita-Thraētaona cognancy is the load-bearing pair across the Indo-Iranian arc of the present book.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the back-link to chapter 13; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the cognancy as the load-bearing Indo-Iranian pair.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The Trita Āptya material is also a back-link to chapter 14 of the present volume (the Indra-Vṛtra chapter), in which the Trita-pre-Indra-stratum reading is briefly anticipated; the present chapter develops the reading at the level of the specific Trita-cluster hymns that the Indra-Vṛtra chapter only references in passing.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the back-link to chapter 14; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the anticipation of the Trita-pre-Indra-stratum reading.]

=== Krishna and Kāliya — bhakti-era reworking

The Krishna-Kāliya episode is a post-Vedic Vaishnava devotional reworking of the Indian dragon-combat tradition, conventionally dated by its earliest fully-narrative witness (the *Harivaṃśa*) to roughly the 4th century CE and consolidated in its most famous and iconographically pervasive form in the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* of the 9th to 10th century CE; the episode is therefore separated from the Rigvedic combat-corpus by more than a thousand years and by a major shift in the religious culture of the subcontinent, and it is treated here as a clearly-bounded post-Vedic transformation rather than as an alternative phrasing of the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra combat.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the post-Vedic Vaishnava devotional reworking of the Indian combat-tradition; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml special_instructions on the no-merge rule with the Rigvedic combat.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The principal narrative occasion of the Krishna-Kāliya episode is the boyhood of the divine child Krishna in the cowherd-pastoral setting of Vraja (Brindavan), where the child-hero confronts the multi-headed serpent Kāliya (Sanskrit कालिय), a *nāga* who has poisoned a deep pool of the Yamunā river and rendered its waters undrinkable for the cowherds and the cattle of the locality.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, on the Vraja-pastoral setting and Kāliya's poisoning of the Yamunā pool; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the episode.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

Kāliya is described in the Vaishnava Purāṇic tradition as a many-headed serpent — the head-count varies across sources from five to seven to one hundred and ten, with the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* version generally giving him a hundred and ten heads in some manuscripts — whose venom has poisoned the deep pool of the Yamunā in which he has taken up residence, killing the birds that fly over the pool and the cattle that drink at its margins.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, on Kāliya's venom-poisoning of the Yamunā pool and the head-count in the Bhāgavata version; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing and head-count variation across sources.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

The setting of the Kāliya pool is the river Yamunā, the major north-Indian river that flows past Brindavan and the cowherd-villages of Vraja in the geographical-religious imagination of the bhakti-era Vaishnava tradition, and the localisation of the combat at a specific reach of a specific named river (rather than at a cosmologically-charged abstract setting) is one of the principal differences between the bhakti-era reworking and the Rigvedic combat-tradition.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 ch. 15, on the Yamunā setting of the Kāliya pool; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the specific-river localisation as a distinguishing feature.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15

The earliest fully-narrative witness to the Krishna-Kāliya episode is the *Harivaṃśa*, a Sanskrit text conventionally dated to roughly the 4th century CE and transmitted as an appendix (the *khila* or "supplement") to the *Mahābhārata*, in which the Krishna boyhood narrative is given its earliest extended treatment, including the Kāliya-subjugation episode within the wider boyhood-feats sequence.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the *Harivaṃśa* as the earliest fully-narrative witness to the Krishna-Kāliya episode; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml family_members entry on the same dating.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Viṣṇu Purāṇa*, conventionally dated to roughly the 5th century CE, is the second principal narrative witness to the Krishna-Kāliya episode, with book 5 of the Purāṇa presenting an extended Krishna boyhood narrative that includes the Kāliya-subjugation episode as one of the load-bearing demonstrations of the divine child's power over the dangers of the Vraja landscape.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the *Viṣṇu Purāṇa* book 5 as the second principal narrative witness to the Krishna-Kāliya episode; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the same dating and locus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The *Bhāgavata Purāṇa*, conventionally dated to roughly the 9th to 10th century CE, is the most famous and iconographically pervasive narrative witness to the Krishna-Kāliya episode, with book 10 chapters 15 to 17 supplying the version that the later devotional, literary, and visual traditions consolidate as the standard form of the episode.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, as the most famous narrative witness and the primary textual anchor for the episode; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the episode.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

[VARIANT: primary=*Harivaṃśa* Viṣṇuparvan (earliest fully-narrative witness, c. 4th c. CE; concise narrative within the wider boyhood-feats sequence) | alt=*Viṣṇu Purāṇa* book 5 (c. 5th c. The narrative occasion of the combat in the Bhāgavata version (*Bhāgavata Purāṇa* 10.15) is that the cowherd-children of Vraja, including the divine child Krishna, find that the cattle and the herd-boys have been sickened or killed by drinking from the Yamunā at the Kāliya-pool, and Krishna determines to address the danger by direct confrontation with the serpent.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 ch. 15, on the narrative occasion of the combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15

The combat itself in the Bhāgavata version (*Bhāgavata Purāṇa* 10.16) is occasioned by Krishna's leap into the Kāliya-pool: the child climbs a *kadamba* tree on the bank, leaps into the deep water, and is at once attacked by the *nāga*, whose multiple heads coil around the child's body in an attempt to crush or to envenom him.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 ch. 16, on the *kadamba*-tree leap and the *nāga*'s coiling attack; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the combat-mechanics.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.16

The combat's resolution in the Bhāgavata version is dance rather than killing: Krishna escapes the *nāga*'s coils, leaps onto Kāliya's many-headed crown, and dances on the heads, his weight and his rhythm progressively crushing the serpent's pride and his physical capacity for resistance until the *nāga* is forced into surrender.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 ch. 16, on the dance-resolution (*Kāliyamardana*) of the combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the resolution.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.16

The resolution of the combat is therefore non-lethal: Kāliya is not killed but subdued, and the narrative ending is the *nāga*'s submission to the child-hero, with Kāliya's wives (the *nāgapatnīs*) interceding for his life and Krishna sparing the serpent on condition that he leave the Yamunā and take up residence elsewhere.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 16–17, on the non-lethal resolution, the *nāgapatnīs*' intercession, and Krishna's conditional sparing of Kāliya; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the non-killing as a structural transformation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.16-17

The non-lethal resolution is the principal structural transformation of the combat-pattern in the bhakti-era reworking: where the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra combat resolves by killing (Indra slays the serpent and splits the obstructing mountain), the Krishna-Kāliya combat resolves by subjugation (Krishna spares the serpent and removes him from the Yamunā), and the substitution of subjugation for killing is genuine, not just an alternative phrasing of the same resolution.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml special_instructions on the non-lethal subjugation as a genuine transformation; Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 16–17, on the subjugation-resolution; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the structural difference between the Vedic killing and the bhakti-era subjugation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The non-lethal resolution is read by the Vaishnava devotional tradition as the demonstration of Krishna's compassion (*kṛpā*) alongside his power: where the Vedic warrior-god demonstrates power by killing the serpent, the bhakti-era child-hero demonstrates a higher-order combination of power-and-compassion by subjugating-without-killing, and the resolution becomes a doctrinal exhibit for the moral-theological character of the divine child.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 16–17, on the *kṛpā* / power-compassion reading of the resolution in the Bhāgavata text; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc doctrinal framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.16-17

The combat is consistently figured in the Vaishnava devotional tradition as an instance of *līlā* (Sanskrit लीला; "sacred play"): the divine child's confrontation with the serpent is construed not as warrior-combat in the Rigvedic register but as the play-action of the divine child, whose serious power is exercised within a frame of childhood-play that the devotional tradition reads as constitutive of Krishna's character.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, on the *līlā* framing of the episode in the Bhāgavata text; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of *līlā* as the load-bearing devotional category.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

The *līlā* framing is a second principal structural transformation of the combat-pattern: where the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra combat is framed as warrior-combat with the storm-god as the warrior-protagonist, the Krishna-Kāliya combat is framed as the divine child's play, and the warrior-pole of the Rigvedic combat-pattern is replaced by the child-hero pole of the bhakti-era pattern.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml special_instructions on the *līlā* framing as a genuine transformation, not an alternative phrasing of warrior-combat; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the same transformation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The child-hero substitution is itself a doctrinal exhibit for the bhakti-era construction of Krishna as the *avatāra* (the descent-form of Viṣṇu) whose divine character is most fully revealed in his childhood and youth in Vraja: where the Rigvedic warrior-god is most fully himself in the moment of the killing-stroke, the bhakti-era divine child is most fully himself in the moment of *līlā*, and the two registers articulate two different theological models of divine action.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the bhakti-era construction of Krishna as the *avatāra* whose character is revealed in *līlā*; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the two theological models.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Krishna-Kāliya episode is centred in the Vaishnava devotional tradition as one of the most widely-depicted moments of the Krishna-cycle: the iconographic moment of Krishna dancing on the multiple heads of the subdued *nāga* — the *Kāliyamardana* ("the trampling of Kāliya") or *Kāliyadamana* ("the subjugation of Kāliya") — is reproduced extensively in temple sculpture, painted manuscripts, popular prints, and modern visual culture, and is one of the principal iconographic markers of Krishna in the Indic visual tradition.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, on the Kāliya episode as the textual basis of the *Kāliyamardana* iconographic tradition; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc iconographic centrality of the moment.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

The iconographic stability of the dance-on-the-serpent's-heads moment across more than a millennium of Indic visual culture — from Gupta-era and post-Gupta temple-sculpture through medieval and early-modern painted manuscripts to twentieth- and twenty-first-century popular prints — is one of the principal pieces of evidence for the centrality of the episode in the Vaishnava devotional imagination.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, as the textual anchor for the *Kāliyamardana* iconographic tradition; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc iconographic stability across Indic visual culture.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

The water-release pattern that is the cosmological core of the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra combat is preserved in the Krishna-Kāliya episode in a transformed form: where Indra's killing of Vṛtra releases the obstructed waters and produces rain, Krishna's subjugation of Kāliya removes the venom from the Yamunā and restores the river to drinkability for the cowherds and the cattle, and the structural function "obstruction-of-waters → resolution → restoration-of-waters" is preserved across the transformation.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the preservation of the water-release pattern across the Vedic-bhakti transformation; West 2007 ch. 6 on the Indo-European storm-god / water-dragon complex as the wider type.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

[INFERENCE: the structural-functional reading of the Krishna-Kāliya episode as a bhakti-era preservation of the Rigvedic water-release pattern is a comparative reading drawn from the wider Indian dragon-combat tradition (Forsyth 1987 ch. 2) and from the Indo-European storm-god / water-dragon complex (West 2007 ch. 6), rather than an explicit thematic identification within the *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* or the *Harivaṃśa* themselves; the bhakti-era texts narrate the venom-poisoning and the river-restoration as devotional events in their own right, and the Vedic-comparative reading is the chapter's editorial reading rather than the bhakti-era texts' self-understanding. | source: Forsyth 1987 ch. 2 (long-arc Indian combat-tradition); West 2007 ch. 6 (IE storm-god / water-dragon complex); brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml cross_cultural_parallels | scope: structural-functional preservation of the water-release pattern across the Vedic-bhakti transformation, flagged for transparency.]

The water-release pattern's preservation across the transformation is partial rather than complete: the cosmological scale of the Rigvedic water-release (the release of the world's rains and rivers) is reduced to the local scale of the bhakti-era restoration (the de-envenoming of a specific reach of a specific river), and the cosmologically-active character of the Vedic combat is replaced by a locally-active character in the bhakti-era reworking.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the cosmological-to-local reduction across the transformation; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the local-scale character of the bhakti-era reworking.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The cosmological-to-local reduction is consistent with the wider transformation of the combat-tradition's frame from cosmological-and-ritual (the Rigvedic frame) to devotional-and-narrative (the bhakti-era frame): where the Rigvedic combat is recited at the *soma* sacrifice as a cosmologically-active warrant for the storm-god's kingship, the Krishna-Kāliya combat is narrated in devotional Purāṇic literature as a doctrinally-active demonstration of the divine child's character.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the frame-transformation from cosmological-and-ritual to devotional-and-narrative; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the same transformation.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Krishna-Kāliya episode is therefore not the bhakti-era replacement of the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra combat — the Rigveda continues to be transmitted, recited, and authoritative within the bhakti-era religious culture — but the bhakti-era reworking of the dragon-combat pattern within a different theological frame and a different narrative register, with the two combats coexisting in the long-arc Indian tradition rather than supplanting one another.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the coexistence of the Rigvedic and bhakti-era combat-traditions within the long-arc Indian religious culture; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml special_instructions on the coexistence rather than replacement.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Krishna-Kāliya episode is also distinct from the post-Vedic brahminicide reframing of the Indra-Vṛtra combat treated at the end of chapter 14 of the present volume: where the *Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa* and the *Mahābhārata* rework the Indra-Vṛtra combat itself within the late-Vedic and epic registers, the Krishna-Kāliya episode is a new combat-narrative with new protagonists and a new setting, even though it inherits the structural pattern of obstruction-of-waters and resolution from the Vedic tradition.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the distinction between the brahminicide reframing of the Indra-Vṛtra combat and the bhakti-era Krishna-Kāliya new-combat-narrative; brief 14-indra-vrtra.yaml on the brahminicide reframing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Krishna-Kāliya episode's *nāga*-adversary is also a distinct narrative-zoological category from the Rigvedic *ahi* / Vṛtra: the *nāga* (Sanskrit नाग) is a broad post-Vedic Indic category of serpent-being with its own moral and cosmological valence (often ambivalent rather than wholly adversarial, and often with a developed mythology of its own that includes royal-and-priestly *nāga*-figures alongside hostile-or-poisonous ones), and Kāliya's classification as a *nāga* rather than as an *ahi* is a marker of the bhakti-era reworking's distinct conceptual frame.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on Kāliya as a *nāga* and on the *nāga* category's distinctness from the Rigvedic *ahi*; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml glossary discipline on the *nāga* category.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The *nāga*-category's ambivalent moral valence is visible in the resolution of the Krishna-Kāliya episode itself: Kāliya is not annihilated but expelled, and his wives' intercession (and Krishna's acceptance of the intercession) implies a moral-personhood for the *nāga* that the Rigvedic *ahi* / Vṛtra does not possess, and this difference of moral-personhood is one of the principal differences between the two combat-traditions at the level of adversary-construction.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 16–17, on the *nāgapatnīs*' intercession and Kāliya's moral-personhood in the Bhāgavata text; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc framing of the *nāga* category's ambivalent valence.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.16-17

The episode's developed iconography of the dance-on-the-serpent's-heads — the child-hero standing on the multiple-headed serpent, often with one foot raised in the dance-pose and one or more hands holding a *vaṃśī* (flute) or assuming a gesture of authority — is one of the long-arc Indic visual tradition's most stable iconographic types, and its visual centrality is the principal cultural-relevance datum for the chapter's devotional-and-iconographic discussion of the episode.footnote:[Tagare 1976–78, *Bhāgavata Purāṇa* book 10 chs. 15–17, as the primary textual source for the dance-on-the-heads iconographic moment; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the long-arc centrality of the *Kāliyamardana* in the Indic visual tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=tagare-bhagavata ; loc=BP 10.15-17

The bhakti-era reworking also carries a forward-looking devotional reading of the combat: in the Vaishnava theological tradition the dance-on-the-serpent's-heads is read as the divine child's gracious chastisement of an adversary who is himself a creature of God, and the combat is understood in the long-arc devotional reception as exemplifying the doctrine that divine action is play-action (*līlā*) within which power and compassion are perfectly combined.footnote:[Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the forward-looking devotional reading of the combat in the Vaishnava theological tradition; brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml cultural_relevance entry on the same reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The bhakti-era Krishna-Kāliya reworking is therefore in this chapter's reading a genuine transformation of the inherited dragon-combat pattern: the warrior-pole becomes the child-hero pole, the killing-pole becomes the subjugation-pole, the cosmological-scale water-release becomes the local-scale river-restoration, and the cosmological-and-ritual frame becomes the devotional-and-narrative frame, with each of the four transformations supported by a documented feature of the bhakti-era texts and irreducible to a paraphrase of the Rigvedic combat.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml special_instructions on the four-fold transformation as a genuine reworking rather than an alternative phrasing; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the four documented transformations.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The four-fold transformation is the chapter's principal contribution to the comparative argument about the long-arc Indian dragon-combat tradition: the tradition is read here as a single tradition that has supported two clearly-distinct combat-narratives across more than a millennium, with the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra combat and the bhakti-era Krishna-Kāliya combat sitting as the two principal exhibits of the tradition's productive plasticity rather than as independent unrelated stories.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the long-arc Indian tradition as a single tradition; Forsyth 1987, ch. 2, on the productive plasticity of the tradition.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

=== Family pattern and forward links

The two sub-cases treated in the chapter — Trita Āptya and Krishna-Kāliya — bracket the Rigvedic Indra-Vṛtra flagship of chapter 14 in the historical-genealogical depth of the Indian tradition: Trita Āptya represents the older Indo-Iranian stratum that the Indra-centred Rigvedic mythology has partially displaced, and Krishna-Kāliya represents the bhakti-era reworking that the post-Vedic Vaishnava tradition has built on the inherited combat-pattern.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the bracketing framing; Benveniste and Renou 1934 on the older stratum; Forsyth 1987 ch. 2 on the bhakti-era reworking.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=benveniste-renou-1934 ; loc=Trita-Thraētaona discussion

The bracketing framing has the consequence that the chapter's reading of the Indian dragon-combat tradition is genealogical-and-arc-shaped rather than synchronic: the chapter does not present the three Indian combats (Trita's, Indra's, Krishna's) as alternative phrasings of a single combat but as three successive moments in a long-arc tradition that has rewritten its own combat-pattern in response to changing religious cultures.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the genealogical-and-arc-shaped reading; Forsyth 1987 ch. 2 on the long-arc framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=forsyth-1987 ; loc=ch. 2

The Trita Āptya / Krishna-Kāliya pairing is itself the documentary case for the long-arc Indian dragon-combat tradition that the present book's Indo-European section traces: the tradition is older and deeper than the Rigvedic combat alone (the Trita-Thraētaona cognancy reaches behind the Rigveda into the Indo-Iranian phase) and is also longer and broader than the Rigvedic combat alone (the bhakti-era Krishna-Kāliya combat reaches forward into the post-Vedic Vaishnava devotional culture), and the chapter's bracketing of the Indra-Vṛtra flagship by the Trita and Krishna sub-cases is the principal documentary support for the long-arc reading.footnote:[Per brief 15-vedic-reflexes.yaml on the long-arc reading and the bracketing structure; Benveniste and Renou 1934 (Indo-Iranian phase) and Forsyth 1987 ch. 2 (bhakti-era reception).]
