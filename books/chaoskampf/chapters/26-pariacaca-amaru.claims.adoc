=== Source overview

The chapter treats the early-colonial Andean narrative cycle in which the storm-wak'a Pariacaca, born as a clutch of falcon-eggs on the mountain heights of the Huarochirí region, opposes and defeats the rival wak'a Huallallo Carhuincho and a sequence of further rival wak'as across the central-Andean landscape, with the serpentine amaru figuring across these episodes as an ally-monster summoned by defeated rivals rather than as a primordial chaos-adversary in the Eurasian sense.footnote:[Per brief 26-pariacaca-amaru.yaml summary on Pariacaca's opposition to and integration of rival wak'as across the Huarochirí landscape and on the amaru as summoned ally-monster of a defeated rival; Salomon & Urioste 1991, *The Huarochirí Manuscript: A Testament of Ancient and Colonial Andean Religion* (Austin: University of Texas Press, 1991; ISBN 9780292730533), translators' introduction and chs. 6, 8, 21 on the Pariacaca cycle as a sequence of wak'a-combats rather than as a single chaoskampf.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 6, 8, 21 (Pariacaca and amaru passages)

The narrative survives in a single early-colonial Quechua-language manuscript conventionally dated to approximately 1608 CE, known in current scholarship as the *Huarochirí Manuscript* and held at the Biblioteca Nacional de España in Madrid as MS 3169 within a wider compilation of materials connected to the early seventeenth-century Spanish ecclesiastical campaign against indigenous Andean religion in the Lima archdiocese.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction on the c. 1608 dating of the *Huarochirí Manuscript*, on its Quechua-language compositional medium, and on its preservation as part of MS 3169 at the Biblioteca Nacional de España in Madrid; on the manuscript's place within the early seventeenth-century materials connected to the anti-idolatry campaign in the Lima archdiocese.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction

The manuscript was compiled within the milieu of the Cuzco-born secular priest Francisco de Ávila, who from approximately 1608 CE onward conducted an anti-idolatry investigation in the Huarochirí province of the Lima archdiocese, and who used the manuscript's documentation of pre-Christian Andean wak'a-religion as evidence in the prosecution of indigenous religious practitioners and as material for his own anti-idolatry sermons and treatises.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction on the *Huarochirí Manuscript*'s compilation within the milieu of Francisco de Ávila's anti-idolatry investigation in the Huarochirí province from approximately 1608 CE onward; on the manuscript's documentary purpose in supplying evidence for the prosecution of indigenous religious practitioners and material for Ávila's anti-idolatry sermons and treatises.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction

The manuscript's authorship is conventionally attributed in current scholarship to a Quechua-speaking indigenous Andean informant or set of informants writing under Ávila's direction, with the language of composition being a colonial-Quechua register that preserves substantial pre-Christian religious vocabulary, narrative structure, and place-name attestation while being filtered through the documentary conventions of the Spanish-ecclesiastical anti-idolatry investigation.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction on the indigenous-Andean Quechua-speaking authorship of the *Huarochirí Manuscript* under Ávila's direction; on the colonial-Quechua register of the composition; on the preservation of pre-Christian religious vocabulary, narrative structure, and place-name attestation alongside the Spanish-ecclesiastical filtering.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction

The *Huarochirí Manuscript* is the sole substantial primary-source attestation for pre-Columbian Huarochirí religion and one of the very few connected Quechua-language narrative-documentary witnesses for any region of the central Andes, with the alphabetic-text witness situation for the Andean material being substantially more constrained than the post-conquest textual situation for Mesoamerican religion treated in the preceding chapters of the present book.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction on the *Huarochirí Manuscript* as the sole substantial primary-source attestation for pre-Columbian Huarochirí religion and as one of the very few connected Quechua-language narrative-documentary witnesses for any region of the central Andes; on the contrast with the more extensive post-conquest textual situation for Mesoamerican religion.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction

The manuscript is organised as a connected sequence of approximately thirty-one chapters and several supplements covering the Huarochirí region's wak'a-cosmography, the Pariacaca cycle, the related cycle of the wak'a Chaupiñamca, the wak'a-genealogies of the constituent ayllus of the region, and the ritual-and-festival apparatus connected to the principal wak'as, with the Pariacaca cycle distributed across several non-contiguous chapters of the manuscript rather than collected as a single connected narrative block.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction and ch. table on the manuscript's organisation as approximately thirty-one chapters with supplements covering wak'a-cosmography, the Pariacaca cycle, the Chaupiñamca cycle, ayllu wak'a-genealogies, and ritual-and-festival apparatus; on the distribution of the Pariacaca cycle across non-contiguous chapters of the manuscript rather than as a single connected narrative block.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 1-31

The principal Pariacaca and amaru passages on which the present chapter draws are concentrated in chapters 6, 8, and 21 of the manuscript: chapter 6 narrates Pariacaca's birth as a clutch of falcon-eggs on the mountain Condorcoto and the birth of his rival Huallallo Carhuincho's children; chapter 8 narrates Pariacaca's combat with Huallallo Carhuincho in the form of a five-day flood-and-storm engagement; chapter 21 supplies further ritual-aetiological material on the Pariacaca cult and on the wak'a-succession pattern that the cycle establishes.footnote:[Per brief 26-pariacaca-amaru.yaml sources.primary entry on the loc-tag "Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 6, 8, 21 (Pariacaca and amaru passages)"; Salomon & Urioste 1991, chs. 6, 8, 21, on the distribution of the Pariacaca and amaru material across these three chapters with the falcon-eggs birth narrative in ch. 6, the flood-and-storm combat with Huallallo Carhuincho in ch. 8, and the ritual-aetiological material on the Pariacaca cult in ch. 21.]

=== The Huarochirí region and the wak'a-cosmography

The narrative's geographical setting is the Huarochirí province on the western slopes of the central Andes east of present-day Lima, Peru — a high-altitude landscape of glaciated mountain peaks, intermontane valleys, and the upper drainages of the rivers that descend from the western Cordillera to the Pacific coast — and the cycle is rooted in specific named landscape features within this geography that function in the narrative as the bodies and the sites of the named wak'as.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction and chs. 6-21 on the Huarochirí provincial geography of the central-Andean western Cordillera east of present-day Lima, on the high-altitude landscape of glaciated peaks and intermontane valleys, and on the rooting of the Pariacaca cycle in specific named landscape features that function as the bodies and the sites of the named wak'as.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction and chs. 6-21

The cycle's principal cosmographic framework is the wak'a-cosmography of pre-Columbian central Andean religion: the landscape is conceived as populated by *wak'as* — sacred power-beings (Quechua; older Spanish-derived spelling: huaca) — that are simultaneously persons with biographies, places (mountains, springs, stones, lakes), and ancestral sources of the human ayllus that descend from them, with the *Huarochirí Manuscript* recording a landscape populated by *wak'as* in hierarchical and combative relationships rather than in a chaos-versus-order opposition.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, translators' introduction on the wak'a-cosmography of pre-Columbian central Andean religion, on the wak'a as simultaneously person, place, and ancestral source, and on the *Huarochirí Manuscript*'s recording of a landscape of wak'as in hierarchical and combative relationships; per glossary 'wak'a' first_mention_gloss on the pattern as wak'a-succession rather than chaos-versus-order.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript translators' introduction

Pariacaca himself is, in the manuscript, simultaneously a person (a divine figure born and acting in narrative time), a mountain (the eponymous Pariacaca peak in the western Cordillera, a high glaciated massif on the boundary between the upper drainages of the Mantaro and Cañete rivers), and an ancestral wak'a of the Yauyos people of the Huarochirí region, whose ayllus trace their descent from him and from his sons, with this triple identity person-place-ancestor being the cycle's load-bearing cosmographic premise rather than an interpretive overlay.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, chs. 6 and 8 on Pariacaca's triple identity as person, mountain, and ancestral wak'a of the Yauyos people of the Huarochirí region; on the eponymous Pariacaca peak as a high glaciated massif on the boundary between the upper Mantaro and Cañete drainages; on the ayllu-descent of the Yauyos from Pariacaca and his sons; per glossary 'Pariacaca' first_mention_gloss on Pariacaca as Andean storm-wak'a of the Huarochirí region.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 6, 8

The cycle's principal antagonist is the rival wak'a Huallallo Carhuincho, an earlier-attested wak'a of the same region whose hegemony the Pariacaca narrative replaces, and who in the manuscript is associated with a fire-and-volcanic-heat thematic register, with child-eating, and with the eastern (Antisuyu, Amazonian-slope) direction of the central-Andean cardinal-quartering — features that distinguish him narratively from Pariacaca's storm-and-rain-and-cold thematic register and the western direction of his association.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, chs. 6, 8 on Huallallo Carhuincho as the rival wak'a whose hegemony Pariacaca replaces, on the fire-and-volcanic-heat thematic register associated with Huallallo, on the child-eating motif, and on the eastern (Antisuyu, Amazonian-slope) direction of his association; on the contrast with Pariacaca's storm-and-rain-and-cold register and the western directional association.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 6, 8

=== Pariacaca's birth: the five falcon-eggs on Mount Condorcoto

The cycle begins with Pariacaca's birth on the mountain Condorcoto in the form of a clutch of five eggs that hatch into five falcons, with the manuscript presenting this avian-multiple birth as the narrative's distinctive opening rather than as a mammalian or anthropomorphic generation, and with the five-fold birth supplying the numerological structure for the cycle's subsequent narrative pattern (Pariacaca's five sons in some renderings, his five-day combat with Huallallo Carhuincho, the five regional ayllus that descend from him).footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 6 on Pariacaca's birth on Mount Condorcoto in the form of a clutch of five eggs that hatch into five falcons, on the avian-multiple-birth pattern as the cycle's distinctive opening, and on the five-fold structure that this birth-narrative supplies for the subsequent narrative pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 6

The five falcons of Pariacaca's birth subsequently transform into five anthropomorphic divine figures — referred to in some passages of the manuscript as the five Pariacacas or as Pariacaca and his four brothers, and in other passages as Pariacaca and his sons — with the five-fold person-multiplicity persisting through the cycle as a load-bearing feature of Pariacaca's narrative identity rather than resolving to a single individual figure as the cycle progresses.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 6 and following on the transformation of the five falcons into five anthropomorphic figures, on the manuscript's varying renderings of these figures as the five Pariacacas / Pariacaca and four brothers / Pariacaca and his sons, and on the persistence of the five-fold person-multiplicity through the cycle as a load-bearing feature rather than a resolved-to-single-individual pattern.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 6

The narrative simultaneously narrates the birth of Pariacaca's narrative foil Huallallo Carhuincho's children in chapter 6, with the parallel-births framing setting up the immediately-following combat between the two wak'a-lineages: the manuscript places the two birth-events in narrative parallel rather than treating Pariacaca's birth as cosmogonically prior to Huallallo's existence, and Huallallo Carhuincho is presented as already established in the region as the dominant wak'a at the moment of Pariacaca's hatching.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 6 on the parallel narration of Pariacaca's birth and the birth of Huallallo Carhuincho's children, on the parallel-births framing as the narrative-structural setup for the subsequent combat between the two wak'a-lineages, and on Huallallo Carhuincho's already-established hegemonic position in the region at the moment of Pariacaca's hatching.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 6

The narrative's pre-existing wak'a-political situation is significant for the cycle's classification within the present book's typology: Pariacaca acts within an already-existing wak'a-cosmos in which Huallallo Carhuincho holds the dominant position, and the crisis the cycle addresses is the displacement of one hegemonic wak'a by another rather than the establishment of cosmic order ex novo — a pattern that places the Pariacaca cycle within wak'a-succession rather than within cosmogonic-combat in the strong scope.md sense.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, chs. 6, 8 on the pre-existing wak'a-political situation in the Huarochirí region at the moment of Pariacaca's birth, on Huallallo Carhuincho's already-hegemonic position, and on the cycle's narrative work as the displacement of one hegemonic wak'a by another rather than the establishment of cosmic order ex novo; per brief 26-pariacaca-amaru.yaml summary on the wak'a-succession framing.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 6, 8

=== The combat with Huallallo Carhuincho: storm-and-flood

Pariacaca's principal combat in the cycle, narrated in chapter 8 of the manuscript, is his five-day engagement with Huallallo Carhuincho, in which the two wak'as confront one another on the high mountain landscape with Pariacaca deploying torrential rain, hail, and lightning from the western side and Huallallo Carhuincho deploying fire and volcanic heat from the eastern side, with the natural-meteorological register of the combat — water-and-cold against fire-and-heat — being the narrative's principal combat-modality.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 8 on Pariacaca's five-day combat with Huallallo Carhuincho, on the high-mountain-landscape setting of the engagement, on Pariacaca's torrential rain / hail / lightning weapons from the western side, on Huallallo's fire-and-volcanic-heat weapons from the eastern side, and on the water-and-cold-versus-fire-and-heat register as the principal combat-modality.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 8

Pariacaca, in the chapter 8 combat-narrative, deploys his storm-power as the five Pariacacas acting jointly: each of the five is presented as releasing rain or hail or lightning from a separate direction, and the combined storm-attack is the cycle's load-bearing image of the five-fold-Pariacaca's combat-effectiveness, with the five-fold image of the storm being a distinctive feature of the Andean material that has no exact parallel in the storm-deity narratives of the Eurasian traditions in the present book's other chapters.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 8 on the five Pariacacas acting jointly in the combat with Huallallo Carhuincho, on each of the five releasing rain / hail / lightning from a separate direction, on the combined storm-attack as the cycle's load-bearing combat-image, and on the five-fold-storm pattern as a distinctive feature of the Andean material; per glossary 'Pariacaca' first_mention_gloss on the storm-wak'a character.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 8

The combat's outcome is the defeat of Huallallo Carhuincho and his expulsion from the Huarochirí region eastward into the Antisuyu (Amazonian-slope) zone, with Huallallo Carhuincho not being killed but rather displaced — a feature of the narrative that distinguishes the Pariacaca cycle from the kill-the-adversary pattern of the Eurasian chaoskampf material and that aligns the cycle with the wak'a-succession reading of Salomon, Urioste, and the wider Andean specialist tradition.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 8 on the combat's outcome as Huallallo Carhuincho's defeat and eastward expulsion into the Antisuyu zone rather than as Huallallo's killing, on the displacement-rather-than-killing pattern as a feature of the Andean cycle that distinguishes it from the kill-the-adversary pattern of Eurasian chaoskampf, and on the alignment of this feature with the wak'a-succession reading.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 8

The post-combat geographical settlement of the cycle's outcome is the establishment of Pariacaca's hegemony over the Huarochirí region's wak'a-cosmography: Pariacaca takes possession of the high mountain peak that bears his name, his five sons (or brothers) take possession of subsidiary peaks and ritual centres in the region, and Huallallo Carhuincho's former sphere is reorganised under Pariacaca's authority — with the manuscript presenting this settlement as a wak'a-political reorganisation of the existing landscape rather than as a cosmogonic establishment of the landscape itself.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, ch. 8 on the post-combat geographical settlement of the cycle's outcome, on Pariacaca's possession of the eponymous mountain peak, on his five sons or brothers taking subsidiary peaks and ritual centres, and on the wak'a-political reorganisation of the existing landscape as the settlement-pattern; per brief 26-pariacaca-amaru.yaml cultural_relevance.function on the political-theological function of wak'a-succession.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript ch. 8

=== The amaru: ally-monster of the defeated rival

The serpentine amaru figures across the Pariacaca cycle as an ally-monster summoned by a defeated rival wak'a in the course of further engagements, with the amaru's narrative role being the discharge of the defeated wak'a's residual combat-capacity rather than the embodiment of a primordial chaos-adversary in the Eurasian chaoskampf sense, and with the amaru's defeat by Pariacaca (or by his sons) typically being a follow-on to the principal wak'a-combat rather than the cycle's central engagement.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, chs. 8 and following on the amaru as ally-monster summoned by a defeated rival wak'a, on the amaru's narrative role as the discharge of the defeated wak'a's residual combat-capacity, and on the amaru's defeat as a follow-on to the principal wak'a-combat; per glossary 'amaru' first_mention_gloss on the amaru as Andean serpent-figure summoned as ally-monster by a defeated rival wak'a rather than as primordial chaos.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 8 and following

The amaru is described in the manuscript as a giant serpent-figure of the lake-and-river waters of the high Andean landscape, often associated with specific lakes and springs in the Huarochirí region and with the discharge of those bodies of water as the amaru moves; in the manuscript's narrative register the amaru is therefore a figure of the lake-being / water-serpent type rather than of the primordial-cosmic-serpent type that is load-bearing in the Eurasian chaoskampf material.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, on the amaru's description as a giant serpent-figure of the lake-and-river waters of the high Andean landscape, on the amaru's specific associations with lakes and springs in the Huarochirí region, on the discharge of those bodies of water as the amaru moves, and on the lake-being / water-serpent typological character of the figure rather than the primordial-cosmic-serpent character of Eurasian chaoskampf material.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=salomon-urioste-huarochiri ; loc=Huarochirí Manuscript chs. 8 and following

The amaru's defeat in the cycle is typically narrated as a cutting, splitting, or driving-into-a-confined-space rather than as a comprehensive destruction, with the defeated amaru's body becoming a feature of the landscape (a lake's outline, a watercourse's bend, a stone formation) rather than being reduced to nothing — a pattern that, like the displacement-rather-than-killing of Huallallo Carhuincho, distinguishes the Andean material from the kill-and-eradicate pattern of much Eurasian chaoskampf.footnote:[Salomon & Urioste 1991, on the amaru's defeat as cutting, splitting, or driving-into-a-confined-space rather than comprehensive destruction, on the defeated amaru's body becoming a feature of the landscape (lake outline, watercourse bend, stone formation), and on the parallel between this displacement-rather-than-killing pattern and the wider cycle's wak'a-succession-rather-than-eradication framing.]
