Below is a clean working catalog for the book. It keeps the full Akkadian narrative tradition in the broad scholarly sense, so it includes Babylonian and Assyrian works within the same literary stream, but separates myth, epic, royal legend, wisdom literature, and popular/visionary narrative so the book does not flatten the genres. A few items are marked as appendix dossier because they matter for the tradition but are too fragmentary or too narrow for a long stand-alone chapter.
I. Cosmogony, creation, and primordial order

1. Enūma Eliš — core chapter. The central Babylonian creation epic: origins of the cosmos, defeat of Tiamat, rise of Marduk, and theological exaltation of Babylon.

2. Atraḫasīs — core chapter. Creation of humankind, divine labor, overpopulation/noise, plague/famine control, and the flood. This is indispensable for any Akkadian creation section. 3. Adapa and the South Wind — core chapter. The antediluvian sage Adapa ascends to heaven and loses the chance at immortality; this is one of the key Akkadian myths and should not be relegated to a footnote. 4. Enmešarra’s Defeat — secondary chapter or appendix dossier. A creation/conflict text from the wider Babylonian cosmogonic dossier. 5. The Toil of Babylon — appendix dossier. Creation-related Babylonian text worth preserving in the research base even if it does not become a full chapter.

6. Uraš and Marduk — appendix dossier. Another creation-related Babylonian narrative piece, probably better treated briefly unless later research shows it is more central to your argument.

7. Damkina’s Bond — appendix dossier. Relevant to the wider Babylonian creation tradition and worth retaining in the catalog. 8. The Slaying of Labbu — secondary chapter or appendix dossier. Dragon-slaying material tied to the wider Babylonian cosmogonic tradition. 9. The Founding of Eridu — secondary chapter or appendix dossier. Foundation/origin material important for the broader creation dossier. 10. The First Brick — secondary chapter or appendix dossier. A short but useful text for the intersection of creation, building, and ordered civilization. 11. Another Dragon-Slaying Episode — appendix dossier. Preserve in the research catalog even if the book only mentions it briefly. 12. The Theogony of Dunnu — secondary chapter or appendix dossier. A distinctive theogonic/genealogical narrative that deserves inclusion because it broadens the creation section beyond Enūma Eliš.
II. Divine conflict, descent, and the underworld

13. The Descent of Ishtar — core chapter. Major underworld narrative centered on Ishtar’s descent and return; it belongs in the book, but the chapter should emphasize the Akkadian composition rather than treating it as merely derivative of the Sumerian descent tradition.

14. Nergal and Ereškigal — core chapter. Essential Akkadian underworld myth explaining how Nergal becomes ruler of the netherworld with Ereškigal. 15. Anzû — core chapter. Divine conflict narrative in which Anzû steals the Tablets of Destiny and is defeated; fundamental for the Akkadian mythic corpus. 16. Erra and Išum — core chapter. First-millennium Babylonian myth of divine violence, urban catastrophe, and restoration; one of the most distinctively Babylonian texts in the corpus.
III. Heroic and mythic epics

17. The Standard Babylonian Epic of Gilgameš — core chapter. Heroic epic rather than pure myth, but too central to Akkadian narrative culture to omit. It should stand in its own section so it does not overshadow the creation and divine myths.

18. Etana — secondary or core chapter. Mythic-royal epic about ascent to heaven and the quest for the plant of birth; it fits naturally beside Gilgameš.
IV. Royal legends and pseudo-historical epics

19. The Birth Legend of Sargon — core chapter in royal legend section. Not a myth in the strict sense, but a central Akkadian royal legend and indispensable if the book aims to preserve the wider narrative culture.

20. The King of Battle (šar tamḫāri) — core or secondary chapter. Sargonic campaign narrative with strong legendary and imperial dimensions; very useful for the book because it belongs firmly to Akkadian royal storytelling rather than to the older Sumerian mythic core. 21. The Cuthaean Legend of Narām-Sîn — core or secondary chapter. Pseudo-autobiographical Narām-Sîn narrative with divine warning, hubris, and disaster; a major royal-legend text. 22. The Tukulti-Ninurta Epic — secondary chapter. Assyrian historical epic in Akkadian, important if the book wants to represent the full Assyro-Babylonian narrative world rather than Babylonia alone.
V. Wisdom, suffering, and dialogic literature

23. Ludlul bēl nēmeqi — core chapter in wisdom section. A major Akkadian composition on unjust suffering, divine abandonment, and restoration; it should stay in the project, but in a clearly labeled wisdom section.

24. The Babylonian Theodicy — core or secondary chapter in wisdom section. A major dialogic reflection on suffering and divine justice; it belongs with Ludlul, not with myth proper. 25. Dialogue of Pessimism (Dialogue between Master and Servant) — secondary chapter. A short Akkadian dialogic composition that broadens the book beyond myth and epic into philosophical and satirical literature.
VI. Visionary and popular narratives

26. Underworld Vision of an Assyrian Prince — secondary chapter. Visionary underworld narrative from Assyrian tradition; not a myth proper, but highly relevant to the imaginative world of Akkadian literature.

27. The Poor Man of Nippur — secondary chapter. Comic Babylonian folktale of humiliation and revenge; it belongs in the book if the aim is to preserve the wider narrative culture, but it should be clearly separated from myth and epic.
