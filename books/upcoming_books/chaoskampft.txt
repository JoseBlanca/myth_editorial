1. Core Chaoskampf corpus

These are the cases I would place in the main spine of the book: strongly attested ancient traditions in which a central combat with a monstrous adversary does major cosmological, regal, cultic, cyclical, or eschatological order-making work.

Marduk vs. Tiamat — canonical core case. Primordial watery chaos, divine combat, and ordered cosmos built from the defeated monster.

Baal vs. Yamm / Lotan — core case. Not cosmogonic in the same way as Enūma Eliš, but clearly central as a sea/serpent combat that establishes divine kingship and ordered rule. Indra vs. Vṛtra — core case. One of the clearest storm-god/dragon/water-release structures in the ancient world. Zeus vs. Typhon — core case. Olympian order is secured through Zeus’s defeat of the monstrous serpent-headed adversary. Tarḫunna / Teshub vs. Illuyanka — core case. The Hittite storm-god versus serpent combat is a major Anatolian instance of the pattern, even though it includes trickery and ritual framing. Ra vs. Apep — core case. This is the great cyclical form of Chaoskampf: not a single founding victory, but the repeated defense of cosmic order against the serpent of chaos. Thor vs. Jörmungandr — core case. You were right to insist on this. Its eschatological setting is a subtype, not a demotion. It is still one of the great thunder-god versus world-serpent combats of ancient myth. Susanoo vs. Yamata no Orochi — core case. Strong early Japanese tradition, direct serpent combat, and major political-symbolic value through the sword found in the serpent’s tail. Apollo vs. Python — lower-confidence core, but still core. It is more cult-foundational and local than Marduk/Tiamat or Indra/Vṛtra, yet the serpent combat is ancient, central, and structurally important.
2. Strong related cases

These are good comparanda and should stay in the book, but in a separate section. The usual reason is one of three: the combat is less central, the attestation is weaker or later, or the motif is a transformed/partial version rather than a classical Chaoskampf.

Cipactli — keep as a strong related case. The world is made from the body of a primordial monster, which makes it one of the best non-Eurasian parallels, but I would still keep it outside the core because the evidence is textually less straightforward in transmission than the strongest ancient literary cases.

Yu the Great / Xiangliu material — keep as related. The ordering of flood-chaos and the founding of political order are central, but monster combat is not always the main engine of the story. Perun vs. Veles — keep as related. Structurally very good, but the direct ancient textual basis is not as secure or as rich as in the Near Eastern, Greek, Vedic, Norse, or Egyptian cases. Vahagn vs. the Vishaps — keep as related. It is clearly relevant, especially as a dragon-slayer tradition, but I would still not promote it to the core because the surviving evidence is later and more fragmentary. Bakunawa — keep as related. It is cosmically important and ritually alive, but it is more an eclipse-devourer opposed by communal action than a paradigmatic hero-versus-chaos combat. Bida — keep as related. It is one of the most interesting inverse variants in your draft, because slaying the serpent destroys the prosperity connected to it. That makes it valuable precisely as a reversal of the usual pattern. Mwindo vs. Kirimu — keep as related. The monster-combat is real, but it sits inside a larger heroic cycle and ends by complicating the simple triumph-of-order model. Pariacaca / Amaru material — keep as related. It is promising, but the Amaru’s status oscillates between adversary, natural power, and sacred being, so it is not a clean core case. Nanabozho / Mishipeshu material — keep as related. Strong water-monster and flood-order themes, but the motif is less classically concentrated than in the best core examples. Väinämöinen vs. Iku-Turso — keep as related. It is a transformed version in which the struggle is mediated through song, magical speech, and banishment rather than a paradigmatic storm-god kill. Revelation’s Red Dragon — keep as related. It preserves ancient chaos-dragon imagery in a powerful apocalyptic form, but I would treat it as a late scriptural reworking of older combat patterns, not as one of the primary ancient prototypes. Frashokereti — keep as related. It belongs in the eschatological chapter, but more as a final renovation of creation after evil’s defeat than as a classic dragon-slaying Chaoskampf.
3. Provisionally retained, but flagged for targeted later research

These should stay in the project for now, exactly as you suggested, but with an explicit warning label. At present they do not fit cleanly enough to use in the main argument.

Mucalinda — flagged. The naga protects the Buddha from the storm; that is serpent symbolism, but not obviously combat against chaos. Keep it under review, not in the main argument.

Antaboga — flagged. The material currently points to a world- or underworld-serpent role, not to a clear adversarial chaos monster. It should remain in the dossier until you do a focused Indonesia-specific review. Whowie — flagged. As it stands, this looks more like a dangerous-creature and communal-hunt narrative than a securely cosmic order-versus-chaos myth. Rainbow Serpent — flagged, not removed. This is exactly the kind of case that can be badly distorted by forcing a Near Eastern dragon-slaying template onto it. Many traditions present the Rainbow Serpent as creator, life-giver, and water-power; some conflictive variants exist, but they need separate culture-specific handling.
Compact final catalog

Core: Marduk/Tiamat; Baal/Yamm-Lotan; Indra/Vṛtra; Zeus/Typhon; Tarḫunna- or Teshub/Illuyanka; Ra/Apep; Thor/Jörmungandr; Susanoo/Orochi; Apollo/Python.

Related: Cipactli; Yu/Xiangliu; Perun/Veles; Vahagn/Vishaps; Bakunawa; Bida; Mwindo/Kirimu; Pariacaca/Amaru; Nanabozho/Mishipeshu; Väinämöinen/Iku-Turso; Revelation’s Red Dragon; Frashokereti. Provisional / suspicious: Mucalinda; Antaboga; Whowie; Rainbow Serpent.
