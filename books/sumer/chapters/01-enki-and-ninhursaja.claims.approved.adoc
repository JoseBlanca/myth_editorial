=== Source overview

The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.1.1 and survives on tablets from both Nippur and Ur, dating to the Old Babylonian period (ca. 2000–1600 BCE).footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, bibliography page.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, bibliography

The Nippur and Ur recensions represent co-equal witnesses that differ in significant ways; the Ur manuscripts include an insertion of international tributes to Dilmun not found in Nippur copies.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 38–39.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 38–39

The text as scholars read it is a composite reconstruction assembled from these overlapping fragments; no single tablet preserves the complete composition. Gaps remain in the Dilmun description and in several dialogue sections.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, composite text.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, composite text

The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by the translations in Black et al. 2004 (pp. 38–50) and Jacobsen 1987 (pp. 181–204).footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 38–50; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 181–204.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 38–50

=== Setting and context

The composition "Enki and Ninhursaja" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.1.1 and survives in multiple Old Babylonian manuscript witnesses from both Nippur and Ur scribal schools.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, composite text, lines 1–278.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, composite text, lines 1–278

The ETCSL composite text follows the Nippur recension as its base but draws on Ur manuscripts to fill gaps and provide alternative readings.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, editorial notes on manuscript base.]

=== Dilmun as paradise

The text opens by describing Dilmun as a land that is pure, clean, and bright — a place where there is no sickness, no old age, no death, and no predatory violence.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 1–30.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 1–30

[LACUNA: Opening invocation partially damaged in Nippur manuscripts (lines 1–13) | source: etcsl 1.1.1, Nippur manuscripts | scholarly_reconstruction: Ur manuscripts preserve portions of the opening and are used to supplement the composite]

Dilmun is generally identified by scholars with the region of modern Bahrain and the eastern Arabian coast, known as a trade emporium linking Mesopotamia with the lands to the east.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 54–59.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 54–59

In its primordial state, Dilmun has no fresh water: no river flows, no well yields water, no rain falls.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 29–39.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 29–39

=== Water provision

The goddess Ninsikila (identified by some scholars with Ninhursaja, but presented separately in the ETCSL text) appeals to Enki to provide Dilmun with fresh water.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 29–39; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 184–186.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 29–39

Enki commands the sun god Utu to bring fresh water from the earth, filling Dilmun's wells and irrigating its fields, transforming it into a garden paradise.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 40–49.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 40–49

After the water provision, Dilmun becomes a prosperous harbour and trading port. The Nippur text describes Dilmun as an emporium on the quay for the land (lines 50–55); the Ur manuscript insertion (lines 49A–49P) names specific trade partners including Tukric, Meluhha, Magan, and others.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 50–55 (Nippur); lines 49A–49P (Ur insertion).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 50–55 and 49A–49P

=== Narrative sequence: Chain of births

Enki copulates with Ninhursaja; she conceives and after a gestation of nine days (rather than nine months) gives birth to the goddess Ninsar (Lady Greenery).footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 69–87; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 188–190.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 69–87

[LACUNA: Transition between water-provision and first plant-eating episode partially broken in Nippur manuscripts (lines 64–78) | source: etcsl 1.1.1, Nippur manuscripts | scholarly_reconstruction: Ur manuscripts overlap but diverge in the tribute section]

Enki then copulates with his daughter Ninsar, who conceives and gives birth to the goddess Ninkurra (Lady Pasture) after the same accelerated nine-day gestation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 88–107.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 88–107

Enki copulates with his granddaughter Ninkurra, who gives birth to the goddess Uttu (goddess of weaving).footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 108–126.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 108–126

Ninhursaja (or Nintud, as she is called in this passage) warns Uttu not to yield to Enki unless he brings her gifts of garden produce — cucumbers, apples, and grapes.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 127–151; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 191–193.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 127–151

Enki brings the required garden produce and Uttu opens the door to him; he copulates with her.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 152–175.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 152–175

=== The forbidden plants

Ninhursaja removes Enki's semen from Uttu's body and plants it in the ground, where eight plants grow.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 186–197.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 186–197

Enki, aided by his minister Isimud (two-faced servant of Enki), discovers the eight plants one by one, determines their nature, and eats each of them.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 202–219; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 194–196.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 202–219

[INFERENCE: The act of eating the plants constitutes a transgression: Enki "decrees the fates" of the plants by consuming them, an act of appropriation that enrages Ninhursaja | basis: The text shows Enki eating the plants followed immediately by Ninhursaja's curse; the causal link is strongly implied | risk: The text does not explicitly label the eating as forbidden or use a word for "transgression"]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 210–220

=== Curse and illness

Ninhursaja pronounces a curse of death on Enki, declaring that she will not look upon him with the "eye of life" until he is dying, and then she withdraws.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, line 220.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, line 220

After the curse, Enki falls gravely ill; his eight ailing body parts are revealed in the healing dialogue that follows.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 220–271; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 197–199.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 220–271

The great gods sit in the dust; Enlil himself cannot cure Enki; the situation appears hopeless.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 220–227.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 220–227

The fox offers to bring Ninhursaja back and succeeds in persuading her to return.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 228–247; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 199–200.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 228–247

=== Eight healing deities

Ninhursaja seats Enki beside her vulva and asks him, one by one, which part of his body hurts.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 247–271.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 247–271

For each of the eight ailing body parts, Ninhursaja gives birth to a healing deity whose name puns on the body part being cured — an aetiological explanation of these deities' origins and domains.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 254–278; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 200–203.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 254–278

Among the eight healing deities is Ninti, whose name means both "Lady of the Rib" and "Lady of Life" — a pun that has been compared to the biblical Eve, created from Adam's rib, whose name means "living."footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 54–59; ETCSL 1.1.1, line ~270.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 54–59

[INFERENCE: Each newly born deity is assigned a specific role or domain by Enki, integrating them into the divine order | basis: The text shows Enki assigning roles to the newly born deities | risk: The phrase "integrating them into the divine order" is scholarly characterisation, not a direct textual statement]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 270–278

=== Cultural and cosmological function

The composition functions cosmologically: it establishes Dilmun as a primordial pure land where sun and fresh water are granted by divine decree, and aetiologically: it explains the origin of eight healing deities through Ninhursaja's curing of Enki's ailments.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 1–30 (cosmological), lines 230–278 (aetiological); Jacobsen 1987, pp. 181–204.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, lines 1–30 and 230–278

The nine-day gestation period (replacing the normal nine months) marks Dilmun as a space operating under different temporal rules — a feature characteristic of paradise or otherworld narratives across cultures.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 188–190.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 188–190

[INFERENCE: The accelerated gestation functions as a narrative marker of Dilmun's otherworldly temporal nature | basis: The pattern of nine days replacing nine months is consistent across all four generations of births in the text | risk: The text does not explicitly state the symbolic function; this is scholarly interpretation]

The chain of incestuous unions may reflect a mythological theme of divine power concentrating through successive generations rather than a narrative about kinship taboos as moderns would understand them.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 188–195.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 188–195

=== Attestation and transmission

Multiple Old Babylonian copies from both Nippur and Ur scribal schools attest wide circulation of the composition in the _edubba_ curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.1, manuscript list

Both Jacobsen and Kramer devote sustained analyses to the text's paradise motif and botanical imagery, making it one of the most discussed Sumerian compositions in modern scholarship.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 181–204; Kramer 1961, pp. 54–59.]
