== The Death of Gilgamesh — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

The composition ETCSL 1.8.1.3 narrates the death of Gilgamesh (Sumerian: Bilgames) and his reception in the nether world as a judge among the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, composite text (fragmentary).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.3

The text survives in two co-equal witnesses: a Nippur version known from fragmentary Old Babylonian tablets, and a Tell Haddad (ancient Me-Turan) version published and analysed by George.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3; George 1999, pp. 195–208.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 195–196

[LACUNA: The Nippur manuscripts are extensively damaged, with multiple large gaps throughout. Funeral preparations and underworld reception sequences are heavily broken. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: Partial; the Tell Haddad version provides significant new material not available from Nippur witnesses, but the two versions do not fully overlap, leaving substantial gaps in both.]

[LACUNA: Ritual content in the Tell Haddad version (columns ii–iv) is partially damaged. The divine judgement scene is incomplete. | source: George 2003, Tell Haddad tablets, col. ii–iv | scholarly_reconstruction: None; the Tell Haddad version is the only witness for its distinctive ritual content.]

The discovery of the Tell Haddad tablets significantly expanded scholarly knowledge of this composition, adding ritual and funerary content not attested in the Nippur fragments.footnote:[George 1999, pp. 195–196.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 195–196

=== Narrative sequence: Confronting mortality

Gilgamesh, having achieved great deeds during his life, is confronted with the inevitability of his own death — the gods have decreed that even the greatest hero must die.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, surviving lines; George 1999, pp. 196–200.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 196–200

The text appears to include a dream or divine communication in which Gilgamesh is told that immortality is not granted to mortals, however great their accomplishments — the gods have reserved eternal life for themselves.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, surviving fragments]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 205–207

[INFERENCE: The divine decree against Gilgamesh's immortality echoes the theme expressed in other Sumerian Gilgamesh compositions — particularly Gilgamesh and Huwawa — that fame, not eternal life, is the only compensation for mortality. | basis: The thematic continuity across the Sumerian Gilgamesh poems is widely noted by scholars. | risk: Given the fragmentary state of the text, the precise formulation of the decree and its relationship to other compositions cannot be fully established.]

=== Narrative sequence: Funeral and offerings

The text describes elaborate funeral preparations for Gilgamesh, including offerings to the gods of the nether world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, surviving fragments; George 1999, pp. 200–204.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 200–204

[VARIANT: primary=ETCSL 1.8.1.3, Nippur version, which has been known longer and forms the basis of the ETCSL composite | alt=George 1999, pp. 200–208, Tell Haddad version, which includes significantly more ritual and funerary content, expanding the underworld reception scene | chosen: Both presented as co-equal | reason: The Nippur and Tell Haddad versions are equally authoritative witnesses to the tradition; neither is subordinate to the other.]

The Tell Haddad version preserves substantially more detail about the funerary ritual: offerings are presented to major underworld deities including Ereshkigal (queen of the nether world), Ningishzida, and other divine figures, in what appears to be a formal protocol for the reception of a dead king.footnote:[George 1999, pp. 200–206.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 200–206

Gilgamesh presents gifts to the underworld deities — a ritual sequence that parallels the gift-giving protocols attested in other Sumerian literary and liturgical texts concerning the nether world.footnote:[George 1999, pp. 202–206.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 202–206

=== Narrative sequence: Reception as underworld judge

After death, Gilgamesh is received in the nether world not as an ordinary shade but as a figure of authority: he is granted the status of a judge among the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, surviving fragments; George 1999, pp. 206–208.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 206–208

Gilgamesh's elevation to judge of the dead places him alongside Ningishzida and other underworld deities, transforming him from a mortal hero into a figure with a permanent divine function in the afterlife.footnote:[George 1999, pp. 206–208]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 205–207

[INFERENCE: The elevation of Gilgamesh to underworld judge functions as a form of apotheosis — a partial divinisation that grants the dead hero a role surpassing that of ordinary mortals while still confining him to the nether world. | basis: George 2003 identifies this elevation as consistent with the Sumerian cult of dead kings, in which deceased rulers received ongoing offerings and were treated as semi-divine intercessors. | risk: The fragmentary state of the text means the precise nature and extent of Gilgamesh's underworld authority cannot be fully determined.]

The composition's eschatological function is to assert that even for the greatest hero, death is absolute — but that extraordinary merit can earn an elevated status within death's domain, as a judge and authority among the dead rather than a mere shade.footnote:[George 1999, pp. 206–208.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 206–208

The royal-legitimation function is significant: by showing Gilgamesh received as a divine judge in the nether world, the text legitimises the cult of dead kings and the ongoing ritual obligations of the living toward deceased rulers.footnote:[George 1999, pp. 200–208.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 200–208
