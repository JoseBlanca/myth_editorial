== Inanna and Enki: The Theft of the _me_ — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

The composition "Inanna and Enki" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.3.1 and survives in multiple Old Babylonian copies from the Nippur scribal schools.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1, composite text; Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1

The text is classified as single-prevalent-reconstructed: the ETCSL composite is the standard scholarly reconstruction, but significant gaps remain in the _me_ catalogues across all known witnesses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1, manuscript list and apparatus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1, manuscript list

The narrative concerns the transfer of the _me_ — the divine powers or cultural norms underpinning all aspects of civilisation — from the city of Eridu, Enki's cult centre, to Uruk (Sumerian: Unug), the city of Inanna.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–64 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 63–64

The _me_ constitute a comprehensive taxonomy of Sumerian civilisation: kingship, priesthood, truth, descent to the underworld, crafts, musical instruments, and dozens more domains are each governed by a specific _me_.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 64–68.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 64–68

The theological subtext reflects an Eridu-to-Uruk transfer of cultural and religious centrality, mapping a historical shift in political and sacral prestige.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–64 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 63–64

[LACUNA: Opening lines 1–20 partially damaged; Inanna's initial preparations for the journey to Eridu are fragmentary. | source: etcsl 1.3.1 | scholarly_reconstruction: partial, other manuscripts preserve portions of the opening.]

=== Inanna's journey to Eridu

Inanna resolves to travel from Uruk to the city of Eridu, the most ancient of Sumerian cities and the dwelling place of Enki, god of wisdom, freshwater, and cunning, and lord of the _abzu_ — the underground freshwater ocean.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1, opening sections; Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–64.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1, opening

Inanna arrives at Eridu and presents herself at the _abzu_, Enki's temple-residence.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 64.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1

=== The banquet and the gift of the _me_

Enki instructs his minister Isimud to welcome Inanna, and a banquet is prepared.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 64.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1

Enki and Inanna drink beer together; as Enki becomes increasingly intoxicated, he offers the _me_ to Inanna in a series of gift-giving speeches.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 64–66.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 64–66

The _me_ are presented in lengthy catalogue sections listing approximately one hundred divine powers; these catalogues are repeated multiple times in the text as Enki offers them and Inanna accepts.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1, catalogue sections; Kramer 1961, pp. 64–68.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1, me catalogues

[LACUNA: The lengthy catalogues of _me_ are fragmentary in multiple places; individual _me_ names are broken or illegible across several passages. Partial overlapping manuscripts restore some items but the full list of approximately one hundred _me_ cannot be completely reconstructed. | source: etcsl 1.3.1, catalogue sections | scholarly_reconstruction: partial, from overlapping manuscripts.]

Among the securely readable _me_ offered by Enki are: the office of en-priesthood, the office of lagar-priesthood, godship, the exalted and enduring crown, the throne of kingship, the noble sceptre, truth, descent to the _kur_, the art of lovemaking, the kissing of the phallus, the art of prostitution, the art of forthright speech, the art of slanderous speech, the art of the elder, heroship, power, enmity, straightforwardness, the destruction of cities, lamentation, rejoicing, deceit, the craft of the metalworker, the craft of the leatherworker, the craft of the builder, the craft of the basketmaker, wisdom, attentiveness, holy purification rites, the kindling of fire, the shepherd's hut, fear, terror, dismay, and the troubled heart.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1, _me_ catalogues (securely readable items); Kramer 1961, pp. 64–68.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1, me catalogues

[INFERENCE: The approximately one hundred _me_ collectively constitute a Sumerian map of the total inventory of civilisation and its institutions, encompassing crafts, religious offices, emotions, sexual practices, social virtues and vices, and political powers. | basis: The catalogues include items from all these domains. | risk: The exact number and sequence of items remain uncertain due to lacunae.]

Inanna accepts each group of _me_ with formulaic acceptance speeches.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 64–66.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 64–66

=== Inanna's departure with the _me_

Having accepted all the _me_, Inanna loads them onto her boat, the Boat of Heaven (Ma-anna), and departs Eridu for Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 66.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 66

=== Enki's recovery and the pursuit

Enki sobers up from his intoxication and realises that the _me_ are gone from the _abzu_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 67.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 67

Enki asks his minister Isimud what has become of the _me_ he gave to Inanna, and Isimud confirms that Enki granted them freely while drinking.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 67.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 67

Enki dispatches Isimud and a series of sea monsters and creatures to intercept Inanna's boat and recover the _me_ at various stopping points along the waterway between Eridu and Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 67–68.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 67–68

The pursuit unfolds in a repeated formulaic pattern: at each stopping point, Isimud (with accompanying creatures) confronts Inanna and demands the return of the _me_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 67–69.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1

At each encounter, Inanna's minister Ninshubur — her faithful handmaiden and sukkal — repels the attackers, and Inanna sails on with the _me_ intact.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 67–69.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 67–69

Enki's attempts to recover the _me_ are repeated at multiple stopping points along the journey, each time failing.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 67–69.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.1

=== Arrival at Uruk and the installation of the _me_

Inanna arrives safely at the quay of Uruk with the Boat of Heaven bearing the _me_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 69–70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 69–70

The _me_ are unloaded and installed in the E-ana, the "House of Heaven," Inanna's great temple in Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 69–70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 69–70

The text catalogues the _me_ once more as they are received in Uruk, confirming that the full set of civilisational powers has been transferred.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 70

There is jubilation in Uruk at the arrival of the _me_; Enki, having failed to retrieve them, accepts the new reality and establishes good relations with Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, p. 70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 70

[INFERENCE: Enki's acceptance of the transfer, without further conflict, suggests the text functions as an aetiology legitimating Uruk's possession of the _me_ — a theological charter rather than a narrative of permanent enmity between the cities. | basis: The text ends with reconciliation, not ongoing conflict. | risk: The damaged state of the ending means this reconciliation may be less emphatic than the reconstruction implies.]

=== Theological and literary significance

The composition was widely copied in Old Babylonian scribal schools, confirming its status as part of the standard _edubba_ curriculum and its importance to Sumerian intellectual tradition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1, manuscript list; Kramer 1961, pp. 64–68.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 64–68

Kramer devoted extensive analysis to the _me_ concept as the foundation of Sumerian cultural self-understanding: the _me_ are not merely divine possessions but the cosmic blueprints that make civilisation possible.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 64–68.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 64–68

The _me_ catalogue constitutes a comprehensive taxonomy of Sumerian civilisation, reflecting temple and royal ideology: everything from the throne of kingship to the art of lamentation has its own _me_.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–64 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 63–64

The composition provides an aetiology for the presence of the _me_ in Uruk's E-ana temple and legitimates Uruk's claim to cultural supremacy over Eridu.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–64 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 63–64

Jacobsen notes the composition in the context of Inanna's broader character as one who acquires power through cunning and personal daring rather than through the established divine hierarchy.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 112–113.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 112–113
