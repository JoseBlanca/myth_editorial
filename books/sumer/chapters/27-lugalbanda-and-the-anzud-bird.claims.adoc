== Lugalbanda and the Anzud Bird — Claims

=== Setting and context

Lugalbanda is alone in the mountains, separated from Enmerkar's army which continues its march toward Aratta; this composition picks up directly from the end of Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, opening lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 215–220.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, opening lines

[LACUNA: Opening lines (1–15) partially damaged; initial context for Lugalbanda's separation from the army fragmentary | source: etcsl 1.8.2.2, lines 1–15 | scholarly_reconstruction: multiple OB manuscripts overlap to restore most of the opening]

Lugalbanda wanders the mountains alone, surviving through the resourcefulness and piety demonstrated in the preceding composition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 12–13.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 1–30

=== Discovery of the Anzud nest

Lugalbanda discovers the nest of the Anzud bird — an enormous divine eagle — high in the mountains, while the parent birds are away hunting.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 30–70; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 322–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 30–70

The nest contains a single chick of the Anzud bird, left alone while its parents forage; Lugalbanda approaches the chick.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 70–90; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 225–230.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 70–90

=== Lugalbanda honors the chick

Rather than harming or ignoring the chick, Lugalbanda treats it with elaborate care: he feeds it, decorates its face with kohl, and places a crown upon its head, honoring it as a divine being.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 90–140; Black et al. 2004, pp. 14–15.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 90–140

Lugalbanda prepares choice foods for the chick and sets out offerings of fat, honey, and bread, treating the young bird with the ritual deference due to a god's offspring.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 100–130; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 326–329.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 326–329

The care Lugalbanda lavishes on the Anzud chick is presented as an act of reciprocity in advance: by honoring the chick, he creates an obligation that the parent bird must fulfill.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 230–235.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 230–235

=== Return of the Anzud bird

The Anzud bird returns to its nest and finds its chick well-fed, adorned, and cared for; it is overjoyed at the state of its offspring.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 140–180; Black et al. 2004, pp. 15–16.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 140–180

The parent Anzud is described with its terrifying divine radiance (_me-lam_), emphasizing the enormous power of the being whose favor Lugalbanda has won.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 140–160; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 329–332.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 140–160

The Anzud bird, grateful for the care shown to its chick, offers Lugalbanda a reward and asks him to name his desire.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 180–210; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 238–242.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 180–210

=== The gift of supernatural speed

Lugalbanda asks for supernatural speed — the ability to travel with tireless, matchless swiftness — rather than wealth, weapons, or other conventional rewards.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 210–240; Black et al. 2004, pp. 16–17.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 210–240

The Anzud bird grants the gift: Lugalbanda will run without tiring, his feet carrying him faster than any other being, a supernatural endowment that transforms him from a stranded soldier into an extraordinary messenger.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 240–270; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 333–336.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 240–270

The Anzud bird imposes one condition: Lugalbanda must not reveal the source of his speed to anyone; the gift must remain secret.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 270–290; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 245–248.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 270–290

[INFERENCE: The secrecy condition creates narrative tension for future episodes and establishes a divine taboo — the gift's power depends on discretion, a common motif in stories of supernatural endowment. | basis: The Anzud explicitly commands silence, and Lugalbanda observes the prohibition. | risk: The narrative function of the secrecy motif is inferred; the text does not explain why secrecy is required.]

=== Lugalbanda rejoins the army

Empowered with supernatural speed, Lugalbanda crosses the mountain terrain that separates him from Enmerkar's army and rejoins his companions, who are astonished by his survival and arrival.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 290–330; Black et al. 2004, pp. 18–19.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 290–330

The army of Uruk is stalled before Aratta, unable to complete its campaign; Enmerkar needs a messenger to travel to Uruk and consult Inanna about how to proceed.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 330–360; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 252–258.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 330–360

[LACUNA: Minor gaps in lines 350–380 where Lugalbanda delivers Enmerkar's message to Inanna | source: etcsl 1.8.2.2, lines 350–380 | scholarly_reconstruction: composite reconstruction from heavily attested manuscripts covers the gaps]

Lugalbanda volunteers for the mission, and his supernatural speed makes him the only soldier capable of making the journey in time; he runs to Uruk and delivers the message to Inanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 360–400; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 338–341.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 360–400

=== Inanna's counsel

Inanna receives Lugalbanda and gives him counsel for Enmerkar: she instructs the army on how to overcome Aratta's defenses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 400–430; Black et al. 2004, pp. 20–21.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 400–430

Lugalbanda races back to the army with Inanna's message, completing the round trip that would have been impossible without the Anzud's gift.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 430–460; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 262–268.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, lines 430–460

The composition concludes with Lugalbanda's indispensable role confirmed: the abandoned soldier has become Enmerkar's most valuable asset through piety and reciprocity rather than martial prowess.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, concluding lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 268–275.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, concluding lines

=== Themes: reciprocity and piety

The narrative arc — from abandoned soldier to indispensable messenger — is driven by the reciprocity theme: Lugalbanda's unsolicited care for the Anzud chick earns a supernatural reward that determines the outcome of Enmerkar's campaign.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 215–220 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 215–220

Lugalbanda's choice to request speed rather than wealth or power demonstrates the text's valorization of practical humility over conventional heroic ambition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 210–240; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 333–336.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 333–336

The composition teaches that piety and reciprocity toward the divine are rewarded with supernatural gifts; Lugalbanda's humility contrasts with the martial valor typically celebrated in heroic poetry.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 215–220.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 215–220

=== Textual transmission

The poem is one of the most heavily attested Sumerian narrative compositions, preserved in numerous Old Babylonian manuscripts from Nippur and other scribal centers.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.2, manuscript list

Jacobsen's literary translation (pp. 320–344) emphasizes the poetic dimension of the encounter, while Vanstiphout's critical edition (pp. 215–275) documents minor variant readings in the Anzud-encounter and gift-granting scenes.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–344; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 215–275.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–344

This composition, paired with Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave, forms a narrative diptych within the Aratta cycle, linking Lugalbanda's personal story to the larger contest between Uruk and Aratta.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 12–13.]
