== Inanna and Gudam — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

The composition "Inanna and Gudam" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.3.4 and survives only in very fragmentary witnesses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4, composite text (very fragmentary).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4

Very few manuscript copies survive and all are heavily damaged, indicating either extremely limited circulation or catastrophic preservation failure; ETCSL reconstructs a composite from these minimal fragments.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 85 (brief summary reference).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4, manuscript list

Black et al. provide only a brief summary rather than a full translation, given the extreme fragmentary state.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, p. 85.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 85

The text is classified as single-prevalent-reconstructed: it is the only known version, reconstructed from very limited fragments.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4

=== Narrative fragments

[LACUNA: Opening lines 1–20 heavily damaged; the origin and nature of Gudam are only partially described. Too few manuscripts survive to restore the opening. | source: etcsl 1.3.4 | scholarly_reconstruction: none available.]

Gudam is a fearsome monster or creature that terrorises the city of Uruk (Sumerian: Unug), Inanna's city; Gudam devours the city's food and drink.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4

[LACUNA: The central narrative is substantially broken; details of Gudam's rampage and the confrontation between the monster and Inanna are largely lost. No parallel witnesses exist to fill these gaps. | source: etcsl 1.3.4, middle sections | scholarly_reconstruction: none available.]

[INFERENCE: The surviving fragments indicate a narrative structure in which a patron deity (Inanna) defends her city (Uruk) against a monstrous threat (Gudam) — a basic pattern of divine urban protection well attested in Mesopotamian literature. | basis: The fragments name Inanna, Gudam, and Uruk, and the resolution involves Inanna's victory. | risk: The actual narrative may have been more complex than this basic pattern; the fragmentary state prevents certainty.]

Inanna confronts and defeats Gudam, protecting her city Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85 (Inanna myths overview).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4

[LACUNA: The conclusion is fragmentary; the resolution and any hymnic closing are mostly lost. | source: etcsl 1.3.4, closing lines | scholarly_reconstruction: none available.]

=== Significance

The composition follows the widespread Near Eastern pattern of a patron deity slaying a city-threatening monster, a motif also seen in Marduk vs. Tiamat and other Mesopotamian traditions.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 83–85

This is the shortest and most damaged text in the Inanna cycle; only the broadest narrative shape — monster threatens city, goddess defeats monster — can be recovered with confidence from the surviving fragments.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Black et al. 2004, p. 85.]
