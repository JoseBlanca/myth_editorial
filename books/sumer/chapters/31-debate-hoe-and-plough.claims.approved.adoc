=== Setting and context

The composition "The Debate between Hoe and Plough" is catalogued as ETCSL 5.3.1 and survives in numerous Old Babylonian copies from Nippur and other scribal school sites, confirming its place in the standard _edubba_ curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, composite text, lines 1–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, composite text, lines 1–190

The text belongs to the Sumerian debate genre (Sumerian _a-da-min_), in which two personified entities argue their merits before a divine judge who renders a verdict.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 72–73

The two disputants are the Hoe (Sumerian _al_), the essential tool for digging irrigation channels and breaking ground, and the Plough (Sumerian _apin_), the seeder-plough used for large-scale cereal agriculture.footnote:[Civil 1994, passim (agricultural terminology and tool vocabulary); Black et al. 2004, p. 210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=civil-1994 ; loc=passim

=== Cosmogonic prologue

The composition opens with a cosmogonic prologue in which Enlil, king of the gods, separates heaven from earth and establishes the conditions for agriculture and civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 1–20

[LACUNA: Minor damage to the cosmogonic prologue opening (lines 1–10) | source: etcsl 5.3.1 | scholarly_reconstruction: Multiple Nippur school copies overlap and restore the prologue nearly completely]

Enlil creates the Hoe and establishes its functions, situating it within the divine order as the primary tool for working the earth.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 10–20; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–211.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 10–20

The prologue establishes a hierarchy of created things, framing agricultural tools as part of the divinely ordained order of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 1–20

=== Narrative sequence: the debate

The Plough initiates the dispute by asserting its superiority over the Hoe, claiming that its role in seeding and ploughing the fields makes it the more important agricultural implement.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~20–50; Black et al. 2004, pp. 211–212.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 20–50

The Plough boasts of its prestige associations: it is linked with large-scale cereal farming, the work of oxen, and the wealth of the great estates.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~50–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 212–213.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 50–80

The Hoe responds by enumerating its indispensable functions: it digs irrigation channels, builds foundations for houses and temples, plants orchards, and clears land for construction.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~80–120; Black et al. 2004, pp. 213–215.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 80–120

The Hoe argues that it serves in every season and every context — in city building, canal digging, and agriculture — whereas the Plough is limited to the sowing season.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~120–150; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–216.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 120–150

The Hoe further claims that it was created first by Enlil, giving it primordial precedence over the Plough.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~10–20 (prologue) and ~130–140 (Hoe's argument); Black et al. 2004, pp. 210, 215.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 10–20, 130–140

The debate follows the standard genre pattern of escalating claims and counter-claims, with each implement marshalling economic, cosmological, and practical arguments for its superiority.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 72–73

The Plough argues that without it the great grain fields would lie barren and the temples would lack offerings, linking its function to the sustenance of the gods themselves.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~50–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 212–213.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 50–80

The Hoe counters that it is the tool of labourers, builders, and city-makers — that civilisation itself begins with digging, not ploughing.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~80–150; Black et al. 2004, pp. 213–216.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 80–150

=== The verdict

Enlil renders the final verdict in favour of the Hoe, declaring it the winner of the contest.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines ~170–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 217–218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 170–190

[INFERENCE: Enlil's verdict for the Hoe reflects the tool's wider utility and its cosmogonic priority (Enlil created it first in the prologue) | basis: The prologue gives Hoe primordial precedence; the verdict is consistent with the debate structure favouring the more versatile entity | risk: The text does not state the reasoning behind the verdict in explicit analytical terms; this is interpretive]

The verdict follows the standard structural pattern of the debate genre: the disputants argue, and the divine judge resolves the conflict by choosing one over the other, restoring cosmic harmony.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73; Black et al. 2004, p. 218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 72–73

=== Cultural and didactic function

The composition functioned didactically in the _edubba_ curriculum: it taught scribal students the vocabulary and social roles of two key agricultural tools through the structure of competitive argument.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 50–170; Civil 1994, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=civil-1994 ; loc=passim

The cosmogonic prologue situates the debate within the divine order, framing the relative merits of human tools as a question ultimately decided by divine authority.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, lines 1–20

Civil's agricultural studies and Black's anthology both treat the text as a canonical example of the Sumerian debate genre, well-attested and nearly complete.footnote:[Civil 1994, passim; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=civil-1994 ; loc=passim

=== Attestation and transmission

Numerous copies recovered from Nippur and other Old Babylonian school sites confirm the composition's standard place in the scribal curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.1, manuscript list

The ETCSL composite is nearly complete, assembled from tablets with strong agreement, and minor translational differences between ETCSL and Black et al. 2004 do not affect the narrative substance.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–218.]
