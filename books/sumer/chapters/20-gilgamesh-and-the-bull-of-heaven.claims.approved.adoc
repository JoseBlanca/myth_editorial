== Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven — Claims

=== Setting and context

The composition ETCSL 1.8.1.2 is a heavily damaged Sumerian text narrating the episode in which Inanna sends the Bull of Heaven (Sumerian: gud an-na; the standard scholarly name for the figure is Gugalanna) against Uruk, and Gilgamesh (Sumerian: Bilgames) and Enkidu slay it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, composite text (heavily damaged).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.2

[LACUNA: The composition is heavily damaged overall. Three versions survive (unknown provenance, Me-Turan, Nibru Ur III fragment), all substantially fragmentary. However, the version of unknown provenance preserves recognizable narrative of the Bull's devastation (Segment B, lines 55–63) and the Me-Turan version preserves the combat conclusion including the killing blow and aftermath (Segment D, lines 33–53). The Akkadian Gilgamesh Tablet VI is a separate tradition and must not be used to fill gaps. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: Partial from Sumerian sources, with important contributions from the Me-Turan manuscripts published by Cavigneaux and Al-Rawi (1993).]

Inanna, goddess of love and war, patron of Uruk, sends the Bull of Heaven — a cosmic beast — against the city, apparently in response to a perceived affront or rejection by Gilgamesh.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, surviving lines; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

[INFERENCE: The motivation for Inanna's sending of the bull appears to involve Gilgamesh's rejection of her advances or some form of insult, consistent with the broader tradition. | basis: The Sumerian fragments preserve enough context to suggest a conflict between Inanna and Gilgamesh as the trigger. | risk: The precise cause is lost in the Sumerian version; this inference is supported by the broader tradition but cannot be confirmed from the surviving Sumerian text alone.]

=== Narrative sequence

The Bull of Heaven descends upon Uruk and causes devastation. The version of unknown provenance preserves details of the destruction: the Bull devours the pasture, drinks the river in great slurps, breaks the palm trees, and submerges Kulaba (Segment B, lines 55–63).footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment B, lines 55–63.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.2, Segment B, lines 55–63

Gilgamesh and Enkidu confront and slay the Bull of Heaven together. While the version of unknown provenance is fragmentary in the combat section, the Me-Turan version preserves the combat conclusion: Gilgamesh smites the Bull's skull with his axe weighing seven talents, Enkidu hits Inanna with a haunch causing her to flee, and they consign the Bull's hide and intestines to the streets (Me-Turan version, Segment D, lines 33–53).footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Me-Turan version, Segment D, lines 33–53.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.2, Me-Turan version, Segment D, lines 33–53

The slaying of the Bull of Heaven constitutes a direct challenge to divine authority, as the beast was sent by a major deity (Inanna) and its destruction by mortal heroes dramatises the limits of divine power.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

=== Iconographic and cultural evidence

Hero-versus-bull combat imagery is one of the most common Mesopotamian seal motifs, appearing from the Early Dynastic period onward. However, the identification of generic bull-combat contest scenes with the specific Gilgamesh Bull of Heaven narrative is debated, except in a small number of explicitly labeled or contextually clear cases.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

The iconographic prevalence of the bull-slaying scene confirms that this episode was central to the Gilgamesh tradition, even though the Sumerian literary text survives in poor condition.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

=== Relationship to the Akkadian tradition

The Akkadian Gilgamesh epic (Standard Babylonian Tablet VI) contains a fully developed version of the Bull of Heaven episode, but George identifies this as a separate literary tradition that developed from, but is not identical to, the Sumerian composition.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

[INFERENCE: The Sumerian and Akkadian Bull of Heaven episodes share a common narrative kernel — Inanna's offence, the bull's attack, the heroes' slaying — but the Akkadian version represents an independent literary development, not a translation. | basis: George 2003 explicitly distinguishes the two traditions. | risk: The degree of direct literary dependence between the Sumerian and Akkadian versions cannot be established given the fragmentary state of the Sumerian text.]
