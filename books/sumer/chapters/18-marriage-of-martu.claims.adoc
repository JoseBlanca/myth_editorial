== The Marriage of Martu — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

Martu (Akkadian: Amurru) is the divine personification of the Amorite west, the god of nomadic peoples who lived beyond the settled cities of Sumer.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, pp. 156–157.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.1, lines 1–10

The composition ETCSL 1.7.1 survives in several Old Babylonian copies, and its ethnographic content has attracted consistent scholarly interest as a rare Sumerian literary witness to Amorite cultural perception from the urban Sumerian viewpoint.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, pp. 156–157.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 156–157

The narrative is set in the ancient city of Shuruppag, during a festival held by the god Numushda, the local deity who serves as father of the bride in the story.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 1–30.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.1, lines 1–30

=== Narrative sequence

A festival is held in Shuruppag, and the god Martu attends.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 1–40.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.1, lines 1–40

During the festival, Martu sees the daughter of Numushda and falls in love with her, desiring to marry her.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 40–70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.1, lines 40–70

Martu presents himself as a suitor, and the question of his suitability — as a nomadic god among settled city-dwellers — becomes the central dramatic tension.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 70–100; Black et al. 2004, pp. 158–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 158–160

A female friend of the bride delivers an extended warning speech describing the customs of nomadic Amorite life in vivid ethnographic detail: the Amorites eat raw meat, have no permanent houses, do not bury their dead properly, dig up truffles in the steppe, and lack the refinements of urban civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 100–140; Black et al. 2004, pp. 159–161.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.1, lines 100–140

[VARIANT: primary=ETCSL 1.7.1, rendering of the ethnographic catalogue of nomadic habits | alt=Black et al. 2004, pp. 159–161, minor editorial choices in rendering the ethnographic catalogue | chosen: A | reason: ETCSL composite represents consistent manuscript tradition and is the standard text.]

The friend's speech functions as an ethnographic catalogue that reflects the urban Sumerian perception of Amorite peoples: the nomads are defined by what they lack relative to settled civilisation, including proper food preparation, permanent dwelling, and funerary rites.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 100–140; Kramer 1961, pp. 101–103.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 101–103

Despite the warnings, the bride willingly accepts Martu as her husband, choosing the nomadic god over the objections raised by her friend.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 140–155; Black et al. 2004, pp. 161–162.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.1, lines 140–155

The bride's acceptance constitutes the resolution of the narrative: the cultural boundary between settled and nomadic life is crossed by an act of voluntary choice, not coercion.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 161–162.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 161–162

=== Cultural significance

The composition functions as a literary comedy of cultural encounter, staging the tension between settled Sumerian civilisation and the nomadic Amorite world in the form of a marriage negotiation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.1, lines 100–155; Black et al. 2004, pp. 156–162.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 156–162

The didactic function of the text lies in the bride's willingness to accept integration across cultural boundaries, a message that may reflect the historical reality of Amorite absorption into Mesopotamian urban society during the late third and early second millennium BCE.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 156–162; Kramer 1961, pp. 101–103.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 156–162

[INFERENCE: The text's sympathetic treatment of the bride's choice — she is not punished for marrying outside her culture — suggests that by the Old Babylonian period, Amorite integration was an accepted social reality that the literary tradition could treat with humour rather than anxiety. | basis: The historical record shows extensive Amorite settlement and political dominance in Mesopotamia by the early second millennium (the First Dynasty of Babylon was Amorite). | risk: The text's tone is debated; some scholars read the friend's speech as genuinely cautionary rather than comic.]
