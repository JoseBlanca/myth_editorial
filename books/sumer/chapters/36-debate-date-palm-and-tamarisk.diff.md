# Line-edit diff: 36-debate-date-palm-and-tamarisk

Each entry shows the original sentence (or clause) on the left of the arrow and the edited version on the right. Factual content, footnotes, and resolved-marker passages are unchanged.

## Paragraph 1 (opening framing)

- "The tablet was published by Cavigneaux in 2003."
  → "Cavigneaux published the tablet in 2003."
  (Killed the passive; active verb tightens subject-verb distance.)

- "Only nineteen Sumerian lines survive; the composition may be a Sumerian rendering of an Akkadian original."
  → "Only nineteen Sumerian lines survive, and the composition may be a Sumerian rendering of an Akkadian original."
  (Comma-plus-"and" is warmer and less clause-stacked than the semicolon here — the two clauses are narrative continuation, not antithetical contrast.)

## Paragraph 2 (scale of loss)

- "Nineteen lines is very little. It is less than a single column of a standard Old Babylonian tablet."
  → "Nineteen lines is very little -- less than a single column of a standard Old Babylonian tablet."
  (Merged two sentences with an em-dash; the second is a gloss on the first and reads more naturally as an apposition than as a separate copular sentence.)

- "Most of the Sumerian debate poems survive on dozens of manuscripts with hundreds of lines"
  → "Most Sumerian debate poems survive on dozens of manuscripts running to hundreds of lines"
  (Dropped "of the" — the category is general, not a specific set. "With hundreds of lines" → "running to hundreds of lines": more active, more specific.)

- "The composition is catalogued among the debate poems and survives in a fragmentary state. Fewer copies survive than for the major debate poems, indicating more limited circulation in Old Babylonian scribal schools."
  → "The composition belongs to the debate genre but reaches us in a fragmentary state, and fewer copies survive than for the major debate poems -- a sign of more limited circulation in Old Babylonian scribal schools."
  (Combined the two sentences into one, with the logical spine: it's a debate poem, but fragmentary, and the scarcity of copies is a sign of limited circulation. "Is catalogued among" → "belongs to": kills the nominalised passive. "Survives in a fragmentary state" → "reaches us in a fragmentary state": active, concrete. "Indicating" → "a sign of": the participial dangled in the original; this ties it cleanly to the clause.)

## Paragraph 3 (genre framing)

- "the domesticated tree that provides fruit, syrup, and fibre, and the wild tree that grows without human care and serves ritual and practical functions"
  → "the domesticated tree that yields fruit, syrup, and fibre, and the wild tree that grows without human care and serves ritual and practical ends"
  (Plainer verb "yields" for trees and their produce; "functions" → "ends" avoids the echo of "functions" later and is more concrete.)

## Section "The arguments" — Date Palm paragraph

- "Date Palm argues its superiority on the grounds of its immense economic value: it provides dates ... making it the most productive tree"
  → "Date Palm argues its superiority on the grounds of immense economic value: it provides dates ... which makes it the most productive tree"
  (Dropped the redundant second "its" — the economic value is clearly Date Palm's from context. Replaced the dangling "-ing" participle "making" with the explicit relative clause "which makes"; the participle risks attaching to "construction" rather than to the whole enumeration.)

- "The argument is practical and quantitative: Date Palm lists the things it gives to human civilisation, and the list is long."
  → "The argument is practical and quantitative. Date Palm lists the things it gives to human civilisation, and the list is long."
  (Broke the sentence at the colon into two; the colon was doing too much work — the second half is not an unpacking of the first but a separate observation.)

- "the products it yields are the products of that partnership between tree and cultivator"
  → "its products are the fruits of that partnership between tree and cultivator"
  (Removed the repetition of "products ... products" that the original awkwardly doubled. "Fruits of that partnership" carries a quiet pun appropriate to the subject — a date palm partnership that literally bears fruit — and tightens the clause.)

## Section "What is lost"

- "Whether the same pattern obtained here, and if so, which tree prevailed, the surviving nineteen lines do not tell us."
  → "Whether the same pattern held here, and if so which tree prevailed, the surviving nineteen lines do not tell us."
  ("Obtained" in the sense of "held, was in force" is archaic and can confuse readers; "held" is the ordinary modern idiom. Also removed the second comma for rhythm — "if so which tree prevailed" reads cleanly as a unit.)

## Section "The composition in context"

- "suggesting a deep and persistent literary lineage"
  → "pointing to a deep and persistent literary lineage"
  ("Suggesting" is one of the limp participles Pinker warns against; "pointing to" gives the same hedge with a concrete verb.)

- "the surviving portions reveal a composition concerned with the relative merits of cultivated and wild nature"
  → "what survives reveals a composition concerned with the relative merits of cultivated and wild nature"
  (Killed the zombie noun "the surviving portions" → clause "what survives". Tighter subject-verb distance; more active.)

- "indicating either more limited circulation in the _edubba_ curriculum or poorer archaeological recovery"
  → "pointing to either more limited circulation in the _edubba_ curriculum or poorer archaeological recovery"
  (Same fix as above — replaces the limp participle "indicating" with the concrete verbal phrase "pointing to". Keeps parallelism with the earlier change.)

- "The ETCSL composite represents the only available reconstruction from limited fragmentary witnesses"
  → "The ETCSL composite is the only available reconstruction from limited fragmentary witnesses"
  ("Represents" is a throat-clearing verb here; "is" says the same thing without the academic hedge.)

## Final paragraph (coda)

- "is one that has not lost its force"
  → "has not lost its force"
  (Dropped the padding "is one that"; the subject ("the question itself") already carries the weight. Pinker calls this "throat-clearing".)

- "where the Garden of Eden represents cultivated paradise set against the wilderness beyond"
  → "where the Garden of Eden stands as cultivated paradise against the wilderness beyond"
  ("Represents ... set against" is a nominalised construction; "stands as ... against" is a single verb phrase with a concrete spatial image, matching the "tension" and "opposition" vocabulary of the surrounding paragraph.)
