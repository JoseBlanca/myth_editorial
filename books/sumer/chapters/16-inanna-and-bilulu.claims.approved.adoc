== Inanna and Bilulu: Vengeance for the Shepherd — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

The composition "Inanna and Bilulu" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.4.4 and comprises approximately 190 lines in the composite text.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4, composite text, lines 1–190.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.4.4

Very few manuscript witnesses survive, indicating limited circulation compared to other Inanna and Dumuzid texts.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.4.4, manuscript list

The text is classified as single-prevalent-reconstructed: only a small number of fragmentary witnesses survive, and the ETCSL composite is the best available reconstruction.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.4.4

Jacobsen's literary translation in _The Harps That Once..._ (1987) remains the most detailed study of this composition; his interpretation of the transformation scene is influential but debated.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 315–325

The narrative is set within the broader Dumuzid dying-god cycle: Dumuzid is dead (or has been taken to the underworld), and Inanna mourns and seeks vengeance.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 97–101.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 97–101

=== Inanna's mourning

[LACUNA: Opening lines 1–20 heavily damaged; the context for Inanna's mourning is partially lost. Very few manuscript witnesses survive to restore the opening. | source: etcsl 1.4.4 | scholarly_reconstruction: none available.]

Inanna mourns the dead Dumuzid, her consort, the shepherd-god; her lament is described in terms of bitter weeping and wandering the steppe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–318.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 315–318

Inanna's mourning echoes the broader tradition of ritual laments for Dumuzid attested across the Ur III and Old Babylonian periods.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–318; Kramer 1961, pp. 97–101.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 97–101

=== Bilulu and Girgire

Bilulu is identified as an old woman (or bandit matriarch) held responsible for Dumuzid's death or complicit in it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 318–320.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 318–320

Girgire is identified as the son of Bilulu, likewise implicated in the events surrounding Dumuzid's death.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 318–320.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 318–320

[INFERENCE: The exact nature of Bilulu's and Girgire's guilt is debated; Jacobsen interprets them as steppe bandits or desert marauders responsible for Dumuzid's death, but the fragmentary text does not spell out the accusation in full. | basis: Jacobsen 1987, pp. 318–320, provides this interpretation from context. | risk: Other scholars may read the relationship differently given the gaps.]

=== Inanna's confrontation and the transformation

Inanna confronts Bilulu and Girgire, seeking vengeance for Dumuzid.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–322.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–322

[LACUNA: Lines 95–130: the central section describing the encounter with Bilulu is fragmentary; the transformation scene is partly broken. Insufficient parallel witnesses exist to fill these gaps. | source: etcsl 1.4.4 | scholarly_reconstruction: none sufficient; Jacobsen fills narrative gaps interpretively.]

Inanna transforms Bilulu into a waterskin used for pouring desert libations — specifically, the water offered to the dead in the steppe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–325

Girgire is transformed alongside his mother, becoming a desert spirit or protective genius associated with the steppe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–325

=== Aetiological function

The transformation provides an aetiology for the desert libation practice: Bilulu-as-waterskin is the vessel through which water is poured for Dumuzid's spirit in the steppe.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–325

The composition thus connects divine vengeance to ritual practice: the punishment of the guilty simultaneously creates the cultic apparatus for mourning the victim.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325; ETCSL 1.4.4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–325

=== Significance

The composition is the final narrative instalment in the Dumuzid dying-god cycle, following the Descent (ETCSL 1.4.1) and the Dream (ETCSL 1.4.3): mourning gives way to vengeance, and vengeance gives way to ritual institution.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 97–101; ETCSL 1.4.4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 97–101

Jacobsen's treatment remains the most detailed study; his interpretation of the transformation is influential but should be read with awareness that it fills significant textual gaps interpretively.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 315–325

The motif of divine transformation as punishment — turning offenders into objects or creatures that serve a specific purpose — is a distinctive form of Sumerian divine justice.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 320–325
