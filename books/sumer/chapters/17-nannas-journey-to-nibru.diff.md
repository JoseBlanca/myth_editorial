### 1. Opening paragraph — kill "The fact that" nominalization and tighten subject-verb distance

- Original: "The fact that the text was valued at both cities is itself significant: this is a composition about the relationship between Ur and Nippur, and it was preserved by the scribes of both."
- Edited: "That both cities valued the text is itself significant: this is a composition about the relationship between Ur and Nippur, and the scribes of both preserved it."

Rationale: "The fact that" is a classic Pinker zombie construction; "That both cities valued the text" says the same thing with the verb restored to real work. The tail clause moves from passive ("was preserved by") to active ("preserved it"), giving the scribes agency.

### 2. Political-geography paragraph — replace "is central to" cliché with an active verb and past tense to match surrounding tense

- Original: "Two cities dominate the landscape of Sumerian civilisation in the late third millennium..."
- Edited: "Two cities dominated the landscape of Sumerian civilisation in the late third millennium..."

Rationale: The late third millennium is past; the present-tense "dominate" reads as historical-present inconsistently mixed with "was the economic powerhouse" in the next sentence. Past tense matches.

### 3. Political-geography paragraph — replace vague "was central to" with concrete "anchored"

- Original: "The axis between these two cities was central to Ur III royal ideology..."
- Edited: "The axis between these two cities anchored Ur III royal ideology..."

Rationale: "was central to" is a weak copula-with-abstract-noun construction. "Anchored" gives the axis something to do. Same factual claim (centrality).

### 4. Nanna paragraph — tighten "simultaneously" filler

- Original: "The composition works on all three levels simultaneously, and the Sumerians would not have seen any contradiction between them."
- Edited: "The composition works on all three levels at once, and the Sumerians would not have seen any contradiction between them."

Rationale: "simultaneously" is heavy Latinate filler where "at once" does the same work with half the syllables. Pinker's classic-style preference for plain Anglo-Saxon.

### 5. Narrative-opening paragraph — convert nominalization "decision to travel" to finite verb

- Original: "The narrative opens with Nanna's decision to travel from Ur to Nippur to visit his father Enlil."
- Edited: "The narrative opens as Nanna decides to travel from Ur to Nippur to visit his father Enlil."

Rationale: "Nanna's decision" is a zombie noun; "Nanna decides" restores the verb and the agent. The temporal "as" replaces the flabby "opens with", making the relation explicit.

### 8. Administrative-inventory paragraph — tighten "is not accidental"

- Original: "The resemblance to bureaucratic records is not accidental."
- Edited: "The resemblance to bureaucratic records is no accident."

Rationale: "is not accidental" hedges behind an adjective; "is no accident" is the idiomatic English form and reads more directly. Same factual claim.

### 9. "This is a composition" paragraph — remove throat-clearing

- Original: "This is a composition in which myth and administration converge."
- Edited: "In this composition, myth and administration converge."

Rationale: "This is a composition in which X" is a there-is/this-is padding construction. Fronting the prepositional phrase puts the subject and verb next to each other and kills the nominalized "composition in which" relative clause.

### 10. Itinerary paragraph — replace "establishing" with plainer verb

- Original: "At each stopping point, Nanna presents gifts to the local deity and receives a welcome, establishing a ritual geography of southern Mesopotamia that maps the sacred landscape with the precision of a surveyor."
- Edited: "At each stop Nanna presents gifts to the local deity and receives a welcome, laying out a ritual geography of southern Mesopotamia that maps the sacred landscape with the precision of a surveyor."

Rationale: "stopping point" is wordy for "stop". "Establishing" — a Latinate verb frequent in academic prose — becomes "laying out", which keeps the metaphor of a surveyor doing physical work. Same factual claim.

### 11. Map paragraph — replace "provides evidence for" with "documents"

- Original: "The itinerary itself, with its sequence of named cities and waterways, provides evidence for the actual geography and canal network of the Ur-Nippur corridor as understood in the Old Babylonian period."
- Edited: "The itinerary itself, with its sequence of named cities and waterways, documents the actual geography and canal network of the Ur-Nippur corridor as understood in the Old Babylonian period."

Rationale: "provides evidence for" is a nominalization-plus-verb pattern where a single strong verb will serve. "Documents" is what the itinerary does.

### 12. Map paragraph, closing sentence — tighten subject-verb distance and kill "as" redundancy

- Original: "When a scribal student copied this text, he was learning the geography of southern Mesopotamia at the same time as he was learning its mythology. The pedagogy, as so often in Sumerian education, was doing several things at once."
- Edited: "When a scribal student copied this text, he was learning the geography of southern Mesopotamia at the same time he was learning its mythology. Sumerian pedagogy, as so often, did several things at once."

Rationale: the second "as" in "at the same time as" is redundant. "The pedagogy... in Sumerian education" circles back to Sumer twice; "Sumerian pedagogy" places the qualifier up front once. "Was doing" collapses to "did" — a simple preterite where the progressive adds no aspectual meaning.

- Edited: "The blessing is the counterpart to the cargo — the return on Nanna's investment, to put it in terms the Ur III accountants would have recognised."

Rationale: "if one wants to put it" is throat-clearing; "to put it" is the standard tight form of the same idiomatic hedge. Same meaning, less throat.

### 14. Same paragraph — "that no barge can carry" → "no barge can carry"

- Original: "Enlil sends back something that no barge can carry: divine favour."
- Edited: "Enlil sends back something no barge can carry: divine favour."

Rationale: the restrictive-clause "that" is optional and here deletable for rhythm. Pinker on streamlining function words.

### 15. Legitimation-mechanics paragraph — kill throat-clearing opener and convert zombie "functions as"

- Original: "The blessing functions as a legitimation device, and it is worth pausing to consider the mechanics of that legitimation."
- Edited: "The blessing legitimates, and the mechanics of that legitimation repay a closer look."

Rationale: "functions as a legitimation device" is pure nominalization — a verb turned into a thing ("legitimation") and handed a helper verb ("functions as"). The simple verb "legitimates" does the whole job. "It is worth pausing to consider" is the classic academic throat-clearing Pinker targets; "repay a closer look" is idiomatic, shorter, and puts the mechanics in subject position where they belong.

### 16. Aetiology paragraph — replace zombie phrase "an aetiology of institutional practice"

- Original: "The composition, then, is not primarily a narrative of adventure or conflict. It is an aetiology of institutional practice. Its ritual-aetiological function is primary..."
- Edited: "The composition, then, is not primarily a narrative of adventure or conflict. It explains an institution. Its ritual-aetiological function comes first..."

Rationale: "aetiology of institutional practice" is dense academic diction that restates what the next sentence ("ritual-aetiological function") says anyway. "It explains an institution" is the plain-English gloss, and it sets up the following sentence rather than competing with it. "is primary" → "comes first" converts copula-plus-abstract-adjective to a verb.

### 17. Royal-legitimation paragraph — tighten "It is a relationship of exchange"

- Original: "The relationship is not one of subordination in any simple sense — Ur is not a vassal paying tribute to an overlord. It is a relationship of exchange."
- Edited: "The relationship is not subordination in any simple sense — Ur is not a vassal paying tribute to an overlord. It is an exchange."

Rationale: "one of subordination" and "a relationship of exchange" are nominalization padding around plain nouns. "Subordination" and "exchange" stand on their own; "relationship" is already the subject of the preceding clause, so repeating it in "a relationship of exchange" is needless.

### 18. Enki-comparison paragraph — replace "proceeds directly" and "participates in"

- Original: "Where Enki's journey proceeds directly from Eridu to Nippur without lingering on intermediate stops, Nanna's journey maps the entire canal corridor between Ur and Nippur, naming each city and cult centre along the route. The text establishes a ritual geography — a sacred landscape in which every settlement between origin and destination participates in the procession."
- Edited: "Where Enki's journey runs directly from Eridu to Nippur without lingering on intermediate stops, Nanna's journey maps the entire canal corridor between Ur and Nippur, naming each city and cult centre along the route. The text lays out a ritual geography — a sacred landscape in which every settlement between origin and destination takes part in the procession."

Rationale: "proceeds" → "runs" (plain Anglo-Saxon verb for a journey); "establishes a ritual geography" and "participates in the procession" are both Latinate nominalization-verbs. "Lays out" and "takes part in" carry the same meaning in plainer English. Classic-style preference throughout.

### 20. Closing paragraph — replace throat-clearing "What we have in this composition, finally, is"
