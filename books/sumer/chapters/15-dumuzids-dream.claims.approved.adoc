=== Setting and context

The composition "Dumuzid's Dream" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.4.3 and comprises approximately 260 lines in the composite text.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, composite text, lines 1–260.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.4.3

The text is well-attested in Old Babylonian copies and forms part of the broader Dumuzid dying-god tradition in the scribal curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, manuscript list; Alster 1972, pp. 7–15.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 7–15

Alster's 1972 monograph provides the critical edition and remains the foundational philological study.footnote:[Alster, B., _Dumuzi's Dream: Aspects of Oral Poetry in a Sumerian Myth_ (Copenhagen: Akademisk Forlag, 1972).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=full edition

The text is classified as single-prevalent: there is broad manuscript agreement and no substantive narrative divergence among witnesses, as confirmed by Alster's collation.footnote:[Alster 1972; ETCSL 1.4.3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=full edition

The narrative is set against the background of Inanna's Descent (ETCSL 1.4.1): Dumuzid has been marked as Inanna's substitute in the underworld, and the _galla_ demons are coming to seize him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–72.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 71–72

=== The prophetic dream

Dumuzid, the shepherd-god and consort of Inanna, has a terrifying prophetic dream and awakes in distress.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 72; Alster 1972, pp. 40–45.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.4.3, lines 1–20

Dumuzid summons his sister Geshtinanna and recounts the dream to her, asking her to interpret it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 20–30; Black et al. 2004, p. 72.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 72

The dream contains a sequence of symbolic images: reeds rise around him, a single reed trembles, twin reeds are removed one by one, trees rise around him and water is poured on his holy hearth.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, dream sequence; Black et al. 2004, pp. 72–73; Alster 1972, pp. 40–50.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 40–50

The dream images include: his drinking cup falls from its peg, his shepherd's crook vanishes, an eagle seizes a lamb from the sheepfold, a falcon catches a sparrow, his goats drag their beards in the dust, and his sheep paw the ground with their bent limbs.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Alster 1972, pp. 40–50.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 40–50

The churning stick lies on the ground and the cup no longer pours; the images systematically dismantle the pastoral symbols of Dumuzid's identity as shepherd.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Alster 1972, pp. 40–50.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.4.3

=== Geshtinanna's interpretation

Geshtinanna interprets each dream image as foretelling Dumuzid's doom: the _galla_ demons are coming to seize him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 30–55; Black et al. 2004, p. 73; Alster 1972, pp. 50–55.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 50–55

[LACUNA: Lines 45–55: several lines in the dream-interpretation sequence are partially damaged. | source: etcsl 1.4.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: Alster 1972 collates multiple witnesses that fill most gaps.]

Geshtinanna tells Dumuzid that each pastoral symbol undone in the dream represents a specific element of his impending capture: the reeds are the demons, the fallen drinking cup is his overthrown status, the scattered flock is his dispersed protection.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Alster 1972, pp. 50–55.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 50–55

=== Flight and transformations

Dumuzid flees across the steppe, pursued by the _galla_ demons.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 55–100; Black et al. 2004, pp. 73–74.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 73–74

Dumuzid appeals to the sun god Utu (brother of his wife Inanna and thus his brother-in-law) to transform him so he may escape.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 74; Alster 1972, pp. 55–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 55–60

Utu grants the transformation: Dumuzid's hands are changed into gazelle hands (hooves) and his feet into gazelle feet, enabling him to run.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 74; Alster 1972, pp. 55–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 55–60

Dumuzid flees to a succession of hiding places, repeatedly requesting and receiving further transformations to escape the pursuing _galla_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 74–75.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 74–75

The flight is described in a repetitive formulaic pattern: Dumuzid reaches a refuge, the _galla_ find him, he flees again — the structure emphasising the inescapability of his fate.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Alster 1972, pp. 55–65.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 55–65

=== Geshtinanna's loyalty and the sheepfold

Dumuzid hides in the sheepfold of his sister Geshtinanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 75; Alster 1972, pp. 65–70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 65–70

The _galla_ demons arrive and interrogate Geshtinanna, demanding to know Dumuzid's location; she refuses to betray her brother despite their threats.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 75; Alster 1972, pp. 65–70.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 75

A friend or companion of Dumuzid (sometimes identified as his unnamed friend) betrays him to the _galla_, revealing his hiding place.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 75–76; Alster 1972, pp. 70–75.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 70–75

=== Capture

The _galla_ demons seize Dumuzid; the capture fulfils every element of the prophetic dream.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 230–260; Black et al. 2004, pp. 76–77; Alster 1972, pp. 75–80.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 75–80

Dumuzid is taken to the nether world; the narrative closes with the inevitability of the dream's fulfilment confirmed.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, closing lines; Black et al. 2004, p. 77.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 77

=== Theological and literary significance

The composition dramatises the inevitability of death through a prophetic dream that unfolds exactly as foretold; foreknowledge does not enable escape.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 1–50; Alster 1972, pp. 40–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 40–60

Alster's monograph established the text as a key exemplar of Sumerian oral-poetic technique: the formulaic repetitions of the flight sequence, the catalogue of transformations, and the dream-interpretation genre all reflect oral compositional patterns.footnote:[Alster 1972, pp. 40–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=alster-1972 ; loc=pp. 40–60

The contrast between Geshtinanna's absolute loyalty and the friend's betrayal constitutes the emotional and ethical core of the narrative.footnote:[Alster 1972, pp. 65–75; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 28–55.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 28–55

Jacobsen discusses the composition in the context of the Dumuzid cycle's pastoral imagery and the tension between the steppe (pastoral freedom) and the underworld (inescapable fate).footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 28–55.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 28–55
