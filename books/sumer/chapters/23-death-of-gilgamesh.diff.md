## Paragraph 1 (opening — catalogue, witnesses, retelling)

**Original:**
> The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.8.1.3 and survives on fragmentary tablets from Nippur (Sumerian: Nibru) and Tell Haddad (ancient Me-Turan).footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, bibliography.] It is fragmentary; the Nippur and Tell Haddad versions differ significantly in ritual content.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, composite text.] The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by ETCSL composite text.footnote:[ETCSL composite text.]

**Edited:**
> The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.8.1.3 and survives on fragmentary tablets from Nippur (Sumerian: Nibru) and Tell Haddad (ancient Me-Turan).footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, bibliography.] The Nippur and Tell Haddad versions differ significantly in ritual content.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3, composite text.] The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite, supplemented by ETCSL composite text.footnote:[ETCSL composite text.]

Dropped the redundant opener and promoted the second clause to a full sentence. Also "the ETCSL composite text" was repeated — dropped "text" on the second reference (the "supplemented by" tail still carries "ETCSL composite text" verbatim per source parity).

## Paragraph 2 (two co-equal witnesses)

**Original:**
> The discovery of the Tell Haddad tablets significantly expanded scholarly knowledge of this composition, adding ritual and funerary content not attested in the Nippur fragments.

**Edited:**
> The Tell Haddad tablets expanded scholarly knowledge of this composition considerably, adding ritual and funerary content not attested in the Nippur fragments.

*Rationale:* "The discovery of the Tell Haddad tablets" is a zombie-noun opening (nominalized event standing in for the agent). Replacing with "The Tell Haddad tablets" puts a concrete subject in subject position, tightens subject-verb distance, and shortens the sentence. "significantly" → "considerably" as a trailing adverb avoids the split between verb and object, and "considerably" reads more naturally in British-scholarly register.

## Paragraph 5 (every other Sumerian Gilgamesh narrative)

**Original:**
> Every other Sumerian Gilgamesh narrative is about what the hero does with his life. This one is about what happens when the life ends.

**Edited:**
> Every other Sumerian Gilgamesh narrative tells what the hero does with his life. This one tells what happens when the life ends.

*Rationale:* "is about" is a vague copula — "tells" is a concrete verb that fits both sentences and creates parallel structure. The pair now reads as two balanced declarations rather than two abstract propositions. Pinker's classic style prefers concrete verbs over "is about."

## Paragraph 7 (Gilgamesh confronts mortality)

**Original:**
> Gilgamesh, having achieved great deeds during his life, is confronted with the inevitability of his own death -- the gods have decreed that even the greatest hero must die.

**Edited:**
> Having achieved great deeds during his life, Gilgamesh confronts the inevitability of his own death -- the gods have decreed that even the greatest hero must die.

*Rationale:* Moving the participial phrase to the front shortens the subject-verb distance (previously "Gilgamesh ... is confronted"). "is confronted with" is a passive with a dummy agent; "confronts" is active and assigns the action to the right party — Gilgamesh does the confronting of his mortality, he isn't the object of some external confronter. No meaning change: the gods decreeing death is the next clause.

**Original:**
> Gilgamesh has slain Huwawa in the Cedar Forest, has crossed mountains and rivers, has done everything that a mortal can do to distinguish himself -- and none of it changes the fundamental arithmetic.

**Edited:**
> Gilgamesh has slain Huwawa in the Cedar Forest, has crossed mountains and rivers, has done everything a mortal can do to distinguish himself -- and none of it changes the fundamental arithmetic.

*Rationale:* Dropped the optional "that" complementizer ("everything that a mortal can do" → "everything a mortal can do") — Pinker notes the "that" often adds no information and lengthens the clause. The parallel triad "has slain / has crossed / has done" reads cleaner without it.

## Paragraph 9 (closing lesson on fame vs. eternal life)

**Original:**
> This is the lesson the tradition has been teaching across every Gilgamesh composition:

**Edited:**
> That is the lesson the tradition has been teaching across every Gilgamesh composition:

*Rationale:* "This is" often points forward; "That is" refers back to the divine decree just stated in the italic parenthetical above. The demonstrative adjustment makes the paragraph transition explicit — the lesson being invoked is the one just named, not a new one the reader is about to meet.

## Paragraph 10 (funeral preparations, Nippur vs. Tell Haddad divergence)

**Original:**
> The Tell Haddad version preserves substantially more: a detailed protocol of offerings, a formal procedure for the reception of a dead king.

**Edited:**
> The Tell Haddad version preserves much more: a detailed protocol of offerings, a formal procedure for the reception of a dead king.

*Rationale:* "substantially more" is Latinate padding; "much more" is plainer and shorter in Pinker's classic-style register. The next paragraph also opens with "substantially more detail" in a different grammatical slot — varying the word here prevents a visible repetition across adjacent paragraphs. (The next paragraph's phrasing was kept because it belongs to a different syntactic frame — "substantially more detail about the funerary ritual" — and rewording it would change emphasis.)

## Paragraph 12 (gifts to underworld deities, parallel with Inanna's Descent)

**Original:**
> Both are acts of submission to the underworld's authority, but the forms differ.

**Edited:**
> Both acts submit to the underworld's authority, but the forms differ.

*Rationale:* "are acts of submission" is a zombie noun ("submission") plus copula. Converting to the active verb "submit" makes the sentence shorter and more direct. The meaning is preserved: both descents are acts of submission; in the edit, both acts are the grammatical subject that submits.

## Paragraph 14 (Gilgamesh elevated to judge of the dead)

**Original:**
> Gilgamesh's elevation to judge of the dead places him alongside Ningishzida and other underworld deities, transforming him from a mortal hero into a figure with a permanent divine function in the afterlife.

**Edited:**
> Elevation to judge of the dead places Gilgamesh alongside Ningishzida and other underworld deities, turning him from a mortal hero into a figure with a permanent divine function in the afterlife.

*Rationale:* Dropping the possessive "Gilgamesh's" and moving the proper noun into object position is a small rhythm improvement — the sentence now has a fuller subject ("Elevation to judge of the dead") before the verb, which matches the formal register of the sentence. "transforming" → "turning" is a plain-English substitute in the Pinker spirit; "transform" is a Latinate verb where the shorter Anglo-Saxon equivalent fits the same slot without meaning loss.

## Paragraph 17 (royal legitimation — all dead kings)

**Original:**
> If Gilgamesh exercises divine authority in the underworld, then all dead kings participate in that reflected authority, and the living owe them something -- offerings, rituals, the ongoing maintenance of their cult.

**Edited:**
> If Gilgamesh exercises divine authority in the underworld, then all dead kings share in that reflected authority, and the living owe them something -- offerings, rituals, the ongoing maintenance of their cult.

*Rationale:* "participate in" is a Latinate compound where "share in" is shorter and plainer with the same meaning in this context (other dead kings enjoy some portion of Gilgamesh's reflected authority). Classic style: prefer the plainer verb when it does the same work.

## Paragraph 19 (closing — damage to the text)

**Original:**
> The Death of Gilgamesh is a composition about the limits of human achievement, and the tablets that preserve it have themselves been subject to the same forces the text describes.

**Edited:**
> The Death of Gilgamesh is a composition about the limits of human achievement, and the tablets that preserve it have themselves suffered the same forces the text describes.

*Rationale:* "have themselves been subject to" is a five-word nominalized passive ("be subject to" + auxiliary). The verb "suffered" does the same work in one word and preserves the meaning — the tablets have undergone the very forces of decay the composition describes. Shorter, more vivid, and thematically apt (the chapter is about mortality and decay).
