== The Flood Story: The Eridu Genesis — Claims

=== Setting and context

The composition survives on a single tablet, CBS 10673, excavated at Nippur; this is the sole Sumerian witness to the flood narrative and all scholarly editions depend on this one manuscript.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, manuscript note; Jacobsen 1987, p. 145.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, manuscript note

The tablet is in extremely poor condition, with large portions of every column broken or illegible; the text that survives represents only fragments of a much longer composition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 97–98.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4

=== Antediluvian civilization (largely lost)

[LACUNA: Columns i–ii (opening) are largely lost; the creation of humans, the establishment of cities, and the institution of kingship survive only in fragments | source: etcsl 1.7.4, columns i–ii | scholarly_reconstruction: no other Sumerian manuscripts preserve these sections; CBS 10673 is the sole witness]

Surviving fragments from the opening columns appear to describe the establishment of cities and the institution of kingship before the flood, suggesting an antediluvian civilization narrative.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, columns i–ii (fragmentary); Kramer 1961, pp. 97–99.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, columns i–ii

[INFERENCE: The antediluvian section likely described the creation of humanity and the founding of the first cities, based on surviving fragments that mention cities and kingship. | basis: Fragmentary lines reference cities (including Eridu) and the descent of kingship from heaven. | risk: The actual content of the lost columns is unknown; this inference rests on very small textual fragments.]

=== The divine decision to destroy

The gods in assembly decide to send a flood to destroy humanity; the reason for this decision is not fully preserved in the surviving text.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iii (fragmentary); Jacobsen 1987, pp. 146–147.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, column iii

[LACUNA: The divine decision to destroy humanity and the circumstances leading to it survive only in broken passages in column iii; details of the gods' deliberation are largely lost | source: etcsl 1.7.4, column iii | scholarly_reconstruction: no other Sumerian manuscripts preserve these sections]

Enlil appears to play a role in the decision to send the flood, consistent with his position as chief god of the pantheon and the deity whose commands the other gods must obey.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iii (fragmentary); Kramer 1961, pp. 99–100.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 99–100

=== Enki's warning to Ziusudra

Enki, god of wisdom and fresh water, warns Ziusudra of the coming flood by speaking through a reed wall, circumventing a divine oath not to reveal the gods' decision directly to a mortal.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iii–iv; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 147–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, column iii–iv

Ziusudra is described as a pious king who receives Enki's warning; his righteousness is the basis for his selection as the survivor.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iii; Jacobsen 1987, p. 147.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=p. 147

The reed-wall device — Enki addressing the wall rather than speaking directly to Ziusudra — is a distinctive narrative element that preserves the god's technical obedience to the divine decree while subverting its intent.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 147–148; Kramer 1961, pp. 99–100.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 147–148

=== The flood and the boat

Ziusudra builds a boat in response to Enki's warning; the details of the boat's construction are largely lost in the surviving text.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iv (fragmentary); Jacobsen 1987, p. 148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, column iv

[LACUNA: Details of the boat construction, the loading of the vessel, and the flood itself are largely lost; only fragments survive | source: etcsl 1.7.4, columns iv–v | scholarly_reconstruction: no other Sumerian manuscripts preserve these sections; Akkadian parallels (Atrahasis, Gilgamesh XI) are distinct traditions and must not be used to fill gaps]

The flood strikes; the surviving fragments indicate a catastrophic deluge that destroys the world, though the Sumerian text preserves very little of the actual flood description.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column v (fragmentary); Kramer 1961, p. 100.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, column v

=== Aftermath and Ziusudra's immortality

After the flood, Ziusudra opens a window in the boat and the sun god Utu shines in; Ziusudra prostrates himself before Utu.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column v–vi; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 148–149.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, column v–vi

Ziusudra offers sacrifices to the gods after the flood, performing the ritual acts of a pious survivor giving thanks for deliverance.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column vi (fragmentary); Jacobsen 1987, p. 149.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=p. 149

The gods — specifically An (the sky god) and Enlil — grant Ziusudra eternal life and settle him in Dilmun, the paradise land associated with purity and divine favor.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column vi; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 149–150.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.7.4, column vi

[LACUNA: The concluding lines describing Ziusudra's apotheosis and settlement in Dilmun are damaged; the full terms of his immortality and the ending of the composition are not fully preserved | source: etcsl 1.7.4, column vi | scholarly_reconstruction: no other Sumerian manuscripts preserve these sections]

=== Textual state and scholarly caution

The Eridu Genesis (as it is conventionally called) is one of the most fragmentary major Sumerian compositions; the surviving text amounts to disconnected passages from a work that was originally much longer.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4; Black et al. 2004, pp. 284–285.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 284–285

Despite its extreme fragmentation, the text has received outsized scholarly attention as the earliest known flood narrative, predating the Akkadian Atrahasis and the Gilgamesh XI flood account.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 145–150; Kramer 1961, pp. 97–101.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 145–150

The Akkadian flood traditions (Atrahasis, Gilgamesh XI) are related but distinct compositions; they must not be conflated with or used to reconstruct the Sumerian Eridu Genesis.footnote:[George 2003 (comparative only); Jacobsen 1987, pp. 145–150.]
