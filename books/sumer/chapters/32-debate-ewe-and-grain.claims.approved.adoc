== The Debate between Ewe and Grain — Claims

=== Setting and context

The composition "The Debate between Ewe and Grain" is catalogued as ETCSL 5.3.2 and survives in multiple Old Babylonian copies from Nippur scribal schools, confirming its wide circulation as a standard curriculum text.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, composite text, lines 1–192; Black et al. 2004, pp. 219–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, composite text, lines 1–192

The text belongs to the Sumerian debate genre and features two divine personifications — Lahar (Ewe), embodying pastoralism and livestock, and Ashnan (Grain), embodying cereal agriculture — who argue their relative merits before a divine audience.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 219–220; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 219–220

=== Cosmogonic prologue: humanity before divine gifts

The composition opens with an extended cosmogonic prologue describing a primordial era in which humans existed without the gifts of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 1–40; Black et al. 2004, pp. 219–220.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 1–40

In this primordial state, Ewe had not yet been created and Grain had not yet appeared: humans ate grass with their mouths like animals, drank water from ditches, and went about naked.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 1–20; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 1–20

The gods created Ewe and Grain in the "creation chamber of the gods" (the _dul-kug_, the holy mound) specifically to relieve humanity's primitive condition and provide them with food and clothing.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 20–40; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 155–160

The prologue establishes that Ewe provides wool for clothing and Grain provides bread for nourishment — the two fundamental necessities that distinguish civilised human life from the animal existence that preceded it.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 30–40; Black et al. 2004, p. 220.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 30–40

Enlil and Enki participate in the creation of the two entities, assigning each a specific role in sustaining human civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 20–40; Black et al. 2004, p. 220.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 20–40

[INFERENCE: The cosmogonic prologue establishes the debate's theological stakes — without Ewe and Grain, humanity reverts to its pre-civilised animal state | basis: The prologue explicitly describes humans living "like animals" before the gods' intervention | risk: The text does not state the prologue's function in explicit metatextual terms; this is structural interpretation]

=== Narrative sequence: the debate

Ewe and Grain are initially content in their divine roles, but at a feast where they drink too much wine, they begin to quarrel over which of them is more valuable to the gods and to humanity.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines ~50–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 220–221.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 50–80

Ewe boasts of providing wool, the material for garments that clothe both humans and kings, and of supplying the fat and milk used in temple offerings and feasts.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines ~80–100; Black et al. 2004, pp. 221–222.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 80–100

[LACUNA: Some lines in the central exchange partially damaged (lines 100–115) | source: etcsl 5.3.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: Overlapping Nippur manuscripts restore most of the passage]

Grain counters by claiming that bread is the staple of human life, that the great temples depend on grain offerings, and that beer — brewed from grain — is the drink of the gods and the social lubricant of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines ~115–150; Black et al. 2004, pp. 222–224.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 115–150

The debate escalates as each contestant belittles the other: Ewe mocks Grain as mere dirt-bound stalks, while Grain mocks Ewe as a dirty, bleating creature.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines ~130–170; Black et al. 2004, pp. 223–224.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 130–170

The argument covers the full range of each entity's contributions: wool and leather vs. bread, beer, and flour; pastoral life vs. agricultural settlement.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 80–180; Black et al. 2004, pp. 221–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 221–225

=== The verdict

Enki renders the verdict in favour of Grain (Ashnan), declaring her superior to Ewe (Lahar).footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines ~180–192; Black et al. 2004, p. 225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 180–192

[INFERENCE: The verdict for Grain reflects the centrality of cereal agriculture to Sumerian civilisation — grain was the basis of the ration system, the temple economy, and the brewing of beer | basis: The economic and ritual primacy of grain in Mesopotamia is well-documented across the corpus | risk: The text does not state the reasoning behind the verdict explicitly; this is scholarly interpretation]

Ewe accepts the verdict and the two are reconciled, restoring the harmonious order that the debate had disrupted.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines ~190–192; Black et al. 2004, p. 225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 190–192

=== Cultural and cosmological function

The composition served a didactic function in the _edubba_: it taught scribal students through competitive argument the relative merits of pastoralism and agriculture as pillars of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 80–180; Black et al. 2004, pp. 219–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, lines 80–180

The cosmogonic prologue describing humanity in a pre-civilised state — eating grass, going naked, drinking from ditches — is one of the most cited passages in Sumerological literature and provides essential evidence for Sumerian conceptions of human origins.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 72–73

=== Attestation and transmission

Multiple Old Babylonian copies from Nippur scribal schools attest the composition's wide circulation and standard curricular status.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 219.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.2, manuscript list

Minor translational differences between ETCSL and Black et al. 2004 in the cosmogonic prologue do not affect the narrative substance.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2; Black et al. 2004, pp. 219–225.]
