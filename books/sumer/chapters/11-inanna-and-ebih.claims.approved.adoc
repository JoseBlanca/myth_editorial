=== Setting and context

The composition "Inanna and Ebih" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.3.2 and survives in numerous consistent Old Babylonian copies, making it one of the best-preserved Inanna narratives.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, composite text, lines 1–184; Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–77.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.2

The text is classified as single-prevalent: the numerous OB copies produce a stable composite text with minimal significant variation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.2, manuscript list

The text was part of the standard scribal curriculum, attesting to its recognised pedagogical and literary value.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 71 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 71

The composition is a sustained exercise in divine martial rhetoric: a hymnic-narrative genre piece that showcases Inanna's self-praising speeches and sublime destructive imagery.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–72 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 71–72

=== Hymnic opening and Inanna's self-praise

The text opens with an extended hymnic passage praising Inanna's terrifying martial power and her _me-lam_ — her terrifying divine radiance.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, p. 71.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.2, lines 1–30

Inanna declares her supremacy in battle: she is the goddess who overwhelms lands, who roars at the earth, who crushes those who do not show her proper reverence.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, opening hymnic section; Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–72.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 71–72

=== Ebih's defiance

Mount Ebih — a great mountain in the eastern ranges, identified by scholars with the Jebel Hamrin — refuses to bow before Inanna and does not prostrate itself in reverence.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, p. 72.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 72

Ebih's luxuriant vegetation, its wild animals, and its general splendour are described; the mountain is presented as flourishing and self-sufficient, indifferent to Inanna's authority.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 72–73

Inanna is enraged by Ebih's refusal to submit and resolves to destroy the mountain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, p. 73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 73

=== Inanna's appeal to An

Inanna travels to the sky god An (Anu), the father of the gods, to seek his support and sanction for her planned assault on Ebih.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, lines approx. 50–75; Black et al. 2004, pp. 73–74.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.2, lines 50–75

An warns Inanna against attacking Ebih, counselling caution: the mountain is fearsome and its power should not be underestimated.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, p. 74.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 74

Inanna rejects An's warning outright; she declares she will attack Ebih regardless of his counsel.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, p. 74.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 74

[INFERENCE: Inanna's defiance of An's counsel and her subsequent independent triumph demonstrate her supremacy even over the highest authority in the divine hierarchy. | basis: She acts alone and succeeds despite the sky god's explicit warning. | risk: Some scholars read An's warning as formulaic rather than a genuine power-contest between the two deities.]

=== The assault on Ebih

Inanna unleashes her full martial fury against Mount Ebih, attacking with weapons of war and cosmic power.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, lines approx. 100–155; Black et al. 2004, pp. 74–76.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.2, lines 100–155

The destruction sequence is described in escalating detail: Inanna sends fire and flood against the mountain, devastating its forests and killing its wildlife.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, pp. 75–76.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 75–76

Inanna pours fire over the mountain's flanks; the vegetation withers and the animals flee or die.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, p. 76.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 76

[LACUNA: Minor gaps in lines 140–155 affecting the description of Inanna's assault on the mountain; some details of the destruction sequence are broken. | source: etcsl 1.3.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: The numerous manuscript copies allow near-complete restoration; only isolated words remain uncertain.]

The mountain Ebih is utterly destroyed; it lies devastated and submissive before Inanna's power.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, pp. 76–77.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 76–77

=== Aftermath and hymnic closing

Inanna declares victory over Ebih, celebrating her triumph in a self-praising speech that mirrors the opening hymnic section.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, closing section; Black et al. 2004, p. 77.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 77

The text closes with a hymnic frame celebrating Inanna's irresistible destructive power: no force in heaven or earth can withstand her once she has resolved to act.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, closing lines; Black et al. 2004, p. 77.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 77

=== Theological and literary significance

The composition demonstrates that Inanna acts independently of the divine hierarchy: she defies An's warning, attacks alone, and triumphs, asserting her rank among the gods through deeds rather than permission.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, lines 50–75; Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–72 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.2, lines 50–75

The motif of Inanna as irresistible destroyer is echoed in royal hymns and inscriptions invoking her patronage in warfare.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 83–85

Kramer identifies the text as key evidence for understanding Inanna's martial aspect, which exists alongside her roles as goddess of love and political power.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 83–85

Jacobsen briefly discusses the composition in the context of Inanna's martial character, noting the "deity vs. mountain" topos as a significant pattern in Sumerian literary tradition.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 112–114.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 112–114

The rhetorical structure of the text is deliberately escalatory: hymnic opening establishing Inanna's power, the mountain's defiance, An's futile warning, and overwhelming destruction, all framed by matching hymnic passages.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–72 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 71–72
