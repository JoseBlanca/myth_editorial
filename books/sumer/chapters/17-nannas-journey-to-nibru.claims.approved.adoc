== Nanna-Suen's Journey to Nibru — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

Nanna-Suen is the moon god, patron deity of the city of Ur (Sumerian: Urim), and son of Enlil, chief god of the Sumerian pantheon and lord of Nippur (Sumerian: Nibru).footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, pp. 147–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 1–10

The composition ETCSL 1.5.1 survives in multiple Old Babylonian copies from Nippur and Ur scribal schools, confirming its place in the _edubba_ curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, manuscript list

The text reflects the political and cultic axis between Ur and Nippur that was central to Ur III royal ideology: Ur as economic powerhouse and seat of Nanna's temple (the Ekishnugal), Nippur as the religious capital and seat of Enlil's temple (the Ekur).footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 147–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 147–148

=== Narrative sequence

Nanna-Suen decides to travel from Ur to Nippur to visit his father Enlil, loading a barge with an elaborate cargo of offerings.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 1–30.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 1–30

The text catalogues the specific goods loaded onto Nanna's barge: cattle, sheep, grain, dairy products, and other agricultural produce, presented in itemised lists that mirror the administrative offering records attested in Ur III economic texts.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 30–80; CDLI administrative corpus for Ur-Nippur deliveries.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 30–80

[INFERENCE: The detailed gift catalogue closely parallels real cultic offerings documented in administrative tablets, suggesting the composition served as a mythic charter for actual barge processions and offering deliveries between Ur and Nippur. | basis: Administrative texts from the Ur III period attest to regular deliveries of goods from Ur to Nippur's Ekur temple. | risk: Direct correspondence between literary and administrative texts cannot be proven; the literary list may be idealised.]

The barge journey follows a detailed itinerary along the canal route from Ur to Nippur, with named stopping points at intermediate cities and cult centres.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 80–170.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 80–170

At each stopping point along the canal route, Nanna presents gifts to the local deity and receives a welcome, establishing a ritual geography of southern Mesopotamia.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 100–170; Black et al. 2004, pp. 149–152.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 100–170

[LACUNA: Several lines (approximately lines 170–195) describing stops along the canal route are partially damaged. | source: ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 170–195 | scholarly_reconstruction: Partial; overlapping manuscripts restore most of the itinerary, but some station names and gift descriptions remain uncertain.]

The itinerary, with its sequence of named cities and waterways, provides evidence for the actual geography and canal network of the Ur–Nippur corridor as understood in the Old Babylonian period.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 80–195; Kramer 1961, pp. 47–49.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 47–49

Nanna-Suen arrives at Nippur and approaches the Ekur, the temple of Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 195–230.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 195–230

Nanna presents his cargo of offerings to Enlil at the Ekur, and the text catalogues the gifts in a second detailed list.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 230–280.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 230–280

=== Enlil's blessing

Enlil, pleased with his son's offerings, pronounces an extended blessing upon Ur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 280–330.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 280–330

The blessing encompasses agricultural abundance, commercial prosperity, and military security for Ur and its people, granted through Enlil's supreme divine authority.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 300–330; Black et al. 2004, pp. 153–155.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 153–155

The blessing functions as a legitimation device: Enlil's endorsement of Ur through Nanna validates the political and cultic pre-eminence of Ur within the Sumerian city-state system.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 300–350; Black et al. 2004, pp. 147–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 300–350

Nanna-Suen returns to Ur bearing his father's blessing, and the composition concludes with praise for the restored prosperity of the city.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 330–350.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, lines 330–350

=== Cultic and political significance

The composition's ritual-aetiological function is primary: it provides a mythic charter for the regular delivery of offerings from Ur to Nippur's Ekur temple, mapping divine action onto attested cultic practice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 1–100; CDLI administrative corpus.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=cdli ; loc=administrative corpus, Ur-Nippur deliveries

The royal-legitimation function operates through the reciprocal logic of the journey: Ur sends offerings to Nippur and receives Enlil's blessing in return, encoding the dependence of secular power (Ur) on religious sanction (Nippur).footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 300–350; Black et al. 2004, pp. 147–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 147–148

The barge procession described in the text has parallels in real ritual practice: administrative texts from the Ur III and Old Babylonian periods document divine barge journeys and offering deliveries between major Sumerian cities.footnote:[CDLI; Kramer 1961, pp. 47–49.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=cdli ; loc=administrative corpus

The composition's manuscript distribution — attested at both Nippur and Ur — indicates it was valued at both the sending and receiving ends of the cultic relationship it describes.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.5.1, manuscript list
