=== Setting and context

The composition "The Debate between Date Palm and Tamarisk" is catalogued as ETCSL 5.3.7 and survives in a fragmentary state, with fewer copies than the major debate poems, indicating more limited circulation in Old Babylonian scribal schools.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, composite text, fragmentary; Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–246.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.7, composite text, fragmentary

The text belongs to the Sumerian debate genre and features two personified trees — the Date Palm (Sumerian _gišimmar_), the supreme cultivated tree of Mesopotamia, and the Tamarisk (Sumerian _šinig_), a hardy wild tree used for ritual purification and fuel.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–245; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 244–245

The debate stages a tension between cultivated and wild nature — between the domesticated tree that provides fruit, syrup, and fibre, and the wild tree that grows without human care and serves ritual and practical functions.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–245.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.7, surviving lines

=== Narrative sequence: the debate

Date Palm argues its superiority on the grounds of its immense economic value: it provides dates (a dietary staple), date syrup, palm wine, fibre for rope-making, and wood for construction — making it the most productive tree in the Mesopotamian landscape.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–245.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 244–245

Date Palm emphasises its status as a cultivated tree — tended, irrigated, and pollinated by human labour — linking its worth to the civilisational enterprise of agriculture and horticulture.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, p. 245.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.7, surviving lines

Tamarisk counters by emphasising its hardiness, its independence from human cultivation, and its ritual purity — the tamarisk is used in purification rites and its wood serves as fuel and building material.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 245–246.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 245–246

=== Cultural function and broader tradition

The composition belongs to a broader Ancient Near Eastern tree-debate tradition that includes Akkadian parallels — debates between trees (cultivated vs. wild, productive vs. ornamental) appear in later Akkadian wisdom literature, suggesting a deep and persistent literary lineage.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–246; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 244–246

Despite its fragmentary state, the surviving portions reveal a composition concerned with the relative merits of cultivated and wild nature — domesticated utility versus natural resilience — a theme with wide resonance in world literature.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–246.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.7, surviving lines

=== Attestation and transmission

Fewer copies survive than for the major debate poems, indicating either more limited circulation in the _edubba_ curriculum or poorer archaeological recovery.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 244.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.7, manuscript list

The ETCSL composite represents the only available reconstruction from limited fragmentary witnesses, and Black et al. 2004 covers only the preserved portions.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7; Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–246.]
