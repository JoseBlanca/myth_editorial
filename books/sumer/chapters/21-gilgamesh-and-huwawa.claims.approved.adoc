== Gilgamesh and Huwawa: The Cedar Forest — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

Two independent Sumerian compositions narrate Gilgamesh's expedition to the Cedar Forest: Version A (ETCSL 1.8.1.5, approximately 202 lines) and Version B (ETCSL 1.8.1.5.1, approximately 168 extant lines with additional gaps). Both are Old Babylonian Sumerian compositions, independent of the Akkadian Cedar Forest episode in the Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh epic.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5 and 1.8.1.5.1; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5 and 1.8.1.5.1

Version A is longer, better preserved, and more widely attested in Old Babylonian copies, making it one of the best-attested Gilgamesh poems in the scribal curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, manuscript list; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–150.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 149–150

Version B is a shorter, distinct composition with its own manuscript tradition, well attested in its own right, though with fewer surviving copies than Version A.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5.1, manuscript list; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5.1, manuscript list

Gilgamesh (Sumerian: Bilgames), king of Uruk (Sumerian: Unug), is driven by the desire for lasting fame as a response to the inevitability of death — the central thematic concern of both versions.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–270.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 269–270

Huwawa (not Humbaba, the later Akkadian form) is the fearsome guardian of the Cedar Forest, appointed by Enlil, who wields seven supernatural terrors — manifestations of _me-lam_, the terrifying divine radiance.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 70–90; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 155–160

Enkidu serves as Gilgamesh's companion and guide through the mountain passes leading to the Cedar Forest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 30–50; Black et al. 2004, pp. 270–271.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 30–50

=== Version A — Narrative sequence

Gilgamesh announces his determination to journey to the Cedar Forest and establish a lasting name, explicitly citing mortality as his motivation: since death is inevitable, fame is the only enduring legacy.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–270.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 1–30

Gilgamesh seeks and receives the approval of the sun god Utu (who has jurisdiction over the Cedar Mountain as a foreign land) for the expedition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 20–40; Black et al. 2004, pp. 270–271.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 20–40

Gilgamesh recruits a company of warriors from Uruk to accompany him on the journey to the Cedar Forest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 40–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 40–60

The expedition crosses mountainous terrain, and the text describes Gilgamesh falling into a mysterious deep sleep after reaching the cedars, from which Enkidu urgently struggles to rouse him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 60–90; Black et al. 2004, pp. 271–273.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 60–90

Upon reaching the Cedar Forest, Gilgamesh and his companions confront Huwawa, whose seven _me-lam_ auras make him supernaturally terrifying.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 90–120; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 155–160

Gilgamesh defeats Huwawa not by direct combat but by a trick: he offers a series of gifts — his sisters as brides, along with food, sandals, and precious stones — and with each offer Huwawa surrenders one of his seven auras, progressively stripping himself of his supernatural protection.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 120–160; Black et al. 2004, pp. 273–275.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 120–160

[LACUNA: Lines 140–160 of Version A contain significant manuscript variation, with different manuscripts preserving different gift-offering sequences. The general trick-and-surrender pattern is secure, but specific content varies between manuscripts. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 140–160 | scholarly_reconstruction: Overlapping manuscripts for Version A allow reconstruction of most gaps; the aura-surrender trick is secure but individual offerings vary by manuscript.]

Once Huwawa has surrendered all seven auras and is defenceless, Huwawa pleads for his life, appealing to Gilgamesh's mercy and invoking the sun god Utu.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 150–170; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 160–165.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 160–165

Enkidu urges Gilgamesh to kill Huwawa immediately, warning against showing mercy to the guardian.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 165–180; Black et al. 2004, pp. 275–276.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 275–276

Enkidu, enraged, decapitates Huwawa.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 180–190; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 165–167.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 180–190

Huwawa's severed head is placed in a leather sack, and the heroes bring it before Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 185–195.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5, lines 185–195

Enlil is angered by the killing of Huwawa, whom he had appointed as guardian, and he redistributes Huwawa's seven auras among the natural world — to the field, the river, the reed-bed, the lion, the palace, the forest, and the goddess Nungal.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 190–202; Black et al. 2004, pp. 276–278.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 276–278

=== Version B — Narrative sequence

Version B opens with a similar motivation — Gilgamesh's desire to enter the Cedar Mountain and establish a name — but places greater emphasis on the psychological dimension: fear, hesitation, and the emotional burden of the quest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5.1, lines 1–30; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 167–170.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 167–170

In Version B, the journey to the Cedar Forest is described with more attention to the heroes' emotional response as they approach Huwawa's domain — the forest is a place of dread, and the text dwells on the fear inspired by the guardian's terrifying presence. Version B's journey section has significant textual gaps, and this characterisation is based on surviving portions supplemented by scholarly inference.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5.1, lines 30–80; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 170–172.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.5.1, lines 30–80

[LACUNA: The central section of Version B (approximately lines 80–110), emphasising fear and hesitation before the forest, is partially broken. Fewer manuscripts for Version B leave some lines unrestored. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.5.1, lines 80–110 | scholarly_reconstruction: Partial; the general tenor of the passage is clear, but specific lines remain uncertain.]

In Version B, the encounter with Huwawa includes a scene in which Huwawa weeps and appeals for mercy, a pathetic detail given greater weight than in Version A.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5.1, lines 110–130; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 172–173.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 172–173

Enkidu again urges the killing in Version B. === Comparison of the two versions

The two versions share the same narrative kernel — journey, confrontation, trick, decapitation, Enlil's anger — but differ in emphasis: Version A foregrounds the cleverness of the trick with the auras and the ethical ambiguity of the killing; Version B foregrounds the psychological experience of fear and the pathos of Huwawa's helplessness.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 149–174

George devotes extensive discussion to distinguishing the two Sumerian versions from each other and from the Akkadian Cedar Forest episode (Tablets IV–V of the Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh epic), demonstrating that all three are independent literary treatments of a shared tradition.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 149–174

=== Thematic significance

The composition raises the central question of Sumerian heroic literature: whether the desire for lasting fame can compensate for the certainty of death.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–270.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 269–270

The ethical ambiguity of the killing is a deliberate feature: Huwawa is doing the duty Enlil assigned, the trick that disarms him is dishonourable, and Enlil's anger at the heroes confirms that their victory comes at a moral cost.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 190–202; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 165–167.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 165–167

[INFERENCE: The redistribution of Huwawa's auras to the natural world after his death suggests an aetiological function: the text explains why certain natural phenomena (the terror of lions, the awesome quality of the forest) possess a numinous, fearsome quality. | basis: The list of recipients — field, river, lion, forest — corresponds to naturally fearsome aspects of the Mesopotamian landscape. | risk: The aetiological reading is plausible but not explicitly stated in the text; the redistribution may also function simply as divine punishment of the heroes.]

The _me-lam_ (divine radiance/aura) wielded by Huwawa represents the same category of supernatural power that inheres in gods, kings, and sacred objects throughout Sumerian literature — its use here as a weapon that can be bargained away adds a distinctive narrative twist.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 120–160; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 155–160

The narrative's didactic function in the _edubba_ context may have involved the question of whether cleverness and ambition justify transgressing divine boundaries — a question the text raises but does not resolve.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–270.]
