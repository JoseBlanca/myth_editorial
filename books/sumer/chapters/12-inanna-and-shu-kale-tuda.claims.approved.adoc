=== Setting and context

The composition "Inanna and Shu-kale-tuda" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.3.3 and survives in Old Babylonian tablets from Nippur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3, composite text; Black et al. 2004, pp. 78–84.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.3

The text is classified as single-prevalent-reconstructed: the ETCSL composite is the only standard reconstruction, but significant gaps remain in the plague description sections across all witnesses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.3, manuscript list

[LACUNA: Opening lines 1–15 are damaged; the initial context for the gardener's circumstances is partially lost. | source: etcsl 1.3.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: partial, some manuscripts preserve fragments of the opening.]

=== Shu-kale-tuda the gardener

Shu-kale-tuda is a mortal gardener who tends a garden but struggles: his garden fails repeatedly and his crops wither.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 78–79.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 78–79

Shu-kale-tuda looks to the stars for guidance and plants a tree (possibly a poplar or Euphrates poplar) that provides shade and finally allows his garden to flourish.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 79.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 79

=== The crime against Inanna

Inanna, wearied from traversing heaven and earth, lies down to rest in the shade of the garden.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 79.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 79

Shu-kale-tuda discovers the sleeping goddess and rapes her.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 79–80.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 79–80

When dawn comes, Inanna awakes, discovers the violation of her body, and is filled with rage; she resolves to find and punish the one who committed the act.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 80.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 80

=== Inanna's search and the three plagues

Inanna searches the land for the perpetrator but cannot identify or find him; Shu-kale-tuda hides among the human population.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 80.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 80

Inanna sends the first plague upon the land to force the revelation of the guilty man.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 80–81.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 80–81

=== Enki's role and the discovery

Shu-kale-tuda goes to the god Enki and seeks his counsel on how to escape Inanna's wrath.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 82.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 82

Despite Enki's counsel, Inanna ultimately locates Shu-kale-tuda; Enki's wisdom cannot ultimately protect the guilty from divine justice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 82–83.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 82–83

=== Judgment

Inanna finds Shu-kale-tuda and brings him to judgment; she pronounces sentence upon him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 83.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 83

Shu-kale-tuda is condemned to death by Inanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 83.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 83

The composition concludes with the paradox that although Shu-kale-tuda is destroyed, his name will be remembered and his story will be told — his infamy is preserved by the very narrative that condemns him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 83–84.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 83–84

[INFERENCE: The paradox of the ending — the criminal is condemned but his name is eternally preserved — functions as a literary reflection on the nature of narrative itself: the story that punishes also immortalises. | basis: The text explicitly states Shu-kale-tuda's name will not be forgotten. | risk: Some scholars may read this as a standard scribal colophon formula rather than deliberate literary irony.]

=== Significance

The composition combines sexual violence, divine pursuit, and cosmic justice in a distinctive narrative structure: crime, three escalating plagues, judgment.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, p. 78 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 78

The text demonstrates that crimes against the divine cannot be hidden: the entire land suffers for one man's transgression until the guilty individual is exposed.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.3
