# Line-edit diff: 14-inannas-descent

Each entry shows the original sentence (or clause) and the edited version. Footnotes, resolved-marker passages, EVIDENCE/COMPARATIVE-HOOK comments, and AsciiDoc structure are unchanged.

## Header paragraphs (introduction to the composition)

- **Original:** "...catalogued as ETCSL 1.4.1 and comprises approximately 415 lines in the composite text."
- **Edited:** "...catalogued as ETCSL 1.4.1 and runs to roughly 415 lines in the composite text."
- *Reason:* "Runs to roughly" is plainer and more concrete than the bureaucratic pair "comprises approximately." "Runs to" gives the line count a sense of length unfolding, more natural than the static "comprises."

- **Original:** "...including CBS 12638 and HS 1480, making it the most extensively attested Sumerian literary composition."
- **Edited:** "...including CBS 12638 and HS 1480, which makes it the most extensively attested Sumerian literary composition."
- *Reason:* The bare "-ing" participle lets two ideas float without a clear connector; an explicit relative ("which makes it") clarifies the logical link between the manuscript count and the conclusion drawn from it.

- **Original:** "The scribes of the _edubba_ did not copy texts at random."
- **Edited:** "Scribes of the _edubba_ did not copy texts at random."
- *Reason:* Drops a definite article that adds nothing — the bare-plural reads as a category claim about scribes in general, which is what the sentence means.

- **Original:** "They copied what mattered, and this text, by the evidence of the manuscript count alone, mattered more than almost anything else in their curriculum."
- **Edited:** "They copied what mattered, and this text, on the manuscript count alone, mattered more than almost anything else in their curriculum."
- *Reason:* "On the manuscript count alone" is shorter and idiomatic; "by the evidence of the manuscript count alone" is the same thought with a layer of nominalization stripped away ("the evidence of" is the zombie phrase, not load-bearing).

- **Original:** "The ETCSL composite draws on these forty-plus Old Babylonian fragments to produce the standard scholarly text."
- **Edited:** "The ETCSL composite draws on the forty-plus Old Babylonian fragments to produce the standard scholarly text."
- *Reason:* "These" double-references the previous sentence; the simple definite article "the" works because the fragments have already been introduced.

- **Original:** "This is a composite-editorial text: every line that appears in the reconstruction has been verified against at least one physical tablet, but the continuous narrative that results is a modern editorial achievement, not something any ancient scribe held in his hands."
- **Edited:** "Every line in the reconstruction has been verified against at least one physical tablet, but the continuous narrative that results is a modern editorial achievement, not something any ancient scribe held in his hands."
- *Reason:* Cuts the throat-clearing label "This is a composite-editorial text:" — the term has just been introduced two sentences above, and the substantive point is the verification-vs-achievement contrast that follows. Also tightens "every line that appears in the reconstruction" -> "every line in the reconstruction" (drops a relative clause that adds no information).

- **Original:** "Sladek's 1974 Johns Hopkins dissertation provides the foundational philological study of the composition, collating manuscript witnesses and establishing the text that all subsequent work relies on."
- **Edited:** "Sladek's 1974 Johns Hopkins dissertation provides the foundational philological study, collating the manuscript witnesses and establishing the text on which all subsequent work relies."
- *Reason:* "Of the composition" is redundant — the composition is the topic of the entire paragraph. "The manuscript witnesses" (definite article) reads as a defined corpus rather than an indefinite category. "On which all subsequent work relies" is grammatically cleaner than the dangling "that all subsequent work relies on" (preposition stranding works fine, but with this much surrounding subordination the pied-piped form scans better).

- **Original:** "The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by Black et al. 2004, pp. 52–62; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 205–232; Sladek 1974."
- **Edited:** "The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite, supplemented by Black et al. 2004, pp. 52–62; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 205–232; and Sladek 1974."
- *Reason:* Drops redundant "text" after "composite" (the term "ETCSL composite" already names a text). Adds the serial "and" before the final item in the supplement list — proper English serial coordination.

- **Original:** "They are not the same story any more than a translation is the same thing as its source."
- **Edited:** "They are no more the same story than a translation is the same thing as its source."
- *Reason:* "No more X than Y" is the canonical English negative comparison; "not... any more than" is the loose colloquial form. The canonical form scans tighter.

- **Original:** "It is the most important single Sumerian text for understanding Mesopotamian eschatology: the nature of death, the geography of the afterlife, and the impossibility of free return."
- **Edited:** "It is the most important single Sumerian text for understanding Mesopotamian eschatology — the nature of death, the geography of the afterlife, the impossibility of free return."
- *Reason:* Em dash in place of colon for a list-as-restatement (the items don't strictly enumerate eschatology; they restate what is meant by it). Drops the final "and" so the three items read as parallel beats rather than a closed enumeration — Pinker's "list as rhythm" preference here.

- **Original:** "Jacobsen's literary translation in _The Harps That Once..._ (1987) smooths over line-level variants for narrative coherence but remains an influential reading."
- **Edited:** "Jacobsen's literary translation in _The Harps That Once..._ (1987) smooths over line-level variants for narrative coherence, but it remains an influential reading."
- *Reason:* The original loses its second subject across the conjunction; readers parse "but remains" as continuing the same VP, which gives a momentary garden-path. Restoring the subject with a comma + "but it" makes the contrast cleaner.

- **Original:** "The story, as the tablets preserve it, goes as follows."
- **Edited:** "The story, as the tablets preserve it, runs as follows."
- *Reason:* "Runs" is a slightly more concrete and writerly verb for narrative unfolding than the bare "goes."

## Inanna's preparation

- **Original:** "Inanna gathers the seven _me_ — the divine powers and cultural blueprints that govern civilization — and transforms them into physical adornments."
- **Edited:** "Inanna gathers the seven _me_ — the divine powers and cultural blueprints that govern civilization — and turns them into physical adornments."
- *Reason:* "Turns" is plainer and crisper than "transforms" (a small Latinate inflation in a sentence already carrying a heavy gloss).

- **Original:** "She places a gold ring on her hand."
- **Edited:** "She slips a gold ring onto her hand."
- *Reason:* "Slips a gold ring onto" is more vivid and physical for the action than the flat "places... on" — also breaks an unintended verb echo in the sequence (the previous sentence already used "sets," "fastens," "places"). "Onto" is the natural preposition for inserting a ring; "on" is bland.

- **Original:** "Before she departs, Inanna does something that no other character in Sumerian narrative does with such cold foresight..."
- **Edited:** "Before she departs, Inanna does something no other character in Sumerian narrative does with such cold foresight..."
- *Reason:* Drops the optional relativizer "that" — the relative is restrictive and bare, which is the standard tighter form in English.

## At the gates of the underworld

- **Original:** "Whether this stated reason is sincere or a pretext is debated by scholars; the text does not resolve her actual motivation for descending."
- **Edited:** "Whether this stated reason is sincere or a pretext is debated; the text does not resolve her actual motivation for descending."
- *Reason:* Cuts "by scholars" — the only people who debate texts in scholarship are scholars; the agent is implicit. Pinker's "throat-clearing" target.

- **Original:** "...scholarly opinions diverge on whether Inanna aims to conquer the underworld or whether her motivation is left deliberately ambiguous."
- **Edited:** "...scholarly opinions diverge on whether Inanna means to conquer the underworld or whether her motivation is left deliberately ambiguous."
- *Reason:* "Means to" is the natural English idiom for stated intention; "aims to" is acceptable but slightly stiff.

- **Original:** "It is not rage that drives Ereshkigal's order — or not rage alone. It is policy."
- **Edited:** "What drives Ereshkigal's order is not rage — or not rage alone. It is policy."
- *Reason:* Removes the it-cleft ("It is not rage that drives") in favour of the wh-cleft ("What drives... is not rage"), which puts the subject (rage) in end-focus where it belongs. The contrast "not rage / it is policy" reads more cleanly with the wh-version.

## The seven-gate stripping

- **Original:** "At the first gate, the _shugurra_ — the crown of the steppe — is removed from Inanna's head."
- **Edited:** "At the first gate, the _shugurra_ — the crown of the steppe — is taken from Inanna's head."
- *Reason:* "Taken" is plainer and more bodily than "removed" (which has a clinical/procedural ring out of place in the dramatic narration). Applied through the gate sequence (gates 2–7) for rhythmic consistency.

- **Original (gate 2):** "...the measuring rod and line of lapis lazuli are removed from her hand."
- **Edited:** "...the measuring rod and line of lapis lazuli are taken from her hand."
- *Reason:* Same as above — consistent verb choice across the seven gates.

- **Original (gate 3):** "...the small lapis lazuli beads are removed from her neck."
- **Edited:** "...the small lapis lazuli beads are taken from her neck."
- *Reason:* Same.

- **Original (gate 4):** "...the twin egg-shaped beads are removed from her breast."
- **Edited:** "...the twin egg-shaped beads are taken from her breast."
- *Reason:* Same.

- **Original (gate 5):** "...the gold ring is removed from her hand."
- **Edited:** "...the gold ring is taken from her hand."
- *Reason:* Same.

- **Original (gate 6):** "...the breastplate called \"Come, man, come\" is removed from her chest."
- **Edited:** "...the breastplate called \"Come, man, come\" is taken from her chest."
- *Reason:* Same.

- **Original (gate 7):** "...the _pala_-garment of ladyship is removed from her body."
- **Edited:** "...the _pala_-garment of ladyship is taken from her body."
- *Reason:* Same — and the seven-fold repetition of "is taken" gives the stripping sequence a more incantatory rhythm than the more bureaucratic "is removed."

- **Original:** "...now stands in the innermost chamber without a single attribute of power remaining."
- **Edited:** "...now stands in the innermost chamber without a single attribute of power left."
- *Reason:* "Left" is monosyllabic and concrete; "remaining" is a longer participle that drags the end of a long sentence. End-weight matters here.

- **Original:** "She is not Inanna any more — not in any sense that the cosmos recognises."
- **Edited:** "She is no longer Inanna in any sense the cosmos recognises."
- *Reason:* Combines the two halves into a single tighter clause. "No longer" replaces the chatty "not... any more"; drops the dash-and-restart structure; drops the optional relativizer "that." Same meaning, fewer joints.

- **Original:** "At each gate, the same formula is repeated: Inanna questions the removal and is given the same answer about the perfection of the nether world's powers."
- **Edited:** "At each gate, the same exchange unfolds: Inanna questions the removal and is given the same answer about the perfection of the nether world's powers."
- *Reason:* "The same formula is repeated" is a passive zombie ("is repeated"); "the same exchange unfolds" is active and supplies the relevant noun (an exchange — a question-and-answer — is what the sentence then describes).

- **Original:** "It has one rule, and the rule applies seven times, and seven times is what it takes..."
- **Edited:** "It has one rule, the rule applies seven times, and seven times is what it takes..."
- *Reason:* Removes the first "and" so the three clauses read as a tight asyndetic-then-conjoined triplet ("X, Y, and Z") rather than an additive chain. Cleaner triadic rhythm.

## Ereshkigal's judgment

- **Original:** "This is not a trial. There is no evidence presented, no defence offered, no deliberation."
- **Edited:** "This is not a trial. No evidence is presented, no defence offered, no deliberation undertaken."
- *Reason:* Drops the existential "There is" (a classic Pinker zombie target — "there is X" can almost always be replaced by "X is..."). Adds parallel "undertaken" to the third item so the three negations are fully parallel ("is presented / [is] offered / [is] undertaken").

- **Original:** "The composition provides the fullest Sumerian account of underworld geography..."
- **Edited:** "The composition gives the fullest Sumerian account of underworld geography..."
- *Reason:* "Gives" is shorter and more direct than the slightly bureaucratic "provides."

- **Original:** "...a formula repeated at each of the seven gates."
- **Edited (punctuation only):** "...— a formula repeated at each of the seven gates."
- *Reason:* Replaces the comma before the appositive with an em dash. The appositive elaborates the just-quoted formula and is long enough to deserve the stronger break.

- **Original:** "The dead in the underworld eat dust and clay and are clothed in feathered garments like birds — standard Mesopotamian underworld imagery shared with other compositions."
- **Edited:** "The dead in the underworld eat dust and clay and wear feathered garments like birds — standard Mesopotamian underworld imagery shared with other compositions."
- *Reason:* Replaces the passive nominal "are clothed in" with the active concrete verb "wear." Also restores active-voice parallel with "eat" earlier in the same sentence ("eat... and wear..." reads as parallel; "eat... and are clothed in..." breaks the pattern).

## Ninshubur's appeals

- **Original:** "Where Enlil and Nanna-Suen saw a goddess who had overreached, Enki sees a problem that requires a solution."
- **Edited:** "Where Enlil and Nanna-Suen saw a goddess who had overreached, Enki sees a problem that demands a solution."
- *Reason:* "Demands" is sharper and more in keeping with Enki's engineering response than the lukewarm "requires." Reflects the urgency the sentence is establishing.

- **Original:** "This is consistent with everything the Sumerian tradition tells us about Enki."
- **Edited:** "This fits everything the Sumerian tradition tells us about Enki."
- *Reason:* "Fits" is one syllable and a vivid concrete verb; "is consistent with" is a four-syllable nominalization-laden Latinism. Pinker's classic-style razor.

- **Original:** "These are beings who exist between categories, who are neither one thing nor the other, and who therefore can pass through boundaries that would stop anyone with a fixed identity."
- **Edited:** "They are beings who exist between categories, neither one thing nor the other, and so they can pass through boundaries that would stop anyone with a fixed identity."
- *Reason:* "These" + "beings" pairs awkwardly when "creatures" was just used — "they" picks up the antecedent more naturally. Drops the relative-clause cascade ("who exist... who are... who therefore can") in favour of an absolute appositive ("neither one thing nor the other") and a coordinated independent clause ("and so they can"). Tighter, clearer chain of reasoning.

- **Original:** "The _kurgarra_ and _galatur_ are understood by some scholars as asexual or transgender cultic functionaries..."
- **Edited:** "Some scholars read the _kurgarra_ and _galatur_ as asexual or transgender cultic functionaries..."
- *Reason:* Removes the agent-buried passive "are understood by some scholars" and gives the agent its proper subject position. "Read X as" is also a more vivid academic verb than the diffuse "understand X as."

- **Original:** "The plan is not to storm the nether world or to argue with Ereshkigal."
- **Edited:** "The plan is not to storm the nether world or argue with Ereshkigal."
- *Reason:* Drops the second "to" — only one infinitive marker is needed across the disjunction (the second is recoverable from parallelism). Shorter, identical meaning.

## Ereshkigal's distress and the revival

- **Original:** "Whether she suffers birthing pains or mourning groans is debated by scholars; the text is ambiguous, and the ambiguity may be deliberate."
- **Edited:** "Whether she suffers birthing pains or mourning groans is debated; the text is ambiguous, and the ambiguity may be deliberate."
- *Reason:* Same as the earlier "by scholars" cut — debate in scholarship is by scholars by definition. Implicit agent.

- **Original:** "Ereshkigal, moved by their participation in her suffering, offers them a gift in gratitude."
- **Edited:** "Moved by their participation in her suffering, Ereshkigal offers them a gift in gratitude."
- *Reason:* Fronts the participial phrase. Puts the cause first, the action second — clearer reading order. Also tightens subject-verb distance: "Ereshkigal... offers" instead of "Ereshkigal, [11-word participle], offers."

## The galla and the ascent

- **Original:** "The _galla_ demons — the _galla_, underworld demons who enforce the laws of the dead — accompany Inanna as she ascends, acting as enforcers who will seize whomever she designates."
- **Edited:** "The _galla_ demons — the _galla_, underworld demons who enforce the laws of the dead — escort Inanna as she ascends, ready to seize whomever she designates."
- *Reason:* "Escort" replaces "accompany" — more concrete and slightly more menacing in this context (an escort under guard). "Ready to seize" replaces the participle-as-relative "acting as enforcers who will seize" — three words for nine, and the dramatic readiness-to-pounce reading is sharper. Note: the doubled gloss (_galla_ demons + _galla_, underworld demons...) is retained because it's structural, not stylistic.

- **Original:** "As Inanna returns to the upper world accompanied by the _galla_ demons, she encounters various deities at each of her cities."
- **Edited:** "As Inanna returns to the upper world with the _galla_ demons at her side, she meets various deities at each of her cities."
- *Reason:* "With the _galla_ demons at her side" is more vivid than the flat passive "accompanied by." "Meets" replaces "encounters" — shorter and plainer; "encounters" is a slight Latinism in a sentence that doesn't need one.

- **Original:** "The _galla_ attempt to seize each one."
- **Edited:** "The _galla_ try to seize each one."
- *Reason:* "Try" replaces "attempt" — Pinker's classic-style preference for the plain Anglo-Saxon over the Latinate. Same meaning, one syllable shorter, less stiff.

- **Original:** "Ninshubur... is the first deity the _galla_ attempt to seize."
- **Edited:** "Ninshubur... is the first deity the _galla_ try to seize."
- *Reason:* Same — "try" for "attempt" for consistency.

- **Original:** "Similarly, the gods Shara and Lulal are found mourning and are spared by Inanna."
- **Edited:** "The gods Shara and Lulal are likewise found mourning and are spared by Inanna."
- *Reason:* Drops the sentence-initial "Similarly," (an academic discourse marker that adds throat-clearing) and folds the parallel into the sentence with "likewise" as an adverbial. Same logical link, less front-loading.

## Dumuzid's complacency

- **Original:** "Dumuzid — the shepherd-god, consort of Inanna — is seated on a magnificent throne, dressed in splendid garments, not mourning her absence at all."
- **Edited:** "Dumuzid — the shepherd-god, consort of Inanna — sits on a magnificent throne, dressed in splendid garments, not mourning her absence at all."
- *Reason:* "Sits" (active simple present) replaces "is seated" (stative-passive). Active and direct; matches the indictment-by-image the paragraph is building.

- **Original:** "The contrast with every other deity Inanna has encountered could not be sharper."
- **Edited:** "The contrast with every other deity Inanna has met could not be sharper."
- *Reason:* "Met" replaces "encountered" — same Pinker preference for the shorter Anglo-Saxon verb. Also tracks the change made in the earlier paragraph from "encounters" to "meets."

- **Original:** "Each of them had done what one does when someone important is dead: they had signalled their loss, performed their grief, made their bodies testaments to the absence of the one who was gone."
- **Edited:** "Each had done what one does when someone important is dead: signalled their loss, performed their grief, made their bodies testaments to the absence of the one who was gone."
- *Reason:* Cuts "of them" (redundant after "Each") and removes the redundant subject + auxiliary "they had" before the colon-introduced list, since the earlier "had done" already provides the auxiliary frame. The three past-participle phrases now read as a clean parallel triplet.

- **Original:** "Dumuzid, in the time that his consort — his wife, the goddess who chose him — was dead on a hook in the underworld, dressed himself magnificently and sat down to enjoy the authority her absence left vacant."
- **Edited:** "Dumuzid, while his consort — his wife, the goddess who chose him — was dead on a hook in the underworld, dressed himself magnificently and sat down to enjoy the authority her absence left vacant."
- *Reason:* "While" is one word for the four-word "in the time that" — same meaning, faster reading. Tightens an already clause-heavy sentence.

- **Original:** "Dumuzid did not mourn, and therefore Dumuzid will die."
- **Edited:** "Dumuzid did not mourn, and so Dumuzid will die."
- *Reason:* "So" replaces "therefore" — natural conversational consequence marker; "therefore" is a logician's connective, slightly out of register for the mythic prose.

## Dumuzid's flight and recapture

- **Original:** "He transforms Dumuzid's hands and feet into the hands and feet of a snake — or a gazelle, depending on the manuscript — allowing him to flee from the _galla_."
- **Edited:** "He turns Dumuzid's hands and feet into the hands and feet of a snake — or a gazelle, depending on the manuscript — letting him flee from the _galla_."
- *Reason:* "Turns" replaces "transforms" (consistency with the earlier change from "transforms" to "turns" in the _me_-into-adornments sentence; also the plainer Anglo-Saxon verb). "Letting" replaces "allowing" — shorter, more colloquial, and more natural with the act of escape.

- **Original:** "...and he does not question whether Dumuzid deserves the rescue."
- **Edited:** "...and he does not weigh whether Dumuzid deserves the rescue."
- *Reason:* "Weigh" is more vivid and appropriate for the deliberative judgment being negated than the generic "question." Utu doesn't pose the question and reject it; he doesn't even weigh it.

- **Original:** "The transformation is different in each witness, but the function is the same."
- **Edited:** "The transformation differs in each witness, but the function is the same."
- *Reason:* Replaces the nominal "is different" with the verbal "differs" — exactly the kind of zombie-noun fix Pinker recommends.

- **Original:** "Dumuzid flees, but he is ultimately recaptured by the _galla_ demons."
- **Edited:** "Dumuzid flees, but he is finally recaptured by the _galla_ demons."
- *Reason:* "Finally" is the natural English temporal closure marker for "after pursuit, at the end"; "ultimately" is a slightly grander Latinism that overshoots the simple act of recapture.

## The fly episode

- **Original:** "It is worth noting that this episode does not appear in all manuscripts."
- **Edited:** "The episode does not appear in all manuscripts."
- *Reason:* Cuts the "It is worth noting that" throat-clearing — Pinker's textbook example of metadiscourse that wastes words. The bare claim is the same and lands harder.

- **Original:** "Some tablets include the fly; others proceed directly to the resolution."
- **Edited:** "Some tablets include the fly; others go straight to the resolution."
- *Reason:* "Go straight to" is plainer and more concrete than "proceed directly to" (a Latinate near-redundancy: "proceed" + "directly" both signal forward motion). Same meaning, fewer syllables.

## Geshtinanna and the seasonal compromise

- **Original:** "The law of kinship is honoured: the sister's love reduces the brother's sentence by half."
- **Edited:** "The law of kinship is honoured: the sister's love halves the brother's sentence."
- *Reason:* "Halves" is a single concrete verb; "reduces... by half" is a four-word periphrasis for the same operation. Pinker would call this textbook nominalization (or rather verbalization through a zombie verb).

## The three-functions paragraph

- **Original:** "The composition operates on three levels simultaneously, and all three are directly present in the text."
- **Edited:** "The composition operates on three levels at once, and all three are directly present in the text."
- *Reason:* "At once" replaces "simultaneously" — the one-syllable Anglo-Saxon for the five-syllable Latinism. Same meaning, plainer register.

- **Original:** "As eschatological charter, it establishes the laws of the underworld..."
- **Edited:** "As eschatological charter, it sets out the laws of the underworld..."
- *Reason:* "Sets out" is more concrete and active than "establishes" (which is one of those Latinate verbs that does no real work the simpler phrasal verb can't do).

- **Original:** "...the Sumerian scribes who shaped this text understood exactly what they were building."
- **Edited:** "...the Sumerian scribes who shaped this text knew exactly what they were building."
- *Reason:* "Knew" is monosyllabic and direct; "understood" is fine but slightly more academic in register, and the sentence is making a strong claim that lands better in plain English.

## Comparative paragraph

- **Original:** "...mirrors the Greek myth of Persephone, who descends to Hades and returns to Demeter, with the seasons tracking her absence and her return."
- **Edited:** "...mirrors the Greek myth of Persephone, who descends to Hades and returns to Demeter while the seasons track her absence and her return."
- *Reason:* The original "with X tracking Y" is a "with-absolute" — grammatically fine but slightly loose. Promoting it to a finite "while" clause makes the temporal coordination explicit and parallel with the main verbs ("descends and returns... while the seasons track"). Cleaner reading order.
