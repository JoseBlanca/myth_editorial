## Paragraph 4 (Version A opening — mortality motive)

**Original:**
> Gilgamesh (Sumerian: Bilgames), king of Uruk (Sumerian: Unug), announces his determination to journey to the Cedar Forest and establish a lasting name, explicitly citing mortality as his motivation: since death is inevitable, fame is the only enduring legacy.

**Edited:**
> Gilgamesh (Sumerian: Bilgames), king of Uruk (Sumerian: Unug), announces his determination to journey to the Cedar Forest and establish a lasting name, citing mortality as his motivation: since death is inevitable, fame is the only enduring legacy.

*Rationale:* "explicitly" is throat-clearing — the sentence already reports that Gilgamesh "cites" mortality, which is itself explicit. Dropping the adverb tightens without loss.

**Original:**
> Therefore the only meaningful act available to a mortal is to do something so extraordinary that the name survives after the body does not.

**Edited:**
> The only meaningful act available to a mortal, therefore, is something so extraordinary that the name survives after the body does not.

*Rationale:* Demoting "Therefore" from sentence-opener to embedded connective makes the logical link feel less heavy-handed. "to do something so extraordinary" → "something so extraordinary" kills the filler infinitive (classic-style directness).

## Paragraph 9 (deep sleep in the cedars)

**Original:**
> The text describes Gilgamesh falling into a mysterious deep sleep after reaching the cedars, from which Enkidu urgently struggles to rouse him.

**Edited:**
> The text describes Gilgamesh falling into a mysterious deep sleep after reaching the cedars, from which Enkidu struggles frantically to rouse him.

*Rationale:* "urgently struggles" is less natural than "struggles frantically"; placing the adverb after the verb reduces the stacking of modifiers before the verb, and "frantically" matches the word used in the next sentence, binding the two sentences.

**Original:**

**Edited:**
> It is not a casual nap. It is a heavy, unnatural slumber -- the kind of thing that, in Sumerian narrative, signals proximity to the supernatural.

*Rationale:* "The sleep" is redundant once "sleep" has just been the topic; "It is described as" is throat-clearing (the text has just described it). Tightens without touching meaning.

## Paragraph 12 (the gift-trick)

**Original:**
> The trick works because each gift appears generous, and Huwawa, accepting, gives away in return one layer of the divine radiance that makes him invulnerable.

**Edited:**
> The trick works because each gift appears generous, and Huwawa, accepting, gives back one layer of the divine radiance that makes him invulnerable.

*Rationale:* "gives away in return" is a wordy near-tautology; "gives back" captures the exchange in two words.

**Original:**
> The _me-lam_ that made him what he was drains away with each transaction, and by the end of the sequence, the fearsome protector of the Cedar Forest is simply a large creature with no defences left.

**Edited:**
> The _me-lam_ that made him what he was drains away transaction by transaction, and by the end of the sequence the fearsome protector of the Cedar Forest is simply a large creature with no defences left.

*Rationale:* "with each transaction" is serviceable, but the duplicative phrasing in the earlier sentence ("with each offer … with each exchange") meant "with each X" had already appeared twice. "transaction by transaction" gives the cadence of accumulation that the passage wants. Removed the comma after "sequence" for a cleaner clause boundary.
