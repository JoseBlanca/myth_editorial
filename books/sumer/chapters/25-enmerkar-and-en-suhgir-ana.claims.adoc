== Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana: The Sorcery Contest — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.
// Source: ETCSL 1.8.2.4; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–169; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–261
// Classification: single-prevalent (ETCSL composite from OB manuscripts)
// This is the final composition in the Aratta diplomatic cycle.

=== Setting and context

En-suhgir-ana is lord of Aratta, the distant wealthy city that has been the rival of Uruk throughout the Aratta cycle; he challenges Enmerkar, king of Uruk, to a new contest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, opening lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, opening lines

The contest in this composition shifts from the diplomatic and rhetorical exchanges of the preceding Enmerkar poem to a direct sorcery challenge: En-suhgir-ana proposes that the conflict be decided through magical power.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 1–40; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 1–40

Inanna remains the divine patron of Uruk whose favor ensures Enmerkar's ultimate victory, continuing the theological framework established in Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, concluding lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, concluding lines

=== The sorcery challenge

En-suhgir-ana boasts to his advisors that he will defeat Enmerkar through magical means, and he proposes sending a sorcerer to overcome Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 40–80; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 110–118.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 40–80

The advisors of En-suhgir-ana help him identify a suitable champion for the magical contest, settling on a figure with powerful sorcerous abilities.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 80–120; Black et al. 2004, pp. 253–254.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 80–120

=== Dispatch of Urgirinuna

Urgirinuna, a witch, is dispatched from Aratta to defeat Uruk through sorcery; she is presented as a formidable practitioner of magic.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 120–180; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 118–130.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 120–180

Urgirinuna travels to the territory of Uruk and begins to exercise her magical powers against the city, threatening its livestock, its rivers, and its people.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 180–220; Black et al. 2004, pp. 255–256.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 180–220

[LACUNA: Transition between the sorcery challenge and the dispatch of Urgirinuna partially damaged in lines 200–220 | source: etcsl 1.8.2.4, lines 200–220 | scholarly_reconstruction: overlapping OB manuscripts restore most of the passage]

The witch's sorcery threatens the pastoral and agricultural foundations of Uruk's prosperity, including its flocks and waterways.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 220–250; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 130–138.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 220–250

=== Sagburu defeats the witch

An old woman of Uruk named Sagburu encounters Urgirinuna and recognizes her as a foreign sorceress threatening the city.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 250–280; Black et al. 2004, pp. 257–258.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 250–280

Sagburu is presented as an unlikely champion — an elderly woman of no apparent supernatural standing who defeats the powerful witch through superior cunning rather than matching sorcery with sorcery.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 280–320; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 138–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 280–320

The old woman uses practical wisdom and domestic knowledge to neutralize Urgirinuna's magical attacks one by one, turning the witch's own sorcery against her.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 320–360; Black et al. 2004, pp. 258–259.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 320–360

Sagburu throws Urgirinuna's magical implements into the river, destroying the witch's power; the Euphrates carries the sorcery away.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 360–390; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 148–155.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 360–390

Urgirinuna is defeated and dies, her body cast into the river; the threat to Uruk is eliminated by Sagburu's intervention.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 390–410; Black et al. 2004, pp. 259–260.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 390–410

=== En-suhgir-ana's submission

When news of Urgirinuna's defeat reaches Aratta, En-suhgir-ana realizes that his magical strategy has failed and that Inanna's protection of Uruk is decisive.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 410–440; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 410–440

En-suhgir-ana acknowledges Enmerkar's supremacy, conceding that Inanna's favor belongs to Uruk; this concession resolves the Aratta cycle's overarching contest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 440–470; Black et al. 2004, pp. 260–261.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 440–470

The poem ends with an affirmation of Inanna's patronage of Uruk and Enmerkar's divinely endorsed supremacy over Aratta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, concluding lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 160–165.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, concluding lines

=== Literary significance

The contest structure — sorcery challenge, dispatch of champion, counter-champion, defeat, submission — follows a clear pattern that the audience would recognize as a test of divine favor.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 97–105

The motif of humble wisdom triumphing over foreign sorcery is the composition's central literary theme: Sagburu's domestic cunning defeats Urgirinuna's exotic magic.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 97–105

The poem affirms that Uruk's strength lies not in matching Aratta's power on its own terms but in possessing a deeper, divinely protected resilience embodied in an ordinary citizen.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, concluding lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 252–253

=== Textual transmission

The text is well-preserved across Old Babylonian manuscripts and forms part of the _edubba_ curriculum as the concluding piece of the Aratta cycle.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, manuscript list

Vanstiphout's critical edition provides seventy pages of text and commentary, documenting minor variant readings in the sorcery-contest sequence.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–169.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 97–169

This composition closes the Aratta diplomatic cycle that began with Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta and continued through the two Lugalbanda poems, bringing the contest between Uruk and Aratta to a definitive conclusion in Uruk's favor.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 252–253
