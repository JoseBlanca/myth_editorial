== The Debate between Bird and Fish — Claims

=== Setting and context

The composition "The Debate between Bird and Fish" is catalogued as ETCSL 5.3.5 and survives in multiple well-preserved Old Babylonian copies, indicating active use in the _edubba_ scribal schools.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, composite text, lines 1–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–240.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, composite text, lines 1–190

The text belongs to the Sumerian debate genre and features two divine personifications — Bird (Sumerian _mušen_) and Fish (Sumerian _ku₆_) — who argue their relative merits before the god Enki, who renders the final verdict.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 234–235

The choice of Enki rather than Enlil as judge distinguishes this debate from others in the genre and reflects the aquatic domain of the dispute: Enki, god of wisdom and the _abzu_ (the underground freshwater ocean), is the appropriate deity to adjudicate a contest involving creatures of the water and the air.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 234–235

=== Prologue: the natural order

The composition opens with a description of the natural order established by the gods, in which Bird and Fish each have their proper domain — the sky and the waters respectively.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 1–30

Bird is described in its natural habitat: nesting in the reeds, singing at dawn, filling the marshes with its cries.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~10–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 234.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 10–20

Fish is described in its domain: swimming in the deep waters, abundant in the rivers and canals, a source of purity and sustenance.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~20–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 20–30

=== Narrative sequence: the debate

Bird initiates the dispute by claiming superiority through flight, song, and beauty — it soars above the world, sings praises to the gods, and adorns the landscape.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~30–60; Black et al. 2004, pp. 235–236.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 30–60

Fish responds by claiming superiority through purity, abundance, and its association with Enki's sacred waters — fish are offerings fit for the gods and a staple of the temple economy.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~60–90; Black et al. 2004, pp. 236–237.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 60–90

[LACUNA: Minor damage to a few lines in the central exchange (lines 85–90) | source: etcsl 5.3.5 | scholarly_reconstruction: The text is exceptionally well-preserved and overlapping copies fill almost all gaps]

The debate escalates from polite self-praise to increasingly personal and aggressive insults, with each contestant denigrating the other's appearance, habits, and social worth.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~90–130; Black et al. 2004, pp. 237–238.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 90–130

Bird mocks Fish as a cold, slimy, mute creature trapped in the water, unable to see the sky or experience the world above.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~100–120; Black et al. 2004, pp. 237–238.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 100–120

Fish mocks Bird as a screeching, filthy creature that scratches in the dirt, devours worms, and fouls its own nest.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~120–140; Black et al. 2004, p. 238.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 120–140

=== Escalation: physical aggression

The debate transcends verbal argument and escalates into physical violence: Bird attacks Fish's nest and destroys Fish's eggs.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~140–170; Black et al. 2004, pp. 238–239.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 140–170

This escalation from rhetoric to physical destruction is exceptional within the debate genre; most other Sumerian debates remain verbal, making the Bird-Fish contest distinctive for its dramatic violence.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234, 238–239; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 238–239

[INFERENCE: The escalation to violence may serve a literary purpose — demonstrating what happens when debate fails to resolve a dispute and force is used instead, necessitating divine intervention to restore order | basis: The divine verdict follows directly upon the violent act, as if provoked by the breakdown of legitimate argument | risk: The text does not explicitly state this pedagogical function; this is interpretive]

=== The verdict

Enki renders the final verdict in favour of Bird, declaring Bird the winner of the contest.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~170–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 239–240.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 170–190

After the verdict, Fish accepts Bird's superiority, and the two creatures are reconciled under Enki's authority.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines ~185–190; Black et al. 2004, p. 240.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, lines 185–190

=== Cultural and literary function

The composition served a didactic function in the _edubba_, teaching scribal students the rich vocabulary of natural history — the names and habits of birds and fish — through the competitive debate structure.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 30–170; Civil 1994, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=civil-1994 ; loc=passim

The text is praised by modern scholars for its vivid imagery, escalating rhetoric, and the dramatic intensity of its action sequence, making it one of the most literarily accomplished debate poems in the Sumerian corpus.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–240.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 234–240

=== Attestation and transmission

Multiple well-preserved copies confirm the composition's active use in Old Babylonian scribal schools.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 234.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.5, manuscript list

Minor translational differences between ETCSL and Black et al. 2004 in animal epithets do not affect the narrative substance.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–240.]
