== Enki and the World Order: The Decrees of Destiny — Claims

=== Setting and context

The composition "Enki and the World Order" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.1.3 and extends to approximately 473 lines, making it one of the longest Sumerian narrative compositions preserved.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, composite text, lines 1–473.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, composite text, lines 1–473

The text is reconstructed from multiple Old Babylonian manuscripts from Nippur; no competing recension from another site is known.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, manuscript list

The composition reveals the Sumerian conception of cosmic bureaucracy more fully than any other single text in the corpus.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 215–225

=== Enki's self-praise and divine authority

The composition opens with a long self-praise speech by Enki, in which he extols his own wisdom, power, and role as organiser of the cosmos under the authority of Enlil and An.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 1–60; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–217.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 1–60

Enki declares that his father An has entrusted him, with Enlil (his elder brother) delegating authority to him, with the task of organising the world — assigning functions, establishing offices, and decreeing the destinies of lands, rivers, and crafts.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 50–80; Kramer 1961, pp. 59–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 50–80

The _me_ — divine powers or cultural blueprints governing civilisation's arts and institutions — are presented as the instruments through which Enki organises the world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 60–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 216–217.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 60–80

=== Tour of the lands

Enki travels to Sumer and blesses it, establishing its abundance and declaring its pre-eminence among all lands.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 80–120; Black et al. 2004, pp. 217–218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 80–120

Enki visits Ur and blesses it as a prosperous city of trade and livestock, the seat of the moon god Nanna-Suen.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 100–120; Black et al. 2004, p. 218.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 100–120

Enki blesses Meluhha (identified with the Indus Valley region or coastal East Africa), praising its exotic trees, gold, tin, and precious stones.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 125–145; Black et al. 2004, pp. 218–219.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 125–145

[LACUNA: Gap in the blessing of Meluhha (lines 141–147); details of trade goods and divine favour partially lost | source: etcsl 1.1.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: none — no duplicate manuscripts fill this passage]

Enki blesses Dilmun, the trading island, decreeing that Dilmun's ships shall carry goods between the lands.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 145–160; Black et al. 2004, p. 219.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 145–160

Enki visits Elam and Markhashi (eastern lands) and decrees their fates, though the passage is partially damaged.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 160–180; Black et al. 2004, p. 219.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 160–180

=== Ordering the rivers and waters

Enki fills the Tigris (Idigna) with sparkling water and appoints the god Enbilulu as its inspector, the "canal-controller of the gods."footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 250–270; Black et al. 2004, pp. 220–221.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 250–270

Enki fills the Euphrates (Buranuna) with life-giving water and establishes it as a source of abundance for the land.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 270–285; Black et al. 2004, p. 221.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 270–285

Enki organises the marshlands, placing fish in the reedbeds and appointing deities to oversee them.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 285–320; Black et al. 2004, pp. 221–222.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 285–320

[LACUNA: Transition passage between appointment of river deities and marsh deities (lines 318–324) damaged | source: etcsl 1.1.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: none — single composite tradition]

Enki organises the sea, establishing sea trade and appointing the goddess Nanshe as its overseer.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 320–340; Black et al. 2004, p. 222.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 320–340

=== Ordering agriculture and crafts

Enki decrees the fate of the plough and assigns Enlil's farmer-god Enkimdu to oversee agriculture.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 340–360; Black et al. 2004, p. 222.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 340–360

Enki establishes grain and the pickaxe, assigning Ashnan (goddess of grain) her domain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 360–375; Black et al. 2004, pp. 222–223.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 360–375

Enki organises brickmaking, building, and construction, assigning the god Kulla to brick-moulding and Mushdamma to architecture.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 375–395; Black et al. 2004, p. 223.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 375–395

Enki organises animal husbandry — the sheepfold, the cattle pen — and assigns Dumuzi (the shepherd-god) as their overseer.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 395–415; Black et al. 2004, pp. 223–224.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 395–415

=== Assignment of divine offices

Enki assigns Utu the sun god to oversee justice and the boundaries between lands.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 368–380.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 368–380

Enki assigns Nisaba, goddess of grain and writing, to be the scribe of the gods and the measurer of the land.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 415–430; Black et al. 2004, p. 224.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 415–430

[LACUNA: Scattered gaps of 3–7 lines throughout the composition; individual deity-appointment passages occasionally incomplete | source: etcsl 1.1.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: none — no parallel witnesses]

The systematic mapping of the world under divine jurisdiction — lands, rivers, crafts, agriculture — constitutes a portrait of cosmic administration as the Sumerians conceived it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 50–400; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 50–400

=== Inanna's complaint

After all other gods have received their domains, Inanna — goddess of love and war — comes before Enki to protest that she has been given no clear portfolio or office.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 430–460; Black et al. 2004, pp. 224–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 430–460

Inanna itemises the powers and offices given to other deities and contrasts them with her own lack of a defined domain, asking Enki what he has allotted to her.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 440–460; Kramer 1961, pp. 61–62.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 61–62

Enki responds to Inanna by listing the powers she does in fact possess — including the ability to destroy the indestructible and to make the powerful weak — suggesting that her domain is precisely the disruption of orderly categories.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 460–473; Black et al. 2004, p. 225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.1.3, lines 460–473

[INFERENCE: Inanna's complaint and Enki's response position her as the goddess who transcends and destabilises the very categories that Enki has established — an outsider to the system of order | basis: The text places her complaint as the compositional climax, after the entire bureaucratic ordering is complete | risk: This reading follows Kramer and Black but involves interpretive framing of Inanna's theological role]

Inanna's complaint at the close of the composition sets up narratively the tensions explored in later Inanna-cycle texts, where she actively seizes or demands the _me_ and descends to the underworld.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 224–225.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 224–225

=== The _me_ concept

The _me_ as presented in this text are distinct from the Akkadian _parsu_ (divine decrees / ritual offices); the Sumerian concept encompasses a broader category of civilisational blueprints including arts, crafts, social institutions, and moral qualities.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–216 (introduction); Kramer 1961, pp. 59–62.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 215–216

The _me_ assignments in this composition mirror the temple-administrative hierarchies attested in contemporary royal inscriptions from the Ur III and Old Babylonian periods.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 59–62.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 59–62

=== Attestation

The composition's length (~470 lines) and its attestation in multiple OB manuscript witnesses from Nippur indicate its importance in the scribal curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, manuscript list.]
