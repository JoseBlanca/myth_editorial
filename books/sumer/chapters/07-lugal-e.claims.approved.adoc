=== Source overview

The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.6.2 and survives on numerous Old Babylonian manuscripts from multiple sites, making it one of the most extensively attested Sumerian compositions.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, bibliography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, bibliography

A very long composition of approximately 730 lines; the ETCSL text is a composite assembled from many overlapping witnesses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, composite text

The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–191; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–272.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–191; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–272.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, composite text

=== Setting and context

The composition known as "Lugal-e" (from its incipit, "O king") is catalogued as ETCSL 1.6.2 and extends to approximately 729 lines, making it one of the longest Sumerian literary compositions preserved.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, composite text, lines 1–729.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, composite text, lines 1–729

The text is attested in numerous Old Babylonian manuscripts from Nippur and elsewhere; it is one of the most copied Sumerian literary compositions in the corpus.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–191.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, manuscript list

The ETCSL composite is assembled from multiple overlapping manuscripts; no single witness is complete, and the composite represents an editorial reconstruction.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, editorial notes; Black et al. 2004, p. 163 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, editorial notes

Jacobsen devotes forty pages to the text and its cosmological implications, making it a standard reference in all surveys of Sumerian literature.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–272.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 233–272

[VARIANT: primary="ETCSL composite text, standard editorial reconstruction from OB manuscripts" | alt="Jacobsen 1987 pp. 233–272 provides a literary translation that smooths over gaps and occasionally follows different manuscript readings, especially in the stone-fate section" | chosen: ETCSL | reason: "ETCSL represents the broader manuscript base; Jacobsen used for literary context"]

=== Prologue and divine warrior

Ninurta is introduced as the warrior god, champion of the gods, and son of Enlil — a deity of terrifying martial power whose weapon is the sentient mace Sharur (literally "smiter of thousands").footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–165.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 1–30

[LACUNA: Opening lines (1–20) partially damaged in several witnesses; invocation and initial battle context fragmentary | source: etcsl 1.6.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: composite reconstruction from multiple OB manuscripts restores most of the opening]

Sharur functions as Ninurta's battlefield counsellor: the mace speaks to Ninurta, providing intelligence about the enemy and urging him to action.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 20–50; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 235–238.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 20–50

=== The threat of Asag

Asag is a monstrous demon of sickness, born of An (heaven) and Ki (earth), who has raised an army of stones against the gods from his stronghold in the _kur_ (the mountain / foreign land / netherworld).footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 30–70; Black et al. 2004, pp. 165–167.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 30–70

The stone army is described in vivid terms: the stones are warriors who rise up en masse from the mountains, forming a vast hostile force.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 50–80; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 238–240.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 50–80

Asag's power is so great that his _me-lam_ (terrifying divine radiance) causes the fish to boil in the rivers and the vegetation to wither — the natural order itself is threatened.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 60–90; Black et al. 2004, pp. 166–167.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 60–90

=== First engagement and Ninurta's retreat

Ninurta advances against Asag but is initially overwhelmed by the demon's power; in some readings, he suffers a tactical retreat.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 90–130; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 240–243.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 90–130

Sharur travels to Enlil's temple in Nippur to report the dire situation and to obtain divine encouragement for Ninurta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 130–160; Black et al. 2004, pp. 168–169.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 130–160

Enlil sends back words of encouragement through Sharur, urging Ninurta to renewed combat.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 160–180; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 243–245.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 160–180

=== The decisive battle

Encouraged by Enlil's message, Ninurta returns to battle and engages Asag with full divine fury.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 180–220; Black et al. 2004, pp. 169–171.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 180–220

The battle is described in terms of cosmic upheaval: storms rage, the earth shakes, mountains tremble, and the sky darkens as Ninurta unleashes his arsenal.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 200–240; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 245–248.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 200–240

Ninurta defeats Asag, slaying the demon in a climactic confrontation that restores the possibility of cosmic order.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 240–270; Black et al. 2004, pp. 171–172.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 240–270

=== The flood crisis and damming of the waters

After Asag's defeat, a new crisis emerges: the primeval waters (the _kur_ waters, the subterranean flood) rush up from beneath the earth and inundate the land, threatening to return the world to its pre-civilised state.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 270–320; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 248–252.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 270–320

The rising waters prevent the fresh water of the Tigris from flowing; agriculture ceases, canals cannot function, and famine threatens the gods and the land.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 290–330; Black et al. 2004, pp. 173–175.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 290–330

Ninurta responds to the flood crisis by heaping up a great dam of stones — using the very stones that had formed Asag's army — to hold back the primeval waters.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 330–370; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 252–255.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 330–370

The dam restrains the hostile waters and channels the Tigris to flow properly, restoring irrigation and agriculture to the land — Ninurta's victory thus creates the fertile landscape of Mesopotamia.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 370–400; Black et al. 2004, pp. 175–177.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 370–400

[INFERENCE: The damming episode functions as an aetiology for the Mesopotamian irrigation system and the geography of the Tigris river basin — Ninurta's stones become the mountains that channel the rivers | basis: Jacobsen 1987 pp. 252–255 explicitly reads this as landscape aetiology | risk: The text may have had additional meanings (ritual, theological) beyond geographical aetiology]

=== The stone-fate catalogue

After establishing the dam, Ninurta addresses the individual stones that formed Asag's army, decreeing the fate of each one — rewarding those that aided him and cursing those that fought against him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 400–650 (approximate); Black et al. 2004, pp. 177–188.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 400–650

The stone-fate catalogue is the longest single section of the composition, encompassing approximately 250 lines and addressing dozens of individual stones and minerals.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 400–650; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 255–266.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 400–650

[LACUNA: Portions of the stone-fate assignment section (lines 330–370) damaged, with gaps in the individual verdicts for some stones | source: etcsl 1.6.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: overlapping manuscripts cover different subsections but no single witness is complete]

Stones that are praised and given beneficial fates include diorite, which is decreed worthy of bearing royal inscriptions, and lapis lazuli, which is honoured for its beauty and association with heaven.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 420–440 (approximate); Black et al. 2004, pp. 178–180.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 420–440

Stones that are cursed include those that served Asag faithfully; they are condemned to be broken, ground, or used for menial purposes.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 440–500 (approximate); Jacobsen 1987, pp. 258–262.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 440–500

The catalogue includes stones used in building (limestone, bitite), stones used as weights and tools (hematite), and decorative stones, reflecting a comprehensive Sumerian mineralogical knowledge embedded in mythological narrative.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 400–650; Black et al. 2004, pp. 177–188.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 177–188

[INFERENCE: The stone-fate catalogue may reflect the practical mineralogical knowledge of the scribal schools, embedded in narrative form for pedagogical transmission | basis: The stone names correspond to known Sumerian mineral terminology attested in lexical lists from the same OB period | risk: The extent to which the catalogue was used as a teaching text for mineralogy vs. pure literary entertainment is uncertain]

The stone-fate assignments suggest a connection to mineralogical lore and temple-building ritual knowledge, as the fates decreed correspond to the actual uses of these stones in Sumerian material culture.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–164 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 163–164

=== Ninurta's mother and the renaming of Ninmah

After the stone-fate decrees, Ninurta's mother comes to the _kur_ to see her son's victory; she is identified in this passage as Ninmah (the "exalted lady").footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 650–690; Black et al. 2004, pp. 189–190.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 650–690

Ninurta honours his mother for her role and, in recognition of his victory at the mountain, renames her Ninhursaja — "Lady of the Mountain" (_hursag_ = mountain) — giving her the name by which she is most commonly known in the Sumerian pantheon.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 690–720; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 267–270.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 690–720

[LACUNA: Closing hymnic section (lines 680–729) has minor gaps in the praise formula and the mother-renaming passage | source: etcsl 1.6.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: multiple manuscripts preserve the ending with minor variation]

The renaming establishes an aetiology for Ninhursaja's principal epithet and connects the mountain goddess to the landscape created by Ninurta's damming of the waters.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 690–729; Black et al. 2004, pp. 190–191.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 690–729

=== Closing hymnic section

The composition closes with a hymnic praise of Ninurta, celebrating his martial exploits and his role as cosmic organiser — the warrior who defeated chaos and built the world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 720–729; Black et al. 2004, p. 191.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 720–729

=== Cosmological function

The composition explains the creation of the Tigris river system and the fertile plains of Mesopotamia as a result of Ninurta's damming of the primeval waters after defeating Asag.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 400–460; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 248–255.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.2, lines 400–460

The text establishes the _kur_ as a place of both threat and transformation: the hostile mountain becomes, through Ninurta's action, the source of the stones that build civilisation and the dam that channels life-giving water.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 252–266.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 252–266

=== Chaoskampf pattern

The composition exemplifies the Chaoskampf pattern — a young warrior god defeats a primordial chaos being to organise the cosmos — a pattern found across ancient Near Eastern mythologies.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–234; Kramer 1961, pp. 80–83.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 233–234

Lugal-e predates the Babylonian Enuma Elish (composed no earlier than the late second millennium BCE) and should not be read as derivative of the Marduk-Tiamat combat; if anything, the influence runs in the other direction.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–234; Black et al. 2004, p. 163.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=jacobsen-1987 ; loc=pp. 233–234

[INFERENCE: Lugal-e represents the Sumerian prototype of the Chaoskampf tradition that later influenced the Babylonian Enuma Elish's Marduk-Tiamat battle | basis: The OB dating of Lugal-e manuscripts predates all known copies of Enuma Elish by several centuries; structural parallels (warrior god, chaos monster, cosmic reorganisation) are extensive | risk: Direct textual transmission from Lugal-e to Enuma Elish is not documented; the relationship may be indirect, through a shared tradition]

=== Attestation and transmission

The composition's wide attestation across multiple OB sites and its length indicate its central importance in the scribal curriculum and Sumerian literary tradition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, manuscript list.]
