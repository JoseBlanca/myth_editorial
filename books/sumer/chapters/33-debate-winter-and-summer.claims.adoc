== The Debate between Winter and Summer — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.
// Source: ETCSL 5.3.3, composite text, lines 1–310. Mostly complete from frequent Nippur school copies.
// Variant classification: single-prevalent (ETCSL composite with good inter-manuscript agreement)

=== Setting and context

The composition "The Debate between Winter and Summer" is catalogued as ETCSL 5.3.3 and survives in frequent Old Babylonian copies from Nippur scribal school sites, confirming its standard place in the _edubba_ curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, composite text, lines 1–310; Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–233.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, composite text, lines 1–310

The text belongs to the Sumerian debate genre and features two divine personifications — Enten (Winter) and Emesh (Summer) — who argue their relative contributions to agriculture before Enlil, who renders the final verdict.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–227; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 226–227

In the Sumerian seasonal calendar of southern Mesopotamia, Enten (Winter) corresponds to the wet season of rain and flooding, while Emesh (Summer) corresponds to the dry season of harvest and heat.footnote:[Civil 1994, passim (agricultural calendar and seasonal terminology); Black et al. 2004, p. 226.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=civil-1994 ; loc=passim

=== Cosmogonic prologue: creation of the seasons

The composition opens with a prologue in which Enlil creates the two seasons and assigns each specific duties to ensure agricultural productivity throughout the year.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–227.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 1–30

Enlil creates Enten (Winter) to manage the rains, the flooding of the rivers, and the fertility of the soil — the processes that replenish the irrigation system and prepare the land for cultivation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 226.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 1–20

Enlil creates Emesh (Summer) to manage the ripening of crops, the harvest, and the abundance that follows from Winter's preparatory work.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 20–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–227.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 20–30

The prologue frames the seasonal cycle as a deliberate divine creation, with each season assigned complementary responsibilities by the chief god.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, p. 226.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 1–30

=== Narrative sequence: the debate

After fulfilling their seasonal duties, Emesh (Summer) and Enten (Winter) bring their produce to Enlil's temple as offerings, and a dispute arises over which has contributed more.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~30–60; Black et al. 2004, pp. 227–228.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 30–60

Emesh boasts of providing the harvest — the ripe grain, the abundant fruit, the prosperity of the fields under the sun — and claims credit for the visible wealth of the land.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~60–100; Black et al. 2004, pp. 228–229.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 60–100

Enten counters that without the winter rains and floods, the irrigation canals would be dry, the soil barren, and Emesh would have nothing to ripen; Winter is therefore the foundational season upon which all agricultural prosperity depends.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~100–140; Black et al. 2004, pp. 229–230.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 100–140

[LACUNA: Several lines in the central exchange partially broken (lines 140–155) | source: etcsl 5.3.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: Overlapping Nippur school tablets restore the passage in large part]

The debate covers the full agricultural cycle: water management, sowing, growth, ripening, and harvest, with each season claiming credit for the stages under its domain.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 50–280; Black et al. 2004, pp. 228–232.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 50–280

Enten accuses Emesh of being merely a parasite on the work Winter has done — enjoying the results without providing the essential foundation of water and fertile soil.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~160–200; Black et al. 2004, pp. 230–231.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 160–200

Emesh retorts that heat and sunlight are indispensable for ripening and that without Summer the grain would rot in waterlogged fields.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~200–250; Black et al. 2004, pp. 231–232.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 200–250

=== The verdict

Enlil renders the final verdict in favour of Enten (Winter), declaring Winter the winner of the contest.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~280–310; Black et al. 2004, pp. 232–233.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 280–310

[INFERENCE: Enlil's verdict for Winter reflects the ecological reality of southern Mesopotamian irrigation agriculture — rain and flooding are the indispensable basis of the entire system, without which no harvest is possible | basis: Southern Mesopotamia is an alluvial floodplain entirely dependent on water management; the text's internal logic consistently privileges the season that provides water | risk: The text does not state the reasoning in explicit ecological terms; this is scholarly interpretation grounded in Civil 1994 and the text's own argument structure]

After the verdict, Emesh (Summer) accepts Enten's (Winter's) superiority, and the two seasons are reconciled; they praise each other and restore harmony.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines ~300–310; Black et al. 2004, p. 233.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 300–310

=== Cultural and cosmological function

The composition served a didactic function in the _edubba_: it taught scribal students the relative contributions of the wet and dry seasons to agricultural productivity through the structure of competitive argument.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 50–280; Civil 1994, passim.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=civil-1994 ; loc=passim

The cosmological prologue frames the seasonal cycle as a divinely ordained structure, with Enlil as both creator of the seasons and ultimate arbiter of their relative worth.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, p. 226.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, lines 1–30

=== Attestation and transmission

Frequent copies from Nippur school tablets confirm the composition's active circulation in the Old Babylonian scribal curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 226.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.3, manuscript list

Minor rendering differences between ETCSL and Black et al. 2004 in seasonal epithets do not affect the narrative substance.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–233.]
