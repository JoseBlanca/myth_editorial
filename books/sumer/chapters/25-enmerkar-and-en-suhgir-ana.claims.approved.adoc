The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.8.2.4 and survives on Old Babylonian tablets.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, bibliography.]

Generally well-preserved.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, composite text.]

The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–169; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–261.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–169; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–261.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, composite text

=== Setting and context

En-suhgir-ana is lord of Aratta, the distant wealthy city that has been the rival of Uruk throughout the Aratta cycle; he challenges Enmerkar, king of Uruk, to a new contest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, opening lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, opening lines

The contest in this composition shifts from the diplomatic and rhetorical exchanges of the preceding Enmerkar poem to a direct sorcery challenge: the conflict is decided through magical power rather than rhetoric.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 114–150; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 114–150

Inanna remains the divine patron of Uruk whose favor ensures Enmerkar's ultimate victory, continuing the theological framework established in Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, concluding lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, concluding lines

=== The sorcery challenge

En-suhgir-ana boasts that he will defeat Enmerkar through magical means; his minister Ansiga-ria identifies a sorcerer, Ur-jirnuna, from Hamazu, as a champion for the magical contest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 114–150; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 110–118.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 114–150

=== Dispatch of Urgirinuna

Ur-jirnuna, a male sorcerer originally from the destroyed city of Hamazu, is dispatched to defeat Uruk through sorcery; he is presented as a formidable practitioner of magic.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 135–180; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 118–130.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 135–180

Ur-jirnuna travels to Eresh (the city of Nisaba), not directly to Uruk, and begins to exercise his magical powers against the livestock — the cows' milk goes to their horns, the goats' milk to their backs.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 170–205; Black et al. 2004, pp. 255–256.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 170–205

The sorcerer's magic threatens the pastoral foundations of the region's prosperity, disrupting the milk production of cows and goats.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 170–205; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 130–138.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 170–205

=== Sagburu defeats the sorcerer

Wise Woman Sagburu of Eresh encounters Ur-jirnuna and recognizes him as a foreign sorcerer threatening the region.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 222–228; Black et al. 2004, pp. 257–258.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 222–228

Sagburu defeats the sorcerer through superior counter-magic in a series of five animal-conjuring contests: she conjures predatory animals from the river (eagle against carp, wolf against ewe, cow against lion, ibex against leopard, gazelle against tiger), each of which overcomes the sorcerer's conjured creature.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 228–248; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 138–148.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 228–248

After defeating Ur-jirnuna's magical creatures one by one, Sagburu throws the sorcerer himself from the bank of the Euphrates and seizes his life-force, killing him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, line 273; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 148–155.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, line 273

=== En-suhgir-ana's submission

When news of Ur-jirnuna's defeat reaches Aratta, En-suhgir-ana realizes that his magical strategy has failed and that Inanna's protection of Uruk is decisive.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 274–281; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 155–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 274–281

En-suhgir-ana acknowledges Enmerkar's supremacy, declaring "From the moment of conception I was not your equal, you are the older brother. I cannot match you ever."footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 274–281; Black et al. 2004, pp. 260–261.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, lines 274–281

The poem ends with an affirmation of Inanna's patronage of Uruk and Enmerkar's divinely endorsed supremacy over Aratta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, concluding lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 160–165.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, concluding lines

=== Literary significance

The contest structure — sorcery challenge, dispatch of champion, counter-champion, defeat, submission — follows a clear pattern that the audience would recognize as a test of divine favor.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 97–105

The composition's central literary theme is local, divinely backed sorcery triumphing over foreign sorcery: Sagburu, titled "Wise Woman," defeats Ur-jirnuna through superior counter-magic rather than domestic cunning.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 97–105

The poem affirms that Uruk's strength lies in possessing a deeper, divinely protected resilience; Sagburu is a titled figure ("Wise Woman") of recognized authority who defeats the foreign sorcerer through superior magical power.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, concluding lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 252–253

=== Textual transmission

The text is well-preserved across Old Babylonian manuscripts (32–37 witnesses) and forms part of the _edubba_ curriculum as one of the four Aratta cycle compositions, with its position in the narrative sequence debated by scholars.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, manuscript list; CDLI P469675.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.4, manuscript list

Vanstiphout's critical edition provides seventy pages of text and commentary, documenting minor variant readings in the sorcery-contest sequence.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–169.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 97–169

This composition is one of the four Aratta cycle texts; it ends with En-suhgir-ana's submission to Enmerkar, bringing the contest between Uruk and Aratta to a conclusion in Uruk's favor.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 252–253
