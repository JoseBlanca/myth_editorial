=== Setting and context

The composition "Ninurta and the Turtle" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.6.3 and survives only in fragmentary form; the text is heavily damaged and no complete witness exists.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3, composite text (fragmentary).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, composite text

Few copies survive and the text's limited attestation indicates it circulated less widely than Lugal-e (ETCSL 1.6.2) or Angim (ETCSL 1.6.1) within the scribal curriculum.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, manuscript list

Black et al. describe the composition briefly as a text in which Ninurta's heroic deed is undercut by Enki's cunning, situating it as a comic or satirical counterpoint to the martial Ninurta cycle.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]

=== The theft and recovery of the Tablet of Destinies

The narrative presupposes that Anzud, the divine lion-headed eagle, has stolen from the abzu.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3, lines 1–15 (fragmentary); Kramer 1961, pp. 82–83.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, lines 1–15

Ninurta, the warrior god, defeats Anzud and recovers the stolen Tablet of Destinies — an act of heroic service to the divine order.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3 (narrative premise recoverable from surviving lines); Kramer 1961, pp. 82–83.]

=== Ninurta's ambition and Enki's alarm

After recovering the Tablet of Destinies, Ninurta apparently seeks to keep its power for himself rather than returning it to the proper divine authority.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3 (mid-section, surviving lines); Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–200.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, mid-section

[INFERENCE: Ninurta's attempt to retain the Tablet of Destinies constitutes the hubristic act that the narrative punishes — the warrior who performed a heroic deed now overreaches by claiming the supreme cosmic prerogative for himself | basis: The surviving text and scholarly summaries consistently describe Ninurta's ambition as the trigger for Enki's countermeasure; Black et al. 2004 p. 199 frames this as hubris-punishment | risk: The fragmentary state of the text means that the precise nature and extent of Ninurta's overreach cannot be confirmed from the Sumerian alone]

Enki, god of wisdom and cunning and lord of the _abzu_, perceives that Ninurta's possession of the Tablet of Destinies threatens the cosmic balance and devises a stratagem to humble the warrior.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3 (mid-section); Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–200.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, mid-section

=== The turtle trap

Enki creates a turtle — a creature fashioned by divine craft — and sets it as a trap for Ninurta.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3 (mid-section, surviving lines); Black et al. 2004, p. 200.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, mid-section

[LACUNA: Middle section (lines 50–80) fragmentary; details of the turtle's creation by Enki and the precise mechanism of the trap are partially lost | source: etcsl 1.6.3 | scholarly_reconstruction: no other Sumerian witness fills these gaps]

The turtle seizes Ninurta — by the feet or legs, according to the surviving text — and drags him into a pit that Enki has dug.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3 (surviving lines in mid-to-late section); Black et al. 2004, p. 200.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3, mid-to-late section

Ninurta, the mighty warrior who defeated Anzud and (in Lugal-e) slew the demon Asag, is now trapped in a pit by a turtle — a deliberately comic reversal of his martial prowess.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 199–201

=== Ninurta in the pit

Trapped in the pit, Ninurta is unable to free himself; the warrior god who conquered the _kur_ is now helpless in a hole dug by the trickster god.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3 (late section, surviving lines); Black et al. 2004, pp. 200–201.]

=== Literary and theological function

The composition functions as a comic or satirical counterpoint to the heroic Ninurta narratives (Lugal-e and Angim), deliberately deflating martial pride through trickster cleverness.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201; ETCSL 1.6.3.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 199–201

The text is didactic in function: it punishes hubris by demonstrating that Ninurta's arrogance after recovering the Tablet of Destinies is checked by Enki's superior cunning — wisdom defeats brute force.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.6.3

Together with Lugal-e and Angim, this composition forms a Ninurta triptych: cosmic battle, triumphant return, and comic deflation — a range of registers that suggests the Sumerian scribes were capable of treating heroism with both exaltation and mockery.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201 (contextual placement within the Ninurta cycle).]
