== Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Source overview

The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.8.1.4 and survives on numerous Old Babylonian tablets from multiple sites.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, bibliography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, bibliography

The text is a composite assembled from many overlapping witnesses; it opens with a cosmogonic prologue and is the basis for Tablet XII of the later Akkadian Gilgamesh.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, composite text.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, composite text

The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by Black et al. 2004, pp. 285–296.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 285–296.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, composite text

=== Setting and context

The composition ETCSL 1.8.1.4 is approximately 303 lines long in the ETCSL composite (Gadotti's 2014 critical edition gives ca. 330 lines from 74 manuscripts) and is one of the most widely attested Sumerian Gilgamesh poems, preserved in numerous Old Babylonian tablets from Nippur and other sites.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–303, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, manuscript list

The text has a composite-editorial classification: the ETCSL composite draws on numerous Old Babylonian tablets, and no single manuscript preserves the whole.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4

The composition is structurally tripartite: a cosmogonic prologue describing the separation of heaven and earth; a huluppu-tree narrative involving Inanna and Gilgamesh; and a nether-world descent-and-report sequence involving Enkidu's ghost.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–303; Black et al. 2004, pp. 285–286.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 285–286

=== Cosmogonic prologue

The composition opens with a cosmogonic prologue that describes the primordial separation of heaven (An) and earth (Ki) and the division of the cosmos between the great gods.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–13.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 1–13

In the beginning, heaven was carried off by An, earth was carried off by Enlil, and the nether world (_kur_) was given to Ereshkigal as a prize — this establishes the fundamental tripartite cosmology of the Sumerian universe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, pp. 286–287.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 1–10

Enki sets sail for the _kur_, and the sea attacks his boat — the keel trembles as if butted by turtles, and waves at the bow rise to devour the king like wolves — a reference to a primordial conflict whose full context is lost.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 10–20.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 10–20

The cosmogonic prologue should be treated as integral to the composition, not as a later scribal addition, because it establishes the cosmological framework — the existence and nature of the _kur_ — that the entire subsequent narrative depends on.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, pp. 285–286.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 285–286

=== The huluppu tree

After the cosmogonic prologue, the narrative shifts to a huluppu tree — a sacred tree that Inanna finds on the bank of the Euphrates after the primordial events.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 20–40.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 20–40

Inanna transplants the huluppu tree to her holy garden in Uruk, intending to fashion from it a throne and a bed when it has grown to maturity.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 30–50; Black et al. 2004, pp. 287–288.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 287–288

Three creatures infest the huluppu tree, preventing Inanna from using it: a serpent that cannot be charmed nests at its roots, an Anzud bird (not Anzu, the later form) sets its young in the crown, and the phantom maid (ki-sikil-lil-la, sometimes identified with Lilith though this connection is contested) builds her dwelling in the trunk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 40–60.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 40–60

Inanna weeps and appeals for help. Gilgamesh responds: he kills the serpent, causing the Anzud bird to flee with its young to the mountains and the phantom maid to flee to the wilderness.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 60–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 288–289.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 60–80

Gilgamesh fells the huluppu tree and gives the trunk to Inanna for her throne and bed. From the roots and crown of the tree, he fashions two ritual objects: the ellag and the ekidma (often rendered as pukku and mekku — possibly a ball and stick, or a drum and drumstick; the identification remains debated).footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 80–100.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 80–100

[INFERENCE: The exact identification of the pukku and mekku is uncertain. Proposals include a ball and mallet used in a game, a drum and drumstick, or ritual objects of unknown function. | basis: Scholarly reviews of the evidence find no definitive identification. | risk: The identification remains one of the most debated questions in Sumerian philology; any specific rendering is provisional.]

=== The loss of the ritual objects

Gilgamesh uses the pukku and mekku in a manner that oppresses the young men and women of Uruk — the text describes their complaints about his behaviour.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 100–120; Black et al. 2004, pp. 289–290.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 289–290

As a consequence of the citizens' cries, the pukku and mekku fall into the nether world (_kur_), beyond Gilgamesh's reach.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 120–140.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 120–140

Gilgamesh sits at the gate of the nether world and laments the loss of his ritual objects, unable to retrieve them himself.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 140–150.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 140–150

=== Enkidu's descent

Enkidu volunteers to descend to the nether world to retrieve the pukku and mekku for Gilgamesh.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 150–160.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 150–160

Gilgamesh gives Enkidu detailed instructions on how to behave in the nether world: a catalogue of taboos that Enkidu must observe to avoid being trapped — he must not wear clean clothes, anoint himself with fine oil, hurl throw-sticks or hold a cornel-wood stick, wear sandals, make noise, kiss or hit his wife, or kiss or hit his child — anything that would identify him as a living person among the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 155–185; Black et al. 2004, pp. 290–292.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 155–185

[LACUNA: Portions of Enkidu's taboo-breaking instructions and descent sequence (approximately lines 150–175) are partially damaged. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 150–175 | scholarly_reconstruction: Numerous OB tablets overlap and allow reconstruction of most gaps; the taboo catalogue is substantially secure.]

Enkidu descends to the nether world and systematically breaks every one of Gilgamesh's taboos, doing everything his companion warned him against.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 185–210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 185–210

As a result of violating the taboos, Enkidu is seized by the nether world and cannot return to the living. He is trapped among the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 210–220; Black et al. 2004, pp. 292–293.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 292–293

=== The appeal to the gods

Gilgamesh appeals to the gods for help in recovering Enkidu. He approaches Enlil in Nippur, but Enlil does not help.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 220–230.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 220–230

Gilgamesh approaches Enki in Eridu, and Enki agrees to help by instructing the sun god Utu to open a hole in the nether world through which Enkidu's ghost can rise.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 230–245.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 230–245

Utu opens a hole in the earth, and the ghost (gidim) of Enkidu rises from the nether world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 245–250.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 245–250

Gilgamesh and the ghost of Enkidu embrace — a moment of reunion between the living and the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 250–255.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 250–255

=== The catalogue of the dead

Gilgamesh questions Enkidu's ghost about conditions in the nether world, and Enkidu delivers an extended catalogue of the dead: a systematic report on the fates of the deceased organised by manner of death, number of children, and social status.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 255–303; Black et al. 2004, pp. 293–296.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 293–296

The catalogue reveals that those who died with many sons fare better in the nether world than those who died childless (he who had seven sons sits on a throne and listens to judgments); the stillborn child plays at a table of gold and silver laden with honey and ghee; the unburied dead eat the scraps and crumbs tossed out in the street.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 260–290.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 260–290

[LACUNA: Parts of Enkidu's ghost's report on afterlife conditions (approximately lines 240–270) are fragmentary in some witnesses. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 240–270 | scholarly_reconstruction: Partial; manuscript overlap covers most but not all of the catalogue of the dead; some conditions described for specific categories of the dead remain uncertain.]

The catalogue constitutes the most detailed Sumerian eschatological text: a systematic taxonomy of the afterlife organised by social and biographical categories.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 255–303.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 255–303

The overall picture of the nether world is characterised by conditional welfare based on earthly circumstances: the quality of the afterlife depends primarily on the number of one's children, burial status, and manner of death during life.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 280–303.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 280–303

=== Relationship to the Akkadian tradition

Akkadian Tablet XII of the Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh epic is a partial translation of this Sumerian composition — specifically, of the descent-and-report section — appended to the epic as a supplement. It is not a variant of the Sumerian text but a direct, if selective, rendering into Akkadian.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4

The cosmogonic prologue and the huluppu-tree narrative that open the Sumerian composition are not included in Akkadian Tablet XII, which begins only at the point of Enkidu's descent.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4

=== Thematic significance

The cosmogonic prologue establishes that the nether world's existence is coeval with the cosmos: it came into being at the moment of the primal separation of heaven and earth and was assigned to Ereshkigal from the beginning.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–13.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 1–13

[INFERENCE: The huluppu-tree episode encodes Inanna's dependence on male heroic action (Gilgamesh) to realise her aspirations (the throne and bed), while simultaneously providing the origin of the objects whose loss drives the second half of the narrative. | basis: The text shows Inanna unable to use the tree until Gilgamesh intervenes. | risk: This is one modern scholarly reading; the text does not make this gendered interpretation explicit.]footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 20–100.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 20–100

The taboo catalogue — Gilgamesh's instructions to Enkidu — functions as a guide to the boundary between life and death: each prohibition marks a behaviour that identifies the living person, and each violation marks Enkidu's transition from living visitor to trapped dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 155–210.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 155–210

The eschatological vision is the composition's most significant cultural contribution: the idea that the quality of one's death and afterlife depends on specific biographical facts — number of children, manner of death, proper burial — rather than on moral conduct during life.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 255–303.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.4, lines 255–303

[INFERENCE: The absence of moral judgement in the nether-world catalogue — fates depend on biography, not ethics — represents a fundamentally different eschatological logic from traditions that organise the afterlife by moral desert. | basis: The catalogue sorts the dead by manner of death, social status, and number of children, with no mention of virtuous or sinful conduct. | risk: The text may be incomplete or selective; absence of evidence for moral judgement is not evidence of its absence in the broader tradition.]
