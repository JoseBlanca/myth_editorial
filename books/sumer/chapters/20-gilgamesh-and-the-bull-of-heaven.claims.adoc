== Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven — Claims

// This is a claims document, not final prose. Each paragraph is one verifiable claim.

=== Setting and context

The composition ETCSL 1.8.1.2 is a heavily damaged Sumerian text narrating the episode in which Inanna sends the Bull of Heaven (Sumerian: gudanna) against Uruk, and Gilgamesh (Sumerian: Bilgames) and Enkidu slay it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, composite text (heavily damaged).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.2

[LACUNA: The majority of lines in this composition are damaged or lost. Large portions of the narrative are missing, including much of the battle sequence. Insufficient Sumerian witnesses survive to restore the text; the Akkadian Gilgamesh Tablet VI is a separate tradition and must not be used to fill these gaps. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: None adequate from Sumerian sources alone.]

Inanna, goddess of love and war, patron of Uruk, sends the gudanna — the Bull of Heaven, a cosmic beast — against the city, apparently in response to a perceived affront or rejection by Gilgamesh.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, surviving lines; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

[INFERENCE: The motivation for Inanna's sending of the bull appears to involve Gilgamesh's rejection of her advances or some form of insult, consistent with the broader tradition. | basis: The Sumerian fragments preserve enough context to suggest a conflict between Inanna and Gilgamesh as the trigger. | risk: The precise cause is lost in the Sumerian version; this inference is supported by the broader tradition but cannot be confirmed from the surviving Sumerian text alone.]

=== Narrative sequence

The Bull of Heaven descends upon Uruk and causes devastation, though the specific details of the destruction are largely lost in the Sumerian witnesses.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, surviving fragments.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.1.2, surviving fragments

Gilgamesh and Enkidu confront and slay the Bull of Heaven together, though the battle sequence itself is heavily fragmentary in the surviving Sumerian tablets.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, surviving fragments; Kramer 1961, pp. 192–193.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 192–193

[LACUNA: The combat sequence between the heroes and the bull is almost entirely lost in the Sumerian witnesses. The precise tactics, dialogue, and aftermath of the slaying cannot be reconstructed from the available Sumerian evidence. | source: ETCSL 1.8.1.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: None from Sumerian sources; George 2003 discusses the fragments but does not conflate them with the Akkadian tradition.]

The slaying of the Bull of Heaven constitutes a direct challenge to divine authority, as the beast was sent by a major deity (Inanna) and its destruction by mortal heroes dramatises the limits of divine power.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

=== Iconographic and cultural evidence

Despite the severe textual damage, the bull-slaying motif is among the most widely attested Mesopotamian mythological scenes in visual art: it appears on cylinder seals from the Early Dynastic through Old Babylonian periods.footnote:[CDLI; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=cdli ; loc=cylinder seal corpus, bull-slaying motifs

The iconographic prevalence of the bull-slaying scene confirms that this episode was central to the Gilgamesh tradition, even though the Sumerian literary text survives in poor condition.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

=== Relationship to the Akkadian tradition

The Akkadian Gilgamesh epic (Standard Babylonian Tablet VI) contains a fully developed version of the Bull of Heaven episode, but George identifies this as a separate literary tradition that developed from, but is not identical to, the Sumerian composition.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=george-2003 ; loc=vol. 1, pp. 10–12

[INFERENCE: The Sumerian and Akkadian Bull of Heaven episodes share a common narrative kernel — Inanna's offence, the bull's attack, the heroes' slaying — but the Akkadian version represents an independent literary development, not a translation. | basis: George 2003 explicitly distinguishes the two traditions. | risk: The degree of direct literary dependence between the Sumerian and Akkadian versions cannot be established given the fragmentary state of the Sumerian text.]
