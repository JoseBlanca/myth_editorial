=== Source overview

The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.2.2 and survives on Old Babylonian manuscripts; the narrative is set in Eresh (the city of Nisaba), not Shuruppag as sometimes stated.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, bibliography.]

=== Setting and context

The composition "Enlil and Sud" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.2.2 and extends to approximately 170 lines, surviving as a well-preserved Old Babylonian composition with no competing recension.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, composite text, lines 1–170.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, composite text, lines 1–170

The text presents the orderly, sanctioned courtship of Enlil and Sud — a narrative that contrasts sharply with the transgressive encounter narrated in "Enlil and Ninlil" (ETCSL 1.2.1, chapter 5).footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 106–112; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 167–168.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 106–112

=== Enlil encounters Sud

Enlil, king of the gods, travels to the city of Eresh, an ancient Sumerian city in the southern alluvial plain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 1–15; Black et al. 2004, p. 108.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 1–15

Enlil encounters the young goddess Sud standing in the street and is struck by her beauty; he propositions her directly.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 15–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 108–109.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 15–30

Sud rejects Enlil's initial advance; she is offended by his blunt approach and rebukes him, saying that she is not a woman who can be taken casually.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 30–45; Black et al. 2004, pp. 109–110.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 30–45

=== The formal courtship

Chastened by the rejection, Enlil undertakes a proper courtship: he sends his minister Nusku as envoy to Sud's mother, the goddess Nisaba, to negotiate a formal marriage.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 45–65; Black et al. 2004, pp. 110–111.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 45–65

[LACUNA: Portion of the bridal negotiation dialogue between Enlil's envoy and Nisaba (lines 55–62) partially damaged | source: etcsl 1.2.2 | scholarly_reconstruction: none — single manuscript tradition]

Nisaba, goddess of grain and writing, acts as the mother who negotiates the marriage terms — she is both Sud's protective parent and a literate, authoritative figure in her own right.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 55–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 111–112.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 55–80

=== Bridal gifts and bride-price

Enlil sends a lavish array of bridal gifts to Nisaba and to Sud's household, including jewellery, precious metals, fine garments, and food provisions.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 80–110; Black et al. 2004, pp. 112–113.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 80–110

The gift catalogue follows the pattern of Sumerian bride-price conventions attested in legal and administrative texts from the same period.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 106–112 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 106–112

[INFERENCE: The detailed gift-list functions as a literary model of the ideal bride-price, reflecting and prescribing the customs of the earthly _edubba_ audience | basis: The close correspondence between the narrative gift-list and attested legal bride-price formulae | risk: The text may be aspirational or mythologically exaggerated rather than a direct reflection of actual practice]

Nisaba accepts the gifts and agrees to the marriage on behalf of her daughter.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 110–125; Black et al. 2004, pp. 113–114.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 110–125

=== The wedding and renaming

The marriage ceremony takes place; Sud is conducted to Enlil's household with full ritual propriety.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 125–145; Black et al. 2004, pp. 114–115.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 125–145

Upon her marriage to Enlil, Sud is formally renamed Ninlil — "Lady of the Wind/Air" — taking the feminine counterpart of Enlil's name and becoming queen of the gods.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 140–160; Black et al. 2004, p. 115.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 140–160

The renaming is presented as a theological act, not merely a change of personal name: Sud acquires the divine status, prerogatives, and cosmic role of Ninlil through the act of sacred marriage.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 150–170; Black et al. 2004, p. 115.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 115

The composition closes with praises of Ninlil in her new exalted status, confirming her place as Enlil's consort and co-ruler of the divine realm.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 160–170; Black et al. 2004, p. 115.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 160–170

=== Contrast with Enlil and Ninlil (chapter 5)

The two Enlil marriage narratives present complementary models: "Enlil and Ninlil" (chapter 5) narrates a transgressive union followed by divine punishment and exile, while "Enlil and Sud" presents the same divine pair united through proper courtship, negotiation, and ritual.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 108–109 (introduction); Jacobsen 1987, pp. 167–168.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 108–109

[INFERENCE: The two compositions may have functioned as contrasting exempla in the scribal curriculum — one modelling transgression and its consequences, the other modelling correct social procedure | basis: Both were part of the OB Nippur curriculum and concern the same divine couple | risk: No ancient colophon or catalogue explicitly links them as a didactic pair]

=== Role of Nisaba

Nisaba's prominence in the negotiation reflects her dual nature as both a mother goddess and the goddess of writing and scholarship — the patron deity of the scribal craft.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 55–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 111–112.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, lines 55–80

=== Cultural function

The composition models the ideal divine marriage protocol that earthly ceremonies reflect, including bride-price, envoy negotiations, maternal consent, and a naming ceremony.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 106–112.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 106–112

The courtship protocol mirrors attested Sumerian marriage customs, making this text a primary source for reconstructing OB marriage ritual and gender norms in divine narrative.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, commentary; Black et al. 2004, pp. 106–112.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.2.2, commentary
