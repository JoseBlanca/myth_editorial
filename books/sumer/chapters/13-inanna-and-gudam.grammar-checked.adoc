== Inanna and Gudam

The composition known as "Inanna and Gudam" is catalogued as ETCSL 1.3.4 and survives on a small number of tablets; CBS 13859 is a complete witness.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4, bibliography.] The text is, however, very fragmentary overall, though CBS 13859 preserves a complete text.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4, composite text.] Very few manuscript copies survive, and all are heavily damaged, indicating either extremely limited circulation or catastrophic preservation failure; ETCSL reconstructs a composite from these minimal fragments.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 85 (brief summary reference).] Black et al. provide only a brief summary rather than a full translation, given the extreme fragmentary state.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, p. 85.] This is, in short, the shortest and most damaged text in the Inanna cycle. It is the only known version, reconstructed from very limited fragments — a single-prevalent-reconstructed text, in the classification used throughout this book.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4.] The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text.footnote:[ETCSL composite text.]

_[At this point the tablet breaks. Opening lines 1–20 heavily damaged; the origin and nature of Gudam are only partially described. Too few manuscripts survive to restore the opening.. none available..]_

What can be recovered from the fragments is this. A fearsome monster or creature called Gudam terrorises Uruk (Sumerian: Unug) — great walled city of southern Mesopotamia, seat of Inanna's temple the E-ana — devouring the city's food and drink.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.] Uruk is the city of Inanna — goddess of love, war, and the morning and evening star — and so the threat to the city is a threat to the goddess herself. The opening, which would have told us where Gudam came from and what kind of creature it was, is largely destroyed. We are left with a monster already rampaging and no backstory to explain it.
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4

_[At this point the tablet breaks. The central narrative is substantially broken; details of Gudam's rampage and the confrontation between the monster and Inanna are largely lost. No parallel witnesses exist to fill these gaps.. none available..]_

_(The surviving fragments indicate a narrative structure in which a patron deity (Inanna) defends her city (Uruk) against a monstrous threat (Gudam) — a basic pattern of divine urban protection well attested in Mesopotamian literature..footnote:[Inference: The fragments name Inanna, Gudam, and Uruk, and the resolution involves Inanna's victory.. Risk: The actual narrative may have been more complex than this basic pattern; the fragmentary state prevents certainty..])_

The middle of the story — which presumably contained the most dramatic material — is substantially broken. We can infer from what survives at either end that the basic narrative shape follows a pattern well attested in Mesopotamian literature: a patron deity defends her city against a monstrous threat. But the details of how Inanna confronted the monster are lost.

'''

What does survive is the resolution, and it contains a surprise. It is not Inanna herself who strikes the decisive blow. A junior fisherman of Inanna strikes Gudam down with a double-axe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85 (Inanna myths overview).] What follows is equally unexpected. Gudam, defeated, pleads with Inanna for mercy — and she grants it. Rather than destroying the creature, she sentences him to live near Zabalam, where he is to serve as a protector of her city Uruk.
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.3.4

This is a remarkable outcome by the standards of monster-slaying narrative anywhere. The monster is not killed but rehabilitated, conscripted into the service of the very city it had been devouring. The predator becomes a guard dog. Whether the original text developed this irony at greater length is impossible to say — the fragments do not permit it.

_[At this point the tablet breaks. The conclusion is fragmentary; the resolution and any hymnic closing are mostly lost.. none available..]_

'''

Only the broadest narrative shape — monster threatens city, goddess defeats monster — can be recovered with confidence from the surviving fragments.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Black et al. 2004, p. 85.] That shape, however, is worth noting. The pattern of a patron deity protecting a city by subduing a monstrous threat is one of the most widespread motifs in Near Eastern mythology, appearing in traditions from Marduk's defeat of Tiamat in Babylon onward.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.] The Greek tradition offers its own parallels — Athena and the various monsters of the Athenian mythological landscape follow a structurally similar pattern.
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 85
// EVIDENCE: source_id=kramer-1961 ; loc=pp. 83–85

What makes this particular version distinctive — to the extent that the fragments allow any judgement at all — is the mercy. In most versions of this pattern, the monster dies. Here, it lives, pardoned and put to work. Whether that detail reflects a theological principle, a narrative taste, or simply the idiosyncrasy of one Sumerian scribe is a question the tablets, in their present condition, cannot answer.
