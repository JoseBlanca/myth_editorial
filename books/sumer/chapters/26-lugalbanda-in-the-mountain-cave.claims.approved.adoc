== Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave — Claims

=== Source overview

The composition is catalogued as ETCSL 1.8.2.1 and survives on Old Babylonian tablets from Nippur and Ur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, bibliography.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, bibliography

Reconstructed from fragments; some segments remain unclear. Part of a linked pair with Lugalbanda and the Anzud Bird.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, composite text.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, composite text

The present retelling follows the ETCSL composite text, supplemented by Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–131; Black et al. 2004, pp. 262–268.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–131; Black et al. 2004, pp. 262–268.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, composite text

=== Setting and context

Lugalbanda is a soldier in the army of Enmerkar, king of Uruk, which is marching toward the distant city of Aratta across mountainous terrain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, opening lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 262–263.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, opening lines

[LACUNA: Opening lines (1–30) describing the cosmic opening and Enmerkar's military mobilization are partially damaged in Nippur manuscripts | source: etcsl 1.8.2.1, lines 1–30 | scholarly_reconstruction: Ur fragments preserve some of the opening but with divergent wording; partial reconstruction possible]

The army of Uruk is on a military campaign against Aratta, placing this composition within the larger Aratta cycle alongside the two Enmerkar poems.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 171–175

=== Lugalbanda's illness and abandonment

During the march through the mountains, Lugalbanda falls gravely ill and is unable to continue with the army.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 71–86; Black et al. 2004, pp. 263–264.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 71–86

His comrades, unable to carry him and unwilling to delay the campaign, leave Lugalbanda in a mountain cave with provisions of food, drink, and bedding, expecting that he will die.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 87–140.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 87–140

The text describes Lugalbanda's isolation and suffering in the cave with vivid detail: he lies alone in the wilderness, cut off from his companions and the civilization of Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 140–150.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 140–150

=== Lugalbanda's piety and offerings

Despite his illness and isolation, Lugalbanda prays to the celestial deities — Utu (the sun god), Inanna, and Nanna-Suen (the moon god) — asking for their aid.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 148–227; Black et al. 2004, pp. 264–265.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 148–227

[INFERENCE: Lugalbanda's piety is correct and meticulous even in a wilderness setting — the text emphasizes that proper devotion is rewarded even outside temple or city. | basis: The three deities each accept his prayers and grant healing. | risk: The text presents the prayers and divine responses without explicit commentary on ritual correctness.]

=== Divine healing

Utu, the sun god, responds first to Lugalbanda's prayers and sends down divine encouragement; Inanna accepts his tears next; and Nanna-Suen accepts his tears and grants him life.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 171–227; Black et al. 2004, pp. 265–266.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 171–227

[VARIANT: primary=ETCSL 1.8.2.1, composite text based on Nippur manuscripts | alt=Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 105–115, notes divergences between Nippur and Ur fragments in the healing sequence | chosen: A | reason: ETCSL composite provides the most complete available reconstruction]

The divine healing restores Lugalbanda to full health; he emerges from the mountain cave, consumes life-saving plants and water, and speeds away "like a horse of the mountains."footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 264–275; Black et al. 2004, pp. 266–267.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 264–275

=== Lugalbanda alone in the wilderness

After his recovery, Lugalbanda finds himself alone in the mountains, separated from the army which has continued its march toward Aratta; he bakes bread and forages in the wilderness.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 264–299.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 264–299

Lugalbanda catches a wild bull and goats in the wilderness, demonstrating his resourcefulness as a survivor.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 300–325; Black et al. 2004, pp. 267–268.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 300–325

Lugalbanda receives a dream vision from the god Zangara, who instructs him to sacrifice the animals he has caught; he performs the sacrifices and makes a banquet invoking An, Enlil, Enki, and Ninhursaja.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 326–393.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 326–393

The composition continues with increasingly fragmentary content involving demonic beings and divine incantations (lines 394–499), followed by a fragmentary Segment B with an unknown number of lines missing; the narrative sets up the continuation in Lugalbanda and the Anzud Bird.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 394–499 and Segment B.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 394–499

=== Themes

The central theme is individual endurance in isolation: Lugalbanda's survival depends entirely on personal piety and divine favor, not on military prowess or social standing.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105 (introduction).]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=vanstiphout-2003 ; loc=pp. 171–175

The divine healing sequence demonstrates that correct ritual behavior — sincere prayer — is rewarded even when performed outside temple or city, in the raw wilderness.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 148–275.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, lines 148–275

=== Textual transmission

The text is reconstructed from fragments found at both Nippur and Ur; the ETCSL composite represents the best available scholarly reconstruction, though gaps remain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, manuscript list; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, manuscript list

The composition's presence in both Nippur and Ur scribal schools indicates it circulated as part of the _edubba_ curriculum in the linked Lugalbanda pair.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, manuscript list.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=1.8.2.1, manuscript list

Vanstiphout's critical edition (pp. 97–131) provides thorough philological treatment, documenting divergences between the Nippur and Ur textual traditions.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–131.]
