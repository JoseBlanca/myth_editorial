=== Setting and context

The composition "The Debate between Copper and Silver" is catalogued as ETCSL 5.3.6 and survives in a fragmentary state, with fewer copies than other debate poems, indicating more limited circulation in Old Babylonian scribal schools.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, composite text, fragmentary; Black et al. 2004, pp. 241–243.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.6, composite text, fragmentary

The text belongs to the Sumerian debate genre and features two personified metals — Copper (Sumerian _urudu_), who argues on the basis of practical utility, and Silver (Sumerian _ku-babbar_), who argues on the basis of prestige, beauty, and exchange value.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 241–242; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 241–242

=== Narrative sequence: the debate

Copper argues that it is the more valuable metal because of its indispensable role in the manufacture of weapons, tools, and agricultural implements — the practical infrastructure of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 241–242.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.6, surviving lines

Copper claims that without it, fields could not be ploughed, canals could not be dug, wars could not be fought, and the essential work of society would cease.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, p. 242.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 242

Silver counters that it is the metal of kings and temples, prized for its beauty, rarity, and function as the standard medium of exchange in trade and temple economies.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 242–243.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.6, surviving lines

Silver argues that prestige, divine favour, and economic power outweigh mere utility — that the metal used for adornment, royal treasure, and commerce holds a higher place in the order of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, p. 242.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=p. 242

=== Cultural function

Despite its fragmentary state, the surviving portions reveal a composition that explores the tension between practical utility and prestige value — a question central to Mesopotamian economic life, where silver served as the standard of exchange but copper was the workhorse metal of daily existence.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 241–243.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 241–243

The composition receives less scholarly treatment than the major debates (Hoe and Plough, Ewe and Grain, Winter and Summer) but is noted for its distinctive economic theme.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 241–243; Kramer 1961, pp. 72–73.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=black-2004 ; loc=pp. 241–243

=== Attestation and transmission

Fewer copies survive than for other debate poems, suggesting more limited circulation in the _edubba_ curriculum or, alternatively, poorer archaeological recovery.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, manuscript list; Black et al. 2004, p. 241.]
// EVIDENCE: source_id=etcsl ; loc=5.3.6, manuscript list

The ETCSL composite represents the only available reconstruction from these limited fragmentary witnesses, and Black et al. 2004 covers only the preserved portions without additional manuscript evidence.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6; Black et al. 2004, pp. 241–243.]
