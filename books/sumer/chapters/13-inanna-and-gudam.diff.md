# Line-edit diff: 13-inanna-and-gudam

Each entry shows the original sentence (or clause) and the edited version. Footnotes, resolved-marker passages, EVIDENCE/COMPARATIVE-HOOK comments, and AsciiDoc structure are unchanged.

## Header paragraph (intro to the composition)

- **Original:** "...survives on a small number of tablets..."
- **Edited:** "...survives on only a handful of tablets..."
- *Reason:* "A handful of" is more concrete and idiomatic than the bureaucratic "a small number of."

- **Original:** "The text is, however, very fragmentary overall, though CBS 13859 preserves a complete text."
- **Edited:** "The corpus as a whole is very fragmentary, even though CBS 13859 preserves a complete text."
- *Reason:* The original is internally awkward — "the text" is ambiguous against the just-named CBS 13859 ("text"). "The corpus as a whole" makes the contrast precise (the corpus is fragmentary; this one witness is complete). Drops the throat-clearing "however." "Even though" is a cleaner concessive than the slightly weaker "though" after a comma.

- **Original:** "Very few manuscript copies survive, and all are heavily damaged, indicating either extremely limited circulation or catastrophic preservation failure..."
- **Edited:** "Few manuscript copies survive, and all are heavily damaged — pointing to either extremely limited circulation or catastrophic preservation failure..."
- *Reason:* "Few" already means "a small number"; "very few" piles on. "Pointing to" replaces the zombie nominalization "indicating" with a more concrete verb. The em dash sharpens the inference relationship.

- **Original:** "Black et al. provide only a brief summary rather than a full translation..."
- **Edited:** "Black et al. offer only a brief summary rather than a full translation..."
- *Reason:* "Offer" is plainer and more natural with "summary" than "provide" (which has bureaucratic overtones).

- **Original:** "This is, in short, the shortest and most damaged text in the Inanna cycle."
- **Edited:** "This is the shortest and most damaged text in the Inanna cycle."
- *Reason:* "In short" is throat-clearing; the sentence is already a summary statement. Pinker's classic-style razor.

## "What can be recovered" paragraph

- **Original:** "A fearsome monster or creature called Gudam terrorises Uruk..."
- **Edited:** "A fearsome creature called Gudam terrorises Uruk..."
- *Reason:* "Monster or creature" is a hedge that says nothing — "creature" already covers the uncertainty (later sentences call it a monster anyway). Tightens subject-verb distance.

- **Original:** "Uruk (Sumerian: Unug) — great walled city of southern Mesopotamia, seat of Inanna's temple the E-ana — devouring the city's food and drink."
- **Edited:** "Uruk (Sumerian: Unug) — the great walled city of southern Mesopotamia, seat of Inanna's temple the E-ana — devouring the city's food and drink."
- *Reason:* Adds the article "the" before "great walled city" — the appositive reads more naturally in English with the determiner.

- **Original:** "Uruk is the city of Inanna — goddess of love, war, and the morning and evening star — and so the threat to the city is a threat to the goddess herself."
- **Edited:** "Uruk is the city of Inanna — goddess of love, war, and the morning and evening star — so a threat to the city is a threat to the goddess herself."
- *Reason:* Drops "and" before "so" (redundant connective). Changes "the threat" to "a threat" — generalises the implication, which is what the sentence is doing.

- **Original:** "We are left with a monster already rampaging and no backstory to explain it."
- **Edited:** "We are left with a monster already on the rampage and no backstory to explain it."
- *Reason:* "On the rampage" is the standard English idiom and reads more vividly than the bare participle "rampaging."

## Middle-of-the-story paragraph

- **Original:** "The middle of the story — which presumably contained the most dramatic material — is substantially broken."
- **Edited:** "The middle of the story — which presumably held the most dramatic material — is substantially broken."
- *Reason:* "Held" is more concrete and active than "contained" (a faint zombie verb).

- **Original:** "We can infer from what survives at either end that the basic narrative shape follows a pattern well attested in Mesopotamian literature..."
- **Edited:** "From what survives at either end we can infer that the basic narrative shape follows a pattern well attested in Mesopotamian literature..."
- *Reason:* Fronts the prepositional phrase to shorten subject-verb distance ("we can infer that...") and to put the evidence first, the inference second — clearer reasoning order.

- **Original:** "But the details of how Inanna confronted the monster are lost."
- **Edited:** "But how Inanna confronted the monster is lost."
- *Reason:* Cuts the zombie noun "the details of"; the embedded question carries the same meaning more directly.

## Resolution paragraph

- **Original:** "What does survive is the resolution, and it contains a surprise."
- **Edited:** "What does survive is the resolution, and it springs a surprise."
- *Reason:* "Springs a surprise" is the active English idiom; "contains a surprise" is the bureaucratic phrasing.

- **Original:** "It is not Inanna herself who strikes the decisive blow."
- **Edited:** "Inanna is not the one who strikes the decisive blow."
- *Reason:* Removes the it-cleft, which adds throat-clearing without changing meaning. Direct subject is shorter and clearer.

- **Original:** "A junior fisherman of Inanna strikes Gudam down with a double-axe."
- **Edited:** "A junior fisherman of hers cuts Gudam down with a double-axe."
- *Reason:* Avoids re-naming Inanna two sentences after she was named; "of hers" flows naturally. "Cuts down" is a slightly more vivid verb pairing for "double-axe" than the abstract "strikes down" (and "strikes" was just used in the previous sentence — repetition broken).

- **Original:** "What follows is equally unexpected."
- **Edited:** "What follows is just as unexpected."
- *Reason:* "Just as" is the natural conversational comparative; "equally" is the slightly stiffer formal alternative. Same meaning.

- **Original:** "Rather than destroying the creature, she sentences him to live near Zabalam..."
- **Edited:** "Rather than destroy the creature, she sentences him to live near Zabalam..."
- *Reason:* After "rather than," the bare infinitive ("destroy") parallels the finite verb "sentences" better than the gerund ("destroying"). Parallel structure.

## "Remarkable outcome" paragraph

- **Original:** "This is a remarkable outcome by the standards of monster-slaying narrative anywhere."
- **Edited:** "By the standards of monster-slaying narrative anywhere, this is a remarkable outcome."
- *Reason:* Fronts the comparative frame so the standard is established before the judgement — the paragraph then unpacks why the outcome is remarkable. Better paragraph-opening transition from the previous paragraph (which ended with the surprising sentence of Gudam).

- **Original:** "...conscripted into the service of the very city it had been devouring."
- **Edited:** "...conscripted into the service of the very city he had been devouring."
- *Reason:* Pronoun consistency — the previous sentence and the next one ("she sentences him," "the predator becomes a guard dog") personify Gudam with masculine pronouns. The grammar-checked version slipped into "it" here only. (No new factual claim — the gender is already established by the surrounding text and by the original ETCSL composite.)

- **Original:** "Whether the original text developed this irony at greater length is impossible to say — the fragments do not permit it."
- **Edited:** "Whether the original text drew out this irony at greater length is impossible to say — the fragments do not allow it."
- *Reason:* "Drew out" is more idiomatic and concrete than "developed" for an irony or theme. "Allow" is plainer than "permit" (Pinker's preference for short Anglo-Saxon verbs over Latinate ones in the same register).

## Final-shape paragraph

- **Original:** "The pattern of a patron deity protecting a city by subduing a monstrous threat is one of the most widespread motifs in Near Eastern mythology, appearing in traditions from Marduk's defeat of Tiamat in Babylon onward."
- **Edited:** "A patron deity protecting a city by subduing a monstrous threat is one of the most widespread motifs in Near Eastern mythology, running through traditions from Marduk's defeat of Tiamat in Babylon onward."
- *Reason:* Cuts the zombie noun "the pattern of" — the gerund clause is itself the pattern, no need to label it. "Running through" is more concrete and continuous than the flat "appearing in," and matches the temporal sweep ("from Marduk... onward").

## Mercy paragraph

- **Original:** "Here, it lives, pardoned and put to work."
- **Edited:** "Here he lives, pardoned and put to work."
- *Reason:* Pronoun consistency with the personified Gudam ("she sentences him," "the predator becomes a guard dog"). Drops the comma after "Here" — short adverb, no pause needed.
