I want to create a comprehensive catalog of stories from the **Sumerian literary tradition**. By "Sumerian literary tradition" I mean compositions originally written in Sumerian, belonging to the cultural tradition of southern Mesopotamian city-states (Eridu, Ur, Uruk, Nippur, Lagash, etc.), from the earliest attestations through the Old Babylonian scribal tradition (c. 2000–1600 BCE), which is where most Sumerian literary texts survive.

**What to include:**

- Mythological narratives (cosmogony, theogony, divine exploits)
- Heroic narratives (Gilgamesh Sumerian poems, Lugalbanda, Enmerkar, etc.)
- Descent/journey narratives
- Debate poems and other literary compositions with mythological content
- Eschatological or underworld narratives

**What to exclude — contamination risks:**

- **Akkadian/Babylonian compositions**: exclude the Standard Babylonian Gilgamesh epic (Sha naqba īmuru), the Enūma Eliš, the Atra-Ḫasīs — these are Akkadian-language works, even when they draw on Sumerian precursors. Include the *Sumerian* Gilgamesh poems (Gilgamesh and Huwawa, Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven, etc.) which are separate compositions.
- **Elamite, Hurrian, and Hittite** literary traditions, even when they retell Sumerian-origin stories.
- **Neo-Assyrian and later** recensions of stories unless the Sumerian original is independently attested.

**For each story, provide:**

1. A working English title and the native Sumerian title/incipit if known.
2. At least one **verifiable identifier**: an ETCSL composition number (e.g., ETCSL 1.4.1), a CDLI P-number, a museum tablet number (e.g., CBS 14061, BM 92687), a DOI, or an ISBN for the critical edition. Generic URLs or Wikipedia links are not sufficient as identifiers.
3. A two-sentence summary of the narrative.
4. The primary source(s) and their state of preservation (complete, fragmentary, heavily damaged).
5. Whether multiple versions or variants exist within the Sumerian tradition, and if so, how they differ.
6. A note on the date range of the surviving manuscripts.

**If you are uncertain** whether a story belongs to the Sumerian tradition vs. an adjacent culture, **include it but flag the uncertainty** with an explanation — do not silently omit it.

**Organize** the catalog by cycle or thematic group: cosmogonic myths, Enlil cycle, Enki cycle, Inanna cycle, heroic narratives (Gilgamesh poems, Lugalbanda, Enmerkar), underworld narratives, debate poems, and miscellaneous.

**Key scholarly references** to ground your search (prioritize these):

- ETCSL (Electronic Text Corpus of Sumerian Literature, Oxford)
- CDLI (Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative)
- Black, Cunningham, Robson & Zólyomi, *The Literature of Ancient Sumer* (2004)
- Kramer, *Sumerian Mythology* (1961) — useful but dated; cross-check with ETCSL
- Jacobsen, *The Harps That Once...* (1987)
- George, *The Babylonian Gilgamesh Epic* (2003) — for distinguishing Sumerian vs. Akkadian Gilgamesh

Aim for comprehensiveness within the Sumerian tradition. A story covered in Black et al. 2004 or listed in ETCSL belongs in the catalog. A story mentioned in one footnote of one monograph probably does not.
