[appendix]
== Characters

A reference guide to the named characters who appear in this book. Physical descriptions are included only where attested in the in-scope sources; where the sources are silent, this is noted explicitly. The inclusion rule is as follows: named divine and mortal figures who act or are acted upon in a chapter receive an entry; generic unnamed creatures (such as the serpent in the huluppu tree), collective groups treated as a body (such as the Anunnaki), and passing named places or artefacts are omitted.

=== Deities

[[char-an]]
==== An

Sky god, father of the gods, highest figure in the traditional Sumerian hierarchy, whose authority stands behind Enlil's executive rule.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 1–60; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–217.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-07-lugal-e, _Ninurta's Exploits_>> — named as the sky-father whose parentage (with Ki) engendered the demon Asag.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 30–70; Black et al. 2004, pp. 165–167.]
* <<ch-08-angim, _Ninurta's Return to Nibru_>> — one of the great gods alarmed by Ninurta's unchecked _me-lam_ on the warrior's return.
* <<ch-11-inanna-and-ebih, _Inanna and Ebih_>> — An warns Inanna against attacking Mount Ebih and is defied.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2, lines approx. 50–75; Black et al. 2004, pp. 73–74.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — in the cosmogonic prologue, An carries off heaven at the primordial separation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, pp. 286–287.]
* <<ch-26-lugalbanda-in-the-mountain-cave, _Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave_>> — invoked by Lugalbanda among the four supreme deities at his wilderness sacrifice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 326–393.]
* <<ch-28-eridu-flood-story, _The Flood Story: The Eridu Genesis_>> — with Enlil, grants Ziusudra eternal life and settles him in Dilmun after the deluge.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column vi; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 149–150.]

*Relevance.* An is the remote sovereign of the Sumerian pantheon, senior to Enlil in genealogy but less active in narrative.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–217.] The compositions repeatedly treat him as the ultimate source of sanction whose delegated authority Enki and Enlil actually exercise; his direct interventions are rare and, as in _Inanna and Ebih_, can be defied without immediate cosmic rupture.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 50–80.]

[[char-ashnan]]
==== Ashnan

Goddess of grain and cereal agriculture, one of the two divine personifications whose creation in the _dul-kug_ (the "holy mound," the creation chamber of the gods) transformed humanity from animal existence to civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 20–40; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 155–160.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — assigned her domain over grain when Enki organises the crafts of civilisation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 360–375; Black et al. 2004, pp. 222–223.]
* <<ch-32-debate-ewe-and-grain, _The Debate between Ewe and Grain_>> — argues her superiority over Lahar (Ewe) at a drunken feast and is declared the winner by Enki.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 180–192; Black et al. 2004, p. 225.]

*Relevance.* Ashnan embodies the theological grounding of cereal agriculture as the economic backbone of Sumerian civilisation.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 219–220.] Her victory in the debate ratifies what the temple ration system and the administrative archives already recorded: grain, not wool, was the measure by which the Mesopotamian economy was counted.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 30–40.]

[[char-dumuzid]]
==== Dumuzid

The shepherd-god, consort of Inanna, whose seasonal death and partial return underlie the central dying-god tradition of Sumerian literature.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 340–355; Black et al. 2004, p. 60.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The texts describe him by his regalia and setting rather than his person: _Inanna's Descent_ notes that on Inanna's return he "sat on a magnificent throne, dressed in splendid garments," but these are his circumstances, not his body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 340–355; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 219–221.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — appointed by Enki as the divine overseer of the sheepfold and cattle-pen.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 395–415; Black et al. 2004, pp. 223–224.]
* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — designated by Inanna as her underworld substitute when she finds him enthroned and un-mourning on her return.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 348–356; Black et al. 2004, p. 60.]
* <<ch-15-dumuzids-dream, _Dumuzid's Dream_>> — dreams of his capture, flees the _galla_ demons across the steppe in successive animal transformations, and is betrayed and seized.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 1–260; Alster 1972, pp. 40–80.]
* <<ch-16-inanna-and-bilulu, _Inanna and Bilulu_>> — the slain shepherd for whom Inanna mourns and takes vengeance on Bilulu and Girgire.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–325.]

*Relevance.* Dumuzid is the central figure of the dying-god cycle, the Sumerian literary engine of seasonal theology and ritual lament.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 205–207; Sladek 1974.] His wailing cult is directly attested in Ur III and Old Babylonian ritual practice, and his alternation with Geshtinanna in the underworld grounds the pastoral and agricultural calendar in narrative.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 400–415; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 225–228.]

[[char-emesh]]
==== Emesh

Divine personification of the dry summer season in the Sumerian agricultural calendar, created by Enlil to manage the ripening of crops and the harvest.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 20–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–227.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-33-debate-winter-and-summer, _The Debate between Winter and Summer_>> — disputes with Enten over seasonal primacy and accepts Enten's victory after Enlil's verdict.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 60–310; Black et al. 2004, pp. 228–233.]

*Relevance.* Emesh personifies the heat and harvest half of the Mesopotamian agricultural year.footnote:[Civil 1994, passim.] The composition's verdict against him reflects an ecological fact of southern Mesopotamia, where rain and flood-water — Enten's domain — underpin every agricultural activity that follows.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 280–310.]

[[char-enbilulu]]
==== Enbilulu

Chthonic deity and canal inspector, one of three underworld gods born in Enlil's descent and, in a separate composition, the divine "canal-controller" of the Tigris.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.1, lines 90–130; ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 250–270.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — appointed by Enki as canal-controller of the Tigris.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 250–270; Black et al. 2004, pp. 220–221.]
* <<ch-05-enlil-and-ninlil, _Enlil and Ninlil_>> — one of the three chthonic deities engendered during Enlil's descent as underworld substitutes allowing Nanna-Suen to ascend.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.1, lines 90–130; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 174–178.]

*Relevance.* Enbilulu exemplifies the Sumerian taste for administrative precision even in theology: he is a specialist whose portfolio is the maintenance of watercourses, above and below.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 250–270.]

[[char-enki]]
==== Enki

God of wisdom, fresh water, and cunning, lord of the _abzu_ beneath Eridu, the organising administrator of the cosmos and the most narratively productive deity in the pantheon.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 1–60; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–217.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-01-enki-and-ninhursaja, _Enki and Ninhursaja_>> — fathers a chain of goddesses in Dilmun, consumes eight forbidden plants, is cursed by Ninhursaja, and is healed through the birth of eight deities.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 1–278; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 181–204.]
* <<ch-02-enki-and-ninmah, _Enki and Ninmah_>> — devises the clay-creation of humanity and wins the drunken contest with Ninmah by creating the helpless Umul.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.2, lines 15–130; Kramer 1961, pp. 68–72.]
* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — tours the cosmos assigning lands, rivers, crafts, and offices; answers Inanna's complaint by acknowledging her as the destabiliser of categories.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 60–473; Black et al. 2004, pp. 215–225.]
* <<ch-04-enkis-journey-to-nibru, _Enki's Journey to Nibru_>> — builds his temple the E-engura and sails by barge to Nippur to receive Enlil's blessing.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.4; Black et al. 2004, pp. 330–333.]
* <<ch-09-ninurta-and-the-turtle, _Ninurta and the Turtle_>> — humiliates the overreaching warrior god by digging a pit and setting a turtle to drag him into it.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]
* <<ch-10-inanna-and-enki, _Inanna and Enki_>> — gives the _me_ to Inanna during a drunken feast and then fails to recover them through successive sea monsters.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–70.]
* <<ch-12-inanna-and-shu-kale-tuda, _Inanna and Shu-kale-tuda_>> — counsels Shu-kale-tuda to hide among the cities but cannot ultimately protect him from Inanna's justice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, p. 81.]
* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — creates the sexless _kurgarra_ and _galatur_ from the dirt of his fingernails to revive the dead Inanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 220–245; Black et al. 2004, p. 58.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — sails toward the _kur_ in the cosmogonic prologue and later instructs Utu to open the hole through which Enkidu's ghost rises.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 10–20, 230–245.]
* <<ch-24-enmerkar-and-the-lord-of-aratta, _Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta_>> — the Incantation of Nudimmud, named for him, concerns the unity or confusion of tongues.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.3, lines 136–155; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 22–26.]
* <<ch-26-lugalbanda-in-the-mountain-cave, _Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave_>> — invoked among the four supreme deities at Lugalbanda's wilderness sacrifice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 326–393.]
* <<ch-28-eridu-flood-story, _The Flood Story: The Eridu Genesis_>> — warns the pious king Ziusudra of the coming flood by speaking through a reed wall.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iii–iv; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 147–148.]
* <<ch-32-debate-ewe-and-grain, _The Debate between Ewe and Grain_>> — renders the verdict for Grain over Ewe.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 180–192; Black et al. 2004, p. 225.]
* <<ch-34-debate-bird-and-fish, _The Debate between Bird and Fish_>> — the marshland habitats of the disputants are assigned by him; the closing doxology praises "Father Enki" whose wisdom guided Shulgi's judgment.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 1–30, 185–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–240.]

*Relevance.* Enki is the pantheon's universal problem-solver, the figure whose cunning supplies solutions when direct force fails and whose administrative ordering underwrites the organised cosmos.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 59–62; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 110–130.] More compositions centre on him than on any other deity, and the range of his interventions — cosmogonic, creational, hydraulic, theological, and comic — makes him the most versatile character in the Sumerian literary corpus.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 31–32.]

[[char-enkimdu]]
==== Enkimdu

Farmer-god whom Enki appoints to oversee agriculture and the plough.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 340–360; Black et al. 2004, p. 222.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — assigned the plough when Enki orders the crafts.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 340–360.]

*Relevance.* A minor administrative deity of the divine "org chart," Enkimdu personifies the institutional subdivision of agricultural labour under Enki's organising authority.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 340–400.]

[[char-enlil]]
==== Enlil

King of the gods, lord of wind and command, supreme authority in Nippur, whose word is decisive in the divine assembly and on whose blessing the cities of Sumer stand or fall.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.1, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, p. 100.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The epithet "Great Mountain" (_kur-gal_) is a title of office, not a literal image.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.1, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, p. 100.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-01-enki-and-ninhursaja, _Enki and Ninhursaja_>> — unable to cure the dying Enki; sits helpless in the dust among the great gods.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.1, lines 220–227.]
* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — delegates cosmic organisation to Enki.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 50–80; Kramer 1961, pp. 59–60.]
* <<ch-04-enkis-journey-to-nibru, _Enki's Journey to Nibru_>> — receives Enki's barge at Nippur and blesses the E-engura and its cult.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.4, closing section; Kramer 1961, pp. 63–64.]
* <<ch-05-enlil-and-ninlil, _Enlil and Ninlil_>> — transgresses against Ninlil, is judged impure and exiled by the divine assembly, and engenders Nanna-Suen and three chthonic deities in the course of his descent.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.1, lines 15–130; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 167–180.]
* <<ch-06-enlil-and-sud, _Enlil and Sud_>> — courts Sud with proper procedure through his vizier Nusku and her mother Nisaba; Sud is renamed Ninlil on their marriage.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 15–170; Black et al. 2004, pp. 108–115.]
* <<ch-07-lugal-e, _Ninurta's Exploits_>> — dispatches encouragement to Ninurta through Sharur during the Asag campaign.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 160–180; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 243–245.]
* <<ch-08-angim, _Ninurta's Return to Nibru_>> — uses his minister Nusku to channel the warrior's returning fury into a ceremonial homecoming.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.1, lines 100–140; Black et al. 2004, pp. 195–196.]
* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — refuses to help the dead Inanna, judging her ambition to be her own.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 173–200; Black et al. 2004, p. 57.]
* <<ch-17-nannas-journey-to-nibru, _Nanna-Suen's Journey to Nibru_>> — receives his son's barge of offerings at the E-kur and blesses Ur.footnote:[ETCSL 1.5.1, lines 280–330.]
* <<ch-21-gilgamesh-and-huwawa, _Gilgamesh and Huwawa_>> — appointed Huwawa to the Cedar Forest; when the guardian is killed he redistributes the seven _me-lam_ into the natural world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 190–202; Black et al. 2004, pp. 276–278.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — carries off earth at the primordial separation; refuses to help Gilgamesh recover Enkidu from the underworld.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–10, 220–230.]
* <<ch-26-lugalbanda-in-the-mountain-cave, _Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave_>> — invoked in Lugalbanda's wilderness sacrifice.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 326–393.]
* <<ch-28-eridu-flood-story, _The Flood Story: The Eridu Genesis_>> — plays a role in the gods' decision to send the flood; with An, grants Ziusudra eternal life in Dilmun.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, columns iii, vi; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 146–150.]
* <<ch-31-debate-hoe-and-plough, _The Debate between Hoe and Plough_>> — creates both implements and awards the verdict to Hoe.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 1–20, 170–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210, 217–218.]
* <<ch-32-debate-ewe-and-grain, _The Debate between Ewe and Grain_>> — collaborates with Enki in creating Ewe and Grain to end humanity's animal condition.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.2, lines 20–40; Black et al. 2004, p. 220.]
* <<ch-33-debate-winter-and-summer, _The Debate between Winter and Summer_>> — creates the seasons and judges in favour of Enten.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–30, 280–310; Black et al. 2004, pp. 226–233.]
* <<ch-35-debate-copper-and-silver, _The Debate between Copper and Silver_>> — renders the verdict for Copper over Silver.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segment I.]

*Relevance.* Enlil is the executive head of the Sumerian pantheon, the deity whose sanction transforms local cultic claims into cosmic authority.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 100–101; Kramer 1961, pp. 43–49.] He is judge, king, father, and — in _Enlil and Ninlil_ — a transgressor held to account by his own assembly, a combination that gives the Enlil compositions their characteristic balance of hierarchy and accountability.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.1, lines 50–65; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 170–172.]

[[char-enten]]
==== Enten

Divine personification of the wet winter season in the Sumerian agricultural calendar, created by Enlil to manage rains, river floods, and the replenishment of irrigation canals.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–20; Black et al. 2004, p. 226.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-33-debate-winter-and-summer, _The Debate between Winter and Summer_>> — defeats Emesh in debate and is declared the winner by Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 60–310; Black et al. 2004, pp. 228–233.]

*Relevance.* Enten embodies the water half of the Mesopotamian agricultural year — the season that makes every subsequent activity possible in the alluvial floodplain where rain falls only in winter.footnote:[Civil 1994, passim; ETCSL 5.3.3, lines 1–30.]

[[char-ereshkigal]]
==== Ereshkigal

Queen of the underworld, elder sister of Inanna, sovereign of the _kur_ from the moment of its separation from heaven and earth.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–10; ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 1–13.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. _Inanna's Descent_ reports her in distress — groaning as if in labour or in mourning — but this is a behaviour, not a body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 245–260; Black et al. 2004, p. 58.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — receives and kills the descending Inanna, and is manipulated by the _kurgarra_ and _galatur_ into releasing the corpse.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 90–280; Black et al. 2004, pp. 55–59.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — named in the cosmogonic prologue as the queen to whom the _kur_ was given as her prize.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 1–10.]
* <<ch-23-death-of-gilgamesh, _The Death of Gilgamesh_>> — receives offerings from the dead Gilgamesh at his reception in the nether world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 200–206.]
* <<ch-30-death-of-ur-namma, _The Death of Ur-Namma_>> — receives the dead king's tribute at his entry into the underworld.footnote:[ETCSL 2.4.1.1, lines 140–170; Flückiger-Hawker 1999.]

*Relevance.* Ereshkigal is the immovable administrator of death, the deity whose "powers are perfect" and whose rites cannot be questioned.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, gate sequence; Sladek 1974.] Her sovereignty is coeval with the cosmos itself, and her enforcement of the law of substitution — no one leaves the nether world without a replacement — is the hinge on which the entire Inanna-Dumuzid cycle turns.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 212–213.]

[[char-galatur]]
==== Galatur

One of two small, sexless creatures fashioned by Enki from the dirt under his fingernails to revive Inanna in the nether world; its companion is the _kurgarra_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 220–235; Black et al. 2004, p. 58; Sladek 1974.]

*Physical description.* Small and sexless; beyond this explicit textual statement, no further physical description survives in the in-scope sources.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 220–245; Sladek 1974.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — slips unnoticed into the _kur_, mirrors Ereshkigal's suffering, receives the corpse as a gift, and sprinkles the "water of life" to revive Inanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 235–280; Black et al. 2004, pp. 58–59.]

*Relevance.* Together with the _kurgarra_, the _galatur_ represents Enki's characteristic method of engineering around cosmic law rather than breaking it: a category-defying being can pass where gods cannot, and empathy exploited can deliver what force never could.footnote:[Sladek 1974; Black et al. 2004, pp. 52–53.] Some scholars connect the pair to Mesopotamian traditions of liminal cultic personnel.footnote:[Sladek 1974; Black et al. 2004, pp. 52–53.]

[[char-geshtinanna]]
==== Geshtinanna

Goddess, sister of Dumuzid, the sibling whose loyalty stands as the ethical counterweight to her brother's complacency.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 380–400; Black et al. 2004, pp. 61–62.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — offers herself as Dumuzid's underworld substitute; Inanna decrees that the two siblings alternate half-years in the _kur_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 380–415; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 225–228.]
* <<ch-15-dumuzids-dream, _Dumuzid's Dream_>> — interprets her brother's dream as foretelling his capture and later hides him in her sheepfold, refusing to betray him to the _galla_ under interrogation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 20–55, 65–70; Alster 1972, pp. 50–75.]

*Relevance.* Geshtinanna is the figure through whom the Sumerian dying-god tradition finds its element of redemption: the substitution that saves Dumuzid half the year is her doing, not Inanna's, and her willingness to descend for her brother is the text's clearest statement that grief is the measure of loyalty.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 380–415; Alster 1972, pp. 65–75.]

[[char-inanna]]
==== Inanna

Goddess of love, war, and the morning and evening star; patron of Uruk and the figure whose independent action generates more narrative material than any other deity in the Sumerian corpus.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–64; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–96.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The texts repeatedly describe her regalia and adornments — the _shugurra_ crown of the steppe, the measuring rod and line of lapis lazuli, beaded jewellery, the breastplate called "Come, man, come," the _pala_-garment of ladyship — but these are accoutrements, not a body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 14–25; Black et al. 2004, pp. 53–54; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 208–209.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-03-enki-and-the-world-order, _Enki and the World Order_>> — complains at the close of the composition that she has been given no clear portfolio; Enki reframes her domain as the destabilisation of categories.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 430–473; Black et al. 2004, pp. 224–225.]
* <<ch-10-inanna-and-enki, _Inanna and Enki_>> — travels to Eridu, receives the _me_ from the drunken Enki, and sails home to Uruk on the Boat of Heaven despite Enki's attempts to retrieve them.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 63–70.]
* <<ch-11-inanna-and-ebih, _Inanna and Ebih_>> — defies An's warning and destroys the defiant Mount Ebih with fire and flood.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.2; Black et al. 2004, pp. 71–77.]
* <<ch-12-inanna-and-shu-kale-tuda, _Inanna and Shu-kale-tuda_>> — raped while sleeping by the gardener Shu-kale-tuda; unleashes three plagues on the land until she finds and condemns him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 78–84.]
* <<ch-13-inanna-and-gudam, _Inanna and Gudam_>> — defeats the monstrous Gudam who terrorises Uruk and, unusually, spares and resettles him as a protector.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]
* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — descends to the underworld, is stripped through seven gates, killed and hung on a hook, revived through Enki's intervention, and designates Dumuzid as her substitute.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 1–415; Sladek 1974.]
* <<ch-16-inanna-and-bilulu, _Inanna and Bilulu_>> — mourns the slain Dumuzid on the steppe and takes vengeance on Bilulu and Girgire, transforming them into ritual objects.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 315–325.]
* <<ch-20-gilgamesh-and-the-bull-of-heaven, _Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven_>> — sends the Bull of Heaven against Uruk after a quarrel with Gilgamesh; flees when Enkidu strikes her with a haunch of the slain beast.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment D, lines 33–53; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — finds the huluppu tree on the Euphrates and appeals to Gilgamesh to clear it of its infesting creatures.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 20–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 287–289.]

[[char-ninurta]]
==== Ninurta

Warrior god, son of Enlil and Ninmah (later renamed Ninhursaja), champion of the gods in the Sumerian Chaoskampf tradition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–165.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. _Angim_ describes his _me-lam_ — the terrifying divine radiance that emanates from gods, monsters, and sacred objects — as blazing with such intensity that it alarms all who behold it, but this is an attribute of his presence, not of his body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.1, lines 25–50; Black et al. 2004, pp. 193–194.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-07-lugal-e, _Ninurta's Exploits_>> — defeats the demon Asag and his stone army, dams the primeval waters with the stones of the defeated enemy to create the Tigris river system, decrees the fates of every stone on the battlefield, and renames his mother Ninhursaja.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1–729; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–272.]
* <<ch-08-angim, _Ninurta's Return to Nibru_>> — returns from the _kur_ laden with trophies and blazing with uncontained _me-lam_; his parents' combined authority and warmth domesticate his battle-fury.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.1; Black et al. 2004, pp. 192–198.]
* <<ch-09-ninurta-and-the-turtle, _Ninurta and the Turtle_>> — after defeating Anzud and recovering the Tablet of Destinies, tries to retain it for himself; Enki traps him in a pit with a turtle.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]

*Relevance.* Ninurta is the Sumerian warrior god par excellence, the protagonist of the Chaoskampf tradition whose defeat of Asag and subsequent engineering of the Mesopotamian landscape make _Lugal-e_ one of the longest and most heavily copied compositions in the corpus.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–272; Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–164.] Taken together, the three Ninurta compositions form a triptych — cosmic battle, triumphant return, and comic deflation — suggesting that the Sumerian scribes treated their warrior champion in all three registers without apparent contradiction.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 199–201.]

[[char-nisaba]]
==== Nisaba

Goddess of grain and writing, patron deity of the scribal craft, mother of Sud, and in her scribal aspect the presiding goddess of every _edubba_ in Sumer.footnote:[ETCSL 1.2.2, lines 55–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 110–112.]

* <<ch-25-enmerkar-and-en-suhgir-ana, _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_>> — her city Eresh is the setting for Urgirinuna's magical attack and Sagburu's counter-sorcery.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 170–228; Black et al. 2004, pp. 255–258.]

*Relevance.* Nisaba is the patroness under whose authority the scribes themselves worked; her prominence in _Enlil and Sud_ — directing the negotiations that restructure the divine household — gave students who copied the text a vision of their own patron goddess setting terms for the king of the gods.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 111–112.]

[[char-numushda]]

[[char-utu]]
==== Utu

Sun god, god of justice and the boundaries between lands, brother of Inanna and brother-in-law of Dumuzid.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 368–380; Black et al. 2004, p. 222.]

*Relevance.* Utu is the Sumerian god whose reach is universal because his daily crossing of the heavens covers every land. He is the go-to deity for travel, justice, and transformation across cultural and species boundaries, and his interventions tend to be granted on the basis of kinship or piety rather than merit.footnote:[ETCSL 1.1.3, lines 368–380; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 167–180.]

*Relevance.* Uttu represents the point in the Dilmun genealogy at which the pattern breaks: the first goddess to impose a condition on Enki, and the one whose union produces the plants whose consumption triggers the composition's crisis.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 191–196.]

=== Heroes and Mortals

[[char-aga]]
==== Aga

King of Kish, the rival city whose ultimatum forces Gilgamesh's decision in the bicameral assembly.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 1–10; Black et al. 2004, pp. 279–280.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-19-gilgamesh-and-aga, _Gilgamesh and Aga_>> — sends envoys demanding Uruk's corvée labour, besieges the city, is captured, and is magnanimously released by Gilgamesh in acknowledgement of a prior debt.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 1–115; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 141–148.]

*Relevance.* Aga is the historical-scale political adversary in the only Sumerian Gilgamesh composition wholly free of supernatural material; his defeat and release enact a Sumerian theory of kingship built on deliberation and reciprocal obligation rather than force alone.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 141–148; Kramer 1961, pp. 187–190.]

[[char-ansiga-ria]]
==== Ansiga-ria

Minister of En-suhgir-ana, identifier of the sorcerer Urgirinuna as champion for the Aratta side.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 114–150; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 110–118.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-25-enmerkar-and-en-suhgir-ana, _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_>> — secures Urgirinuna for the sorcery contest.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 114–150.]

*Relevance.* A minor courtly figure whose function is to lend plausible bureaucratic texture to En-suhgir-ana's escalation into sorcery.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 110–118.]

[[char-bilulu]]
==== Bilulu

Old woman — or bandit matriarch — held responsible, with her son Girgire, for Dumuzid's death.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 318–320.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. Jacobsen's reconstruction from the fragmentary text identifies her as an old woman of the steppe; beyond that, a specific physical portrait is not provided.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 318–320.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-16-inanna-and-bilulu, _Inanna and Bilulu_>> — confronted by Inanna on the steppe and transformed into a waterskin used for desert libations to the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]

*Relevance.* Bilulu's transformation supplies the aetiology for the desert libation practice in the Dumuzid cult, in which the vessel that holds the water poured for the dead shepherd is, by the text's logic, the punished killer herself. Sumerian divine justice here does not destroy offenders but repurposes them into the ritual apparatus that mourns the victim.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]

[[char-birhurturre]]
==== Birhurturre

A warrior of Uruk who ascends the city wall during Aga's siege.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 75–85; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 145–146.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-19-gilgamesh-and-aga, _Gilgamesh and Aga_>> — his appearance on the wall provokes a violent reaction from Aga's forces.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 75–85.]

*Relevance.* Birhurturre is a named member of Uruk's defence whose appearance marks one of the climactic moments of the siege sequence.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 145–146.]

[[char-enkidu]]
==== Enkidu

Companion and servant of Gilgamesh, his partner across the Sumerian Gilgamesh compositions.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 30–50; Black et al. 2004, pp. 270–271.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1 (Gilgamesh and Aga); 1.8.1.2 (Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven); 1.8.1.4 (Gilgamesh, Enkidu and the Nether World); 1.8.1.5 (Gilgamesh and Huwawa) — none of these Sumerian compositions provides a physical description of Enkidu.] (The Sumerian texts in scope do not describe his body; the familiar hairy, wild-man portrait belongs to the Akkadian Gilgamesh tradition, which is out of scope for this volume.)

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-19-gilgamesh-and-aga, _Gilgamesh and Aga_>> — serves as counsellor and officer in the defence of Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 70–85; Black et al. 2004, pp. 282–283.]
* <<ch-20-gilgamesh-and-the-bull-of-heaven, _Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven_>> — with Gilgamesh slays the Bull; strikes Inanna with a haunch of the beast, provoking her flight.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment D, lines 33–53; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
* <<ch-21-gilgamesh-and-huwawa, _Gilgamesh and Huwawa_>> — accompanies Gilgamesh to the Cedar Forest; in Version A, urges the killing of the disarmed Huwawa and decapitates him.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 180–190; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 165–167.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — volunteers to descend after the lost _pukku_ and _mekku_, violates every taboo Gilgamesh warned him against, is trapped among the dead, and returns as a ghost to deliver the catalogue of afterlife conditions.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 150–303; Black et al. 2004, pp. 290–296.]

*Relevance.* Enkidu in the Sumerian poems is the indispensable second — the companion whose counsel, combat, and ghostly report shape the action in every composition in which he appears. Unlike the Akkadian Gilgamesh epic's Enkidu, here he is not a wild-man foil but the hero's steady partner, and his extended testimony on the fates of the dead in _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_ is the fullest surviving Sumerian statement on the afterlife.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–195; Black et al. 2004, pp. 285–296.]

[[char-enmerkar]]
==== Enmerkar

King of Uruk, son of Utu, legendary founder of the first dynasty, credited with the invention of writing in _Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.3, lines 33–37; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 15–17.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-24-enmerkar-and-the-lord-of-aratta, _Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta_>> — demands the submission of Aratta; conducts the long contest of messengers and riddles; invents writing when the messenger's memory fails.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.3, lines 1–636; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 1–95.]
* <<ch-25-enmerkar-and-en-suhgir-ana, _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_>> — the king whose side wins the sorcery contest against Aratta through Sagburu's counter-magic.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–169.]
* <<ch-26-lugalbanda-in-the-mountain-cave, _Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave_>> — leads the army of Uruk across the mountains, with Lugalbanda as one of his soldiers.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, opening lines.]
* <<ch-27-lugalbanda-and-the-anzud-bird, _Lugalbanda and the Anzud Bird_>> — army stalled before Aratta; dispatches Lugalbanda as supernatural messenger to consult Inanna in Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 330–460.]

*Relevance.* Enmerkar is the peak literary figure of the Aratta cycle, and his invention of writing in the diplomatic contest with Aratta is one of the most celebrated aetiological passages in Sumerian literature — a claim that the cuneiform tablets on which it was copied quietly vindicated.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 236–251; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 1–10.]

[[char-en-suhgir-ana]]
==== En-suhgir-ana

Lord of Aratta in _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_, the Aratta king who challenges Enmerkar to the sorcery contest and, defeated, concedes Uruk's seniority.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, opening and closing lines; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-25-enmerkar-and-en-suhgir-ana, _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_>> — dispatches Urgirinuna against Uruk; after the sorcerer's defeat, acknowledges Enmerkar as the older brother whom he cannot match.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 114–281; Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–261.]

*Relevance.* En-suhgir-ana is the named Aratta king in the final act of the cycle. His concession closes the longer contest between Uruk and Aratta that the unnamed lord had sustained through diplomacy and riddles.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253.]

[[char-gilgamesh]]
==== Gilgamesh

King of Uruk, two-thirds divine hero, Sumerian form Bilgames, the most famous figure in Mesopotamian literature.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–270.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. _Gilgamesh and Aga_ notes that when he ascended the wall "his physical splendour and terrifying presence" had a decisive effect on the besiegers, but the text does not describe those features; it records the effect, not the form.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 85–100.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-19-gilgamesh-and-aga, _Gilgamesh and Aga_>> — consults the elders and the fighting men, chooses resistance, defeats Aga, and releases him in acknowledgement of a prior obligation.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1, lines 1–115; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 141–148.]
* <<ch-20-gilgamesh-and-the-bull-of-heaven, _Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven_>> — slays the Bull of Heaven with an axe of seven talents.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment D, lines 33–53.]
* <<ch-21-gilgamesh-and-huwawa, _Gilgamesh and Huwawa_>> — journeys to the Cedar Forest with Enkidu, tricks Huwawa out of his seven auras, and returns with the guardian's head.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5 and 1.8.1.5.1; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — clears the huluppu tree of its infesting creatures for Inanna; fashions the _pukku_ and _mekku_; sits at the gate of the nether world to receive Enkidu's ghost.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 20–303.]
* <<ch-23-death-of-gilgamesh, _The Death of Gilgamesh_>> — confronts his mortality, receives elaborate funeral rites, and is elevated to the status of judge among the dead.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.3; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 195–208.]
* <<ch-30-death-of-ur-namma, _The Death of Ur-Namma_>> — sits as a judge of the dead and receives Ur-Namma's offerings.footnote:[ETCSL 2.4.1.1, lines 180–200; Flückiger-Hawker 1999.]

*Relevance.* Gilgamesh is the central heroic figure of the Sumerian narrative corpus, the king around whom five distinct compositions cluster without forming a unified epic. The Sumerian poems treat him across political, heroic, cosmological, and eschatological registers; taken together they articulate the bargain that his tradition insists on — fame, not eternal life, as the only compensation for mortality.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.1–1.8.1.5; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 141–208; Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–296.]

[[char-girgire]]
==== Girgire

Son of Bilulu, implicated with his mother in the death of Dumuzid.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 318–320.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-16-inanna-and-bilulu, _Inanna and Bilulu_>> — transformed by Inanna into a desert spirit or protective genius of the steppe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.4; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]

*Relevance.* Girgire is a secondary target of Inanna's vengeance, and like his mother is repurposed rather than destroyed — absorbed into the ritual apparatus of the Dumuzid cult as a steppe presence.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–325.]

[[char-gudam]]
==== Gudam

A monstrous creature who terrorises Uruk, devouring its food and drink.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The opening lines that would have described him are lost in the tablet's damage.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4, opening section (fragmentary).]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-13-inanna-and-gudam, _Inanna and Gudam_>> — subdued by a junior fisherman of Inanna's with a double-axe, then spared by the goddess and resettled near Zabalam as a protector of Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.4.]

*Relevance.* Gudam is unusual within the Sumerian patron-versus-monster pattern: rather than being destroyed, he is rehabilitated and put to work for the city he had been devouring, a distinctive form of Sumerian divine justice.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, p. 85; Kramer 1961, pp. 83–85.]

[[char-lord-of-aratta]]
==== Lord of Aratta

The unnamed king of Aratta in _Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta_, Enmerkar's diplomatic adversary.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.3, lines 200–240; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 32–40.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-24-enmerkar-and-the-lord-of-aratta, _Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta_>> — contests Enmerkar's demands, claims Inanna's favour for himself, matches riddle with counter-riddle, and ultimately accepts some form of accommodation after receiving Enmerkar's unreadable tablet.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.3, lines 200–636; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 32–90.]

*Relevance.* The lord of Aratta is a worthy adversary whose articulate resistance is the reason the composition is a contest rather than a proclamation. His defeat by writing — a message he cannot read — is the cycle's clearest demonstration that Uruk's advantage is ultimately technological.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 72–85.]

[[char-lugalbanda]]
==== Lugalbanda

Soldier and later king of Uruk, father of Gilgamesh in tradition, the pious individual whose personal devotion becomes the theme of a linked pair of compositions.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, opening lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 262–263.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The text compares his restored running to that of a "horse of the mountains," but this is a simile for speed, not a body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 264–275.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-26-lugalbanda-in-the-mountain-cave, _Lugalbanda in the Mountain Cave_>> — falls sick on the march to Aratta, is left for dead in a cave, and recovers through his prayers to Utu, Inanna, and Nanna-Suen.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.1, lines 71–325; Black et al. 2004, pp. 262–268.]
* <<ch-27-lugalbanda-and-the-anzud-bird, _Lugalbanda and the Anzud Bird_>> — cares for the Anzud chick and receives from its parent the gift of supernatural speed; returns to the stalled army at Aratta and runs Enmerkar's message to Inanna in Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2; Black et al. 2004, pp. 12–22.]

*Relevance.* Lugalbanda is the Sumerian exemplar of piety and reciprocity rewarded: the abandoned soldier who becomes indispensable through attention, humility, and proper sacrifice.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–344.] The two compositions that carry his name were heavily copied in the scribal curriculum and made him, alongside Gilgamesh, one of the most widely known Sumerian heroes.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, manuscript list.]

[[char-sagburu]]
==== Sagburu

"Wise Woman" of Eresh, the titled figure whose counter-sorcery defeats the foreign sorcerer Urgirinuna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 222–273; Black et al. 2004, pp. 257–258.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-25-enmerkar-and-en-suhgir-ana, _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_>> — defeats Urgirinuna through five rounds of animal-conjuring and throws him from the bank of the Euphrates.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 222–273; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 138–155.]

*Relevance.* Sagburu is the only Sumerian narrative figure called "Wise Woman" in a formal sense and one of the few women in the corpus to prevail in a public magical contest. Her victory encodes the argument that Uruk's protection does not depend on imported specialists but runs through the local and divinely favoured territory itself.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 252–253; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]

[[char-shu-kale-tuda]]
==== Shu-kale-tuda

A mortal gardener, the perpetrator of the central crime in _Inanna and Shu-kale-tuda_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 78–79.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-12-inanna-and-shu-kale-tuda, _Inanna and Shu-kale-tuda_>> — rapes the sleeping Inanna in the shade of the tree he planted; hides in the cities through three plagues; is eventually found by Inanna and condemned to death.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 78–84.]

*Relevance.* Shu-kale-tuda is the mortal whose crime against a goddess drives the most sustained plague-and-pursuit narrative in the corpus. The composition closes on the paradox that the text condemning him preserves his name: his infamy is the very medium of his posthumous survival.footnote:[ETCSL 1.3.3; Black et al. 2004, pp. 83–84.]

[[char-shulgi]]
==== Shulgi

Historical second king of the Third Dynasty of Ur (reigned ca. 2094–2047 BCE), son of Ur-Namma; invoked in the text by the divine-sonship epithet "son of Enlil," conventional for Ur III royal titulature.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 170–190; Flückiger-Hawker 1999 on Ur III royal titulature.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-34-debate-bird-and-fish, _The Debate between Bird and Fish_>> — renders the final verdict in favour of Bird after the contestants resort to violence; the closing doxology praises "Father Enki" whose wisdom is understood to have guided the king's judgment.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 170–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 239–240.]

*Relevance.* Shulgi's appearance as judge in _Bird and Fish_ puts a historical Ur III monarch at the centre of a mythological debate — one of the few in-scope compositions in which a named king delivers the verdict rather than a god. The role reflects the Ur III dynasty's self-presentation as guarantor of cosmic and social order, continuous with the posthumous dignity granted to his father Ur-Namma in the royal underworld narrative.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235; Flückiger-Hawker 1999, commentary.]

[[char-ur-namma]]
==== Ur-Namma

Historical founder of the Third Dynasty of Ur (reigned ca. 2112–2095 BCE), builder of the great ziggurat at Ur, promulgator of one of the earliest known law codes.footnote:[ETCSL 2.4.1.1, introductory context; Kramer 1961, pp. 128–129.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The text compares his dying body to "a crushed vessel" — a simile for desolation, not a bodily portrait.footnote:[ETCSL 2.4.1.1, lines 1–30; Flückiger-Hawker 1999.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-30-death-of-ur-namma, _The Death of Ur-Namma_>> — dies of illness, lying abandoned on the battlefield "like a crushed vessel" (the opening lines are partly reconstructed); is prepared with elaborate funerary rites, presents gifts to Ereshkigal, Gilgamesh, Ningishzida, and the seven chief porters of the netherworld, and is given a throne among the dead. He laments the walls and temples he could not finish.footnote:[ETCSL 2.4.1.1; Flückiger-Hawker 1999.]

*Relevance.* _The Death of Ur-Namma_ is the most detailed Sumerian account of a king's entry into the underworld, and the composition functioned as royal propaganda for the Ur III dynasty — demonstrating that even in death the king of Ur retained dignity and commanded respect from the powers below.footnote:[Flückiger-Hawker 1999, commentary; Black et al. 2004, pp. 56–60.]

[[char-urgirinuna]]
==== Urgirinuna

Male sorcerer from the destroyed city of Hamazu, En-suhgir-ana's hired champion in the magical contest against Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 135–180; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 118–130.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-25-enmerkar-and-en-suhgir-ana, _Enmerkar and En-suhgir-ana_>> — redirects the milk of cows and goats to the wrong extremities as a first magical assault on Sumer; defeated in five rounds of animal-conjuring by Sagburu and killed by being thrown from the riverbank.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.4, lines 170–273; Black et al. 2004, pp. 255–258.]

*Relevance.* Urgirinuna is the Sumerian literary portrait of the unaffiliated supernatural specialist — the rootless technician whose skill is formidable but whose lack of divine patronage leaves him structurally inferior to the local defender.footnote:[Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 97–105.]

[[char-ziusudra]]
==== Ziusudra

Pious king, the righteous survivor of the flood in the Sumerian Eridu Genesis.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, column iii; Jacobsen 1987, p. 147.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-28-eridu-flood-story, _The Flood Story: The Eridu Genesis_>> — warned by Enki through a reed wall, builds a boat, survives the flood, prostrates himself before Utu, offers sacrifices, and is granted eternal life in Dilmun by An and Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.4, columns iii–vi; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 147–150.]

*Relevance.* Ziusudra is the Sumerian flood hero, the earliest surviving figure of the pattern later elaborated by the Akkadian Atrahasis and Utnapishtim and, at further remove, by the biblical Noah. His translation into Dilmun is the only Sumerian instance in the corpus of a mortal raised to the condition of the gods.footnote:[Kramer 1961, pp. 97–101; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 149–150.]

=== Creatures and Monsters

[[char-anzud]]
==== Anzud

Divine lion-headed eagle, a creature of terrifying size whose wingspan darkens the mountains.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 30–70; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 322–325.]

*Physical description.* Of enormous wingspan, the parent bird radiates the _me-lam_, the divine radiance that emanates from gods, monsters, and sacred objects.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 140–160.] The lion-headed-eagle identification is iconographic rather than textual: it derives from the wider Mesopotamian visual tradition — cylinder seals and the Imdugud relief from Tell al-'Ubaid — rather than from any surviving physical description in the Sumerian compositions.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 329–332; the lion-head identification is iconographic, based on cylinder seals and the Imdugud relief from Tell al-'Ubaid.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-09-ninurta-and-the-turtle, _Ninurta and the Turtle_>> — the composition presupposes that Anzud has stolen the Tablet of Destinies from the _abzu_ and has been defeated by Ninurta before the surviving text begins.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.3, lines 1–15; Kramer 1961, pp. 82–83.]
* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — nests in the crown of Inanna's huluppu tree with its young; Gilgamesh's killing of the serpent at the roots sends the Anzud fleeing to the mountains.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 40–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 287–289.]
* <<ch-27-lugalbanda-and-the-anzud-bird, _Lugalbanda and the Anzud Bird_>> — grants Lugalbanda supernatural speed in gratitude for his care of its chick, imposing a secrecy condition on the gift.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.2.2, lines 140–290; Black et al. 2004, pp. 15–17.]

*Relevance.* Anzud is the great divine bird of Sumerian tradition, a being whose capacity for both theft and generosity makes him the pivot around which several of the corpus's most consequential narratives turn.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 320–344; Vanstiphout 2003, pp. 215–275.]

[[char-asag]]
==== Asag

Monstrous demon of sickness, born of An and Ki, commander of the stone army in _Lugal-e_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 30–70; Black et al. 2004, pp. 165–167.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The text describes the effects of his _me-lam_ — fish boil in the rivers, vegetation withers — but these are consequences of his presence, not a portrait of his body.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 60–90; Black et al. 2004, pp. 166–167.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-07-lugal-e, _Ninurta's Exploits_>> — raises an army of stones from the mountains against the gods and is defeated by Ninurta in the central battle of the composition.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 30–270; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 238–248.]

*Relevance.* Asag is the primordial adversary of the Sumerian Chaoskampf tradition, the enemy whose defeat and whose subsequent structural role in the landscape allow Ninurta to reshape southern Mesopotamia into its cultivable geography.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 233–272; Kramer 1961, pp. 80–83.]

[[char-bull-of-heaven]]
==== Bull of Heaven

Cosmic beast sent by Inanna against Uruk; in Sumerian _gud an-na_ and known in scholarship as Gugalanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment B–D; ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 80–90.]

*Physical description.* A giant bull, large enough to devour the pasture, drink the river in great slurps, break the palm trees, and submerge the district of Kulaba within Uruk.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment B, lines 55–63.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-20-gilgamesh-and-the-bull-of-heaven, _Gilgamesh and the Bull of Heaven_>> — attacks Uruk; slain jointly by Gilgamesh and Enkidu, its skull shattered by Gilgamesh's seven-talent axe.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.2, Segment D, lines 33–53; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]
* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — identified with Gugalanna, the husband of Ereshkigal, whose funeral rites Inanna claims as her reason for descending.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 80–90; Black et al. 2004, p. 55.]

*Relevance.* The Bull of Heaven is the first attested instance of the cosmic-bull-slaying motif in world literature, and its story was so widely represented in cylinder-seal iconography that the visual tradition outlasted the textual one.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 10–12.]

[[char-galla]]
==== Galla demons

Underworld demons who enforce the laws of the dead; they do not eat food, drink water, accept bribes, or hear pleas.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 285–300; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 217–218.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The texts specify what they do not do — they do not eat, drink, or accept bribes — rather than how they look.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 285–300.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — escort the revived Inanna to the surface, seize Dumuzid as her substitute, and pursue him through his transformations.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 285–375.]
* <<ch-15-dumuzids-dream, _Dumuzid's Dream_>> — pursue Dumuzid across the steppe through his successive transformations, interrogate Geshtinanna without result, and are led to him by a friend's betrayal.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.3, lines 55–260; Alster 1972, pp. 55–80.]
* <<ch-29-ningishzidas-journey, _Ningishzida's Journey to the Nether World_>> — seize Ningishzida and drag him to the nether world.footnote:[ETCSL 1.7.3; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 57–59.]

*Relevance.* The _galla_ are the mechanical arm of underworld enforcement — the texts' recurring instrument for demonstrating that death's claims are non-negotiable and that no deity's status lifts a body from their grip once it is theirs.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 217–218; ETCSL 1.4.1.]

[[char-huwawa]]
==== Huwawa

Guardian of the Cedar Forest, appointed by Enlil, wielder of seven _me-lam_ — the divine radiances that guard his domain.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 90–160; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 155–160.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources beyond the attribution of the seven auras.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5, lines 90–160.] (The familiar Akkadian portrait of a giant with a lion's face belongs to _Humbaba_ in the Akkadian tradition, which is out of scope.)

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-21-gilgamesh-and-huwawa, _Gilgamesh and Huwawa_>> — tricked by Gilgamesh into surrendering his seven auras one by one for gifts; pleads for mercy; decapitated by Enkidu. In Version B the guardian's weeping appeal is treated with particular weight.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.5 and 1.8.1.5.1; George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174.]

*Relevance.* Huwawa stands at the centre of the Sumerian corpus's most morally ambiguous episode: a righteous guardian undone by a deception his divine mandate did not anticipate. The composition's refusal to resolve the ethics of his killing — Enlil's anger at the heroes is on record — makes him a figure through whom the literature tests the costs of heroic fame.footnote:[George 2003, vol. 1, pp. 149–174; Black et al. 2004, pp. 269–278.]

[[char-kurgarra]]
==== Kurgarra

One of two small, sexless creatures fashioned by Enki from the dirt under his fingernails to revive Inanna; its companion is the _galatur_.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 220–235; Black et al. 2004, p. 58; Sladek 1974.]

*Physical description.* Small and sexless; beyond this explicit textual statement, no further physical description survives in the in-scope sources.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 220–245.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-14-inannas-descent, _Inanna's Descent to the Nether World_>> — with the _galatur_, mourns in sympathy with Ereshkigal, receives the corpse as a gift, and sprinkles the "food of life" to revive Inanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.4.1, lines 235–280; Black et al. 2004, pp. 58–59.]

*Relevance.* With the _galatur_, the _kurgarra_ is Enki's engineered answer to the problem of the underworld's borders: a being without fixed category can cross where gods and mortals cannot.footnote:[Sladek 1974; Black et al. 2004, pp. 52–53.]

[[char-kisikil-lilla]]
==== Phantom maid (ki-sikil-lil-la)

Phantom maid who dwells in the trunk of Inanna's huluppu tree.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 40–60.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources. The Sumerian name _ki-sikil-lil-la_ denotes her category rather than her appearance; the identification with Lilith is contested.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 40–60.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-22-gilgamesh-enkidu-and-the-nether-world, _Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World_>> — one of the three creatures infesting the huluppu tree; flees to the wilderness when Gilgamesh clears the tree.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 40–80.]

*Relevance.* The phantom maid is the trunk-dweller in the tripartite infestation of the sacred tree; her presence completes the vertical cosmology in miniature — serpent at the roots, Anzud in the crown, phantom in the trunk — that Gilgamesh dismantles for Inanna.footnote:[ETCSL 1.8.1.4, lines 40–80; Black et al. 2004, pp. 287–289.]

[[char-sharur]]
==== Sharur

Ninurta's sentient mace, literally "smiter of thousands," the warrior god's battlefield counsellor.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–165.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources beyond its identity as a mace.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, passim — Sharur is consistently named and functions as Ninurta's mace-weapon; see Black et al. 2004, pp. 163–165.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-07-lugal-e, _Ninurta's Exploits_>> — speaks, evaluates situations, flies from the battlefield to Enlil at Nippur, and carries encouragement back to Ninurta during the Asag campaign.footnote:[ETCSL 1.6.2, lines 20–180; Jacobsen 1987, pp. 235–245.]

*Relevance.* Sharur gives the Sumerian warrior-god narrative its most distinctive literary device: a weapon with a mind, whose counsel transforms Ninurta from a mere force of destruction into a warrior whose strength is paired with intelligence.footnote:[Jacobsen 1987, pp. 235–245.]

=== Personified Concepts

[[char-bird]]
==== Bird (Mušen)

Divine personification of the avian realm in _The Debate between Bird and Fish_.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources beyond the category itself; the text celebrates Bird's beauty, plumage, and song but does not individualise the figure.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 60–90.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-34-debate-bird-and-fish, _The Debate between Bird and Fish_>> — boasts of beauty and of its place in the E-kur; exchanges insults and violence with Fish; is declared the winner by King Shulgi.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 30–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–240.]

*Relevance.* Bird embodies the aesthetic and liturgical dimension of Mesopotamian ecological categories; the verdict in her favour ties those dimensions to the royal and temple order that Shulgi represents.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235.]

[[char-copper]]
==== Copper (Urudu)

Divine personification of copper and the practical tools made from it — hoes, adzes, axes, sickles.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segment D.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-35-debate-copper-and-silver, _The Debate between Copper and Silver_>> — argues utility against prestige, mocks Silver for being buried between uses, and wins the verdict from Enlil.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segment D–I.]

*Relevance.* Copper represents the indispensability of the workaday metal on which Mesopotamian agriculture and construction depended; the verdict in its favour echoes the Hoe's victory in _Hoe and Plough_ — utility ranking above ceremony.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segment I.]

[[char-date-palm]]
==== Date Palm (Gišimmar)

Divine personification of the date palm, the supreme cultivated tree of Mesopotamia.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–245.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-36-debate-date-palm-and-tamarisk, _The Debate between Date Palm and Tamarisk_>> — argues superiority on grounds of economic yield — dates, syrup, palm wine, fibre, timber — and of its status as a cultivated tree tended by human labour.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines.]

*Relevance.* Date Palm represents the productive, cultivated aspect of the Sumerian horticultural world; the loss of the composition's verdict leaves open whether the text ultimately preferred cultivated productivity or untended resilience.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–246.]

[[char-fish]]
==== Fish (Ku₆)

Divine personification of fish and the aquatic realm in _The Debate between Bird and Fish_.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 1–30; Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–235.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources; Bird's insults impute a flabby mouth and an unpleasant smell, but these are rhetorical attacks rather than attested description.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 100–120.]

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-34-debate-bird-and-fish, _The Debate between Bird and Fish_>> — opens hostilities; claims superiority through purity and abundance; destroys Bird's nest and loses the verdict to Shulgi.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.5, lines 30–190.]

*Relevance.* Fish embodies the economic value of the marsh and river economies that fed the Mesopotamian temple; the composition's unusual escalation into physical violence marks Fish as the most dramatically treated of the debate disputants.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 234–240.]

[[char-hoe]]
==== Hoe (Al)

Divine personification of the hoe, the essential implement for digging irrigation channels, breaking ground, laying foundations, and planting orchards.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 80–150; Civil 1994, passim.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-31-debate-hoe-and-plough, _The Debate between Hoe and Plough_>> — disputes with the Plough; argues from range and year-round utility; cites its primordial creation by Enlil; is declared the winner.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 1–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–218.]

*Relevance.* The Hoe's victory ratifies the Sumerian conviction that infrastructure — canals, foundations, the constant maintenance of the irrigated landscape — is prior to and more fundamental than the seasonal operations built atop it.footnote:[Civil 1994, passim; Black et al. 2004, pp. 217–218.]

[[char-plough]]
==== Plough (Apin)

Divine personification of the seeder-plough used for large-scale cereal agriculture, drawn by oxen.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 20–80; Civil 1994, passim.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-31-debate-hoe-and-plough, _The Debate between Hoe and Plough_>> — opens the dispute on grounds of prestige and its role in temple-offering grain; loses Enlil's verdict to the Hoe.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.1, lines 20–190; Black et al. 2004, pp. 211–218.]

*Relevance.* The Plough stands for the institutional prestige of Mesopotamian cereal agriculture; its loss in the debate reflects the composition's underlying argument that ceremonial and institutional primacy is not the same as fundamental necessity.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 210–218.]

[[char-silver]]
==== Silver (Ku-babbar)

Divine personification of silver, the metal of banquets, adornment, and royal treasure.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segment A.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-35-debate-copper-and-silver, _The Debate between Copper and Silver_>> — claims superiority through prestige and ceremonial function; mocked by Copper for being buried between uses; loses the verdict to Copper.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segments A–I.]

*Relevance.* Silver represents the ceremonial and prestige-metal economy of Mesopotamia; its defeat by Copper carries the same moral as the Hoe's victory over the Plough — workaday utility ranked above display.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.6, Segment I.]

[[char-tamarisk]]
==== Tamarisk (Šinig)

Divine personification of the tamarisk, a hardy wild tree used for ritual purification and fuel.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines; Black et al. 2004, pp. 245–246.]

*Physical description.* No physical description survives in the in-scope sources.

*Appears in:*

* <<ch-36-debate-date-palm-and-tamarisk, _The Debate between Date Palm and Tamarisk_>> — argues superiority through hardiness, independence from cultivation, and ritual purity.footnote:[ETCSL 5.3.7, surviving lines.]

*Relevance.* Tamarisk represents the virtue of self-sufficient, uncultivated nature — the steppe against the garden — in one of the few places in the Sumerian corpus where that side of the dichotomy gets its case argued at length.footnote:[Black et al. 2004, pp. 244–246.]
